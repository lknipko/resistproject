CIVIC ACTION PLATFORM: PRIORITY ISSUE IDENTIFICATION AND RESOURCE MAPPING
A Strategic Foundation Report for Citizen Engagement (2025-2026)
Executive Summary: The Structural Transformation of the Republic
As of January 24, 2026, the United States operates under a fundamentally altered governance structure, distinct from the political and legal norms that defined the previous half-century. This report serves as the comprehensive foundational document for the launch of a new wiki-based civic action platform. Its purpose is to provide a rigorous, exhaustive analysis of the government actions taken since January 2025, moving beyond superficial news cycles to examine the deep mechanics of the current administration's legislative and executive agenda.
The past twelve months have witnessed a synchronized realignment of the federal government's three branches. The legislative achievement of the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (OBBBA) 1 has permanently reshaped the tax code and the social safety net, shifting the nation from a model of social insurance to one of private asset accumulation and fiscal devolution. Simultaneously, the executive branch has aggressively utilized Article II authority to redefine the boundaries of the civil service through Schedule F 3 and to challenge the constitutional definition of citizenship itself via Executive Order 14160.4 Crucially, these shifts have been cemented—rather than checked—by a Supreme Court that has severely curtailed the power of the federal judiciary to intervene, most notably through the dismantling of universal injunctions in Trump v. CASA, Inc..5
For the citizen seeking to understand and act within this new reality, the old maps no longer apply. The centralization of executive personnel power is paradoxically paired with a radical decentralization of legal rights and public health policy, creating a fragmented landscape where a citizen’s rights and access to care are increasingly determined by state lines and local jurisdiction.
This report identifies seven priority issue areas that must form the core of the civic action platform. For each area, we provide a forensic analysis of key developments, identify the primary source documents necessary for verification and legal action, and outline meaningful, high-impact opportunities for civic engagement. The analysis assumes a sophisticated user base—activists, legal professionals, policy researchers, and concerned citizens—who require actionable intelligence rather than rhetoric.

Priority Issue Area 1: The Transformation of the Fiscal & Social Safety Net
The "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (OBBBA) and Medicaid Restructuring
The defining legislative event of the 119th Congress is the enactment of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), Public Law 119-21, signed by President Donald Trump on July 4, 2025.6 This massive reconciliation package, passed following the debt ceiling crisis of early 2025, represents a philosophical overhaul of the American social contract. It merges permanent tax reductions with profound structural changes to federal healthcare funding and nutrition assistance, effectively ending the era of the Great Society-style safety net.
Key Developments and Structural Analysis
1. The Debt Ceiling as a Lever for Permanent Change The context for the OBBBA was the reinstatement of the federal debt limit on January 2, 2025.8 Following months of "extraordinary measures" by the Treasury Department 9, the OBBBA resolved the crisis by raising the debt ceiling by $5 trillion to a new limit of $41.1 trillion.10 However, this increase was purchased at the cost of significant structural reforms to mandatory spending. The Act permanently codifies the individual income tax rates from the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA), which were set to expire in 2025.6 This locks in the 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, and 37% brackets, fundamentally altering the long-term revenue baseline of the federal government and necessitating deep cuts elsewhere to achieve budget neutrality under reconciliation rules.
2. The Dismantling of Medicaid Financing (Fiscal Federalism) The most consequential provisions of the OBBBA target Medicaid, not just through direct cuts, but through the alteration of fiscal federalism mechanisms. The Act prohibits states from using provider taxes to finance their share of Medicaid spending.12 Historically, nearly all states have used taxes on hospitals, nursing homes, and care facilities to generate revenue that is then matched by the federal government. By banning this practice, the OBBBA effectively caps the federal government's liability while forcing state legislatures into a "fiscal trap": they must either raise general income/sales taxes to replace the lost provider tax revenue or slash Medicaid services.
Furthermore, the Act introduces strict work requirements for Medicaid eligibility and mandates a 6-month eligibility reverification cycle.12 The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) estimates that these administrative burdens, combined with the financing changes, will result in 7.8 million Americans losing health insurance coverage by 2034.14 This provision creates an "enrollment cliff" where eligible citizens lose coverage simply due to the friction of paperwork and the inability of underfunded state agencies to process verifications in time.
3. Targeted Funding Restrictions ("Prohibited Entities") Section 71113 of the OBBBA introduces a specific prohibition on federal Medicaid funds flowing to "Prohibited Entities," defined by criteria that effectively target organizations primarily engaged in reproductive health services, such as Planned Parenthood.15 This provision applies retroactively to entities that received more than $800,000 in Medicaid payments in FY 2023. While this section has been challenged in court (Planned Parenthood v. Kennedy), the Supreme Court’s ruling in Trump v. CASA (limiting nationwide injunctions) means that relief may be geographically limited, creating a patchwork of access where clinics in some states close while others remain open pending litigation.
4. Nutrition Assistance Retrenchment Title I of the OBBBA addresses the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP). Crucially, Section 10001 prohibits the USDA from increasing the cost of the Thrifty Food Plan (TFP) based on re-evaluations of the market basket.1 The TFP is the basis for calculating SNAP benefits. By freezing the composition of the TFP and allowing only CPI-based inflation adjustments, the Act effectively decouples SNAP benefits from the changing reality of nutritional science and dietary patterns. Over time, this results in a real-dollar decrease in the purchasing power of benefits relative to the actual cost of a healthy diet. Additionally, the Act expands work requirements for "Able-Bodied Adults Without Dependents" (ABAWDs), further restricting access.16

To support citizen research, the platform must host or link to the following primary documents:
* Legislative Text: One Big Beautiful Bill Act, H.R. 1, 119th Congress (2025), specifically Title I (Agriculture) and Title IV (Energy and Commerce/Medicaid)..1
* Public Law: P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025..2
* Fiscal Analysis: Congressional Budget Office (CBO) Cost Estimate for H.R. 1, outlining the $840.2 billion reduction in Medicaid outlays and the coverage loss projections..14
* Legal Challenge: Planned Parenthood v. Kennedy, Preliminary Injunction Order regarding Section 71113 (D. Mass, July 2025)..15
* State Impact Reports: State-level budget analyses detailing reliance on provider taxes (e.g., from State Budget Officers or think tanks).
Strategic Insight: The "Starve the Beast" Mechanics
The OBBBA does not merely cut funding; it alters the architecture of the welfare state. By attacking the provider tax mechanism, the federal government acts to "starve the beast" at the state level. The political fallout is designed to occur in state capitols, where governors and state legislators will be blamed for cutting services or raising taxes. This decentralizes the conflict, making it harder for a national coalition to oppose the changes, as the battles will be fought in 50 separate budget hearings. The freeze on the Thrifty Food Plan is a similar long-term structural degradation—a "quiet cut" that compounds annually, avoiding the headline shock of a direct benefit reduction while achieving the same result over a decade.
Action Opportunities for the Platform
1. State Budget Watchdogs: The platform must create a "State Budget Tracker." As the provider tax ban takes effect (Jan 1, 2026, for some provisions), state legislatures will be drafting emergency budgets. Citizens need tools to identify:
   * How much revenue their state loses from the provider tax ban.
   * Whether the Governor proposes to backfill this with general funds or cut Medicaid reimbursement rates.
   * Dates of legislative committee hearings where testimony is accepted.
2. "Enrollment Defense" Corps: With the 6-month reverification rule and work requirements, the primary threat to coverage is now administrative friction. The platform should host training modules for "Medicaid Navigators"—volunteers who can help neighbors scan documents, navigate complex state portals, and submit reverification paperwork on time. This is a direct service model of civic action.
3. Clinic Defense Funds: In states where Section 71113 forces the closure of reproductive health clinics due to Medicaid defunding, communities must organize alternative municipal funding streams or private philanthropy to keep doors open. The platform can map these "at-risk" clinics.
4. SNAP Impact Monitoring: Users can participate in a distributed data collection project, tracking the actual cost of a healthy diet in their zip code versus the SNAP allotment, providing real-world data to challenge the "frozen" Thrifty Food Plan metrics in future litigation or legislation.

Priority Issue Area 2: The Redefinition of Citizenship & Immigration
Executive Order 14160 and the Challenge to Birthright Citizenship
On Inauguration Day, January 20, 2025, President Trump signed Executive Order 14160, titled "Protecting the Meaning and Value of American Citizenship".4 This executive action represents the most direct challenge to the 14th Amendment’s Citizenship Clause since its ratification, fundamentally redefining who is recognized as a U.S. citizen at birth.
Key Developments and Structural Analysis
1. The "Subject to Jurisdiction" Theory
EO 14160 directs federal agencies to adopt a restrictive interpretation of the 14th Amendment phrase "subject to the jurisdiction thereof." The administration argues that this phrase implies a requirement of complete political allegiance to the United States, which they contend foreign nationals cannot possess. Consequently, the order asserts that children born in the U.S. are not citizens if their parents are:
* Unlawfully present in the United States; or
* Lawfully present but in a "temporary" status (e.g., tourists, students, temporary workers) without the consent of the U.S. government to settle permanently.4
2. Bureaucratic Implementation: Passport Denials The impact of EO 14160 was immediate and bureaucratic. The order directs the State Department and the Social Security Administration to deny passports and SSNs to children who fall under the new exclusions. On July 25, 2025, U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) issued Policy Alert IP-2025-0001, outlining the "Implementation Plan of Executive Order 14160".18 This plan instructs officers to request proof of parental status during applications for benefits for U.S.-born children.
Crucially, the policy creates a new administrative category: "Registered Non-Citizen Births." Children born on U.S. soil to excluded parents are issued birth certificates by states (which control vital records) but are denied federal recognition of citizenship, effectively rendering them stateless if their parents' home countries also do not claim them.
3. The Legal Battle: Trump v. Barbara Civil rights organizations immediately challenged the order. The lead case, Trump v. Barbara (Docket No. 25-365), is currently a class action before the Supreme Court.19 While a district court in New Hampshire initially granted a preliminary injunction blocking the order for the class of plaintiffs, the Supreme Court's subsequent ruling in Trump v. CASA (see Issue Area 7) creates significant uncertainty about the scope of protection for those not explicitly named in the suit. The administration has continued to enforce the policy in jurisdictions or against individuals not covered by specific court orders, creating a chaotic landscape where a child's citizenship recognition depends on whether their parents have joined a lawsuit.
Primary Sources for the Platform
* Executive Action: Executive Order 14160, "Protecting the Meaning and Value of American Citizenship," 90 FR 8449 (Jan. 29, 2025)..4
* Agency Guidance: USCIS Policy Alert IP-2025-0001, "Implementation Plan of Executive Order 14160" (July 25, 2025)..18
* Litigation Filings: Trump v. Barbara, Brief for the Petitioners (U.S. Supreme Court, 2026)..19
* Amicus Briefs: League of Women Voters Amicus Brief in Washington v. Trump (May 2025)..21
Strategic Insight: Executive Modification of the Constitution
The administration is attempting to amend the Constitution via executive order, bypassing the Article V amendment process. By framing the issue as one of regulatory interpretation of the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) and passport issuance rules, they force the judiciary to rule on administrative law grounds first. The "temporary status" inclusion is particularly aggressive—it targets not just undocumented immigrants but legal residents (students, H1B workers), vastly expanding the pool of affected children. This creates a "chilling effect" on legal immigration, as highly skilled workers may avoid the U.S. if their children will be denied citizenship.
Action Opportunities for the Platform
1. "Proof of Status" Kits: For parents who are citizens or Lawful Permanent Residents (LPRs), the burden of proof has shifted. They must now aggressively document their status at the time of their child's birth to avoid erroneous denial. The platform should provide checklists of necessary documents (e.g., physical presence evidence, visa timestamps) to bring to the hospital and passport agency.
2. Litigation Recruitment Portal: Because universal injunctions are off the table, the legal strategy relies on class actions. The platform should serve as a secure intake portal for families denied passports, connecting them with litigators (ACLU, CASA, etc.) to join the Barbara class or file parallel suits in other circuits.
3. State Vital Records Advocacy: While the federal government controls passports, states control birth certificates. Activists must pressure State Registrars of Vital Statistics to refuse any federal request to alter birth certificates or mark them as "non-citizen," maintaining the state-level recognition of jus soli.

Priority Issue Area 3: Economic Security & The Financialization of Social Policy
"Trump Accounts" and the Shift to Private Asset Welfare
While the OBBBA retrenched the traditional safety net, it simultaneously introduced a new vehicle for social policy: the "Trump Account" (Section 70204 of the OBBBA). This policy represents a shift toward "asset-based welfare," prioritizing the accumulation of private capital over social insurance.
Key Developments and Structural Analysis
1. The Mechanics of the Trump Account The OBBBA establishes a tax-advantaged savings account for every eligible child born in the U.S. between 2025 and 2028.7 The accounts are structured as follows:
* Seed Funding: The federal government provides a one-time $1,000 contribution at birth (or upon account opening).
* Contribution Limits: Families can contribute up to $5,000 annually with after-tax dollars. These contributions grow tax-deferred.
* Employer Match: Employers can contribute up to $2,500 annually per child, which is tax-free to the employee and deductible for the employer.7
* Lock-Up Period: Funds generally cannot be withdrawn until the child turns 18, at which point the account converts to a traditional IRA.23
2. The Wealth Transfer Implications While the $1,000 seed grant is progressive (reaching all eligible newborns), the structure of the account heavily favors wealthy families. A family that maxes out the $5,000 contribution annually for 18 years will transfer over $90,000 in principal (plus investment growth) to their child tax-advantaged. Low-income families, who cannot afford to save, will only have the initial $1,000 plus modest growth. Critics argue this effectively creates a tax shelter for intergenerational wealth transfer under the guise of child support, exacerbating wealth inequality.24
3. Corporate Integration Major corporations have begun integrating Trump Accounts into their benefits packages. For example, Dell has pledged additional contributions for children in specific zip codes.25 This signals a shift where child welfare becomes a component of private employment benefits rather than a public guarantee.
Primary Sources for the Platform
* Legislative Text: One Big Beautiful Bill Act, Section 70204 ("Working Families Tax Cuts")..7
* Regulatory Guidance: IRS Notice 2025-57 and Draft Form 4547, "Trump Account Election(s).".26
* Financial Analysis: Vanguard Report, "What to know about new Trump accounts for kids" (2026)..23
Strategic Insight: The Privatization of the Safety Net
The simultaneous cutting of Medicaid (social insurance) and creation of Trump Accounts (private assets) is a deliberate ideological swap. The government is retreating from the role of protecting citizens from risk (health coverage) and moving toward a role of facilitating risk-taking (investment). For the civic platform, the challenge is dual: helping low-income families capture the free $1,000 while politically organizing against the regressive nature of the contribution caps.
Action Opportunities for the Platform
1. Access Facilitation: The IRS Draft Form 4547 26 is the key to accessing the $1,000. Many unbanked or transient families will miss this. The platform should host a "Claim Your Thousand" campaign with simplified, multilingual guides on how to file Form 4547 and open a custodial account at zero-fee institutions.
2. Employer Equity Campaigns: Employees at major corporations should use the platform to organize internal petitions demanding that their employer provide the full $2,500 match to all employees' children, not just executives or salaried staff, to mitigate the inequality of the program.
3. Financial Literacy Integration: Embed compound interest calculators to show parents the value of even small contributions ($10/month), demystifying the investment aspect for non-financial users.

Priority Issue Area 4: Public Health Federalism & The Vaccine Schism
The HHS/CDC Split and Pharmacy Scope of Practice
In 2025, the unified federal public health architecture fractured. Under the leadership of HHS Secretary Kennedy 27, the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) fundamentally altered the national approach to vaccination, moving from collective mandates to "Shared Clinical Decision-Making" (SCDM).
Key Developments and Structural Analysis
1. The "Routine" vs. "SCDM" Reclassification In January 2026, HHS announced wide-ranging changes to the childhood immunization schedule. Crucially, it removed six vaccines—including COVID-19, Influenza, Hepatitis A, and Hepatitis B—from the "routine" recommendation list for all children. Instead, these were reclassified as recommended only for high-risk groups or under Shared Clinical Decision-Making (SCDM).28 This action bypassed the traditional review process of the CDC's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP), signaling a shift of authority from scientific bodies to political appointees.
2. The Pharmacy Access Crisis This reclassification triggered an unintended (or perhaps intended) legal domino effect. In 25 states, laws authorize pharmacists to administer vaccines only if they are on the CDC's "routine" schedule. When HHS removed COVID-19 and Flu vaccines from this schedule, pharmacists in those states immediately lost the legal authority to administer them without a prescription.27 This created chaos in rural areas where pharmacies are the primary access point for healthcare.
3. The Blue/Red State Divergence The U.S. is now split into two public health regimes. "Blue" states (e.g., New York, California) have issued executive orders or passed legislation to "decouple" their state rules from the federal schedule. For instance, New York Governor Kathy Hochul issued Executive Order 52.5 in January 2026, declaring a disaster due to "federal actions" to legally authorize pharmacists to continue vaccinating.31 "Red" states have generally adhered to the new HHS guidance, resulting in reduced access and coverage.
Primary Sources for the Platform
* Agency Decision: HHS Decision Memo on Childhood Vaccine Schedule (Jan 2026)..28
* State Executive Order: New York Executive Order 52.5, "Extending the Declaration of a Disaster... Due to Federal Actions Related to Vaccine Access" (Jan 23, 2026)..31
* FDA Guidance: FDA 2025-2026 Formula Use for COVID-19 Vaccines..33
* Professional Analysis: American Pharmacists Association (APhA) updates on scope of practice changes..30
Strategic Insight: Administrative Lawfare
The administration utilized a "passive-aggressive" regulatory strategy. By simply changing a classification (Routine -> SCDM) rather than banning vaccines, they triggered pre-existing state "trigger laws" that restricted access. This allows the federal government to claim they are preserving "choice" while effectively dismantling the infrastructure of mass vaccination. The result is that a citizen’s ability to get a flu shot at a CVS now depends entirely on their state governor’s willingness to issue emergency orders overriding federal guidance.
Action Opportunities for the Platform
1. The "Pharmacy Map": The platform must host a dynamic map showing the legal status of pharmacy vaccination in each state. Users need to know: "Can I walk into a pharmacy today, or do I need a doctor's prescription first?"
2. Decoupling Legislation: In states where pharmacists are blocked, citizens must lobby state legislatures to pass "Decoupling Acts"—laws that independently authorize pharmacists to administer WHO-approved vaccines, removing the dependency on the federal HHS schedule.
3. Insurance Appeal Generators: While the ACA mandates coverage for "recommended" preventive care, the SCDM status creates a grey area. Insurers may start charging copays. The platform should provide template appeal letters citing the specific statutory language that (arguably) still mandates coverage despite the HHS reclassification.

Priority Issue Area 5: The Administrative State & Civil Service
Schedule F, Deregulation, and the Hollow State
The structure of the federal workforce and the regulatory process itself are being overhauled to consolidate executive power and dismantle the "administrative state."
Key Developments and Structural Analysis
1. The Return of Schedule F Via Executive Order 14171 ("Restoring Accountability to Policy-Influencing Positions"), signed January 20, 2025, the President reinstated the Schedule F civil service category.3 This order strips civil service protections (tenure, due process for firing) from federal employees deemed to be in "policy-influencing" roles. The Office of Personnel Management (OPM) issued proposed rules in April 2025 estimating that 50,000 career positions could be reclassified.34 This allows the administration to purge subject-matter experts (scientists, economists, lawyers) who resist implementation of questionable orders and replace them with political loyalists.
2. The "10-for-1" Deregulation Mandate On January 31, 2025, the President signed Executive Order 14192, "Unleashing Prosperity Through Deregulation".35 This order mandates that for every one new regulation issued, agencies must identify and repeal ten existing regulations. This effectively freezes the regulatory apparatus. Agencies like the EPA or OSHA cannot issue new safety standards because they cannot find enough "old" rules to repeal to pay the "regulatory debt."
Primary Sources for the Platform
* Executive Action: Executive Order 14171, "Restoring Accountability to Policy-Influencing Positions" (Jan 20, 2025)..3
* Executive Action: Executive Order 14192, "Unleashing Prosperity Through Deregulation" (Jan 31, 2025)..35
* Agency Rulemaking: OPM Proposed Rule on Schedule Policy/Career (April 2025)..34
* Union Response: National Treasury Employees Union (NTEU) litigation filings against Schedule F..37
Strategic Insight: The Institutional Memory Wipe
Schedule F is not just about firing people; it is about erasing institutional memory. By removing career staff who understand the history and legality of regulations, the administration removes the internal "brakes" on executive power. The "10-for-1" rule complements this by making the process of governing mathematically impossible for agencies, leading to a de facto paralysis of federal oversight in areas like environmental protection and labor rights.
Action Opportunities for the Platform
1. Whistleblower Secure Channels: The platform must partner with organizations (like Whistleblower Aid) to provide encrypted channels for federal employees to leak information about politically motivated purges or data suppression.
2. "Save the Regulation" Campaigns: When agencies propose repealing 10 rules to pass a new one, they must open a public comment period. The platform should identify high-value regulations slated for repeal (e.g., clean water standards) and organize "comment brigades" to flood the Federal Register with substantive defense of those rules, creating a legal record that makes repeal harder to justify in court.
3. Expertise Tracking: Create a "Brain Drain" dashboard tracking the departure of senior experts from key agencies (FDA, CDC, NOAA), alerting the public when critical capabilities (e.g., hurricane forecasting, pandemic surveillance) are degraded by staffing cuts.

Priority Issue Area 6: Digital Rights, Education, and the "Culture War" Courts
The Convergence of Tech Regulation and Social Conservatism
The cultural conflicts of the last decade have crystallized into binding Supreme Court precedents and corporate restructuring, focusing on who controls the digital and educational lives of citizens.
Key Developments and Structural Analysis
1. Digital Sovereignty: The TikTok Resolution After months of delays, a deal regarding the TikTok ban was finalized on January 24, 2026.38 Instead of a total ban, a new "TikTok U.S." joint venture was formed with Oracle and Walmart. While this kept the app online, it effectively places the primary communication platform of young Americans under the oversight of U.S. corporate partners allied with the administration, raising concerns about algorithmic manipulation and censorship of dissent.39
2. Age Verification and the End of Anonymity In Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton (June 2025), the Supreme Court upheld a Texas law requiring age verification for adult websites.40 The Court applied "intermediate scrutiny," ruling that the burden on adults to prove their identity was "incidental" to the state's interest in protecting children. This ruling provides a constitutional green light for states to mandate digital ID checks for all internet platforms, ostensibly to protect minors, but effectively ending online anonymity.
3. Parental Rights and Educational Opt-Outs In Mahmoud v. Taylor (June 2025), the Supreme Court ruled that parents have a constitutional right to opt their elementary school children out of lessons involving LGBTQ+ themes, overturning lower courts.42 This decision weaponizes the "religious exercise" clause to disrupt public school curricula, allowing individual parents to veto inclusive education for their children and forcing schools to segregate instruction.
4. Transgender Rights Rollback In United States v. Skrmetti (June 2025), the Court affirmed a Tennessee ban on gender-affirming care for minors.44 Crucially, the Court ruled that such bans are subject only to "rational basis review"—the lowest level of judicial scrutiny—because they regulate "medical procedures" rather than discriminating based on sex. This effectively ends the federal constitutional argument for trans rights in healthcare.
Primary Sources for the Platform
* Court Opinion: Free Speech Coalition, Inc. v. Paxton, No. 23-1122 (U.S. June 27, 2025)..40
* Court Opinion: Mahmoud v. Taylor, 606 U.S. ___ (2025)..42
* Court Opinion: United States v. Skrmetti, 603 U.S. ___ (2025)..44
* Corporate Announcement: TikTok U.S. Joint Venture Press Release (Jan 2026)..38
Strategic Insight: The "Protect the Children" Trojan Horse
A consistent theme across these developments (Paxton, Mahmoud, Skrmetti) is the use of "child protection" as the legal wedge to rollback broader rights (anonymity, inclusive education, medical autonomy). By framing these as issues of minor safety, the Court allows the state to impose burdens that would be unconstitutional if applied to adults directly. The Paxton ruling, in particular, is the most dangerous for digital civic organizing, as it lays the groundwork for a "Real ID" internet where dissent can be tracked.
Action Opportunities for the Platform
1. School Board Monitoring: With Mahmoud as precedent, the battle shifts to local school boards defining what constitutes "opt-out" material. The platform should host a "Curriculum Watch" tool for parents to support inclusive education and oppose broad opt-out policies that disrupt classrooms.
2. Digital Privacy Education: Users need immediate education on the limits of VPNs and the risks of third-party age verification vendors. The platform should promote the use of decentralized, encrypted communication tools (e.g., Signal) that are resistant to centralized ID mandates.
3. Trans Refuge Mapping: With Skrmetti allowing state bans, the platform must maintain an up-to-date map of "Sanctuary States" where gender-affirming care remains legal and protected, including logistics for medical travel.

Priority Issue Area 7: The Judicial Landscape Shift
The End of Universal Injunctions (Trump v. CASA)
Perhaps the most tactically significant development for civic action—and the one that explains why many of the above policies are still in effect—is the Supreme Court's decision in Trump v. CASA, Inc. (June 27, 2025).5
Key Developments and Structural Analysis
1. The Death of the Nationwide Injunction In a 6-3 decision authored by Justice Barrett, the Court held that federal district courts lack the statutory authority to issue "universal" or "nationwide" injunctions that bar the enforcement of a policy against non-parties.5 The Court ruled that an injunction can only protect the specific plaintiffs in the lawsuit to the extent necessary to provide them "complete relief."
2. The Asymmetric Enforcement Consequence
This ruling fundamentally breaks the mechanism of accountability used during previous administrations. Previously, if a judge in Hawaii found an Executive Order unconstitutional, they could stop it nationwide. Now, if a judge in California rules against EO 14160 (Birthright Citizenship), that ruling only protects the plaintiffs in that California courtroom. The administration is free to continue enforcing the unconstitutional policy in Texas, Florida, and everywhere else.
3. The Rise of State Standing The Court left a slight opening for States as plaintiffs, noting that relief for a State might need to be broader.49 This elevates State Attorneys General to the role of primary defenders of civil rights, as they are the only actors with standing to seek relief that covers a broad population (their state's residents).
Primary Sources for the Platform
* Court Opinion: Trump v. CASA, Inc., 606 U.S. ___ (2025)..5
* Legal Analysis: CRS Report LSB11331, "Supreme Court Decision in Trump v. CASA, Inc.".49
* Law Review Analysis: "Universal Injunctions and Birthright Citizenship," Michigan Law Review (2025)..50
Strategic Insight: Fragmented Federalism
We have entered an era of fragmented federalism. Rights are no longer uniform. A citizen in New York may have protection from a federal vaccine policy because their AG sued, while a citizen in New Jersey does not. This forces civic action to pivot from national test cases to a "guerrilla litigation" strategy involving thousands of plaintiffs across every circuit.
Action Opportunities for the Platform
1. The "Plaintiff Matchmaker": NGOs can no longer just sue "on behalf of the public." They need actual, injured plaintiffs in every single jurisdiction to secure relief. The platform should serve as a secure database connecting affected citizens (e.g., parents denied passports) with legal teams looking for plaintiffs in specific judicial districts.
2. AG Accountability: Citizens must pressure their State Attorneys General to join multi-state litigation. The platform should track which AGs have joined which lawsuits, making "failure to sue" a political liability.
3. Jurisdictional Rights Checker: The wiki must provide a tool where a user inputs their zip code and sees exactly which federal policies are enjoined in their area and which are active. "Is EO 14160 blocked in my state?" is the new essential question.

Priority Issue Area 8: Federal Law Enforcement Escalation & Civil Rights
The Minneapolis Killings and the Expansion of DHS Authority
In January 2026, the enforcement of federal immigration policy moved from bureaucratic implementation to lethal confrontation, marked by the killing of two U.S. citizens by federal agents in Minneapolis, Minnesota. These events have catalyzed a nationwide debate on the militarization of the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and the civil rights of bystanders and observers.

Key Developments and Structural Analysis
1. The Killing of Renee Nicole Good (January 7, 2026) On January 7, 2026, Renee Nicole Good, a 37-year-old U.S. citizen, was shot and killed by an ICE officer in Minneapolis. According to witnesses and video footage, Good was attempting to drive away from a confrontation involving ICE agents when she was shot. ICE officials claimed she "weaponized her vehicle," labeling the incident an act of "domestic terrorism" and asserting the officer fired in self-defense. However, Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey and witnesses disputed this account, describing the agents' use of force as reckless. This incident marked a significant escalation in the use of lethal force during routine enforcement operations.

2. The Killing of Alex Pretti (January 24, 2026) Less than three weeks later, on January 24, 2026, federal agents in Minneapolis shot and killed Alex Pretti, also a 37-year-old U.S. citizen and a registered nurse at the VA Health Care System. Video evidence indicates Pretti was attempting to defend a legal observer who had been shoved and sprayed with chemical agents by federal officers. Pretti was reportedly pepper-sprayed and tackled before being shot by an agent. This second killing of a U.S. citizen interventionist suggests a shift in rules of engagement toward aggressive neutralization of anyone perceived as interfering with federal operations.

3. Aggressive Suppression of Observers The Minneapolis operations have been characterized by aggressive tactics against non-targets, including legal observers and protesters. Reports indicate agents—often unmarked or wearing masks—have utilized chemical irritants, pepper spray projectiles, and physical force against individuals recording their actions. These tactics have raised profound Fourth Amendment questions regarding the rights of citizens to observe and document police activity in public spaces.

4. Legislative Reinforcement: The FY2026 DHS Funding Bill Despite public outrage, the House of Representatives passed H.R. 7147, the Department of Homeland Security Appropriations Act, 2026, on January 22, 2026. The bill provides $64.4 billion in funding, including approximately $10 billion for ICE. Crucially, it increases funding for detention beds and enforcement operations while rejecting Democratic amendments to add oversight or limit funding. The passage of this bill, with support from seven House Democrats, signals a legislative endorsement—or at least a refusal to check—the agency's expanded operational mandate.

Strategic Insight: The Accountability Vacuum
A critical barrier to justice in these cases is the Supreme Court's limitation on Bivens actions (lawsuits against federal officials for constitutional violations). Unlike state police, who can be sued under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, federal agents enjoy near-total immunity from civil damages unless a specific statute authorizes it. With no such statute for ICE agents, families of victims like Good and Pretti have limited legal recourse. This "accountability vacuum" emboldens aggressive tactical choices on the ground.

Action Opportunities for the Platform
"Day of Truth" Coordination: Support the ongoing "Day of Truth & Freedom" protests and economic blackouts (business closures) in Minnesota and expand them nationally. The platform can list participating businesses and organized events.

Senate Pressure Campaign: As H.R. 7147 moves to the Senate, users should be directed to target the Senate Appropriations Committee. The goal is to strip the increased enforcement funding or attach "strings" requiring independent investigation of the Minneapolis killings before funds are released.

Legal Observer Training: In light of the targeting of observers, the platform should host "Know Your Rights" training specifically for documenting federal agents safely, emphasizing distance, live-streaming (to prevent evidence destruction), and de-escalation.

Bivens Reform Advocacy: Use the stories of Renee Good and Alex Pretti to lobby for the Ending Qualified Immunity Act or specific legislation creating a cause of action against federal agents, closing the Bivens loophole.


Conclusion: The Architecture of Resistance
The civic landscape of 2026 is defined by a paradox: the centralization of executive personnel power (Schedule F) coupled with the fragmentation of legal rights (CASA). The government has successfully utilized "One Big Bill" to alter the economic baseline and Executive Orders to alter the constitutional baseline.
For the user of this platform, the takeaway is clear: Help is not coming from a single court ruling. The era of the "silver bullet" lawsuit is over. Meaningful action now requires:
1. Granularity: Understanding specific state laws and local exemptions (e.g., for vaccines).
2. Bureaucratic Literacy: Navigating forms (IRS 4547, Medicaid reverification) as a form of resistance.
3. Distributed Litigation: inundating the courts with individual claims to overwhelm the administration's capacity to enforce unconstitutional orders.
This report provides the intellectual blueprint for building the tools to facilitate that work.
