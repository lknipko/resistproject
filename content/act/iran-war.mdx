# Iran War Response

## Quick Summary

The US-Iran war is now on **Day 69**. **Over 3,636 people have been killed in Iran** (1,701 civilians, 1,221 military). **13 US service members killed, 381+ wounded.** The ceasefire was extended indefinitely on April 21 but has been repeatedly violated — on **May 7, three US destroyers were attacked in the Strait of Hormuz** by coordinated Iranian forces; the US struck back. Congress failed to restrain the war: the House War Powers vote failed **213-214** (April 16, one vote short) and the Senate failed **47-50** (April 30). On **May 1, Trump declared hostilities "terminated"** to sidestep the 60-day War Powers deadline — legal scholars reject this interpretation. Oil prices hit **$118/barrel**. The constitutional question is now urgent: the War Powers deadline has passed and the president is claiming the law doesn't apply.

**May 14-15 — Trump-Xi Summit** · Beijing. Iran expected to dominate agenda alongside trade issues.

**June 12 — FISA Section 702 Deadline** · Congress extended surveillance authority by 45 days; next expiration.


<Callout variant="warning" title="The 60-Day War Powers Clock — EXPIRED">

The War Powers Act deadline passed on **May 1, 2026**. Congress failed to authorize the war in **four separate votes** across both chambers.

**Trump's response:** He wrote to Congress claiming hostilities are "terminated" because of the ceasefire. Defense Secretary Hegseth claimed the ceasefire "pauses" the clock. **Legal scholars and multiple senators (including Republican Susan Collins) reject both arguments.** A ceasefire does not terminate hostilities under the War Powers Act — especially one violated by both sides, culminating in the **May 7 Strait of Hormuz battle** where three US destroyers were attacked.

**What this means:** The president is now conducting an unauthorized war past the legal deadline, claiming the law doesn't apply. Congress's remaining leverage is the **power of the purse** — blocking war funding until authorization is granted.

</Callout>

## Quick Actions

### Contact Congress: War Powers

Congress has voted **four times** on War Powers resolutions — all failed by narrow margins. The House vote on April 16 failed **213-214** (one vote short). The Senate vote on April 30 failed **47-50**. The 60-day War Powers deadline has now **passed** with no authorization. The president is claiming the law doesn't apply.

**What to say:**

- The Constitution gives Congress alone the power to declare war (Article I, Section 8)
- The president launched a full-scale war without a declaration or AUMF
- The 60-day War Powers deadline passed on May 1 — the president claims it doesn't apply
- Congress must demand a standalone war authorization vote — not through reconciliation or riders
- Vote NO on any supplemental war funding without a concurrent authorization vote
- Over 3,636 people have been killed in Iran; 13 US service members killed, 381+ wounded

### File FOIA Requests on War Decision-Making

Request documents about the decision to strike Iran without congressional authorization. FOIA requests create accountability pressure and can uncover the legal justifications (or lack thereof) behind the war. File at each agency's FOIA portal or use [MuckRock](https://www.muckrock.com/) for guided filing and tracking. Key agencies to target: Department of Defense, State Department, and National Security Council. The [EFF](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/03/foilies-2026) tracks government transparency failures.

### [+] Legal Support

**ACLU constitutional challenge:**
The ACLU is the primary organization pursuing the constitutional argument against the unauthorized war. Donate to support their work:
- [ACLU.org — Donate](https://www.aclu.org/donate)
- Their core argument: the Constitution requires specific congressional authorization; no existing AUMF covers the Iran conflict

**The 60-day War Powers deadline has passed:**
The War Powers Act required congressional authorization by May 1, 2026. The president instead declared hostilities "terminated" — a claim contradicted by the May 7 Hormuz battle. Congress's remaining leverage is the **power of the purse**: blocking supplemental war funding until authorization is granted. Citizen pressure on Congress to use this lever is the most actionable path.

**Monitor litigation:**
- Follow Just Security ([justsecurity.org](https://www.justsecurity.org)) for analysis of legal challenges and international law questions

### [+] For Republican Constituents

These arguments may be effective when contacting Republican representatives:

- **Reagan invoked the War Powers Act himself** — he notified Congress about the Lebanon deployment in 1982. Respecting the Act is not weakness, it is constitutional order.
- **No existing AUMF covers Iran.** The 2001 and 2002 AUMFs targeted al-Qaeda and Iraq. Congress should vote explicitly on Iran — that is its constitutional role.
- **The 60-day clock is the law.** Congress passed the War Powers Act with bipartisan support specifically to prevent a president from committing the nation to war alone. That principle applies regardless of which party holds the White House.
- **An unauthorized war precedent is dangerous.** A future Democratic president could cite this precedent to launch military operations without congressional approval.
