# AI & Data Centers: The Hidden Environmental Cost

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The explosive growth of artificial intelligence is creating a massive, largely invisible environmental footprint. Global data centers consumed **448 trillion watt-hours** of electricity last year -- more than all but 10 countries. That electricity produced **208 million metric tons of CO2**, roughly equal to Argentina's total emissions. Data centers consumed **1.2 trillion gallons of water** for cooling. By 2030, a UN report projects water and energy use will **double**, with the water footprint equaling the basic annual domestic water needs of all **1.3 billion people** in Sub-Saharan Africa.

Communities across the U.S. are pushing back. Between March and June 2025, community opposition blocked or delayed **$98 billion** in data center projects, and at least **25 projects were canceled** in 2026. These facilities compete with homes and farms for electricity and water, strain aging power grids, and can lower local air quality.

### [+] Why AI Uses So Much Energy

Traditional data centers (cloud storage, web hosting, streaming) have existed for decades. AI has dramatically increased energy demands because:

- **Training large AI models** requires thousands of specialized processors (GPUs) running for weeks or months. Training a single large language model can consume as much electricity as 130 U.S. homes use in a year.
- **AI inference** (actually using the model to answer questions, generate images, etc.) is less intensive per query but runs billions of times per day across millions of users.
- **Cooling requirements** scale with computing power. High-density AI chip clusters generate extreme heat, requiring massive cooling systems that consume electricity and water.
- **Redundancy:** Data centers maintain backup power systems, redundant cooling, and emergency generators that add to total energy footprint.

The rapid buildout of AI capacity means new natural gas power plants, nuclear plant extensions, and even coal plant restarts are being proposed specifically to power data centers.

### [+] Water: The Hidden Resource

Data centers use water in two primary ways:

**Direct cooling:** Many data centers use evaporative cooling systems that consume millions of gallons of water daily. A single large data center can use 1-5 million gallons of water per day -- equivalent to a small city.

**Indirect water use:** The electricity powering data centers also requires water for generation (thermal power plants use water for cooling). Every kilowatt-hour generated by coal or natural gas requires additional water.

**Competition with communities:**
- In drought-affected regions, data centers compete directly with farms, homes, and ecosystems for shrinking water supplies
- The EPA has criticized the Trump administration's approach of promoting water reuse for data centers rather than regulating their water demand directly
- Virginia's data center corridor in Loudoun County -- the largest concentration in the world -- has raised concerns about groundwater depletion

### [+] Community Opposition

Data center proposals have faced growing resistance from communities concerned about:

- **Electricity costs:** New data centers strain local grids, potentially raising rates for residential customers [source: Consumer Reports](https://www.consumerreports.org/data-centers/ai-data-centers-impact-on-electric-bills-water-and-more-a1040338678/)
- **Water competition:** In drought-prone areas, data center water use conflicts with agricultural and municipal needs
- **Noise pollution:** Cooling systems and backup generators create continuous noise
- **Air quality:** Diesel backup generators emit particulates and NOx
- **Land use:** Large data center campuses consume farmland and open space
- **Traffic:** Construction and operations increase local traffic

Between March and June 2025, community opposition led to **$98 billion in data center projects** being blocked or delayed. At least **25 projects were canceled** in 2026 in response to local objections.

### [+] The Corporate Players

Major tech companies are the largest data center operators:

- **Microsoft** (Azure, OpenAI partnership) -- Has pledged carbon negative by 2030 but water consumption increased 34% in 2023
- **Google** -- Water use increased 20% in 2023; building new data centers across the U.S.
- **Amazon** (AWS) -- Largest cloud provider; building data centers near nuclear plants
- **Meta** -- Training AI models requiring massive compute clusters
- **Apple** -- Expanding data center footprint for Apple Intelligence

These companies have made sustainability pledges, but their absolute resource consumption continues to grow faster than efficiency improvements.

## Analysis

### What This Means

The AI boom is creating a new category of industrial-scale energy and water consumer that barely existed a decade ago. Key concerns:

- **Grid strain:** Utilities and grid operators did not plan for this level of new demand. Some regions face brownout risks during peak periods.
- **Climate impact:** If data center growth is powered by fossil fuels, it will significantly increase U.S. carbon emissions at exactly the moment they need to decrease.
- **Water justice:** Data centers located in drought-prone areas compete with vulnerable communities for water.
- **Regulatory gap:** No federal framework governs data center environmental impacts. Siting, water use, and emissions are regulated patchily at the state and local level.
- **Greenwashing risk:** Companies purchasing renewable energy credits (RECs) may claim "carbon neutral" operations while the actual grid runs on fossil fuels.

### Who's Affected

- **Ratepayers** nationwide -- new data center demand raises electricity costs for everyone
- **Drought-affected communities** -- data centers consume water that farms, homes, and ecosystems need
- **Residents near data centers** -- noise, air quality, traffic, and property impacts
- **Rural communities** -- targeted for large data center campuses due to cheap land and tax incentives
- **Everyone** -- climate impacts of increased energy consumption

### File a Public Comment on Proposed Data Centers (15 minutes)

When a data center is proposed in your area, it typically requires local zoning approval. Attend or submit written comments to your:
- **County planning commission** or **zoning board**
- **City council** during public comment periods
- **State environmental review** process (if applicable)

Ask about: water consumption projections, electricity source, backup generator emissions, noise studies, and community benefit agreements.

### Check if Data Centers Are Planned Near You (10 minutes)

- Search your local planning department website for "data center" applications
- Check your utility's integrated resource plan for projected data center load
- Monitor local news for data center development announcements
- Review county zoning maps for industrial or special-use permits

### Submit Comments on EPA Data Center Water Policy (15 minutes)

The EPA has been developing guidance on data center water use. Visit [regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov) and search for data center water rules to submit comments urging stronger regulation.

## Sustained Actions

### Demand Grid Transparency

Your utility should disclose:
- What percentage of new electricity demand comes from data centers
- Whether data center contracts include preferential rates (subsidized by other ratepayers)
- What new generation capacity is being built to serve data centers
- Whether data centers are actually using renewable energy or just purchasing credits

Contact your state public utility commission and request this information through public records requests.

### Organizations to Support

- **Consumer Reports** ([consumerreports.org](https://www.consumerreports.org)) — Investigating data center impacts on utility bills and communities
- **Environmental Defense Fund** ([edf.org](https://www.edf.org)) — Research on data center energy and water sustainability
- **Sierra Club** ([sierraclub.org](https://www.sierraclub.org)) — Local chapter campaigns against poorly sited data centers
- **Clean Air Task Force** ([catf.us](https://www.catf.us)) — Advocating for clean energy to power data centers
- **Institute for Local Self-Reliance** ([ilsr.org](https://www.ilsr.org)) — Resources for communities negotiating with large developers

### Support Clean Energy for Computing

Advocate for policies that require data centers to:
- Source electricity from **new** renewable generation (not just credits)
- Use **closed-loop** or **air cooling** instead of evaporative systems in water-stressed regions
- Invest in **local grid upgrades** to offset their demand impact
- Share **waste heat** with surrounding communities for heating or industrial use (the EU is exploring this)

[source: EU Environment - Data Center Waste Heat](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/news/ai-data-centre-waste-heat-could-be-used-water-purification-and-carbon-capture-2026-03-30_en)

### Push for FOIA Transparency

Many data center deals involve tax incentives, subsidized utility rates, or water allocations negotiated behind closed doors. Use FOIA requests to your state and local governments to uncover:
- Tax abatement agreements and their terms
- Water allocation permits and projected consumption
- Utility rate agreements and cost impacts on other customers
- Environmental review documents and exemptions
