# Bird Population Decline

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** North America has lost nearly 3 billion birds -- 29% of the total population -- since 1970, according to a landmark 2019 study in *Science*. The causes are well-documented: habitat loss, outdoor cats, window collisions, pesticides, and light pollution. In April 2025, the Trump administration reinstated a policy removing protections against incidental killing of migratory birds, effectively giving industries a free pass on bird deaths. The 2025 State of the Birds report found 75% of North American bird species are declining, with 229 species needing urgent conservation action.

Birds are indicators of ecosystem health. Their decline signals broader environmental collapse.

## Facts

### The Scale of Loss

A 2019 study published in *Science* analyzed 50 years of data from breeding bird surveys and weather radar migration monitoring, concluding that North America lost approximately **2.9 billion breeding birds** since 1970 -- a 29% decline. The losses span virtually all habitats and bird families:

- **Grassland birds:** Down 53% (more than 700 million birds)
- **Shorebirds:** Down 37%
- **Boreal forest birds:** Down 33%
- **Aerial insectivores** (swallows, nightjars): Down 32%
- **Only wetland birds and raptors** showed increases, thanks to targeted conservation like the Clean Water Act and DDT ban

[source: Cornell Lab - 3 Billion Birds Gone](https://www.birds.cornell.edu/home/bring-birds-back/) | [source: Rosenberg et al., Science 2019](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaw1313)

### 2025 State of the Birds

The March 2025 State of the Birds report confirmed the crisis continues:

- **75%** of North American bird species are declining
- **229 species** (one-third of all North American species) need conservation action to preserve or restore populations
- Habitat loss remains the primary driver
- Climate change is shifting ranges and disrupting migration timing

[source: North American Bird Conservation Initiative - State of the Birds](https://www.stateofthebirds.org/)

### Migratory Bird Treaty Act Weakened

The Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA), enacted in 1918, is the primary federal law protecting migratory birds. It protects over 1,000 species and has been credited with saving numerous species from extinction.

In April 2025, the Department of the Interior reinstated a Trump-era legal opinion that **removes protections against "incidental take"** -- the killing or harming of migratory birds as a side effect of industrial activity. This means:

- Power lines, communication towers, oil waste pits, and wind turbines that kill birds are no longer subject to enforcement
- An estimated **1 billion birds are killed annually** by industrial "incidental take"
- Companies no longer need to implement bird-safe practices to avoid liability
- The MBTA effectively only applies to intentional killing (hunting without a permit)

Legislation called the **Migratory Bird Protection Act** was introduced in May 2025 to codify incidental take protections, establish a Migratory Bird Recovery Fund, and create an authorization process for unavoidable incidental take.

[source: Audubon - MBTA Weakened](https://www.audubon.org/press-room/department-interior-weakens-key-migratory-bird-treaty-act-protections-increasing-risk) | [source: American Bird Conservancy](https://abcbirds.org/news/mbta-april-2025/) | [source: DOI Press Release](https://www.doi.gov/pressreleases/interior-department-ensures-migratory-bird-treaty-act-works-birds-and-people)

### [+] Major Causes of Bird Death

| Cause | Estimated Annual U.S. Bird Deaths |
|-------|----------------------------------|
| Outdoor cats | 1.3-4.0 billion |
| Window collisions (buildings) | 365-988 million |
| Vehicle collisions | ~214 million |
| Power lines | ~22 million |
| Communication towers | ~6 million |
| Pesticides | ~72 million (direct poisoning) |
| Light pollution | Disrupts migration, causes exhaustion and collisions |
| Habitat loss | Primary long-term driver of population decline |

Cats are by far the largest direct source of bird mortality. The majority of kills are by unowned or feral cats, but pet cats allowed outdoors contribute significantly. A single outdoor cat kills an estimated 10-30 birds per year.

Window collisions are the second-largest direct killer. Most occur at low-rise residential buildings, not skyscrapers. Birds cannot perceive clear or reflective glass as a barrier.

[source: Smithsonian Migratory Bird Center](https://nationalzoo.si.edu/migratory-birds/causes-bird-mortality) | [source: Loss et al., Nature Communications 2013](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms2380)

### [+] Pesticides and Neonicotinoids

Neonicotinoid insecticides, the most widely used class of insecticides globally, pose a particular threat:

- Applied as seed coatings on over 150 million acres of U.S. cropland
- Persist in soil and water for months to years
- Toxic to insects at parts-per-billion levels, decimating the food base for insectivorous birds
- A 2019 study found migrating white-crowned sparrows exposed to neonicotinoids lost weight and delayed migration, reducing survival
- The EU banned outdoor use of three neonicotinoids in 2018; the U.S. has not followed

[source: Eng et al., Science 2019](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaw7000)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The weakened MBTA means industries have no incentive to adopt bird-safe practices -- billions of dollars in mitigation measures may be abandoned
- Without incidental take protections, enforcement is limited to intentional killing, which accounts for a tiny fraction of bird deaths
- Continued habitat loss and pesticide use will accelerate declines without stronger protections
- Bird declines indicate broader ecosystem collapse -- birds control insect populations, pollinate plants, and disperse seeds
- Climate change compounds all other threats by shifting habitats faster than populations can adapt

### Who's Affected

- **Ecosystems:** Birds control insects (a single swallow eats 850+ insects/day), pollinate plants, and disperse seeds
- **Agriculture:** Insect-eating birds provide an estimated $4.6 billion in pest control services annually in the U.S.
- **Public health:** Without natural insect control, pesticide use increases
- **Indigenous communities:** Many species are culturally significant and central to traditional practices
- **Outdoor recreation:** Birdwatching is a $41 billion industry supporting 681,000 jobs

### Make Your Windows Bird-Safe (30 minutes - $10-50)

Window collisions kill up to 988 million birds annually in the U.S. Most strikes happen at residential buildings. Effective, affordable solutions:

1. **Window decals or stickers:** Place decals no more than 2 inches apart across the outside of windows. Brands: [Feather Friendly](https://www.featherfriendly.com), [WindowAlert](https://windowalert.com) (~$10-25)
2. **Acopian BirdSavers:** Paracord strings hung 4 inches apart over windows -- highly effective, DIY or purchase at [birdsavers.com](https://birdsavers.com) (~$20-50 per window)
3. **Tempera paint or soap:** Draw patterns on the outside of problem windows (removable, free)
4. **Move bird feeders:** Place feeders either within 3 feet of windows (too close for lethal impact) or more than 30 feet away
5. **Close blinds:** Partially closed blinds reduce reflectivity and help birds see the barrier

### Keep Cats Indoors (ongoing)

Outdoor cats kill 1.3-4.0 billion birds annually in the U.S. -- more than all other human-caused mortality combined:

- **Keep pet cats indoors** or build a "catio" (enclosed outdoor space)
- **If your cat goes outside:** Use a [Birdsbesafe collar cover](https://www.birdsbesafe.com) (brightly colored, reduces bird catches by 87%)
- **Support Trap-Neuter-Return (TNR)** programs for feral cats in your community
- **Talk to neighbors:** Share information about the impact of outdoor cats on local bird populations

### Join Citizen Science Bird Monitoring (10 minutes to start)

Your observations contribute directly to conservation science:

1. **eBird** ([ebird.org](https://ebird.org)) -- Log any bird sighting, anywhere, anytime. Over 1 billion observations in the database. Used by researchers, land managers, and conservationists worldwide. Free app.
2. **Audubon Christmas Bird Count** ([audubon.org/conservation/science/christmas-bird-count](https://www.audubon.org/conservation/science/christmas-bird-count)) -- Annual winter census held December-January. Join a local count circle -- no expertise needed.
3. **Great Backyard Bird Count** ([birdcount.org](https://www.birdcount.org)) -- Four-day event each February. Count birds for 15+ minutes and submit online.
4. **Project FeederWatch** ([feederwatch.org](https://feederwatch.org)) -- Count birds at your feeder November-April. ~$18/year supports Cornell Lab research.

### Turn Off Lights During Migration (spring and fall)

Light pollution attracts and disorients migrating birds, causing exhaustion, collisions, and death:

1. **Turn off non-essential outdoor lights** during spring migration (March-May) and fall migration (August-November)
2. **Close blinds at night** to reduce light escaping from windows
3. **Advocate for Lights Out programs:** Many cities have official Lights Out campaigns -- push your city to join. See [Audubon Lights Out](https://www.audubon.org/conservation/project/lights-out)
4. **Use motion-sensor lights** instead of always-on fixtures

## Sustained Actions

### Plant Native Plants

Native plants support native insects, which support birds. Non-native plants often support zero native insect species:

- **Find native plants for your zip code:** [National Wildlife Federation Native Plant Finder](https://www.nwf.org/nativeplantfinder/)
- **Prioritize keystone species:** Oaks, willows, birches, and native cherries support the most caterpillars (bird food)
- **Eliminate or reduce lawn:** Replace with native meadow, ground covers, or food gardens
- **Avoid pesticides:** A single neonicotinoid-treated seed can kill a songbird
- **Leave leaf litter:** Provides habitat for insects that birds depend on

### Buy Bird-Friendly Coffee

- Most coffee is grown on sun plantations that destroy tropical bird habitat
- **Smithsonian Bird Friendly certified coffee** is shade-grown, organic, and supports forest habitat for migratory birds that winter in the tropics
- Find certified brands at [nationalzoo.si.edu/migratory-birds/bird-friendly-coffee](https://nationalzoo.si.edu/migratory-birds/bird-friendly-coffee)
