# Environmental Carcinogens

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Millions of Americans live near facilities that release known carcinogens into the air, water, and soil. In Louisiana's "Cancer Alley," cancer incidence rates soar up to 47 times EPA's acceptable level. Nationally, 1,340 Superfund sites contain hazardous waste, and 73 million Americans live within 3 miles of one. Meanwhile, the current administration is rolling back carcinogen standards -- reconsidering the 2024 asbestos ban, weakening formaldehyde risk evaluations, and proposing deregulatory changes to TSCA chemical review.

You have a legal right to know what toxic chemicals are released in your community. Use it.

## Facts

### Known Environmental Carcinogens

The following chemicals are classified as known or probable human carcinogens by EPA, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), or the National Toxicology Program (NTP) -- and are routinely released by industrial facilities in the United States:

| Chemical | Cancer Types | Common Sources |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Benzene** | Leukemia, blood cancers | Refineries, gasoline, chemical plants |
| **Vinyl chloride** | Liver cancer (angiosarcoma) | PVC manufacturing, chemical spills |
| **Formaldehyde** | Nasopharyngeal cancer, leukemia | Building materials, chemical plants |
| **Asbestos** | Mesothelioma, lung, ovarian, laryngeal cancer | Building demolition, mining, legacy contamination |
| **PFAS (PFOA/PFOS)** | Kidney, testicular cancer | Firefighting foam, nonstick coatings, manufacturing |
| **Ethylene oxide** | Lymphoma, leukemia, breast cancer | Medical device sterilization, chemical plants |
| **1,3-Butadiene** | Leukemia, lymphoma | Synthetic rubber, petroleum refining |
| **Trichloroethylene (TCE)** | Kidney cancer, liver cancer, non-Hodgkin lymphoma | Metal degreasing, dry cleaning, Superfund sites |

[source: EPA - IRIS Assessments](https://www.epa.gov/iris) | [source: NTP - Report on Carcinogens](https://ntp.niehs.nih.gov/whatwestudy/assessments/cancer/roc)

### [+] Cancer Alley: Environmental Racism in Action

An 85-mile stretch along the Mississippi River between Baton Rouge and New Orleans -- known as "Cancer Alley" -- is home to over 200 petrochemical plants that process approximately 25% of all US petrochemical products. The health consequences are devastating:

- Cancer incidence rates up to **47 times higher** than EPA's acceptable risk level
- Residents exposed to more than **10 times the level** of hazardous air pollution compared to other Louisianans (EPA 2016, 2020)
- A yearly average of **447 unique carcinogens** released in the region during 2015-2020
- In predominantly Black districts of St. James Parish, cancer risks reach **104-105 cases per million**, compared to 60-75 per million in predominantly white districts
- The region spans 10 parishes hosting **300+ manufacturing facilities, 150+ petrochemical plants, and 15 refineries**

This is not an accident. The concentration of toxic industry in predominantly Black communities is directly connected to the history of slavery and systemic racism in Louisiana. Formerly enslaved people's communities were targeted for industrial development because residents had less political power to resist.

[source: Johns Hopkins - Cancer Alley Hazards](https://publichealth.jhu.edu/2025/the-shocking-hazards-of-louisianas-cancer-alley) | [source: UN Human Rights - Environmental Racism in Louisiana](https://news.un.org/en/story/2021/03/1086172) | [source: FracTracker Alliance - Cancer Alley Data](https://www.fractracker.org/2024/12/visualizing-environmental-injustice-with-interactive-data-cancer-alley-louisiana/)

### [+] East Palestine: A Chemical Disaster

On February 3, 2023, a Norfolk Southern freight train derailed in East Palestine, Ohio, releasing hazardous materials including **vinyl chloride** (a known human carcinogen), ethylene glycol, ethylhexyl acrylate, butyl acrylate, and isobutylene. Three days later, responders conducted a "vent and burn" of five vinyl chloride tanker cars, releasing volatile organic compounds into the air.

- **Five chemicals** released were human or animal carcinogens
- **48% of soil samples** exceeded cancer regional screening levels (2025 Kent State study)
- Residents reported headaches, respiratory irritation, skin rashes, and eye irritation
- The EPA stated there is "no ongoing exposure endangering public health" -- but researchers say long-term cancer risks cannot be assessed yet
- Vinyl chloride causes a rare liver cancer (hepatic angiosarcoma) that may take **10-30 years** to develop after exposure

[source: EPA - East Palestine Derailment](https://www.epa.gov/east-palestine-oh-train-derailment) | [source: Kent State - Environmental Impact Study](https://www.kent.edu/sustainability/news/researchers-publish-groundbreaking-study-environmental-impact-east-palestine) | [source: PMC - Health Consequences of Ohio Derailment](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10049189/)

### [+] Superfund Sites and Carcinogen Contamination

The EPA's National Priorities List includes **1,340 active Superfund sites** -- the nation's most contaminated locations. The most common carcinogenic contaminants found at these sites include:

- **Arsenic + nickel + cadmium** found together at 496 sites
- **Benzene + arsenic + trichloroethylene** found together at 451 sites
- **Benzene + vinyl chloride + trichloroethylene** found together at 420 sites

**73 million Americans** live within 3 miles of a Superfund site. Proximity to these sites has been shown to decrease life expectancy, and the effect is worse in low-income communities and communities of color.

Superfund cleanup funding has collapsed from **$2.6 billion in 1999 to approximately $537 million in 2024**. The proposed FY2026 budget cuts it further to ~$600 million -- a 67% cut from FY2024 enacted levels. Complex sites that remain on the list will take decades to clean up at current funding levels.

[source: GAO - Superfund Cleanup Report](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-108408) | [source: EPA - Superfund Contaminants](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/contaminants-superfund-sites) | [source: Nature Communications - Superfund and Life Expectancy](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-021-22249-2)

### [+] PFAS: The "Forever Chemicals"

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) are a class of thousands of synthetic chemicals that do not break down in the environment. PFOA, the most well-studied PFAS compound, was classified as a **known human carcinogen** by IARC in 2023 (upgraded from "possible"). PFOS was classified as a **possible human carcinogen** the same year.

- **100+ million Americans** have PFAS in their drinking water
- PFAS contamination linked to **kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, immune suppression, and reproductive harm**
- EPA finalized the first national PFAS drinking water standards in April 2024, but enforcement is being delayed
- PFAS research funding and state remediation grants have been cut

[source: IARC - PFOA Classification](https://www.cancer.org/cancer/risk-prevention/chemicals/teflon-and-perfluorooctanoic-acid-pfoa.html) | [source: NIEHS - PFAS and Cancer](https://www.niehs.nih.gov/news/factor/2025/2/science-highlights/pfas-water-cancer)

### [+] Pesticides and Cancer

**Glyphosate** (Roundup) is the world's most widely used herbicide. IARC classified it as "probably carcinogenic to humans" in 2015, linking it to non-Hodgkin lymphoma. The EPA maintains it is "not likely" carcinogenic, but a 2025 review of 40+ studies found glyphosate may significantly raise the risk of chronic liver disease even at low exposure. Bayer has paid over **$10 billion** to settle glyphosate cancer lawsuits.

**EPA labeling failure:** Only **1.4% of pesticide products** containing "probable" or "likely" carcinogen ingredients carry EPA cancer warnings on their labels. For "possible" or "suggestive" carcinogens, it's just 1.1%.

[source: EPA - Glyphosate](https://www.epa.gov/ingredients-used-pesticide-products/glyphosate) | [source: The New Lede - EPA Cancer Label Warnings](https://www.thenewlede.org/2026/03/epa-cancer-label-warnings-pesticides/)

### [+] Regulatory Rollbacks

The current administration is weakening protections against carcinogens on multiple fronts:

**Asbestos ban reconsidered:** EPA's 2024 ban on chrysotile asbestos -- the only form still imported into the US, which causes mesothelioma, lung cancer, ovarian cancer, and laryngeal cancer -- is being reconsidered. In June 2025, EPA requested litigation be paused while it initiates new rulemaking, estimating 30 months for the process. Industry groups argue "permanent workplace controls" may be sufficient instead of a ban.

**Formaldehyde standards weakened:** EPA's August 2024 IRIS review confirmed formaldehyde causes cancer and poses "unreasonable risk." But in December 2025, EPA released a revised draft using a "sensory irritation" threshold of 0.3 ppm, abandoning the linear no-threshold model that assumes no safe exposure level for genotoxic carcinogens. Public health advocates say this approach caters to industry.

**TSCA chemical review process rolled back:** A proposed rule would revise EPA's chemical risk evaluation process under TSCA in ways the agency itself describes as "deregulatory in nature," making it harder to restrict toxic chemicals.

**Value of Statistical Life (VSL) removed:** EPA stopped monetizing the health benefits of regulations, making it mathematically impossible to justify new protections against carcinogens in cost-benefit analyses.

[source: EPA - Asbestos Actions](https://www.epa.gov/asbestos/epa-actions-protect-public-exposure-asbestos) | [source: Chemical & Engineering News - Formaldehyde](https://cen.acs.org/policy/chemical-regulation/EPA-reverses-course-safe-formaldehyde/103/web/2025/12) | [source: The Regulatory Review - EPA Chemical Rollbacks](https://www.theregreview.org/2025/10/28/bradley-epa-proposes-rollbacks-to-chemical-regulation/)

### [+] Your Right to Know

The **Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act (EPCRA)** of 1986 created the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI), which requires industrial facilities to publicly report annual releases of over 770 toxic chemicals. This data is your legal right.

- **TRI Explorer:** Search releases by ZIP code, city, or facility at [enviro.epa.gov/triexplorer](https://enviro.epa.gov/triexplorer/)
- **ECHO (Enforcement and Compliance History Online):** Check facility violations at [echo.epa.gov](https://echo.epa.gov)
- **EPA Cleanups Near Me:** Find Superfund and brownfield sites at [epa.gov/superfund/search-superfund-sites-where-you-live](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/search-superfund-sites-where-you-live)
- **EJScreen:** EPA's environmental justice screening tool overlays pollution, health, and demographic data at [ejscreen.epa.gov](https://ejscreen.epa.gov)

[source: EPA - TRI Program](https://www.epa.gov/toxics-release-inventory-tri-program)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Carcinogen exposure is **not random** -- it follows patterns of race, income, and political power
- Regulatory rollbacks will increase exposure for millions of Americans, with health effects manifesting years or decades later
- The collapse of Superfund funding means contaminated sites will remain hazardous for longer
- Removing the Value of Statistical Life from regulatory analysis makes it impossible to justify new carcinogen protections on economic grounds
- Community monitoring and right-to-know data are more important than ever as federal enforcement declines
- Environmental cancer is preventable -- these are policy choices, not inevitable outcomes

### Who's Affected

- **Environmental justice communities:** Low-income neighborhoods and communities of color are disproportionately located near industrial pollution sources -- this is documented and systematic
- **Fence-line communities:** Residents living adjacent to petrochemical plants, refineries, and chemical manufacturing face the highest exposures
- **Workers:** Industrial, agricultural, and construction workers face occupational carcinogen exposure, especially as workplace protections are weakened
- **Rural communities near agriculture:** Pesticide drift exposes nearby residents, especially children
- **Communities near Superfund sites:** 73 million Americans live within 3 miles of one
- **Military families:** Bases contaminated with PFAS from firefighting foam, TCE from degreasing operations
- **Everyone:** PFAS contamination affects 100+ million Americans' drinking water

## Quick Actions

### Check What's in Your Community (10 minutes)

Before anything else, find out what toxic facilities and contamination exist near you:

1. **Check TRI releases near you:** [EPA TRI Explorer](https://enviro.epa.gov/triexplorer/) -- Enter your ZIP code to see what chemicals are being released by nearby facilities
2. **Check Superfund sites near you:** [EPA Cleanups Near Me](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/search-superfund-sites-where-you-live) -- Search by address for the nearest contaminated sites
3. **Check facility violations:** [EPA ECHO](https://echo.epa.gov) -- See if nearby facilities have been cited for pollution violations
4. **Check environmental justice data:** [EPA EJScreen](https://ejscreen.epa.gov) -- See pollution burden overlaid with demographic data for your census tract
5. **Check your water for PFAS:** [EWG PFAS Contamination Map](https://www.ewg.org/interactive-maps/pfas_contamination/) -- See if PFAS has been detected in your water system

### Request Your Water Be Tested

- Contact your **local water utility** and request their annual Consumer Confidence Report (they're required to send it)
- If you're concerned about PFAS, request specific PFAS testing -- many states offer free or subsidized testing through their health departments
- For private wells, contact your **county health department** for testing options
- **Home PFAS test kits** are available through companies like Cyclopure (~$150) and SimpleLab Tap Score

### File a FOIA Request for Facility Data

If a facility near you concerns you, you can request detailed emissions data through the Freedom of Information Act:

1. **Start with publicly available data** at TRI Explorer and ECHO (see above)
2. If you need more detail, submit a FOIA request to EPA at [foia.gov](https://www.foia.gov) or the [EPA FOIA portal](https://www.epa.gov/foia)
3. **Include in your request:** facility name and address, specific chemicals you're asking about, time period, and the types of records you want (emissions reports, inspection records, violation notices)
4. Specify that you're requesting information under the Freedom of Information Act, 5 U.S.C. § 552
5. You can also FOIA your **state environmental agency** -- they often have more detailed local records

**Tip:** Request a fee waiver if you're using the information for public interest purposes. FOIA responses can take weeks to months, so file early.

[source: EPA - The FOIA Request Process](https://www.epa.gov/foia/foia-request-process)

### Attend a Superfund Community Advisory Group (CAG) Meeting

If there's a Superfund site near you, it likely has a Community Advisory Group -- a forum for residents to get information and provide input on cleanup decisions. Find yours through the [EPA Superfund site search](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/search-superfund-sites-where-you-live) and look for "Community Involvement" in the site profile.

### Get Health Screening

If you live near an industrial facility, Superfund site, or in an area with known contamination:

- Ask your doctor about screening for cancers associated with your specific exposure (e.g., liver cancer for vinyl chloride, kidney cancer for PFAS/TCE)
- Request **blood or urine biomonitoring** for specific chemicals if your doctor supports it
- Contact your **state health department** about health studies or registries for your area
- Check if your community has an **ATSDR (Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry) health assessment** at [atsdr.cdc.gov](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov)

### Report Cancer Clusters

If you suspect elevated cancer rates in your community:

1. **Document cases:** Gather information (with consent) about cancer diagnoses in your area -- types, dates, ages, and locations
2. **Contact your state health department** and request a cancer cluster investigation
3. **File a report with CDC/ATSDR** through [atsdr.cdc.gov](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/hacsummary.html)
4. **Connect with local environmental health groups** -- many have experience guiding communities through the investigation process
5. **Preserve records** -- keep copies of all correspondence with agencies

### Push for State-Level Protections

States can set standards stricter than federal requirements:

- **PFAS standards:** Several states (Michigan, New Jersey, New Hampshire) have set PFAS drinking water limits stricter than EPA
- **State Superfund programs:** Can fund cleanups when federal funding is insufficient
- **Right-to-know expansions:** Push for state laws requiring disclosure of chemicals beyond the federal TRI list
- **Buffer zones:** Advocate for minimum distances between industrial facilities and homes, schools, and hospitals
- **Cumulative impact laws:** New Jersey (2020) requires DEP to consider cumulative pollution burden on overburdened communities when issuing permits -- push your state to adopt similar laws

### Reduce Your Personal Exposure

While systemic change is essential, you can reduce individual exposure:

- **Filter your water** -- Activated carbon filters remove many contaminants; reverse osmosis removes PFAS
- **Check your home for asbestos** before renovating (common in pre-1980 homes) -- hire a certified inspector
- **Test for radon** (second leading cause of lung cancer) -- test kits are ~$15 at hardware stores
- **Choose organic produce** when possible, especially for the [Dirty Dozen](https://www.ewg.org/foodnews/dirty-dozen.php) (highest pesticide residue)
- **Avoid burning treated wood, plastics, or trash** -- releases dioxins and other carcinogens
- **Check product safety** at EWG's [Skin Deep database](https://www.ewg.org/skindeep/) for personal care products
