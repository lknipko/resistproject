# Citizen Science & Community Monitoring

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** With EPA enforcement down 70% and monitoring budgets slashed, citizen science has never been more important. You can monitor your own air and water quality, track wildlife, document pollution, and contribute to research that holds polluters accountable. The data you collect has been used in court cases, influenced policy, and filled critical gaps in government monitoring.

This page is your guide to getting started -- no science degree required.

## Facts

### Why Citizen Science Matters Now

- EPA compliance inspections have been **halved**
- Air quality monitoring grants reduced by **over 50%**
- State environmental grant funding cut **70%**
- Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program proposed for elimination
- Scientific advisory boards reconstituted with industry-aligned members

When government monitoring declines, community monitoring fills the gap. Citizen-collected data has been:
- Admitted as evidence in environmental lawsuits
- Used by state agencies to identify pollution hotspots
- Published in peer-reviewed scientific journals
- Used to trigger EPA enforcement actions

### [+] Air Quality Monitoring

**PurpleAir** ([purpleair.com](https://www.purpleair.com))
- Community-operated PM2.5 sensors (~$250 for an outdoor sensor)
- Real-time data feeds into a global map
- Data has been used by researchers at Stanford, UC Berkeley, and EPA
- Over 40,000 sensors worldwide creating the densest air quality network in history

**AirNow** ([airnow.gov](https://www.airnow.gov))
- Official EPA air quality index (AQI) for your location
- Fire and Smoke Map for wildfire air quality
- Free alerts for unhealthy air quality days

**OpenAQ** ([openaq.org](https://openaq.org))
- Open-source platform aggregating global air quality data
- APIs for researchers and developers

### [+] Water Quality Monitoring

**Volunteer Water Monitoring Programs**
- Most states have established volunteer monitoring programs
- Training typically provided by state agencies or watershed organizations
- Common parameters: pH, dissolved oxygen, temperature, turbidity, conductivity
- Equipment ranges from simple test kits (~$30) to professional meters (~$500)

**EWG Tap Water Database** ([ewg.org/tapwater](https://www.ewg.org/tapwater/))
- Look up contaminants detected in your water system
- Compare results to health guidelines vs. legal limits
- Find out if your water utility has violations

**PFAS Testing**
- Home test kits available through [cyclopure.com](https://www.cyclopure.com) and others (~$150)
- State health departments often offer free or subsidized testing
- Document results -- they create a record for your community

### [+] Wildlife & Biodiversity Tracking

**iNaturalist** ([inaturalist.org](https://www.inaturalist.org))
- Photo-based species identification using AI
- Over 150 million observations contributed globally
- Data used by conservation researchers worldwide
- Free app for iOS and Android

**eBird** ([ebird.org](https://ebird.org))
- Cornell Lab of Ornithology's bird observation platform
- Used to track migration patterns, population changes, and habitat use
- Over 1 billion bird observations contributed

**BioBlitz Events**
- Organized community species inventories in a specific area and time
- Often hosted by parks, nature centers, and conservation organizations
- Great way to get started with citizen science as a family or group

### [+] Pollution Documentation

When you observe pollution -- illegal dumping, spills, unusual discharges, smoke, or odors:

1. **Document:** Photos with timestamps, GPS coordinates, description
2. **Report:** State environmental agency hotline, EPA's [tips and complaints](https://echo.epa.gov/report-environmental-violations)
3. **Record:** Keep your own log -- dates, photos, weather conditions
4. **Share:** Local watershed groups, environmental organizations, local media

Your documentation creates an evidence trail that can trigger investigations.

## Analysis

### The Power of Community Data

Citizen science is not a consolation prize for lost government monitoring. It has unique advantages:

- **Density:** 40,000 PurpleAir sensors provide more data points than EPA's ~1,000 official monitors
- **Speed:** Community reports can trigger faster responses than scheduled inspections
- **Accountability:** Independent data keeps both government and industry honest
- **Litigation:** Courts have increasingly accepted citizen-collected environmental data
- **Engagement:** People who monitor their environment become advocates for protecting it

### Limitations to Be Aware Of

- Consumer sensors are less precise than professional instruments
- Data quality depends on proper calibration and siting
- Not all agencies accept citizen data for regulatory purposes
- Interpreting results requires understanding context and background levels

## Quick Actions

### Start Monitoring Today

**Air Quality (5 minutes):**
1. Download the AirNow app or visit [airnow.gov](https://www.airnow.gov)
2. Set up alerts for unhealthy air quality in your area
3. Check the [PurpleAir map](https://map.purpleair.com/) for sensors near you

**Water Quality (30 minutes):**
1. Look up your water system on [EWG's Tap Water Database](https://www.ewg.org/tapwater/)
2. Contact your water utility for their annual Consumer Confidence Report
3. Check if your state has a volunteer water monitoring program

**Wildlife (10 minutes):**
1. Download iNaturalist (free) and take a photo of any plant or animal
2. The AI will suggest an identification -- experts will confirm
3. Your observation becomes part of a global biodiversity dataset

### Report Environmental Violations

- **EPA Tips:** [echo.epa.gov/report-environmental-violations](https://echo.epa.gov/report-environmental-violations)
- **State Agencies:** Search "[your state] environmental violations hotline"
- **Document everything:** Photos, dates, GPS coordinates, weather conditions

## Sustained Actions

### Invest in Equipment

| Item | Cost | What It Monitors |
|------|------|-----------------|
| PurpleAir sensor (outdoor) | ~$250 | PM2.5 particulate matter |
| Water test kit (basic) | ~$30 | pH, nitrates, hardness, chlorine |
| Professional water meter | ~$500 | Dissolved oxygen, conductivity, temperature |
| PFAS home test | ~$150 | PFAS "forever chemicals" |
| iNaturalist app | Free | Species identification |

### Join Programs

- **SciStarter** ([scistarter.org](https://scistarter.org)) -- Directory of 3,000+ citizen science projects
- **Zooniverse** ([zooniverse.org](https://www.zooniverse.org)) -- Online citizen science research from home
- **Your state's volunteer monitoring program** -- Contact your state environmental agency
- **Local watershed organizations** -- Find yours through the [Waterkeeper Alliance](https://waterkeeper.org)

### Start a Monitoring Group

1. Recruit neighbors and community members
2. Choose a focus (air, water, wildlife, or all three)
3. Get basic equipment and training
4. Establish regular monitoring schedules
5. Share data publicly through established platforms
6. Connect with local environmental organizations for support
