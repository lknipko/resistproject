# Clean Water Protections

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Federal clean water protections are being dismantled on multiple fronts. The "Waters of the United States" (WOTUS) definition has been narrowed, removing protections from wetlands and streams. PFAS ("forever chemicals") drinking water standards face delays. The Clean Water State Revolving Fund has been cut 75%, and Superfund cleanup funding slashed 67%. EPA enforcement of water quality violations is down approximately 70%.

These cuts affect every community in America -- the water you drink, the rivers you swim in, and the land that filters your groundwater.

## Facts

### WOTUS Rollback

The Clean Water Act protects "waters of the United States" from pollution, but the definition of which waters qualify has been contested for decades. In *Sackett v. EPA* (2023), the Supreme Court dramatically narrowed WOTUS, eliminating federal protections for:

- Wetlands not directly connected to navigable waters
- Ephemeral and intermittent streams
- Isolated ponds and lakes
- Groundwater-fed wetlands

The EPA then finalized a conforming rule in 2023. The current administration has further narrowed enforcement, effectively abandoning oversight of many remaining protected waters.

[source: Supreme Court - Sackett v. EPA](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/22pdf/21-454_4g15.pdf) | [source: EPA WOTUS Rule](https://www.epa.gov/wotus)

### [+] PFAS Drinking Water Standards

In April 2024, EPA finalized the first-ever national drinking water standards for PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances), setting maximum contaminant levels for six PFAS compounds. These "forever chemicals" are linked to cancer, thyroid disease, immune system damage, and developmental harm.

The current administration has:
- Delayed enforcement deadlines for PFAS standards
- Proposed reducing EPA's PFAS research budget
- Cut funding to states for PFAS testing and remediation
- Industry groups have challenged the standards in court

An estimated 100+ million Americans have PFAS in their drinking water.

[source: EPA PFAS Drinking Water Rule](https://www.epa.gov/sdwa/and-polyfluoroalkyl-substances-pfas) | [source: EWG PFAS Contamination Map](https://www.ewg.org/interactive-maps/pfas_contamination/)

### [+] Budget Cuts to Water Programs

| Program | FY2024 Enacted | FY2026 Proposed | Cut |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|-----|
| Clean Water State Revolving Fund | ~$1.6 billion | ~$400 million | 75% |
| Superfund Cleanup | ~$1.8 billion | ~$600 million | 67% |
| State Environmental Grants | ~$4.0 billion | ~$1.2 billion | 70% |

The Clean Water State Revolving Fund provides low-interest loans to communities for water infrastructure -- wastewater treatment, stormwater management, and drinking water systems. A 75% cut means thousands of communities will delay or cancel critical water projects.

[source: EPA FY2026 Budget in Brief](https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2025-05/fy-2026-epa-bib.pdf)

### [+] Lead and Copper Rule

The Biden-era Lead and Copper Rule Improvements (LCRI), which required removal of all lead service lines within 10 years, is being "reviewed" with potential weakening or delays. An estimated 9.2 million homes still receive water through lead pipes.

[source: EPA Lead and Copper Rule](https://www.epa.gov/ground-water-and-drinking-water/revised-lead-and-copper-rule)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Communities without robust state environmental agencies will see water quality monitoring gaps
- Superfund site cleanups will stall -- there are currently 1,336 sites on the National Priorities List
- PFAS contamination will continue spreading without federal enforcement
- Low-income communities and communities of color are disproportionately affected
- Rural communities dependent on federal grants for water infrastructure face the biggest impacts

### Who's Affected

- **Communities near Superfund sites:** 73 million Americans live within 3 miles of a Superfund site
- **Communities with PFAS contamination:** 100+ million Americans affected
- **Homes with lead pipes:** 9.2 million households
- **Rural communities:** Dependent on federal water infrastructure grants
- **Environmental justice communities:** Disproportionately located near contaminated sites

## Quick Actions

### Check Your Water Quality

1. **Look up your water system:** [EPA's Drinking Water Watch](https://sdwis.epa.gov/ords/sfdw_pub/r/sfdw/sdwis_fed_reports_public/200)
2. **Check for PFAS:** [EWG PFAS Contamination Map](https://www.ewg.org/interactive-maps/pfas_contamination/)
3. **Test your water:** Get a certified lab test (~$30-150) through your state health department
4. **Check for lead:** Free lead testing available in most states -- contact your water utility

## Sustained Actions

### Test and Document

- Get your water tested annually -- create a baseline record
- Report water quality concerns to your state environmental agency
- Document changes in local water bodies (color, smell, algae blooms)
- Join community water monitoring programs

### Support Organizations

- **Clean Water Action** ([cleanwateraction.org](https://www.cleanwateraction.org)) -- Grassroots water advocacy
- **Waterkeeper Alliance** ([waterkeeper.org](https://www.waterkeeper.org)) -- 300+ local waterkeeper organizations monitoring rivers, lakes, and coastlines
- **Natural Resources Defense Council** ([nrdc.org](https://www.nrdc.org)) -- Environmental litigation including water quality
- **Environmental Working Group** ([ewg.org](https://www.ewg.org)) -- PFAS testing and advocacy

### Push for State-Level Protection

Many states have stronger water protections than federal law:
- Contact your state legislators about state PFAS standards
- Support state Superfund programs that can fill federal gaps
- Advocate for state-level lead pipe replacement funding
- Push for state water quality monitoring to compensate for EPA enforcement declines
