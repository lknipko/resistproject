# Climate Change: Why It Matters

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Climate change is not a future problem. In 2024, the U.S. experienced 27 billion-dollar weather disasters costing $182.7 billion. Heat killed over 2,300 Americans in 2023 alone. Wildfires burned nearly 9 million acres in 2024 — far above the 40-year average. Hurricanes Helene and Milton caused $100+ billion in combined damage. Home insurance premiums have risen 49% since 2019, with insurers fleeing high-risk states entirely. Food prices for climate-affected items rose 15.6% in a single year. The Colorado River is in its third decade of megadrought — the worst in 1,200 years.

This is what climate change costs right now, every year, in American lives and dollars.

## Facts

![U.S. billion-dollar weather disasters from 2000 to 2025, showing rising frequency and costs](/environment/charts/billion-dollar-disasters.png)

### Key Points

- **2,325 Americans died from heat in 2023** — more than double the annual toll from 1999 — and heat deaths have been accelerating since 2016 [source: CDC Heat & Health Tracker](https://ephtracking.cdc.gov/Applications/heatTracker/)
- **27 billion-dollar disasters in 2024** totaling $182.7 billion in damage — the second-highest year on record [source: NOAA Billion-Dollar Disasters](https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/access/billions/)
- **Since 1980, the U.S. has suffered 403 billion-dollar weather disasters** costing over $2.9 trillion total [source: Climate Central Billion-Dollar Disasters](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-matters/billion-dollar-disasters-2025)
- **Home insurance premiums rose 49% since 2019** — more than double general inflation — and insurers canceled nearly 2 million homeowner policies between 2018-2023 [source: Yale E360 - Insurance Out of Reach](https://e360.yale.edu/features/climate-change-home-insurance)
- **Over 20 billion pounds of crop losses in 2024** from drought, heat, wildfires, flooding, and hurricanes — totaling $20.3 billion in agricultural damage [source: American Farm Bureau - 2024 Crop Losses](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/hurricanes-heat-and-hardship-counting-2024s-crop-losses)

### [+] Health Impacts

**Heat-Related Deaths**

Heat is now the deadliest weather hazard in the United States. CDC data shows heat-related deaths rose from 1,069 in 1999 to 2,325 in 2023 — a 117% increase. The seven most deadly years have all occurred since 2016.

[source: JAMA - Heat-Related Deaths 2000-2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12228637/) | [source: ABC News - 2,000 Die Annually from Heat](https://abcnews.com/Health/us-braces-extreme-heat-statistics-show-2000-die/story?id=124136576)

These numbers are almost certainly undercounted. Heat exacerbates heart attacks, strokes, kidney failure, and respiratory illness, but those deaths are often attributed to the underlying condition rather than heat exposure. Over 120,000 Americans were admitted to emergency rooms for heat-related illness in 2023 alone.

[source: The Lancet - Increasing Burden of Heat-Related Mortality](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/eclinm/article/PIIS2589-5370(24)00444-9/fulltext)

**Air Quality and Wildfire Smoke**

Wildfire smoke is a growing public health crisis. The January 2025 Los Angeles wildfires contributed to an estimated 440 excess deaths in LA County — far exceeding the 30 official fire fatalities — driven by smoke-related heart and lung conditions and healthcare disruptions.

[source: Boston University - LA Wildfire Excess Deaths](https://www.bu.edu/sph/news/articles/2025/death-count-for-2025-la-county-wildfires-likely-hundreds-higher-than-official-records-show/)

**Who Is Most Vulnerable**

- **Outdoor workers:** Construction, agriculture, delivery — limited ability to escape heat
- **Children and elderly:** More susceptible to heat illness and respiratory damage
- **Low-income communities:** Less access to air conditioning, healthcare, and evacuation resources
- **Communities of color:** Disproportionately live in urban heat islands and near pollution sources

### [+] Economic Costs

**Billion-Dollar Disasters Are Accelerating**

The three highest years for billion-dollar disasters have all been in the last three years: 2023 (28 events), 2024 (27 events), and 2025 (23 events). From 1980 through 2025, the U.S. has suffered 426 separate billion-dollar weather and climate disasters, costing over $3.1 trillion.

[source: NOAA - 2024 Billion-Dollar Disasters](https://www.climate.gov/news-features/blogs/beyond-data/2024-active-year-us-billion-dollar-weather-and-climate-disasters) | [source: Climate Central - 2025 in Review](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-matters/2025-in-review)

**Insurance Market Collapse**

The insurance industry is retreating from climate-exposed areas:

- Insurers canceled nearly **2 million homeowner policies** between 2018-2023 — over four times the normal rate
- Average homeowner premiums rose **30-40% nationally** in five years; Florida premiums increased $1,450 between 2020-2023
- Progressive, Allstate, and State Farm have fled high-risk states or stopped writing new policies
- California's FAIR Plan (insurer of last resort) tripled from 140,000 to 610,000 policies between 2018-2025
- **14% of owner-occupied homes are now uninsured** nationally — up 6% from 2023 to 2024

A congressional committee has called this a "looming economic threat" with potential to trigger a financial crisis similar to 2008.

[source: Center for American Progress - Property Insurance Crisis](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/managing-the-climate-change-fueled-property-insurance-crisis/) | [source: Yale Law Journal - The Uninsurable Future](https://yalelawjournal.org/essay/the-uninsurable-future-the-climate-threat-to-property-insurance-and-how-to-stop-it)

**Infrastructure Damage**

Most U.S. roads, bridges, and transit systems were designed for historical weather patterns that no longer apply. Flooding threatens 60,000 miles of coastal roads and bridges. Climate-related road damage alone could cost $20 billion by end of century. In 2023, extreme heat caused a New York City bridge to expand and jam open; Fort Lauderdale's airport runways flooded during historic rainfall.

[source: Pew - Climate Risks to Infrastructure](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/issue-briefs/2024/09/climate-change-poses-risks-to-neglected-public-transportation-and-water-systems)

### [+] Food & Agriculture

**Crop Losses**

In 2024, weather and fire events caused over $20.3 billion in crop and rangeland losses — 11.1% of NOAA's total disaster costs. The breakdown:

| Cause | Crop Loss |
|-------|-----------|
| Drought, heat, and wildfires | $11+ billion |
| Flooding and hurricanes | $6.7 billion |
| Corn (flooding in Upper Midwest) | $3.85 billion |
| Forage (drought in TX, SD, KS, OK) | $3.8 billion |
| Soybeans | $3.2 billion |
| Cotton (Texas — 3rd consecutive year) | $1.8 billion |

[source: American Farm Bureau - 2024 Crop Losses](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/hurricanes-heat-and-hardship-counting-2024s-crop-losses)

**Global Yields Declining**

Research shows global yields of barley, maize, and wheat are already 4-13% lower than they would have been without climate trends. In the U.S. Midwest, heat stress is the strongest driver of reduced yield stability, with impacts worsening between 1982 and 2020.

[source: Stanford - Heat and Drought Hurting Crop Yields](https://woods.stanford.edu/news/heat-and-drought-are-quietly-hurting-crop-yields)

**Rising Food Prices**

Climate-affected food prices are rising far faster than general inflation:

- **Beef:** Up 10.3% year-over-year due to Great Plains drought shrinking cattle herds
- **Coffee:** Up 14.5% for U.S. consumers after Brazilian drought devastated crops
- **Cocoa:** Prices spiked 280% in April 2024 after heat waves in West Africa
- **Overall:** Climate-impacted foods rose 15.6% compared to August 2024

Projected warming by 2035 could drive food price inflation up 1.4-1.8 percentage points per year permanently.

[source: Climate Central - Climate Change and Food Prices](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-matters/climate-change-and-food-prices) | [source: USDA Food Price Outlook](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-price-outlook/summary-findings)

### [+] Water & Drought

**The Colorado River Crisis**

The Colorado River Basin is in its third decade of megadrought — the worst in 1,200 years. The river supplies water to 40 million people across seven states and irrigates 5.5 million acres of farmland.

- **Lake Mead** is projected to stay in Level 1 Shortage through 2026, approximately 20 feet below the shortage trigger elevation
- **Lake Powell** is approximately 162 feet below full pool and could approach minimum power pool by December 2026, threatening Glen Canyon Dam's ability to generate hydropower
- **Nevada's water allocation** has been cut 21,000 acre-feet annually under shortage conditions

[source: Bureau of Reclamation - 2026 Operating Conditions](https://www.usbr.gov/newsroom/news-release/5211) | [source: Western Water - Colorado River Outlook Darkens](https://www.western-water.com/2026/02/17/colorado-river-runoff-outlook-darkens/)

**Groundwater Depletion**

The Ogallala Aquifer, which irrigates 30% of America's crops across eight Great Plains states, is being pumped faster than it recharges. Parts of Kansas and Texas have seen water levels drop over 150 feet. Some areas may be functionally depleted within 25 years.

**Drinking Water Risks**

Drought concentrates contaminants in reservoirs and rivers. Lower water levels in treatment plants increase the risk of harmful algal blooms, which produce toxins that are difficult to remove through standard treatment. Communities across the Southwest and Great Plains face growing threats to their drinking water supply.

### [+] Extreme Events

**Hurricanes Are Getting Stronger**

About 80% of major hurricanes (Category 3-5) undergo rapid intensification. Climate change is making this more frequent and more extreme:

- **Hurricane Helene (2024):** Intensified from Category 1 to Category 4 in 24 hours. Made landfall with 140 mph winds — strongest hurricane on record in Florida's Big Bend. Caused catastrophic flooding in western North Carolina (30+ inches of rain). Over 230 deaths. $78.7 billion in damage.
- **Hurricane Milton (2024):** One of the fastest-intensifying Atlantic hurricanes ever. $60+ billion in damage. Combined with Helene, over $100 billion in two weeks.
- **Hurricane Melissa (2025):** Underwent extreme rapid intensification — 70 mph increase in 24 hours — over waters made up to 700 times more likely to be that warm due to climate change.

[source: Climate Central - Hurricane Rapid Intensification](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-matters/hurricane-rapid-intensification) | [source: NOAA - Hurricane Helene Report](https://www.nhc.noaa.gov/data/tcr/AL092024_Helene.pdf)

**Wildfires Are Expanding**

In 2024, nearly 9 million acres burned — far exceeding the 40-year average of approximately 5 million acres. The fire season is extending beyond traditional months:

- In January 2025, California's Palisades and Eaton fires burned over 37,000 acres, destroyed 12,000+ structures, and caused $76-131 billion in economic losses — in what was historically the middle of winter
- Snow is melting earlier in spring and fall precipitation is arriving later, creating longer dry periods
- California is approaching a year-round fire season

[source: NIFC - 2024 Wildfire Annual Report](https://www.nifc.gov/sites/default/files/NICC/2-Predictive%20Services/Intelligence/Annual%20Reports/2024/annual_report_2024.pdf) | [source: Britannica - LA Wildfires 2025](https://www.britannica.com/event/Los-Angeles-wildfires-of-2025)

**Climate Displacement**

Over 1.2 million Americans were displaced by weather-related disasters in a single recent year. Entire communities have been forced to relocate — the Isle de Jean Charles community in Louisiana was formally relocated due to sea-level rise and land loss. There is currently no legal framework in the United States to protect climate-displaced people.

[source: Urban Institute - Climate Migrants](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/who-are-americas-climate-migrants-and-where-will-they-go) | [source: NPR - Climate Migration Changing the US](https://www.npr.org/2024/03/26/1239904742/how-climate-driven-migration-could-change-the-face-of-the-u-s)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- **This is not about the future.** The $182.7 billion cost of 2024 disasters, the 2,325 heat deaths in 2023, the insurance market collapse — these are current costs Americans are paying right now.
- **Every system is interconnected.** Drought raises food prices, which hits low-income families hardest. Wildfires destroy homes and worsen air quality hundreds of miles downwind. Hurricanes damage infrastructure that communities cannot afford to rebuild.
- **Costs are accelerating.** The three most disaster-heavy years on record are 2023, 2024, and 2025. Insurance markets are failing. Infrastructure designed for a different climate is deteriorating faster than it can be repaired.
- **Adaptation is no longer optional.** Even with aggressive emission reductions, decades of warming are already locked in. Communities must prepare for more heat, more flooding, more fire, and less water.
- **Federal support is being cut.** NOAA's billion-dollar disaster tracking was axed in 2025. EPA science advisory boards have been dissolved. Climate research funding is being reduced across agencies. States and communities are increasingly on their own.

### Who's Affected

- **Coastal communities:** Sea-level rise, hurricane intensification, flooding, and insurance collapse
- **Agricultural regions:** Drought, heat stress on crops and livestock, water allocation cuts
- **Southwest and Great Plains:** Megadrought, groundwater depletion, water rationing
- **Low-income families:** Higher food prices, inability to afford insurance or air conditioning, fewer resources to evacuate or rebuild
- **Communities of color:** Disproportionately located in flood zones, urban heat islands, and near pollution sources
- **Outdoor workers:** Construction, agriculture, and delivery workers face deadly heat exposure
- **Rural communities:** Wildfire risk, limited emergency services, longer recovery times
- **Everyone with a mortgage:** Lenders may require insurance that is increasingly unavailable or unaffordable

### Check Your Climate Risk

- **Risk Factor** ([riskfactor.com](https://riskfactor.com)) — Free flood, fire, heat, and wind risk scores for any U.S. address
- **FEMA Flood Maps** ([msc.fema.gov](https://msc.fema.gov/portal/home)) — Official flood zone maps for your property
- **AirNow** ([airnow.gov](https://www.airnow.gov)) — Real-time air quality and wildfire smoke forecasts
- **Drought Monitor** ([droughtmonitor.unl.edu](https://droughtmonitor.unl.edu)) — Current drought conditions across the U.S.

### Prepare Your Household

- **Create a disaster supply kit** — FEMA recommends supplies for 72 hours minimum ([ready.gov](https://www.ready.gov))
- **Review your insurance** — Check flood and fire coverage; standard homeowner policies often exclude both
- **Sign up for local alerts** — Register for your county's emergency notification system
- **Know your evacuation routes** — Identify at least two routes and a meeting point for your household

## Sustained Actions

### Support Climate and Disaster Organizations

- **Climate Central** ([climatecentral.org](https://www.climatecentral.org)) — Independent research and journalism on climate impacts; took over NOAA's billion-dollar disaster tracking after federal cuts
- **American Red Cross** ([redcross.org](https://www.redcross.org)) — Disaster relief and preparedness; volunteer opportunities for local disaster response teams
- **Citizens' Climate Lobby** ([citizensclimatelobby.org](https://citizensclimatelobby.org)) — Bipartisan grassroots advocacy for carbon pricing policy; local chapters in every congressional district
- **Union of Concerned Scientists** ([ucsusa.org](https://www.ucsusa.org)) — Science-based climate policy advocacy and accountability campaigns
- **Rebuild by Design** ([rebuildbydesign.org](https://www.rebuildbydesign.org)) — Community-driven climate resilience infrastructure projects

### Push for Local Climate Action

- **Attend city council and county commissioner meetings** — advocate for updated building codes, heat action plans, and flood mitigation investments
- **Support local climate adaptation plans** — many cities are developing resilience strategies and need public input
- **Advocate for cooling centers and green infrastructure** — tree canopy, reflective surfaces, and public cooling reduce urban heat deaths
- **Join or start a Community Emergency Response Team (CERT)** — FEMA-trained volunteer teams that respond to local disasters ([ready.gov/cert](https://www.ready.gov/cert))
