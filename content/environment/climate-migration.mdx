# Climate Migration: The Coming Displacement Crisis

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The World Bank's *Groundswell* report projects **216 million people** will be displaced within their own countries by climate impacts by 2050 — driven by water scarcity, crop failure, and rising seas. Currently, **22–29 million people** are displaced by weather-related disasters annually. There is **no international legal protection** for climate migrants — the 1951 Refugee Convention does not cover climate displacement. In the U.S., Alaska Native villages are already relocating due to permafrost thaw and coastal erosion, and federal funding for their moves has been frozen by the current administration.

Climate displacement is not a future scenario. It is happening now, in the U.S. and worldwide, with no adequate legal or policy infrastructure to respond.

## Facts

### The Scale: World Bank Groundswell Projections

The World Bank's landmark *Groundswell* report (updated 2021) finds that climate change could force **216 million people across six world regions** to move within their countries by 2050 — under a pessimistic emissions scenario with minimal development investment.

Regional breakdown by 2050:
- **Sub-Saharan Africa:** 86 million internal climate migrants
- **East Asia and Pacific:** 49 million
- **South Asia:** 40 million
- **North Africa:** 19 million
- **Latin America:** 17 million
- **Eastern Europe and Central Asia:** 5 million

Hotspots of internal climate migration could emerge **as early as 2030**. However, the report also found that aggressive emissions reductions and inclusive development could reduce the scale of climate migration by **up to 80 percent**.

[source: World Bank Groundswell Report](https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2021/09/13/climate-change-could-force-216-million-people-to-migrate-within-their-own-countries-by-2050)

### [+] Current Displacement: Already Happening

Weather-related disasters currently displace an estimated **22–29 million people per year** globally. In 2020, approximately 29 million people were newly displaced by weather events. These numbers do not include slow-onset displacement from sea level rise, desertification, or chronic drought.

Drivers of current and near-term displacement:
- **Sea level rise** threatening Pacific island nations, Bangladesh, and US coastal communities
- **Drought and crop failure** in sub-Saharan Africa and Central America
- **Wildfire** in California, Colorado, Canada, and Australia
- **Extreme heat** making parts of the Middle East, South Asia, and the US Southwest seasonally uninhabitable
- **Permafrost thaw and coastal erosion** destroying Alaska Native communities

[source: Migration Data Portal — Environmental Migration](https://www.migrationdataportal.org/themes/environmental_migration_and_statistics_old)

### [+] The Legal Gap: No Protection for Climate Migrants

The **1951 Refugee Convention** and its 1967 Protocol are the only binding global treaties that define refugee status and rights. They define a refugee as someone fleeing persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a social group. Climate displacement does not meet this definition.

No binding international treaty recognizes climate migrants:
- **"Climate refugee" has no legal standing** in international law
- The **Climate Displaced Persons Act** (S.3340) was introduced in the 118th US Congress but did not pass
- **Temporary Protected Status (TPS)** in the US can cover nationals of countries experiencing "environmental disasters," but is limited, discretionary, and temporary
- Some regional frameworks (1969 African Union Convention, 1984 Cartagena Declaration) have broader refugee definitions that could cover climate displacement, but they are not universally ratified

[source: Georgetown Environmental Law Review — The Legal Gap for Climate-Displaced People](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/environmental-law-review/blog/when-climate-change-meets-immigration-law-the-legal-gap-for-climate-displaced-people/)

### [+] US Domestic Cases: Alaska Native Communities

At least **144 Alaska Native communities** face threats from erosion, flooding, permafrost thaw, or some combination, according to the Alaska Native Tribal Health Consortium. Many voted to relocate more than 20 years ago. Progress has been agonizingly slow due to lack of federal funding and coordination.

**Newtok (Yup'ik Nation):**
- Located on the Ninglick River in western Alaska, experiencing coastal erosion at approximately **70 feet per year**
- Community voted to relocate to new village of Mertarvik in 2003
- Relocation took **decades and an estimated $160 million** for roughly 300 residents
- Selected for $25 million in federal funding — but FEMA halted unpaid grants in April 2025

**Shishmaref (Inupiaq Nation):**
- Inhabited for over 4,000 years; approximately 600 residents
- Community voted to relocate in 2001 due to coastal erosion and loss of protective sea ice
- More than **$25 million** spent on shoreline protection to "hold back the sea" while relocation remains stalled
- Full relocation estimated to cost $180 million+; funding has not been secured

**The Pattern:**
Many Alaska Native communities are caught in limbo — physically split between old village sites and new relocation sites, forced to maintain infrastructure in two locations, without a clear federal commitment to fund the full relocation. The Biden administration committed $135 million for tribal community relocations; the status of that commitment is uncertain under the current administration.

[source: DOI — $135M Tribal Relocation Commitment](https://www.doi.gov/pressreleases/biden-harris-administration-makes-135-million-commitment-support-relocation-tribal-communities-affected-climate-change) | [source: US Climate Resilience Toolkit — Newtok](https://toolkit.climate.gov/case-study/relocating-village-newtok-alaska-due-coastal-erosion) | [source: US Climate Resilience Toolkit — Shishmaref](https://toolkit.climate.gov/case-study/relocating-native-village-shishmaref-alaska-due-coastal-erosion)

### [+] Louisiana: America's First Climate Refugees

**Isle de Jean Charles** in Terrebonne Parish, Louisiana is the most documented case of US domestic climate migration. The island once encompassed more than **22,000 acres**; today only **320 acres** remain due to coastal erosion, land subsidence, and rising seas.

- In 2016, the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development awarded Louisiana **$48.3 million** to plan and implement a structured, voluntary resettlement for Isle de Jean Charles residents
- This became the **first federally funded community-scale climate relocation** in US history
- Louisiana's coastal master plan projects sea levels rising **1.41 to 2.72 feet by 2067**, putting more than **1.2 million people** at risk of coastal flooding statewide

However, reporting from 2025 found that the Isle de Jean Charles resettlement was plagued by broken promises, community division, and unresolved questions — raising warnings about how the US will handle the much larger wave of climate displacement to come.

[source: Isle de Jean Charles Resettlement Project](https://isledejeancharles.la.gov/) | [source: Inside Climate News — First Climate Relocation](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/28092025/louisiana-isle-de-jean-charles-climate-relocation/)

### [+] Other US Domestic Climate Migration Hotspots

**California and Western Wildfire:**
- Repeated catastrophic wildfires (Camp Fire, Dixie Fire, Marshall Fire) have permanently displaced tens of thousands of residents
- Some communities — particularly in the Sierra Nevada foothills and the wildland-urban interface — face conditions that may make long-term habitation untenable without massive fire prevention investment

**Miami and South Florida:**
- "Sunny day flooding" from tidal inundation occurs regularly in Miami Beach and other low-lying areas
- Property values in the lowest-lying areas have begun to decline relative to comparable higher-elevation properties
- Studies project that large portions of South Florida below 3 feet in elevation could experience chronic flooding by mid-century

**Norfolk, Virginia:**
- One of the fastest-sinking cities in the US due to land subsidence combined with sea level rise
- The Navy's largest base is located in Norfolk — climate displacement has national security implications
- City government is actively planning for managed retreat of some neighborhoods

**Phoenix and the Southwest:**
- Extreme heat events in Phoenix, Tucson, and the broader Southwest are intensifying
- Heat-related mortality disproportionately affects outdoor workers, elderly residents, and low-income communities without air conditioning
- The Colorado River, which supplies water to 40 million people across seven states, is in crisis

### The Racial Justice Dimension

Climate displacement is not racially neutral. In the US:
- Communities of color are disproportionately located in climate-vulnerable areas (coastal plains, floodplains, heat islands in urban cores)
- Alaska Native, Pacific Islander, and Black communities are on the front lines of displacement
- Redlining historically concentrated Black and brown communities in lower-lying, more flood-prone urban areas
- Federal disaster assistance programs have documented racial disparities in who receives aid and how much

[source: CFR — Migration, Displacement, and Climate Change](https://education.cfr.org/learn/reading/migration-displacement-climate-change)

## Analysis

### What This Means

Climate migration is one of the most consequential and least-planned-for consequences of climate change. The implications include:

- **Political instability** as large populations move across and within borders
- **Infrastructure strain** in receiving communities
- **Cultural erasure** for place-based indigenous communities
- **Loss of irreplaceable knowledge** as peoples who have lived in specific landscapes for generations are forced to relocate
- **Economic disruption** as property values in vulnerable areas decline and housing demand spikes in receiving areas
- **A legal vacuum** in which millions of displaced people have no recognized rights or formal status

The World Bank finding that emissions reductions could cut projected displacement by 80% means **climate migration is not inevitable at scale** — it is a policy choice.

### Who's Affected

- **Alaska Native communities:** Facing complete loss of ancestral homelands; relocation underway but chronically underfunded
- **Pacific Island nations:** Kiribati, Tuvalu, and Marshall Islands face near-total inundation; some have negotiated land rights elsewhere
- **Coastal residents in Louisiana, Florida, Virginia, and the Carolinas:** Sea level rise and subsidence
- **Agricultural workers and rural poor in Central America:** Drought-driven migration already straining US immigration system
- **Low-income urban residents:** Trapped in heat islands and flood zones without resources to relocate
- **Future generations:** Who will inherit a world reshaped by the choices made in the next decade

### Contact Your State Department Delegation

Climate migration is also an international issue. The US State Department's commitment to international climate finance — which funds adaptation and relocation programs in the most vulnerable countries — directly affects the scale of future displacement and migration pressure on the US border.

- Contact your senators who sit on the **Senate Foreign Relations Committee**
- Urge them to support international climate finance and adaptation funding
- Oppose withdrawal from international climate agreements and funding mechanisms

### Support Managed Retreat Planning in Your Community

If you live in a coastal, wildfire-prone, or extreme-heat region:

1. Attend city and county planning meetings where sea level rise, flood maps, or wildfire risk are discussed
2. Ask your local government whether they have a climate vulnerability assessment and managed retreat plan
3. Support "buy-out" programs that voluntarily relocate homeowners from the most vulnerable areas
4. Oppose development approvals in high-risk zones that will create future displacement liability

### Engage With Climate Refugee Legal Advocacy

The Climate Displaced Persons Act (S.3340, 118th Congress) was the first US legislation to create a dedicated legal category for climate-displaced people. While it did not pass, advocacy for its reintroduction continues.

- Contact your senators and ask them to reintroduce and cosponsor the Climate Displaced Persons Act
- Sign petitions and contact [Climate Refugees](https://www.climate-refugees.org) for campaign updates

### Monitor Climate Displacement in Your Community (Citizen Science)

Document local climate vulnerability to build the evidentiary record for managed retreat and adaptation funding:

- **NOAA Sea Level Rise Viewer** ([coast.noaa.gov/slr](https://coast.noaa.gov/slr/)) — See how sea level rise scenarios affect your neighborhood; use the maps in local planning meetings
- **First Street Foundation Flood Factor** ([floodfactor.com](https://www.floodfactor.com)) — Look up current and future flood risk for any address; share with city planners and neighbors
- **iNaturalist — Phenology Tracking** — Document how climate shifts are changing plant bloom times, bird migration, and other biological indicators in your area; this data contributes to climate science

### Volunteer With Disaster Recovery Organizations

Climate displacement happens in disasters. Being on the ground builds understanding of what communities need:

- **Team Rubicon** ([teamrubiconusa.org](https://teamrubiconusa.org)) — Veteran-led disaster relief; volunteers deploy after hurricanes, floods, and wildfires
- **Red Cross Disaster Action Teams** — Local volunteer teams that respond to climate-related emergencies and document displacement
- **All Hands and Hearts** ([allhandsandhearts.org](https://allhandsandhearts.org)) — Long-term community recovery from climate disasters; volunteers work on the ground for weeks at a time

### Choose Banks and Investments That Don't Finance Fossil Fuel Expansion

Reducing the speed of climate change directly reduces displacement:

- Move your savings to a bank or credit union that does not finance new fossil fuel projects — check [bankforgood.org](https://bankforgood.org) or [BankingOnClimateChange.org](https://www.bankingonclimate.com)
- If you have a 401(k) or pension, request a fossil-fuel-free investment option from your employer
- Divesting from fossil fuels reduces capital available for the emissions driving climate migration
