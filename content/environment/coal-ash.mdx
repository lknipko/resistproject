# Coal Ash Contamination

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Coal-fired power plants produce approximately 130 million tons of coal ash (coal combustion residuals, or CCR) every year -- the largest category of industrial waste in America. Coal ash contains arsenic, mercury, lead, cadmium, chromium, and selenium. There are roughly 775 coal ash disposal sites at 300 facilities across the country, and according to industry's own monitoring data, 94% of unlined coal ash ponds are contaminating groundwater with toxins above levels EPA deems safe for drinking water.

The Trump administration is delaying cleanup deadlines by years, pushing enforcement to underfunded state agencies, and proposing to let the largest unlined toxic ponds keep operating. Communities near these sites -- disproportionately low-income and communities of color -- face continued exposure to contaminated water. The reopened public comment period on EPA's proposal to weaken these coal-ash rules — expanding "beneficial use" exemptions and rescinding requirements for legacy disposal areas — **closed June 29, 2026**, with litigation considered likely once the rule is finalized.

## Facts

### What Is Coal Ash?

Coal ash is the waste left after burning coal in power plants. It includes:

- **Fly ash** -- fine particles captured from flue gases
- **Bottom ash** -- heavier particles that fall to the furnace bottom
- **Boiler slag** -- molite material from wet-bottom boilers
- **Flue gas desulfurization materials** -- byproducts of scrubbing sulfur from emissions

Coal ash is stored in surface impoundments (wet ponds) or dry landfills. The toxics in coal ash are not chemically bound -- they leach into groundwater, especially from unlined wet ponds. A single coal plant can produce hundreds of thousands of tons per year.

[source: EPA - Coal Ash Basics](https://www.epa.gov/coalash/coal-ash-basics)

### [+] Toxic Contents and Health Impacts

Coal ash contains concentrated heavy metals and toxins:

| Contaminant | Health Effects | At-Risk Populations |
|------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Arsenic** | Bladder, skin, kidney, and lung cancer; cardiovascular disease; neurological damage | Communities on well water near ash ponds |
| **Lead** | Brain damage, developmental delays, kidney disease; no safe level in children | Children, pregnant women |
| **Mercury** | Neurological damage, developmental harm, kidney damage | Developing fetuses, infants, children |
| **Selenium** | Reproductive failure in wildlife, liver damage in humans | Fishers, communities consuming local fish |
| **Cadmium** | Kidney disease, bone damage, lung cancer | Long-term exposed populations |
| **Chromium (VI)** | Lung cancer, liver and kidney damage | Workers, nearby residents |

People living next to an unlined coal ash pond who rely on a well for drinking water face a 1-in-50 chance of getting cancer from arsenic exposure alone -- nine times higher than the cancer risk from smoking a pack of cigarettes a day.

[source: Physicians for Social Responsibility - Coal Ash Hazardous to Human Health](https://psr.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/coal-ash-hazardous-to-human-health.pdf) | [source: EPA - Coal Combustion Residuals](https://www.epa.gov/coalash)

### [+] The Scale of Contamination

- **~775 coal ash disposal units** at ~300 coal-fired power facilities nationwide
- **94% of unlined ponds** contaminate groundwater above safe drinking water levels (per industry's own monitoring data)
- **Over 60 million cubic yards** of legacy coal ash identified at just 19 former power plant sites so far
- Contamination detected in groundwater, rivers, lakes, and private drinking wells near disposal sites

[source: Earthjustice - Coal Ash Contaminated Sites Map](https://earthjustice.org/feature/coal-ash-contaminated-sites-map) | [source: EPA - Coal Ash Enforcement](https://www.epa.gov/enforcement/national-enforcement-and-compliance-initiative-protecting-communities-coal-ash)

### [+] Major Disasters

**Kingston, Tennessee (December 22, 2008):**
A dike failure at TVA's Kingston Fossil Plant released 5.4 million cubic yards of coal ash slurry into the Emory River and surrounding land -- the largest industrial spill in U.S. history. The muck contained arsenic, lead, mercury, and radium. Of roughly 900 cleanup workers, more than 30 have died and over 200 are sick or dying from cancers and respiratory diseases linked to coal ash exposure. A federal jury ruled in 2018 that the cleanup contractor failed to inform workers about the dangers or provide proper protective equipment.

The excavated coal ash was shipped to a landfill in Perry County, Alabama -- a predominantly Black community where arsenic contamination subsequently rose to 80 times safe drinking water standards.

[source: EPA - Kingston TVA Coal Ash Spill Response](https://www.epa.gov/tn/epa-response-kingston-tva-coal-ash-spill) | [source: NRDC - Kingston Cleanup Workers](https://www.nrdc.org/stories/hundreds-workers-who-cleaned-countrys-worst-coal-ash-spill-are-now-sick-and-dying)

**Dan River, North Carolina (February 2014):**
A storm drain collapsed at a 50-year-old Duke Energy ash pond near Eden, NC, releasing 39,000 tons of ash and 27 million gallons of contaminated water into the Dan River. Contaminants were detected 70 miles downstream. Only a fraction of the spilled ash was recovered.

[source: Wikipedia - Kingston Fossil Plant Spill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingston_Fossil_Plant_coal_fly_ash_slurry_spill)

### [+] Environmental Justice

Coal ash sites are disproportionately located in low-income communities and communities of color:

- Over half of U.S. coal plants are located in low-income communities
- Nearly half of EPA-designated "high hazard" ash ponds in the Southeast are in low-income areas
- The Kingston spill cleanup waste was shipped from a predominantly white Tennessee county to a predominantly Black Alabama community
- Communities of color are more likely to rely on private wells near coal ash sites, with less access to alternative water supplies or legal resources

[source: Southeast Coal Ash - Public Health Impacts](http://www.southeastcoalash.org/about-coal-ash/public-health-impacts/) | [source: Earthjustice - Coal Ash in Neighborhoods](https://earthjustice.org/experts/lisa-evans/toxic-coal-ash-used-in-neighborhoods-poses-health-risks-even-decades-later)

### [+] Trump Administration Rollbacks

**Closure Deadline Extensions (November 2025):**
EPA proposed extending the deadline for closing unlined coal ash ponds larger than 40 acres from October 2028 to October 2031 -- giving 11 coal-fired power plants three additional years to continue dumping ash in unlined ponds. Docket: EPA-HQ-OLEM-2025-2864.

[source: Federal Register - CCR Closure Deadline Extension](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/11/28/2025-21597/hazardous-and-solid-waste-management-system-disposal-of-coal-combustion-residuals-from-electric)

**Monitoring Delay (February 2026):**
EPA finalized a rule extending deadlines for groundwater monitoring to February 2031 and public reporting to January 2032 -- a three-year delay. Without monitoring, contamination goes undetected and unreported.

[source: Earthjustice - EPA Delays Coal Ash Cleanup](https://earthjustice.org/press/2026/trump-epa-delays-cleanup-of-hundreds-of-coal-ash-dumps-in-advance-of-larger-rollback)

**Shifting to State Enforcement (March 2025):**
EPA announced plans to transfer coal ash regulation to state agencies. Many states lack the funding, staff, and political will to enforce coal ash rules -- especially states with powerful utility lobbies.

[source: Harvard EELP - Coal Ash Rule Tracker](https://eelp.law.harvard.edu/tracker/coal-ash-rule/)

**Pattern of Delay:**
A July 2025 EPA rule extending compliance deadlines was withdrawn in September 2025 after adverse public comments, only to be replaced by even longer delays in subsequent rules. The strategy: delay enforcement until cleanup requirements can be permanently weakened.

[source: EPA - Coal Ash Rule](https://www.epa.gov/coal-combustion-residuals/coal-ash-rule)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Unlined coal ash ponds will continue leaking toxins into groundwater for years longer than originally required
- Communities near the largest, most dangerous ash ponds get no relief until at least 2031
- Shifting to state enforcement creates a patchwork where protections depend on where you live -- and states with the most coal ash often have the weakest enforcement
- Without groundwater monitoring, contamination can spread undetected for years before it reaches drinking water wells
- The cost of eventual cleanup increases with every year of delay, as contaminant plumes spread

### Who's Affected

- **Communities near 775+ coal ash disposal sites** across the country -- check the Earthjustice map below
- **Private well users** near coal plants who have no municipal water alternative
- **Low-income communities and communities of color** disproportionately located near disposal sites
- **Workers** at coal plants and cleanup sites exposed to ash dust
- **Downstream communities** whose rivers and lakes receive runoff from disposal sites
- **Fishers and subsistence communities** consuming contaminated fish from affected waterways

### Get Your Water Tested

If you live near a coal ash disposal site:
1. **Contact your state health department** for certified testing labs near you
2. **Request testing for:** arsenic, lead, selenium, boron, sulfate, and other CCR-related contaminants
3. **Cost:** Typically $50-200 for a comprehensive heavy metals panel
4. **Document results** -- they can be used in legal actions and public comments
5. **If contamination is found:** Contact your state environmental agency AND your local Waterkeeper organization

### File a FOIA Request

If you want to know what a coal plant near you is reporting (or not reporting) about groundwater contamination:

1. **Federal FOIA:** Submit to EPA at [epa.gov/foia](https://www.epa.gov/foia) for CCR compliance data, inspection reports, and enforcement actions
2. **State FOIA:** Contact your state environmental agency for state-level monitoring data and permit records
3. **Request:** Groundwater monitoring reports, corrective action plans, and closure/post-closure plans for specific facilities

### Attend Utility Company Public Meetings

Utilities operating coal ash ponds are often required to hold public meetings. Find these through:
- Your state environmental agency's public notice page
- Your local utility's CCR compliance website (required under the CCR rule)
- Local newspaper legal notices
- Ask your state representative's office for upcoming hearings on utility regulation

## Sustained Actions

### Support Legal Advocacy Organizations

- **Earthjustice** ([earthjustice.org/feature/coal-ash-contaminated-sites-map](https://earthjustice.org/feature/coal-ash-contaminated-sites-map)) -- Leading litigation to enforce coal ash cleanup nationwide; their coal ash program has won landmark cases requiring closure of leaking ponds
- **Sierra Club Beyond Coal** ([sierraclub.org/coal](https://www.sierraclub.org/coal)) -- Campaign to retire coal plants and clean up coal ash sites
- **Appalachian Citizens' Law Center** ([appalachianlawcenter.org](https://www.appalachianlawcenter.org)) -- Free legal help for Appalachian communities affected by coal operations
- **Southern Environmental Law Center** ([southernenvironment.org](https://www.southernenvironment.org)) -- Litigation on coal ash in the Southeast
- **Southeast Coal Ash** ([southeastcoalash.org](http://www.southeastcoalash.org)) -- Coalition tracking coal ash contamination and cleanup across the Southeast

### Community Organizing

- **Form or join a local watchdog group** -- connect with neighbors near coal ash sites to share water testing results, attend utility meetings together, and coordinate pressure on local officials
- **Contact your state public utility commission** -- these agencies regulate electricity rates and can influence whether utilities pay for cleanup vs. passing costs to ratepayers
- **Engage county and city officials** -- local governments can pass resolutions demanding state and federal action, zone against new disposal sites, and require buffer zones around existing sites
- **Document health impacts** -- encourage neighbors to report health concerns to your state health department and ask for community health assessments
- **Connect with environmental justice networks** -- organizations like the [Environmental Justice Collaborative Problem-Solving program](https://www.epa.gov/environmentaljustice) can provide support and resources

### Community Health Screening

If you live within 3 miles of a coal ash disposal site, consider:
- **Well water testing** for arsenic, lead, selenium, boron, and sulfate (annually)
- **Blood testing** for lead levels, especially for children under 6
- **Cancer screening** discussions with your doctor, mentioning proximity to coal ash
- **Requesting a community health assessment** from your state health department -- document the request in writing
- **Tracking health patterns** in your neighborhood -- clusters of cancer, respiratory illness, or developmental issues may indicate environmental contamination

### Push for State-Level Protection

Many states can act even when federal enforcement fails:
- **Demand state adoption of federal CCR rules** if your state hasn't already
- **Advocate for state-level hazardous waste classification** of coal ash
- **Support state Superfund designation** for contaminated sites
- **Push for mandatory well water testing** within buffer zones around ash ponds
- **Advocate for utility rate cases** that require utilities (not ratepayers) to fund cleanup

### Consumer Actions

- **Support clean energy** -- every coal plant that retires stops generating new ash. Choose renewable energy options through your utility if available
- **Oppose new coal ash landfills** in your community -- attend zoning hearings and submit comments
- **Check construction materials** -- coal ash is used as a filler in concrete, road base, and structural fills. While "encapsulated" uses (in concrete) are generally considered safe, unencapsulated use in fills near waterways or homes can leach toxins
