# Colorado River Crisis

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The Colorado River supplies water to 40 million people across seven states, 30 tribal nations, and northern Mexico -- but more water is allocated on paper than the river actually carries. A 26-year megadrought made worse by climate change has pushed Lake Powell to **24% capacity** and Lake Mead to **33% capacity**. The Upper Colorado River Basin experienced the **lowest snowpack totals in recorded history** in 2026. A broad coalition is urging Congress to provide at least **$2 billion** in emergency drought funding. The operating rules that govern the entire system expire at the end of 2026, and the Bureau of Reclamation aims to finalize post-2026 rules by **October 1, 2026**. Agriculture uses roughly 80% of the river's water, while tribal nations -- who hold some of the oldest and largest water rights -- still lack legal allocations for much of their land.

If Lake Mead drops another 20 feet, Hoover Dam hydropower production will be reduced by 40%.

This is not a future crisis. It is happening now.

## Facts

### The Compact: A Century of Overallocation

The Colorado River Compact, signed in 1922, divided the river between the Upper Basin (Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, New Mexico) and the Lower Basin (Arizona, California, Nevada). The compact allocated 15 million acre-feet per year based on flow measurements taken during an abnormally wet period. Subsequent tree-ring studies have shown the river's long-term average flow is closer to 13.2-14.3 million acre-feet per year -- meaning the compact promised more water than the river has ever reliably provided.

Additional allocations to Mexico (1.5 million acre-feet under a 1944 treaty) and evaporation losses push total paper commitments well above actual supply.

[source: Congressional Research Service - Colorado River Management](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R45546) | [source: Eos - Fixing the Flawed Compact](https://eos.org/features/fixing-the-flawed-colorado-river-compact)

### [+] Lake Powell and Lake Mead: The System in Freefall

<div style={{textAlign: 'center'}}><img src="/ourhome/photos/lake-powell.jpg" alt="Lake Powell, Utah from Landsat satellite showing the dramatic bathtub ring of exposed rock. Photo: NASA (public domain)" style={{maxWidth: '50%'}} /></div>

| Reservoir | Capacity (June 2026) | Critical Elevation | Status |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Lake Powell | ~24% full | 3,490 ft (min power pool) | Approaching hydropower threshold |
| Lake Mead | ~33% full | 1,050 ft (Tier 2 shortage) | Level 1 Shortage declared |

The Colorado River system is at approximately **36% of total capacity** -- a figure the Bureau of Reclamation called "alarming." The Upper Colorado River Basin experienced the lowest snowpack totals in recorded history, peaking three weeks early. Federal forecasters expect only 2.3 million acre-feet of inflow into Lake Powell during the 2026 spring runoff season -- roughly one-third of normal. Lake Mead is projected to decline by an additional 20 feet, which would reduce Hoover Dam hydropower production by 40%. A broad coalition of basin cities, farmers, utilities, and conservationists is urging Congress to provide at least **$2 billion** in emergency federal drought funding. [source: The Nature Conservancy - Colorado River Funding](https://www.nature.org/en-us/newsroom/colorado-river-federal-drought-funding/) | [source: AZ Family - Reservoirs Near Threshold](https://www.azfamily.com/2026/04/23/colorado-river-crisis-deepens-reservoirs-near-key-threshold/)

"Dead pool" occurs at elevation 3,370 feet, where water can no longer flow through the dam under normal operations. At that point, the 40 million people downstream lose their water supply.

[source: Bureau of Reclamation 24-Month Study](https://www.usbr.gov/lc/region/g4000/24mo.pdf) | [source: Bureau of Reclamation Weekly Reservoir Data](https://www.usbr.gov/lc/region/g4000/weekly.pdf)

### [+] Shortage Declarations and Mandatory Cuts

Lake Mead is under a Level 1 Shortage Condition, requiring mandatory cutbacks:

- **Arizona:** 512,000 acre-feet reduction (~18% of annual allocation)
- **Nevada:** 21,000 acre-feet reduction (~7% of annual allocation)
- **Mexico:** 80,000 acre-feet reduction (~5% of annual allotment)

Upper Basin states (including Utah) face a different constraint: the compact requires them to deliver 7.5 million acre-feet per year to the Lower Basin. As flows decline, upper basin states may be forced to curtail their own use to meet this obligation -- even before their allocations are fully developed.

[source: Bureau of Reclamation - Colorado River Operations](https://www.usbr.gov/lc/region/g4000/24mo.pdf)

### [+] Post-2026: The Rules Expire

The 2007 Interim Guidelines, 2019 Drought Contingency Plans, and international agreements governing Colorado River operations all expire at the end of 2026. In January 2026, the Bureau of Reclamation published a Draft Environmental Impact Statement with four action alternatives for post-2026 operations:

- **No Action:** Continue current rules (unsustainable)
- **Enhanced Coordination:** Distribute storage between Lake Powell and Lake Mead
- **Maximum Operational Flexibility:** Incentivize proactive conservation, stabilize system storage
- **Supply Driven:** Operate Glen Canyon Dam based on historical natural flow

The seven basin states were given a February 14, 2026 deadline to reach agreement. They failed. Utah's Colorado River Commissioner Gene Shawcroft announced the states could not agree, saying: "The upper division states of Utah, Wyoming, Colorado and New Mexico are pivoting our immediate focus to deal with the real enemy on this river -- hydrology." If states cannot agree, the federal government will impose its own plan. Reclamation aims to finalize post-2026 rules by October 1, 2026.

[source: Bureau of Reclamation - Post-2026 Operations](https://www.usbr.gov/ColoradoRiverBasin/post2026/index.html) | [source: Bureau of Reclamation - Draft EIS](https://www.usbr.gov/ColoradoRiverBasin/post2026/draft-eis/index.html)

### [+] Agriculture: 80% of the Water

Approximately 80% of Colorado River water goes to irrigated agriculture, while cities use roughly 18%. Of the agricultural water, 62% irrigates cattle feed -- alfalfa and grass hay. Alfalfa alone consumes an estimated 32% of the entire river's flow.

Despite their smaller share, cities in the basin have reduced per-capita water use by 18% since 2000 even as population grew 24%. Agricultural efficiency improvements have been far slower.

[source: Colorado River Science - Agricultural Water Use](https://coloradoriverscience.org/Agricultural_water_use) | [source: NASA Landsat - Meat of the Matter](https://landsat.gsfc.nasa.gov/article/meat-of-the-matter-colorado-river-over-consumed/)

### [+] Utah's Position

Utah's compact allocation is 23% of the Upper Basin's share -- approximately 1.71 million acre-feet per year. Utah currently uses far less than its full allocation and has historically argued for the right to develop that remaining water. However, some of Utah's planned projects -- including the controversial Lake Powell Pipeline -- face questions about whether the water they depend on will actually be available as the river shrinks.

In the 2026 legislative session, Utah lawmakers have been reshaping water and land policy, with multiple bills addressing water conservation, development, and the state's negotiating position on the Colorado River.

[source: Utah Division of Water Resources - Colorado River](https://water.utah.gov/interstate-streams/colorado-river/) | [source: Deseret News - Utah Water Policy 2026](https://www.deseret.com/environment/2026/02/27/environmental-bills-2026-utah-legislative-session/)

### [+] Tribal Water Rights: The Largest Unresolved Claims

Thirty federally recognized tribal nations have lands in the Colorado River Basin. Under the Winters Doctrine (1908), tribes hold water rights with priority dates as early as the creation of their reservations -- often predating state allocations. Yet nearly half of basin tribes still have unresolved water rights claims.

Key developments:
- **Navajo Nation** secured a water rights settlement in Utah (2022) and New Mexico (2009), but still lacks a legal water allocation in Arizona -- the tribe's largest land base. The Northeastern Arizona Indian Water Rights Settlement Act of 2025 is pending in Congress.
- **Southern Ute and Ute Mountain Ute Tribes** have quantified rights but want the same opportunities as non-native neighbors to access and develop Colorado River water.
- **Historic MOU:** In April 2024, the Upper Colorado River Commission and tribal leaders signed a memorandum of understanding making bi-monthly meetings with tribes mandatory for the first time in the commission's 76-year history.

Tribal water rights represent an estimated 2.6-3.2 million acre-feet per year -- roughly 20% of the river's total flow. These rights exist on paper but lack the infrastructure for full use.

[source: Native American Rights Fund - Tribal Interests in Colorado River](https://narf.org/tribal-interests-colorado-river/) | [source: Bureau of Reclamation - Tribal Water Study](https://www.usbr.gov/lc/region/programs/crbstudy/tribalwaterstudy.html)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The Colorado River is structurally overcommitted -- even in normal years, allocations exceed supply
- Climate change is reducing flows further, with average temperatures in the basin rising and snowpack declining
- The expiration of operating rules in 2026 creates both risk and opportunity for restructuring
- Agricultural water use is the largest lever for conservation, but politically the most difficult to address
- Tribal water rights, long ignored, must be central to any sustainable agreement
- Utah faces a dilemma: develop its remaining allocation or accept that the water may not physically exist

### Who's Affected

- **40 million people** in Arizona, California, Colorado, Nevada, New Mexico, Utah, and Wyoming depend on Colorado River water
- **30 tribal nations** whose water rights predate all state allocations
- **5.5 million acres of farmland** irrigated by the river, producing winter vegetables, alfalfa, cotton, and livestock feed
- **Hydropower users:** Glen Canyon Dam and Hoover Dam together generate enough electricity for ~4.5 million homes
- **Ecosystems:** The Colorado River Delta in Mexico, once a vast wetland, has been almost entirely dry since the 1960s
- **Utah residents:** Municipal water, agricultural irrigation, and recreation (Lake Powell tourism) all depend on the river

### Conserve Water at Home (Colorado Basin)

If you live in the Colorado River Basin (Utah, Colorado, Arizona, Nevada, New Mexico, Wyoming, or Southern California), every gallon matters:

1. **Landscape:** Replace grass with xeriscaping -- outdoor irrigation is 50-70% of residential use in the West
2. **Fix leaks:** A dripping faucet wastes 3,000+ gallons per year
3. **Shorter showers:** Cutting 2 minutes saves ~1,500 gallons/year
4. **Full loads only:** Run dishwashers and washing machines only when full
5. **Check your utility:** Many Western utilities offer free water audits, rebates for efficient fixtures, and turf replacement programs
6. **Utah-specific:** The [Utah Division of Water Resources](https://water.utah.gov/) offers conservation resources and rebates

### Reduce Water-Intensive Food

Since 80% of Colorado River water goes to agriculture and 32% goes to cattle feed alone, your food choices directly affect the river:

- **Reduce beef consumption** -- beef requires ~1,800 gallons of water per pound, largely through irrigated feed crops
- **Choose less water-intensive proteins** -- chicken (468 gal/lb), lentils (704 gal/lb), and tofu require far less water
- **Buy local and seasonal** -- reduces demand for irrigated desert agriculture
- **Support water-efficient farms** -- look for producers using drip irrigation, deficit irrigation, or dry farming techniques

### Support Water Banking and Fallowing Programs

Several programs pay farmers to temporarily fallow fields or switch to less water-intensive crops, stretching existing supplies:

- **System Conservation Pilot Program** -- Bureau of Reclamation pays irrigators to conserve water that stays in the river
- **Demand Management** -- Upper Basin states exploring voluntary, compensated, temporary water reductions
- **Support legislation** that funds these programs -- they are the fastest way to reduce agricultural consumption without destroying farm communities

### Citizen Water Monitoring

- Join your local **Waterkeeper** organization ([waterkeeper.org](https://www.waterkeeper.org)) to monitor river health
- Report water waste and illegal diversions to your state engineer's office
- Participate in streamflow monitoring programs through the USGS or local watershed groups
- Document changes in river conditions, riparian habitat, and water quality

### Get Involved in Land Use Planning

- Attend city and county planning meetings where water-intensive developments are proposed
- Advocate for water-smart growth policies: water budgets for new development, mandatory efficiency standards, reclaimed water requirements
- Push back on developments that assume unlimited water supply in arid regions
