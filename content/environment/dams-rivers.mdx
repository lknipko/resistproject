# Dams & River Ecosystems

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** America's 91,000 dams have blocked fish migration, trapped sediment, warmed rivers, and devastated aquatic ecosystems for decades. But a growing dam removal movement is proving that rivers can recover remarkably fast. The Klamath River -- site of the largest dam removal in history -- saw 7,700 salmon return within months. The Elwha River rebounded with 2-4x more fish within five years. In 2024 and 2025 combined, over 200 dams were removed, reconnecting more than 7,400 miles of rivers. Meanwhile, 73% of America's dams will be over 50 years old by 2025, with at least 4,000 in poor condition posing safety risks. The science is clear: removing obsolete dams is one of the most effective and fastest-acting conservation tools we have.

## Facts

### How Dams Damage Rivers

Dams fundamentally alter every aspect of a river ecosystem:

- **Fish passage blocked:** Over 40% of spawning and rearing habitat once available to Columbia Basin salmon has been permanently blocked by dams. Fish that evolved to migrate hundreds of miles to spawn are cut off from their natal streams.
- **Sediment trapped:** Sand, gravel, and rocks essential for spawning habitat get trapped in reservoirs, while downstream channels become scoured and simplified -- straight and ditch-like with less habitat for fish.
- **Water temperature altered:** Reservoirs heat water through solar radiation (insolation), raising temperatures to levels lethal to cold-water species like salmon and trout. Warm reservoir outflows can raise downstream temperatures for miles.
- **Natural flow regime destroyed:** Rivers depend on seasonal flood pulses to maintain habitat, trigger spawning, and redistribute sediment. Dams flatten this cycle into artificial, steady releases.
- **Nutrient cycling disrupted:** Salmon carry ocean-derived nutrients upstream when they spawn and die. Blocked migration starves headwater ecosystems of nitrogen and phosphorus that feed everything from insects to bears to streamside forests.

[source: NOAA - How Dams Affect Water and Habitat](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/how-dams-affect-water-and-habitat-west-coast) | [source: American Rivers - How Dams Damage Rivers](https://www.americanrivers.org/threats-solutions/restoring-damaged-rivers/how-dams-damage-rivers/)

### The Klamath River: Largest Dam Removal in History

In October 2024, the largest dam removal project ever completed took down four dams on the Klamath River in southern Oregon and northern California. The results exceeded even the most optimistic scientific projections:

- **7,700 Chinook salmon** passed the former Iron Gate Dam site between October and December 2024, averaging 588 fish per day
- Chinook were recorded ascending into **Upper Klamath Lake** and the **Williamson and Sprague Rivers** -- tributaries that had not seen salmon reproduction in over 100 years
- **Harmful algal blooms** that had plagued the reservoir-choked river **declined precipitously** after dam removal
- Water quality improved dramatically, with microcystin toxin levels dropping
- The speed and scale of recovery exceeded expectations and the most optimistic modeling

The project was driven by a coalition of the Yurok, Karuk, and Klamath tribes, conservation groups, states, and federal agencies. For these tribes, salmon are not just a food source -- they are central to cultural and spiritual identity.

[source: NOAA Fisheries - Klamath Dam Removal](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/feature-story/final-step-klamath-river-dam-removal-opens-path-returning-salmon) | [source: CalTrout - One Year After Klamath Dams](https://caltrout.org/news/1-year-anniversary-klamath-dams/)

### [+] The Elwha River: A Decade of Recovery

  The Elwha River in Washington's Olympic Peninsula had two large dams removed between 2011 and 2014 -- at the time, the largest dam removal in US history. A decade of monitoring shows remarkable ecosystem recovery:

  - Fish populations increased 2-4x within five years, including bull trout, Chinook salmon, and steelhead
  - Adult Chinook returns reached 7,600 in 2019 -- the highest since the late 1980s
  - Summer steelhead, a life history strategy thought to be permanently lost, re-emerged
  - An estimated two-thirds of the sediment trapped behind the dams has moved downstream, with 90% reaching coastal habitats
  - The river delta expanded dramatically as sediment rebuilt the coastline
  - New habitat was created as released sediment and wood reshaped the riverbed

  Full ecosystem recovery will take decades, but the trajectory is overwhelmingly positive.

  [source: NOAA - Elwha River Restoration Case Study](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/feature-story/elwha-river-restoration-case-study-adaptive-management-salmon-recovery) | [source: NPS - Elwha Ecosystem Restoration](https://www.nps.gov/olym/learn/nature/elwha-ecosystem-restoration.htm)

### [+] Lower Snake River Dams: The Fight for Salmon and Orca

  The four Lower Snake River dams in Washington remain the most contested dam removal debate in America. The stakes:

  - The Snake River historically produced one-third to one-half of the Columbia Basin's salmon -- millions of Chinook, steelhead, coho, and sockeye
  - NOAA concluded in 2022 that removing these four dams is "essential" to avoiding extinction of Snake River salmon
  - Breaching would restore approximately one million missing Chinook salmon to the waters where Southern Resident orcas feed -- a population that has dwindled to just 72 individuals
  - In December 2023, the Biden administration announced support for preparing to breach the dams through an agreement with four tribal nations, two states, and conservation groups
  - In June 2025, President Trump signed a Presidential Memorandum withdrawing from the Columbia Basin Restoration Initiative and revoking Biden-era support for native fish restoration
  - Columbia River salmon restoration funding was cut $1.5 billion through Army Corps of Engineers budget reductions

  [source: NOAA - Snake River Salmon Essential](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/west-coast/endangered-species-conservation/dams-west-coast) | [source: Earthjustice - Snake River Dam Timeline](https://earthjustice.org/feature/salmon-timeline-snake-river-litigation)

### [+] Colorado River: Glen Canyon Dam and Lake Powell

  Glen Canyon Dam, which created Lake Powell on the Colorado River, exemplifies the tradeoffs of large dams on Western rivers:

  - Declining water levels have exposed structural concerns and changed water release temperatures
  - Warmer water released from the shrinking reservoir has enabled invasive fish (smallmouth bass) to establish below the dam, threatening native species in the Grand Canyon
  - In 2024, the Bureau of Reclamation initiated experimental cold-water flows to disrupt invasive fish spawning
  - A new management approach under negotiation would base water releases on actual river flows rather than reservoir levels -- a break from decades of practice
  - The dam traps an estimated 30,000+ acre-feet of sediment annually that once rebuilt Grand Canyon beaches and maintained native fish habitat

  [source: Bureau of Reclamation - Glen Canyon Dam](https://www.usbr.gov/uc/water/crsp/cs/gcd.html) | [source: CRS - Colorado River Management](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R45546)

### America's Aging Dam Infrastructure

The scale of the aging dam problem is staggering:

| Statistic | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Total dams in the US (National Inventory) | ~91,000 |
| Average age of NID dams | 61 years |
| Dams over 50 years old by 2025 | 73% |
| Dams in poor condition (safety risk) | 4,000+ |
| Estimated cost to rehabilitate non-federal dams | $157.5 billion |
| Dams removed in 2024 | 108 (tied record) |
| Dams removed in 2025 | 100 |
| River miles reconnected in 2025 | 4,893 (record) |

Over 43% of dam removals in 2024 were motivated by safety concerns, liability, or economic considerations -- not environmental advocacy. Many of these dams no longer serve their original purpose (mill power, small-scale irrigation) but continue to block fish passage and degrade ecosystems.

[source: American Rivers - 2024 Dam Removals](https://www.americanrivers.org/2025/03/big-year-for-dam-removals-in-2024/) | [source: American Rivers - 2025 Dam Removals](https://www.americanrivers.org/2026/03/100-dams-down-2025-a-big-year-for-reconnecting-rivers-in-the-u-s/) | [source: The Regulatory Review - Removing America's Aging Dams](https://www.theregreview.org/2024/09/21/removing-americas-aging-dams/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The Klamath and Elwha removals prove that dam removal produces rapid, measurable ecosystem recovery -- often faster than predicted
- Many of America's 91,000 dams are obsolete structures that no longer serve their original purpose but continue to cause ecological harm
- The estimated $157.5 billion rehabilitation cost for aging dams means many will eventually fail or need removal regardless -- proactive removal is safer and cheaper
- Withdrawal from the Columbia Basin Restoration Initiative and cuts to Army Corps funding threaten the most significant salmon recovery opportunity in decades
- Dam removal is not anti-energy -- it is about retiring structures whose costs now far exceed their benefits while investing in modern alternatives
- Indigenous communities bear disproportionate cultural and food sovereignty losses from dammed rivers

### Who's Affected

- **Tribal nations:** Salmon and steelhead are central to the culture, diet, and treaty rights of dozens of Pacific Northwest tribes. Dams have violated treaty-guaranteed fishing rights for over a century.
- **Fishing communities:** Commercial and recreational fishing economies depend on healthy salmon and steelhead runs that dams have decimated
- **Southern Resident orcas:** Only 72 remain. Their primary food source -- Chinook salmon -- has been devastated by Snake River dams
- **Downstream communities:** Aging dams in poor condition pose flood and failure risks to communities below them
- **Taxpayers:** Maintaining obsolete dams costs billions; removal often costs less than long-term maintenance
- **Rural communities:** Many small dams block local fish populations that support subsistence fishing and recreation

### Submit Public Comments on Dam Projects

The Army Corps of Engineers and Bureau of Reclamation regularly open public comment periods on dam operations, relicensing, and removal proposals. Search [regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov) for relevant dockets:

| Search Term | What You'll Find |
|------------|-----------------|
| "dam removal" + "environmental impact" | Dam removal EIS comment periods |
| "FERC" + "relicensing" | Federal Energy Regulatory Commission dam relicense reviews |
| "fish passage" | Fish passage improvement project proposals |

**Tip:** When dams come up for FERC relicensing (every 30-50 years), public comments can influence whether fish passage is required or whether the dam is removed instead. Check [ferc.gov](https://www.ferc.gov/) for relicensing proceedings in your state.

## Sustained Actions

### Join Your Local Watershed Organization

Watershed councils and river keeper organizations are the frontline advocates for river health in your community:

- **Waterkeeper Alliance** ([waterkeeper.org](https://www.waterkeeper.org)) -- Find your local waterkeeper among 300+ organizations worldwide. They monitor water quality, advocate for river protection, and organize cleanups.
- **Your state's watershed council** -- Search "[your state] watershed council" to find local groups. Many participate directly in dam removal planning and stream restoration projects.
- **River Network** ([rivernetwork.org](https://www.rivernetwork.org)) -- Connects river advocates and provides training for community-based water stewardship

### Volunteer for River Restoration

- **Trout Unlimited** ([tu.org](https://www.tu.org)) -- 300+ local chapters with over 650,000 volunteer hours annually on stream restoration, tree plantings, and habitat work. Visit [tu.org/volunteer](https://www.tu.org/volunteer/) to find your chapter.
- **American Rivers** ([americanrivers.org](https://www.americanrivers.org)) -- National advocacy and local dam removal campaigns. Their goal: 400 dams removed by 2027 and 30,000 by 2050.
- **The Nature Conservancy** ([nature.org](https://www.nature.org/en-us/what-we-do/our-priorities/tackle-climate-change/climate-change-stories/removing-barriers-river-health/)) -- Funds and coordinates dam removal and river reconnection projects nationwide
- **Local conservation corps** -- Many states have conservation corps programs where you can participate in hands-on stream restoration work

### Support Tribal-Led Conservation

Indigenous communities have been leading the fight for river restoration for decades. The Klamath dam removal was driven by tribal advocacy:

- **Support tribal fish and wildlife programs** -- Many tribes run salmon hatcheries, habitat restoration, and monitoring programs that accept donations and volunteers
- **Amplify tribal voices** -- Share tribal perspectives on river restoration in your advocacy
- **Learn the history** -- Understanding how dams violated treaty fishing rights provides essential context for why dam removal is a justice issue, not just an environmental one

### Adopt a Stream or River

Many states have "adopt-a-stream" programs where individuals or groups commit to monitoring and maintaining a section of waterway:

- Regular water quality monitoring (temperature, dissolved oxygen, turbidity)
- Trash and debris removal
- Invasive species identification and removal
- Reporting pollution and erosion issues to state agencies
- Planting riparian buffers (streamside trees and vegetation)

### Consumer and Investment Choices

- **Support businesses that fund river restoration** -- 1% for the Planet members include companies donating to river conservation
- **Choose sustainably sourced hydropower** -- Not all hydropower is equal; support utilities investing in fish-friendly turbines and fish passage
- **Divest from companies blocking dam removal** -- Some utilities actively lobby against removing obsolete dams to avoid decommissioning costs
