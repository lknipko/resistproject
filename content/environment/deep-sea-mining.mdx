# Deep-Sea Mining: Destroying the Ocean Floor for Minerals

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Mining companies are seeking permits to scrape **21 billion tons** of polymetallic nodules off the deep ocean floor, targeting the Clarion-Clipperton Zone — a region where **88–92% of known species are new to science**. The Trump administration signed an executive order in April 2025 to fast-track permits, bypassing international governance. As of early 2026, **40 countries** have announced opposition to deep-sea mining, and the International Seabed Authority has called U.S. unilateral action "prohibited under international law."

The race to mine the deep ocean is accelerating faster than science can assess the damage. Sediment plumes from mining can travel over 4 kilometers from the extraction site, and the nodules themselves take **1–10 million years to regrow** after removal.

## Facts

### What Is Deep-Sea Mining?

Deep-sea mining targets three types of mineral deposits on the ocean floor:

- **Polymetallic nodules** — Potato-sized rocks containing manganese, nickel, cobalt, and copper that form over millions of years on abyssal plains. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone (CCZ) between Hawaii and Mexico holds an estimated **21 billion tons** of nodules, including 5.95 billion tons of manganese, 270 million tons of nickel, 230 million tons of copper, and 50 million tons of cobalt. [source: ISA Clarion-Clipperton Zone Data](https://www.isa.org.jm/exploration-areas/clarion-clipperton-zone/)
- **Seafloor massive sulfide deposits** — Mineral-rich formations around hydrothermal vents, home to unique chemosynthetic ecosystems found nowhere else on Earth.
- **Cobalt-rich crusts** — Thin layers of minerals on seamount surfaces, forming over millions of years in some of the ocean's most biologically productive zones.

Mining companies covet these deposits primarily as a source of cobalt, nickel, and manganese for electric vehicle batteries — framing deep-sea extraction as a "green" solution to mineral supply chain concerns.

[source: World Resources Institute - Deep-Sea Mining Explained](https://www.wri.org/insights/deep-sea-mining-explained)

### Biodiversity of the Deep Sea

The deep ocean is among the least explored and most biodiverse environments on Earth. Scientific surveys of the Clarion-Clipperton Zone have found that **88–92% of species collected are new to science**, meaning most life there has never been described or studied. [source: Deep Sea Conservation Coalition](https://deep-sea-conservation.org/solutions/no-deep-sea-mining/)

The nodules themselves are not just rocks — they are habitat. Sponges, corals, worms, sea cucumbers, and countless invertebrate species live on or among them. Removing the nodules eliminates the physical substrate these organisms depend on, with **no prospect of recovery on any human timescale** given the nodules' growth rate of 1–10 millimeters per million years.

[source: IUCN - Deep-Sea Mining Impacts](https://www.iucn.nl/en/story/the-impact-of-deep-sea-mining-on-biodiversity-climate-and-human-cultures/)

### The Sediment Plume Problem

Mining operations create two types of sediment plumes that can spread far beyond the extraction site:

- **Collector plumes** generated at the seafloor during nodule collection
- **Discharge plumes** from wastewater returned to the water column

Research has documented that sediment clouds travel **over 4 kilometers** from the mining site, with particle concentrations rising up to **100 times higher** than background levels. These plumes bury filter feeders, clog fish gills, block light penetration, and disrupt food webs across wide areas. Noise and light pollution from mining vessels and equipment add further stress on marine mammals and sensitive species.

[source: PMC - Deep-Sea Mining Impact Research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11009680/)

### [+] International Governance: The ISA's Failures

The International Seabed Authority (ISA) — a UN body created by the Law of the Sea Treaty — is supposed to govern mining of "the common heritage of mankind" in international waters. Its record raises serious concerns:

- The ISA has **granted 31 exploration contracts** covering over 1.5 million square kilometers of ocean floor, most in the CCZ.
- The ISA failed to finalize commercial mining regulations at its July 2025 session after failing to reach consensus. Negotiations continue in 2026.
- Critics argue the ISA is structurally compromised: its budget depends on fees paid by mining contractors, creating a conflict of interest.
- The ISA Secretariat condemned the U.S. unilateral permitting push in 2025, stating that exploitation of resources in the international seabed area by individual nations is "prohibited under international law."

[source: Congress.gov - Seabed Mining CRS Report](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47324)

### [+] The Trump Administration's Push to Mine Unilaterally

On April 24, 2025, President Trump signed **Executive Order 14285**, directing NOAA to expedite permits for deep-sea mining under the Deep Seabed Hard Mineral Resources Act (DSHMRA) — a 1980 U.S. law that predates modern international seabed governance.

The push is explicitly framed as countering China's influence over critical mineral supply chains. Key events:

- **July 2025:** The Metals Company (TMC) submitted applications to NOAA for both exploration licenses and a commercial recovery permit — the first company to apply for actual extraction, not just exploration.
- **January 21, 2026:** NOAA issued a final rule streamlining the permitting process, allowing simultaneous review of exploration and commercial recovery applications.
- **December 2025:** NOAA published a Federal Register notice opening a public comment period on TMC's applications.

The U.S. is not a party to the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), which established the ISA. The Trump administration argues this gives the U.S. authority to mine international waters under its own law — a position rejected by most of the international community.

[source: Federal Register - TMC Applications](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/23/2025-23795/deep-seabed-mining-notice-of-receipt-of-applications-for-deep-seabed-mining-exploration-licenses-and) | [source: Mongabay - TMC Application](https://news.mongabay.com/2025/04/the-metals-company-applied-to-the-u-s-for-a-deep-sea-mining-license/)

### [+] Norway's 2024 Seabed Mining Approval

On January 9, 2024, Norway's parliament voted 80–20 to open its continental shelf — including Arctic waters — to exploratory deep-sea mining. Norway became one of the first countries in the world to formally authorize seabed mineral exploration.

The decision drew sharp criticism from environmental groups:

- **WWF Norway** filed a lawsuit against the Norwegian government, arguing it failed to adequately assess environmental consequences.
- **Greenpeace** released a report warning that Arctic biodiversity faces unprecedented threats from mining-induced sediment disturbance in cold, slow-recovery environments.
- The European Parliament issued a briefing raising legal and environmental concerns about Norway's decision.

Norway's move is seen as a bellwether: if one of the world's most environmentally progressive nations proceeds with seabed mining, it may legitimize the broader industry.

[source: Norway Green-Lights Seabed Mining - C&EN](https://cen.acs.org/environment/water/Norway-green-lights-exploratory-seabed/102/web/2024/01) | [source: Greenpeace - Arctic Seabed Mining](https://www.greenpeace.org/international/press-release/69799/arctic-biodiversity-under-threat-from-deep-sea-mining-plans-greenpeace-report-warns/)

### [+] Nations Calling for a Moratorium

As of December 2025, **40 countries** have announced opposition to deep-sea mining or called for a moratorium or precautionary pause, including:

- France, Germany, New Zealand, Chile, Costa Rica, Palau, Fiji
- The European Parliament passed a resolution calling for a global moratorium
- Multiple Pacific Island nations whose fisheries and coastal cultures depend on healthy oceans

[source: Deep Sea Conservation Coalition - Moratorium](https://deep-sea-conservation.org/solutions/no-deep-sea-mining/)

### [+] Alternatives to Deep-Sea Mining

The industry argues deep-sea mining is necessary to supply minerals for the clean energy transition. The evidence for this is contested:

- **LFP batteries:** Lithium iron phosphate batteries — which require no cobalt or nickel — now supply **nearly half the global EV market**, up from less than 10% in 2020. [source: IEA Critical Minerals Outlook 2025](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-critical-minerals-outlook-2025/beyond-nmc-batteries-supply-chain-issues-for-emerging-battery-technologies)
- **Battery recycling:** According to the IEA, metal recovery from recycling could reduce new mining demand by **40% for cobalt and copper** and 25% for lithium and nickel by 2050. [source: EESI - Circular Strategies for Critical Minerals](https://www.eesi.org/articles/view/from-scrap-to-supply-circular-strategies-for-critical-minerals)
- **Terrestrial mining reform:** Improving environmental and labor standards at existing mines is less destructive than opening new frontier extraction zones.

Fewer than 15% of U.S. lithium-ion batteries are currently recycled — a gap representing a significant opportunity to reduce demand for new mining of any kind.

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The U.S. government is attempting to authorize commercial deep-sea mining **unilaterally**, bypassing the international governance body created specifically to manage these shared resources.
- If permitted, mining could destroy ecosystems that took **millions of years to form** — and that science has barely begun to document.
- The "critical minerals for clean energy" justification is weakening as LFP battery chemistry reduces cobalt and nickel demand, and recycling infrastructure scales up.
- The ISA's inability to finalize regulations creates a governance vacuum that mining companies are racing to exploit.
- Once sediment plumes are released and nodules removed, there is **no practical remediation** possible on any human timescale.

### Who's Affected

- **Deep-sea ecosystems:** Unknown species, unique chemosynthetic vent communities, and nodule-dependent fauna face extinction before they are even described.
- **Fishing communities:** Sediment plumes and food web disruption could affect commercially important fish populations across the Pacific.
- **Pacific Island nations:** Whose food security, cultural heritage, and sovereignty are tied to the ocean.
- **Future generations:** The deep ocean is a global commons — its destruction is irreversible.
- **International law:** U.S. unilateral action undermines the Law of the Sea framework and sets a precedent for other nations to ignore international governance.

### Submit Public Comments on NOAA's TMC Applications

NOAA opened a public comment period on The Metals Company's mining applications in December 2025. Submit comments opposing the permits:

- Visit [regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov) and search for docket **NOAA-NMFS-2025-0091** (or search "deep seabed mining")
- Comments should address: environmental impact assessment inadequacy, international law concerns, alternatives to deep-sea mining, and the need for scientific review before any permit is issued

### Volunteer With Ocean Monitoring Programs (Citizen Science)

Document what's at stake by participating in ocean health monitoring:

- **iNaturalist — Marine Life Projects** ([inaturalist.org](https://www.inaturalist.org)) — Record and identify marine species; your observations contribute to the baseline scientific record of what exists in ocean ecosystems
- **REEF Fish Survey Project** ([reef.org](https://www.reef.org)) — Volunteer underwater survey program for divers and snorkelers; population data supports marine protection arguments
- **CoralWatch** ([coralwatch.org](https://coralwatch.org)) — Monitor coral health from any coastal location; data goes directly to researchers tracking ocean conditions
