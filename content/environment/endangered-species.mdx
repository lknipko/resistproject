# Endangered Species & ESA Rollbacks

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The Endangered Species Act -- the law that saved the bald eagle, peregrine falcon, and humpback whale -- is under systematic attack. Four proposed rules would gut protections for 1,682 listed U.S. species. The blanket rule protecting threatened species has been eliminated. Critical habitat designations now require economic analysis. Fish and Wildlife Service funding has been slashed 32%, and over 400 staff have been laid off. The ESA has prevented the extinction of 99% of species under its protection -- that track record is now at risk.

Meanwhile, Congress is moving to delist gray wolves and grizzly bears by legislation rather than science, and 50 million acres of sage-grouse habitat have been opened to oil and gas development. A federal court struck down key rollbacks in March 2026, but the administration is pressing forward with new rules.

## Facts

### Key Points

- **1,682 U.S. species** are currently listed as endangered or threatened under the ESA, including 744 animals and 938 plants [source: FWS Listed Species Boxscore](https://ecos.fws.gov/ecp/report/boxscore)
- **99% of listed species** have been saved from extinction by the ESA [source: DOI - 50 Years of ESA Success](https://www.doi.gov/blog/endangered-species-act-celebrating-50-years-success-wildlife-conservation)
- **Four proposed rules** announced November 19, 2025 would reverse Biden-era ESA protections [source: FWS ESA Regulation Revisions](https://www.fws.gov/project/endangered-species-act-regulation-revisions)
- **32% budget cut** proposed for Fish and Wildlife Service (from $1.68B to $1.14B) [source: Defenders of Wildlife](https://defenders.org/newsroom/defenders-condemns-administrations-dangerous-proposed-budget-cuts-wildlife-conservation)
- **400+ FWS staff laid off** through DOGE-driven workforce reductions [source: NPR - Interior Budget Cuts](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/20/nx-s1-5333655/interior-department-budget-cuts-doge)
- **50 million acres** of sage-grouse habitat opened to oil and gas leasing [source: Earthjustice - Sage-Grouse Lawsuit](https://earthjustice.org/press/2026/lawsuit-filed-over-trump-administration-abandonment-of-sage-grouse-protections)

### [+] ESA Regulatory Changes

On November 19, 2025, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service proposed four rules that would fundamentally weaken ESA protections. These proposals reverse Biden-era regulations and return to the framework from the first Trump administration.

**1. Elimination of the Blanket 4(d) Rule**

The "blanket rule" automatically extended take prohibitions (making it illegal to harm, harass, or kill) to all newly listed threatened species. The proposed rule eliminates this automatic protection and instead requires species-specific rules for each threatened species -- a process that can take years, leaving newly listed species unprotected in the interim.

[source: Endangered Species Law & Policy - 4(d) Rule](https://www.endangeredspecieslawandpolicy.com/esa-regulations-blanket-4-d-rule)

**2. Economic Analysis in Critical Habitat Designations**

The proposed rules require economic impact analysis when designating critical habitat. Under the new framework, FWS must prepare a draft economic analysis and allow public comment, creating a process to exclude areas from critical habitat based on economic, national security, or other impacts.

[source: Holland & Knight - ESA Regulatory Amendments](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/11/reversing-course-for-endangered-species-act)

**3. Narrowed Definition of "Harm"**

In April 2025, the administration proposed rescinding the current definition of "harm" to species, which includes habitat destruction. Since habitat loss is the primary driver of extinction for most species, narrowing this definition would remove the key legal mechanism for protecting the places species need to survive.

[source: Georgetown Environmental Law Review](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/environmental-law-review/blog/habitat-at-risk-the-trump-administrations-rollback-of-wildlife-habitat-safeguards/)

**4. Weakened Interagency Consultation**

The proposed rules revise the interagency consultation framework (Section 7), which requires federal agencies to consult with FWS before taking actions that could affect listed species. The changes increase agency discretion and reduce the scope of what triggers consultation requirements.

[source: Harvard EELP - ESA Regulations Tracker](https://eelp.law.harvard.edu/tracker/endangered-species-act-regulations/)

### [+] Species at Risk

**Gray Wolf**

The House passed H.R. 845, the "Pet and Livestock Protection Act," which would delist the gray wolf nationwide by legislation rather than through scientific review. FWS has also announced it will not develop a national recovery plan for the gray wolf, despite the species remaining endangered across much of the contiguous United States.

- House vote: December 2025
- Senate action: Pending
- FWS Director Brian Nesvik has publicly advocated for delisting

[source: Daily Montanan - House Wolf Delisting Vote](https://dailymontanan.com/2025/12/18/house-votes-to-delist-gray-wolves-from-endangered-species-list/) | [source: NACo - FWS Wolf Recovery Plan](https://www.naco.org/news/us-fish-and-wildlife-service-announces-it-will-not-develop-recovery-plan-gray-wolf)

**Grizzly Bear**

The Grizzly Bear State Management Act of 2025 (H.R. 281) would direct Interior to remove the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem grizzly population from ESA protection. The federal delisting rule has been delayed until December 2026, with the agency citing staffing shortages and a large volume of public comments.

- Greater Yellowstone population: ~1,000 bears
- House Natural Resources Committee approved H.R. 281 in July 2025
- FWS Director Nesvik has advocated for delisting

[source: Daily Montanan - Grizzly Delisting](https://dailymontanan.com/2025/07/17/house-committee-signs-off-on-delisting-grizzly-bear/) | [source: Save the Yellowstone Grizzly](https://savetheyellowstonegrizzly.org/grizzly-final-rule-delayed-until-december-2026/)

**Greater Sage-Grouse**

BLM drastically amended resource management plans in December 2025, opening approximately 50 million acres of sagebrush habitat in 10 western states to oil and gas development. The revised plans reduced development buffers around sage-grouse breeding sites (leks) from the scientifically recommended 3-5 miles to 0.6 miles or less. The species has gone extinct in North Dakota and populations have declined 13-24% since 2013.

[source: Earthjustice - Sage-Grouse Protections](https://earthjustice.org/press/2026/lawsuit-filed-over-trump-administration-abandonment-of-sage-grouse-protections) | [source: Western Watersheds Project](https://www.westernwatersheds.org/2026/03/lawsuit-challenges-trumps-gutting-of-greater-sage-grouse-protections-in-nine-western-states/)

### [+] Utah Species

Utah is home to several species that depend on ESA protections and are directly affected by current rollbacks.

**Utah Prairie Dog** (Threatened)

Listed as threatened under the ESA, the Utah prairie dog is found only in southwestern Utah. Populations have rebounded through decades of cooperative conservation between state, federal, and local partners, but continued ESA protections are essential for maintaining these gains. Utah's Species Protection Account allocated approximately 48% of its $4 million fund toward ESA-listed species recovery, including the prairie dog.

[source: Utah DWR - Prairie Dog Recovery](https://wildlife.utah.gov/news/utah-wildlife-news/1893-how-the-dwr-and-partners-are-helping-utah-prairie-dogs-recover-this-endangered-species-day.html) | [source: Utah DWR - Species Protection Account](https://wildlife.utah.gov/news/utah-wildlife-news/2110-species-protection-account-allocates-four-million-dollars-to-fund-30-projects-in-2025-that-help-wildlife-recover.html)

**June Sucker** (Threatened)

Endemic to Utah Lake, the June sucker was listed as endangered and has been reclassified to threatened as recovery efforts progressed. A hatchery program raises thousands of June suckers each year for reintroduction. Refuge populations are maintained as insurance. Cuts to FWS funding and ESA regulatory weakening could undermine this long-running recovery program.

[source: Utah DWR - ESA Anniversary & Fish Recovery](https://wildlife.utah.gov/news/utah-wildlife-news/1716-fiftieth-anniversary-of-the-endangered-species-act-and-how-the-dwr-and-partnering-agencies-are-helping-listed-fish-species-recover.html)

**Bonytail Chub** (Endangered)

One of the rarest fish in North America, the bonytail (Gila elegans) is native to the Colorado River Basin and can live up to 50 years. No reproducing wild populations are known to exist. Each year, 11,000 PIT-tagged bonytail are stocked from the Wahweap warmwater hatchery. In 2022, biologists found two larval bonytail in Lake Powell -- the first evidence of natural reproduction in years.

[source: Colorado River Recovery Programs](https://coloradoriverrecovery.org/information/fish-species/bonytail/) | [source: Utah DWR - Bonytail Tracking](https://wildlife.utah.gov/news/wildlife-blog/1383-tracking-the-recovery-of-a-rare-utah-fish.html)

**Greater Sage-Grouse in Utah**

While not ESA-listed, the greater sage-grouse was kept off the list in 2015 due to state conservation commitments. Utah manages approximately 4.3 million acres of sage-grouse habitat. BLM's December 2025 plan amendments opening habitat to energy development directly undermine the conservation framework that kept the species off the list.

[source: Utah DWR - Greater Sage-Grouse](https://wildlife.utah.gov/greater-sage-grouse.html) | [source: Defenders of Wildlife - Sage-Grouse](https://defenders.org/wildlife/sage-grouse)

### [+] ESA Success Stories at Risk

The Endangered Species Act has a remarkable track record. These recoveries demonstrate what the law can achieve -- and what is at risk if protections are weakened.

![A bald eagle in flight — recovered from 417 nesting pairs to over 316,000 birds thanks to the ESA. Photo: U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (public domain)](/environment/photos/bald-eagle.jpg)

**Bald Eagle**

- 1963: 417 nesting pairs in the lower 48 states
- 2007: Delisted from ESA
- 2020: 71,467 individual birds in the lower 48
- 2024: Estimated 316,000 bald eagles across every state except Hawaii

The recovery required habitat protection, pesticide bans (DDT), and decades of federal investment. Without the ESA framework that coordinated these efforts, recovery would not have been possible.

[source: Audubon - Bald Eagle Success Story](https://www.audubon.org/news/the-bald-eagle-and-endangered-species-act-american-success-story)

**Peregrine Falcon**

- 1975: 324 known nesting pairs in the U.S.
- 1999: Delisted from ESA
- 2020: Estimated 72,000 individuals in U.S. and Canada
- Recovery included captive breeding, habitat protection, and reintroduction of 6,000+ falcons since 1974

[source: FWS - The Peregrine Falcon Is Back](https://www.fws.gov/story/2000-08/peregrine-falcon-back-0)

**Humpback Whale**

Once hunted to near extinction, most humpback whale populations have recovered enough to be delisted. The ESA's international coordination framework was essential to ending commercial whaling and protecting critical habitat.

[source: Endangered Species Coalition - Success Stories](https://www.endangered.org/success-stories/)

**The Pattern:** Every success story required decades of sustained federal commitment, habitat protection, and the legal framework the ESA provides. The current rollbacks undermine each of these pillars.

### [+] Litigation Status

**March 2026: Federal Court Strikes Down Key Rollbacks**

The U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California vacated several ESA regulatory provisions, finding that rules issued in 2019 and revised in 2024 conflict with federal law. The court struck down four of six challenged provisions. The case was brought by the Center for Biological Diversity, Sierra Club, and WildEarth Guardians, represented by Earthjustice.

[source: Earthjustice - Court Restores ESA Protections](https://earthjustice.org/press/2026/federal-court-strikes-down-president-trumps-attacks-against-endangered-species-act-restores-bedrock-environmental-law-to-pre-trump-status) | [source: Court Order (PDF)](https://earthjustice.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/62_order-on-cross-msjs-and-motion-for-stay-on-remand-3.30.26.pdf)

**Sage-Grouse Habitat Lawsuit (March 2026)**

Montana Wildlife Federation, Defenders of Wildlife, and The Wilderness Society sued BLM over its abandonment of sage-grouse protections across 10 western states. The Ninth Circuit had previously upheld a decision striking down first-term Trump administration sage-grouse leasing.

[source: Defenders of Wildlife - Sage-Grouse Lawsuit](https://defenders.org/newsroom/lawsuit-filed-over-trump-administration-abandonment-of-sage-grouse-protections)

**Gray Wolf Delisting Challenge**

Earthjustice and the Endangered Species Coalition have opposed the congressional delisting approach (H.R. 845), arguing that removing species by legislation rather than science undermines the ESA's core framework. The bill has passed the House and awaits Senate action.

[source: Earthjustice - Gray Wolf Response](https://earthjustice.org/press/2025/earthjustice-responds-to-house-vote-to-strip-gray-wolves-of-endangered-species-act-protections)

**Pending Challenges to November 2025 Rules**

Multiple organizations have signaled they will challenge the four proposed ESA rules once finalized. The comment period closed December 22, 2025. Legal challenges are expected from Earthjustice, Center for Biological Diversity, Defenders of Wildlife, and state attorneys general.

[source: Endangered Species Coalition](https://www.endangered.org/trump-administration-proposes-fundamentally-weakening-endangered-species-act-with-harmful-new-rules/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- **Protection gap for threatened species:** Without the blanket 4(d) rule, newly listed threatened species could wait years for species-specific protections. During that gap, habitat destruction and take could proceed legally.
- **Economic veto over science:** Requiring economic analysis in critical habitat designations introduces a mechanism to shrink protected areas based on development interests, even when the science supports larger designations.
- **Congressional bypass of science:** Delisting species by legislation (gray wolf, grizzly bear) sets a precedent that any species protection can be overridden by political pressure rather than biological evidence.
- **Compounding workforce cuts:** With 400+ FWS staff laid off and a 32% budget cut, the agency lacks capacity to write species-specific rules, process listing petitions, or enforce existing protections -- even if the law stays on the books.
- **Habitat fragmentation accelerates:** Opening 50 million acres of sage-grouse habitat to energy development fragments one of America's most threatened ecosystems, with cascading effects on hundreds of species that share sagebrush habitat.

### Who's Affected

- **Ecosystems:** Endangered species are often keystone or indicator species. Losing them triggers cascading ecosystem collapse -- pollinators, predators, and prey are interconnected.
- **Wildlife recreation economy:** Americans spend $140 billion annually on wildlife-related recreation. Yellowstone and Grand Teton alone generate $753 million per year in wildlife viewing value. [source: Defenders of Wildlife - Economic Benefits](https://defenders.org/sites/default/files/publications/economic-benefits-of-the-endangered-species-act.pdf)
- **National parks and refuges:** 325.5 million visitors spent $26.4 billion in gateway communities, supporting 415,400 jobs. Wildlife viewing is the primary reason for visiting for 77% of Yellowstone visitors. [source: DOI - Refuge Visitor Spending](https://www.doi.gov/pressreleases/visitor-spending-national-wildlife-refuges-boosts-local-economies-32-billion)
- **Western ranching communities:** Wolf and grizzly delisting could trigger unmanaged hunting that crashes populations, then relisting -- creating the regulatory uncertainty ranchers want to avoid.
- **Utah communities:** Utah prairie dog, June sucker, and bonytail chub recovery programs depend on ESA framework and federal funding. Sage-grouse habitat management affects millions of acres of Utah public lands.
- **Tribal nations:** Many endangered species are culturally significant to Indigenous communities. Salmon, wolves, grizzly bears, and sage-grouse have deep ties to tribal heritage and treaty rights.
- **Future generations:** The ESA's 99% success rate represents a 50-year investment in biodiversity. Extinction is permanent.

### Comment on Proposed ESA Rules

When new ESA rules are published for comment, submit your comments at [regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov). Search for:

| Docket | Issue | Agency |
|--------|-------|--------|
| FWS-HQ-ES-2025-0168 | Blanket 4(d) Rule Elimination | FWS |
| FWS-HQ-ES-2025-0170 | Critical Habitat Economic Analysis | FWS |
| FWS-HQ-ES-2025-0169 | Interagency Consultation Changes | FWS |

Tips for effective comments:
- Be specific about which species or habitats matter to you and why
- Reference personal experience (wildlife watching, hunting, fishing, hiking)
- Cite the ESA's 99% success rate and economic benefits
- Note the scientific consensus on development buffers for sage-grouse (3-5 miles, not 0.6)

### Report Wildlife Observations

Contribute to species monitoring through citizen science:

- **iNaturalist** ([inaturalist.org](https://www.inaturalist.org)) -- Log wildlife observations with photos and GPS. Data feeds into the Global Biodiversity Information Facility and is used by researchers and agencies for species assessments.
- **eBird** ([ebird.org](https://ebird.org)) -- Report bird sightings. Cornell Lab of Ornithology uses this data to track population trends for species like the sage-grouse.
- **Utah Wildlife Reporting** ([wildlife.utah.gov](https://wildlife.utah.gov)) -- Report rare species sightings to the Utah Division of Wildlife Resources.

## Sustained Actions

### Support Organizations Fighting for the ESA

- **Center for Biological Diversity** ([biologicaldiversity.org](https://www.biologicaldiversity.org)) -- Files more ESA lawsuits than any other organization. Currently challenging the November 2025 proposed rules and fighting for listing of hundreds of candidate species.
- **Defenders of Wildlife** ([defenders.org](https://defenders.org)) -- Leading the legal challenge to sage-grouse habitat rollbacks and opposing wolf and grizzly delisting. Strong focus on coexistence programs for ranchers and predators.
- **National Wildlife Federation** ([nwf.org](https://www.nwf.org)) -- America's largest wildlife conservation organization. Works on habitat protection, outdoor recreation access, and wildlife corridor connectivity across all 50 states.
- **Earthjustice** ([earthjustice.org](https://earthjustice.org)) -- The legal arm of the environmental movement. Won the March 2026 court victory striking down ESA rollbacks. Represents dozens of groups in ESA litigation.
- **WildEarth Guardians** ([wildearthguardians.org](https://wildearthguardians.org)) -- Focuses on species protection in the American West. Co-plaintiff in the March 2026 ESA case.
