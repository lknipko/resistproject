# EPA Endangerment Finding Rescission

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** On February 12, 2026, EPA rescinded the 2009 Endangerment Finding -- the legal foundation for ALL federal greenhouse gas regulation. **This takes effect April 20, 2026 -- just 18 days away.** After that date, EPA will have no legal authority to regulate CO2, methane, or other greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act. 24 states and 10 cities have sued to block it.

This is the single most consequential environmental action of the current administration. Every federal climate regulation -- vehicle emission standards, power plant rules, methane limits -- depends on the Endangerment Finding.


## Facts

### What Is the Endangerment Finding?

In 2009, EPA formally found that greenhouse gases "endanger both the public health and the public welfare of current and future generations." This scientific determination -- the Endangerment Finding -- is the legal prerequisite for EPA to regulate greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act.

Without it, EPA has no statutory authority to limit emissions of CO2, methane, nitrous oxide, hydrofluorocarbons, perfluorocarbons, or sulfur hexafluoride.

[source: EPA 2009 Endangerment Finding](https://www.epa.gov/ghgemissions/endangerment-and-cause-or-contribute-findings-greenhouse-gases-under-section-202a-clean)

### What Regulations Are Affected

Without the Endangerment Finding, these regulations lose their legal foundation:

- **Vehicle emission standards** (GHG tailpipe rules for cars and trucks)
- **Power plant emission standards** (Clean Power Plan and successors)
- **Methane rules** for oil and gas operations
- **Hydrofluorocarbon (HFC) phase-down** requirements
- **Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program** (GHGRP)
- **All future federal GHG regulations** under the Clean Air Act

[source: EPA Clean Air Act GHG Regulatory History](https://www.epa.gov/clean-air-act-overview/clean-air-act-and-climate-change)

### The Lawsuit

On March 20, 2026, a coalition led by Massachusetts, California, New York, and Connecticut filed suit in the U.S. Court of Appeals for the DC Circuit. The coalition includes 24 states, 10 cities, and multiple counties including Santa Clara County. This is one of the largest state-led environmental lawsuits in history.

The plaintiffs argue:
- EPA ignored the overwhelming scientific consensus on climate change
- The rescission is "arbitrary and capricious" under the Administrative Procedure Act
- EPA failed to provide adequate scientific basis for reversing its 2009 finding
- The original Endangerment Finding was upheld by the DC Circuit in *Coalition for Responsible Regulation v. EPA* (2012)

[source: MPR News](https://www.mprnews.org/story/2026/03/20/states-cities-sue-epa-over-repeal-of-endangerment-finding-central-to-climate-fight)

## Analysis

### What This Means

The Endangerment Finding is not just another regulation -- it is the **scientific and legal prerequisite** for all federal greenhouse gas regulation. Without it:

- No new federal climate rules can be issued under the Clean Air Act
- Existing GHG regulations lose their legal foundation and can be challenged in court
- The U.S. effectively has no federal climate policy
- State-level climate action becomes the primary mechanism for emissions reduction
- International climate commitments become unenforceable

### Who's Affected

- **Everyone who breathes:** Greenhouse gas emissions drive both climate change and worsen air quality
- **Coastal communities:** Rising sea levels and intensified hurricanes
- **Agricultural regions:** Changing precipitation patterns, droughts, and heat waves
- **Environmental justice communities:** Disproportionately impacted by fossil fuel pollution
- **Clean energy industry:** Regulatory certainty undermined, potentially slowing investment

### Legal Outlook

The DC Circuit lawsuit is the most promising path to blocking the rescission. Key factors:

- The DC Circuit upheld the original Endangerment Finding in 2012
- The scientific evidence for climate change has only strengthened since 2009
- Courts typically require agencies to show a "reasoned basis" for reversing prior findings
- However, the Supreme Court's recent skepticism of agency authority (*West Virginia v. EPA*, 2022) adds uncertainty
- A preliminary injunction could pause the rescission before April 20
