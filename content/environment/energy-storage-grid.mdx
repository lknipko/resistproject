# Energy Storage & Grid Modernization: The Key to Clean Energy

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Lithium-ion battery pack prices fell from **~$1,200/kWh in 1991 to $115/kWh in 2024** — a **97% cost reduction** that makes grid-scale storage economically viable. The U.S. deployed a record **11.9 GW of new storage in 2024**, an 80% increase over the prior year. Yet **2,289 GW** of generation capacity sits stuck in interconnection queues — nearly twice total current U.S. generating capacity — waiting up to a decade for grid connection approvals.

The technology is ready. The obstacle is infrastructure: transmission lines too old and too few, interconnection queues that take 10+ years, and utility business models that profit from fossil "peaker" plants they don't want storage to replace. The IRA's storage investment tax credit, FERC Order 2023 queue reforms, and emerging technologies like iron-air batteries are beginning to shift the equation.

## Facts

### The Cost Revolution in Battery Storage

The economics of energy storage have changed fundamentally:

- **1991:** Lithium-ion battery packs cost approximately **$1,200/kWh**
- **2023:** Prices reached a new low of **$139/kWh**
- **2024:** Prices fell further to **$115/kWh** — a second consecutive record low
- **Trajectory:** BloombergNEF projects continued cost declines through the 2020s

For comparison: at $115/kWh, a 4-hour grid-scale battery system costs roughly $460/kW of capacity — competitive with many gas peaker plants that only run during peak demand hours.

[source: Morgan Lewis - 2025 Utility-Scale Storage Update](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2025/03/a-2025-update-on-utility-scale-energy-storage-procurements)

### Record U.S. Storage Deployment in 2024

The U.S. energy storage market achieved record growth in 2024:

- **11.9 GW** of new storage installed in 2024
- Q3 2024 alone: **3.8 GW** — an 80% increase over the same quarter in 2023
- Total U.S. grid storage has reached approximately **50 GW** of rated power capacity
- **15.2 GW** of additional storage is projected for 2025

[source: FERC State of the Markets 2024](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/SMR-2024) | [source: nZero - FERC 2025 Infrastructure Update](https://nzero.com/blog/fercs-2025-energy-infrastructure-update-26-gw-of-new-capacity-is-rewriting-americas-power-mix/)

### Why Storage Is the Key to Renewable Energy

Solar and wind are intermittent — they produce power when the sun shines or wind blows, not necessarily when demand peaks. Storage solves the intermittency problem:

- **Time-shifting:** Store midday solar surplus and dispatch it during evening peak demand
- **Frequency regulation:** Respond in milliseconds to stabilize grid frequency
- **Replacing peakers:** Grid-scale batteries can fulfill the same role as gas "peaker" plants that run only 100–200 hours per year
- **Resilience:** Distributed storage and microgrids maintain power during outages and extreme weather events

Without adequate storage, renewable penetration above roughly 30–40% of the grid mix creates reliability challenges. With storage, 80–100% clean grids become technically feasible.

### Pumped Hydro: The Original Grid Battery

Pumped storage hydropower (PSH) remains the dominant form of grid energy storage in the U.S.:

- PSH accounts for approximately **70% of U.S. utility-scale energy storage** capacity (down from 93% in 2019 as battery storage expands rapidly)
- The U.S. fleet includes about **22 GW of generating capacity** and **550 GWh of energy storage** across facilities in every region
- PSH works by pumping water uphill when electricity is cheap and releasing it through turbines when demand is high
- New PSH projects face 10–15 year permitting and construction timelines, making them complementary to — not a replacement for — battery storage scale-up

[source: DOE - How Pumped Storage Hydropower Works](https://www.energy.gov/cmei/water/how-pumped-storage-hydropower-works) | [source: University of Michigan - US Grid Energy Storage Factsheet](https://css.umich.edu/publications/factsheets/energy/us-grid-energy-storage-factsheet)

### [+] The IRA's Storage Tax Credit

The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), enacted in August 2022, created the first standalone investment tax credit (ITC) for energy storage:

- **Effective January 1, 2023:** Standalone battery storage now qualifies for the ITC regardless of whether it is paired with solar
- **Base credit: 30%** of project cost (with prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements met)
- **Up to 50%** with "energy community" or domestic content adders
- **Residential systems:** 30% credit for home battery systems of 3 kWh or larger under Section 25D
- **Transferability:** Credits can be sold to third-party buyers, unlocking financing for projects without large tax liabilities

Prior to the IRA, a battery could only claim the ITC if co-located with a solar project. The standalone ITC fundamentally changed project economics, enabling the 2024 deployment surge.

[source: EPA - IRA Summary](https://www.epa.gov/green-power-markets/summary-inflation-reduction-act-provisions-related-renewable-energy) | [source: McGuireWoods - IRA Storage Tax Credit](https://www.mcguirewoods.com/client-resources/alerts/2022/12/inflation-reduction-act-creates-new-tax-credit-opportunities-for-energy-storage-projects/)

### [+] The Interconnection Queue Crisis

The single largest bottleneck to clean energy deployment is the grid interconnection queue — the approval process for connecting new generation and storage to the grid:

- At the end of 2024, **2,289 GW** of generation and storage capacity sat in U.S. interconnection queues — nearly twice the total current generating capacity of the entire country
- The average time from queue entry to approval has stretched to **5 years or more**; many projects wait **10+ years**
- The queue contains more renewable energy and storage capacity than has ever been built — the backlog represents trillions of dollars of clean energy that cannot reach consumers
- In 2024, **47% less solar** and **32% less battery storage** capacity entered the queue compared to 2023, partly due to political uncertainty and tariff concerns

**FERC Order 2023** (issued July 2023) is the most significant interconnection reform in decades:
- Replaces first-come, first-served serial review with "cluster" processing of similar projects
- Increases project readiness requirements to reduce speculative applications clogging the queue
- Requires grid operators to study projects in groups, increasing efficiency
- In 2024, grid interconnection agreements jumped **33%** to a record 75 GW, attributed partly to Order 2023 reforms

[source: FERC Order 2023](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-directs-nations-largest-grid-operator-create-new-rules-embrace-innovation-and) | [source: SSII - Interconnection Queue Trends](https://www.ssii.org/making-sense-of-interconnection-queue-trends/)

### [+] Emerging Storage Technologies

Lithium-ion dominates today's storage market, but several technologies are advancing for applications where lithium-ion falls short (particularly long-duration storage beyond 4–8 hours):

**Iron-Air Batteries (Form Energy, others):**
- Store energy for up to **100 hours** — far longer than lithium-ion's typical 4 hours
- Made from iron, water, and air — among the cheapest and most abundant materials on Earth
- Form Energy raised over $400 million in funding; commercial deployments beginning in 2025
- Ore Energy delivered the **world's first grid-connected iron-air battery** in the Netherlands in 2025
- Cost target: ~$20/kWh — a fraction of lithium-ion costs for long-duration applications

**Iron Flow Batteries (ESS and others):**
- Store energy for up to **12 hours**, with unlimited cycling and 25-year design life with no capacity fade
- Use iron, salt, and water — nontoxic and domestically abundant
- Suited for commercial and industrial applications requiring daily cycling

**Pumped Hydro (advanced):**
- New "closed-loop" designs avoid river impacts, enabling more sites
- Advanced PSH projects are receiving renewed federal interest for their long-duration storage capability

**Hydrogen (longer-duration, seasonal):**
- Green hydrogen produced by electrolysis could provide seasonal storage (summer solar → winter heat)
- Technology is maturing but costs remain high; primarily relevant for multi-day to seasonal storage needs

[source: Form Energy - Iron-Air Battery](https://formenergy.com/technology/battery-technology/) | [source: ESS - Iron Flow Batteries](https://www.batterytechonline.com/industry-outlook/ess-iron-flow-batteries-powering-clean-safe-electrification)

### [+] The Texas Grid Failure: A Cautionary Case

The February 2021 Texas winter storm grid failure is the most consequential grid reliability failure in recent U.S. history:

- At its peak, **52,000 MW of power capacity went offline** and **4.5 million homes and businesses lost power**
- ERCOT later revealed the grid came within **4 minutes and 37 seconds** of a complete statewide collapse, which could have taken weeks to restore
- At least **246 people died** directly from the disaster (some estimates are significantly higher)
- Root cause: Natural gas infrastructure and generators were not winterized, despite warnings going back to a 1989 cold snap

Key lessons for storage and grid modernization:
- **Distributed storage and microgrids** would have maintained power for hospitals, emergency services, and vulnerable residents during the outage
- **Transmission congestion** was already identified as a major vulnerability before the storm; the 2021 disaster nearly doubled congestion costs to an estimated **$2.8 billion** in wasted energy in 2022
- **Vehicle-to-grid (V2G)** technology — enabling EVs to discharge power back to the grid during emergencies — could have provided significant backup capacity if infrastructure existed

[source: DEITABASE - 2021 Texas Grid Failure](https://limos.engin.umich.edu/deitabase/2024/12/27/2021-texas-power-grid-failure/) | [source: Inside Climate News - Texas Transmission Congestion](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/10072023/texas-power-grid-transmission-lines-renewable-energy/)

### [+] Vehicle-to-Grid and Virtual Power Plants

Two emerging technologies are turning consumers into grid assets:

**Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G):**
- Modern EV batteries hold 60–100 kWh — more than most home battery systems
- V2G-capable vehicles can export power to the home (V2H) or directly to the grid (V2G) during peak demand or outages
- Research suggests V2G could play a **substantial role preventing involuntary load shedding** during grid emergencies
- Requires bidirectional chargers (hardware) and utility programs (currently limited but expanding)

**Virtual Power Plants (VPPs):**
- Aggregate thousands of home batteries, smart thermostats, EV chargers, and water heaters into a coordinated grid resource
- Can dispatch the equivalent of a peaker plant without building any new infrastructure
- Growing programs from utilities including Green Mountain Power, Pacific Gas & Electric, and others
- FERC and state regulators are developing rules to ensure VPP resources are compensated for grid services they provide

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The clean energy transition is being slowed not by technology or economics — battery costs have fallen 97% — but by **grid infrastructure and regulatory bottlenecks** that were designed for the fossil fuel era.
- The IRA's standalone storage ITC was a genuine policy breakthrough, triggering record deployment. But IRA provisions face rollback risk from the current Congress.
- FERC Order 2023 is beginning to clear the interconnection backlog, but 2,289 GW still waits. At current rates of queue clearance, it would take decades to connect all pending clean energy projects.
- Texas showed that grid reliability failures are not primarily a renewable energy problem — they are a grid investment and resilience problem. Storage and distributed resources make grids more resilient, not less.
- Utilities that profit from fossil peaker plants have a financial incentive to delay storage interconnection. State public utility commissions and FERC must act to align utility incentives with grid modernization.

### Who's Affected

- **Electricity ratepayers:** Grid congestion and outdated infrastructure cost consumers billions annually in inefficiency
- **Clean energy workers:** Interconnection delays strand projects, costing jobs and investment
- **Communities near fossil peakers:** Gas peaker plants are disproportionately located in low-income communities and communities of color
- **Rural and disaster-prone areas:** Microgrids and distributed storage provide resilience that centralized grids cannot
- **Climate:** Every year of delay in grid modernization is another year of unnecessary fossil fuel combustion

## Quick Actions

### Contact Your State Public Utility Commission

Your state PUC (sometimes called PSC or PUC) regulates local utilities and can require storage procurement, interconnection reform, and demand response programs:

- Find your state PUC at [naruc.org/about-naruc/about-naruc/commissioners/](https://www.naruc.org/about-naruc/about-naruc/commissioners/)
- Submit a comment urging your PUC to:
  - Require utilities to procure grid-scale storage as an alternative to new fossil peaker plants
  - Implement demand response programs that pay customers to reduce usage during peaks
  - Support distributed energy resources and virtual power plant programs

### Ask Your Utility About Demand Response and Storage Programs

Contact your electric utility (find them on your bill) and ask:

- Do you offer a **demand response** program? (Get paid to reduce usage during peak hours)
- Do you offer a **community solar** program? (Buy into a shared solar array without rooftop installation)
- Do you offer incentives for **home battery storage**?
- Are you planning to **retire gas peaker plants** and replace them with storage?

Utilities that receive customer pressure on these questions are more likely to bring storage proposals to their state PUC.

### Explore Home Battery Backup

If you own a home, consider:

- **Tesla Powerwall**, **Enphase IQ Battery**, or **Franklin WH** systems (10–13 kWh typical)
- The IRA's 30% residential storage tax credit (Section 25D) applies to standalone batteries of 3 kWh or larger — no solar required
- Check your utility for additional rebates (many utilities offer $1,000–$3,000 incentives)
- In disaster-prone areas, a home battery can maintain critical loads (refrigerator, medical equipment, lights) for 12–24 hours during outages

### Participate in Community Solar

If rooftop solar isn't an option, community solar programs let you subscribe to a share of a local solar farm:

- Find programs at [EnergySage Community Solar](https://community.energysage.com/)
- Many programs require no installation and offer 5–15% savings on your electric bill

### Submit Comments on Your State's Integrated Resource Plan

Most utilities file a long-term Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) with their state public utility commission every 3–5 years, outlining what power plants they plan to build or retire. These proceedings accept public comments:

- Find your state's IRP proceeding at your state PUC website (naruc.org has a directory: [naruc.org/about-naruc/about-naruc/commissioners/](https://www.naruc.org/about-naruc/about-naruc/commissioners/))
- Submit comments urging your utility to plan for battery storage instead of new gas peaker plants
- **Vote Solar** ([votesolar.org](https://votesolar.org)) often coordinates comment campaigns when IRP dockets are open — sign up for their state alerts

### Volunteer With Grid Literacy and Energy Justice Organizations

- **Rewiring America** ([rewiringamerica.org](https://www.rewiringamerica.org)) — Electrification advocates; volunteers help households navigate IRA rebates and home storage incentives
- **Clean Energy Corps** — Various state-level programs that train community members to educate neighbors about energy efficiency and storage options
- Your local **Sierra Club chapter** likely has an energy committee working on state utility commission proceedings — a high-impact local volunteer opportunity

### Track Your Home Energy Use With Smart Monitoring (Citizen Action)

Understanding your energy use is the first step toward storage and demand-response participation:

- **Sense Home Energy Monitor** or similar devices show real-time usage and identify which appliances are energy hogs
- Enroll in your utility's **time-of-use rate** — you'll pay less for electricity used during off-peak hours, which incentivizes shifting loads and charging storage when the grid is cleanest
- Share your data and experience with neighbors — community education accelerates local adoption
