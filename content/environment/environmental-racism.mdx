# Environmental Justice & Pollution Inequality

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** In the United States, race is the strongest predictor of proximity to pollution -- stronger even than income. People of color experience higher-than-average exposure from source types causing 75% of overall pollution, while white people are exposed to lower-than-average concentrations from sources causing 60% of exposure. This disparity holds across all income levels, in urban and rural areas, and in every region of the country.

This is not coincidence. Decades of redlining, discriminatory zoning, and industrial siting decisions concentrated polluting facilities in Black, Latino, Indigenous, and low-income communities. Now, the federal office created to address these disparities has been shut down and $1.7 billion in environmental justice grants cancelled.

## Facts

### Key Points

- **Race outweighs income:** Air pollution exposure disparities persist for people of color at every income level, in every state, and in urban and rural areas alike
- **Redlining's toxic legacy:** Formerly redlined neighborhoods have 50%+ higher NO2 levels than the highest-graded areas, nearly 80 years after the maps were drawn
- **EPA EJ office eliminated:** The Office of Environmental Justice was closed on March 12, 2025, and 168 staff placed on leave. Over 400 grants totaling $1.7 billion cancelled
- **EJScreen removed:** EPA's environmental justice mapping tool was removed from the agency website on February 5, 2025
- **Cumulative harm:** Communities near pollution don't face a single hazard -- they face overlapping exposures to bad air, contaminated water, toxic soil, noise, heat, and poverty simultaneously

### [+] The Data: Who Lives Near Pollution

Multiple peer-reviewed studies have quantified the racial and economic dimensions of pollution exposure in the United States.

**Race and Air Pollution (Science Advances, 2021):**
A nationwide study published in *Science Advances* found that PM2.5 (fine particulate matter) polluters "disproportionately and systemically" affect people of color. Nearly all major emission categories -- industry, vehicles, agriculture, construction -- contribute to the disparity. The study concluded that race, not income, is the primary driver, pointing to "disparate siting" of polluting facilities in communities of color.

[source: Tessum et al., Science Advances](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.abf4491)

**Income Does Not Protect People of Color (EPA Study):**
An EPA-published study found that exposure to air pollution is higher for people of color regardless of region or income. Even high-income communities of color face greater pollution burdens than low-income white communities. This confirms that racial disparities cannot be explained by economic factors alone.

[source: EPA Science Matters](https://www.epa.gov/sciencematters/study-finds-exposure-air-pollution-higher-people-color-regardless-region-or-income)

**Mortality Disparities (New England Journal of Medicine, 2023):**
A study published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* found that air pollution and mortality intersect at the junction of race and social class, with Black Americans and low-income populations experiencing significantly higher pollution-related death rates.

[source: NEJM - Air Pollution and Mortality](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMsa2300523)

**National Trends Over 40 Years (Nature Communications, 2023):**
An analysis of U.S. emissions from 1970 to 2010 found that while overall air pollution decreased, racial and ethnic disparities in exposure persisted or worsened. Counties with median family income over $75,000 saw larger emission reductions than lower-income counties.

[source: Nature Communications](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-023-43492-9)

**Proximity to Pollution Sources (American Journal of Public Health):**
Disparities in the distribution of particulate matter emission sources by race and poverty status show that pollution-emitting facilities are systematically concentrated in minority and low-income census tracts.

[source: PMC/AJPH](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5844406/)

### [+] Case Studies

#### Flint, Michigan: Lead-Poisoned Water

In April 2014, the city of Flint switched its water source from Lake Huron (via Detroit) to the Flint River to save money -- without applying corrosion control treatment. Lead leached from aging pipes into drinking water, exposing approximately 100,000 residents, including 9,000 children, to dangerously elevated lead levels.

**Key facts:**
- Flint's population was 54% Black and had a poverty rate of 41% at the time of the crisis
- State officials dismissed resident complaints for over a year despite mounting evidence
- Blood lead levels in children under 5 doubled in some zip codes
- 12 people died from Legionnaires' disease linked to the contaminated water
- Over 28,000 pipes were excavated and nearly 11,000 lead pipes replaced (completed July 2025)
- EPA lifted its emergency order in 2025 after nine straight years of passing water tests
- Residents are still waiting on payouts from $600+ million in legal settlements; about 26,000 people are expected to receive payments
- Lawsuits against the EPA are expected to reach federal courtrooms in 2026

[source: NRDC - Flint Water Crisis](https://www.nrdc.org/stories/flint-water-crisis-everything-you-need-know) | [source: Michigan Public - 2025 Milestone](https://www.michiganpublic.org/environment-climate-change/2025-06-02/a-hard-fought-milestone-for-flints-water-but-uncertainty-ahead) | [source: Michigan Public - EPA Lawsuits 2026](https://www.michiganpublic.org/criminal-justice-legal-system/2025-12-29/flint-water-crisis-lawsuits-against-epa-may-finally-end-up-in-federal-courtrooms-in-2026)

#### Cancer Alley, Louisiana: Petrochemical Corridor

An 85-mile stretch of the Mississippi River between Baton Rouge and New Orleans contains over 200 petrochemical plants processing about 25% of U.S. petrochemical products. Black communities along this corridor face cancer incidence rates up to 47 times higher than the EPA's acceptable risk level.

**Key facts:**
- Average lifetime cancer risk from air toxics for Cancer Alley residents is more than double that of other Louisiana residents (2014-2018 data)
- Industrial facilities are sited on historical plantation lands, concentrating pollution burdens on predominantly Black communities
- A 2024 Human Rights Watch report titled *"We're Dying Here"* documented the crisis as a human rights violation
- In March 2025, the Trump DOJ dropped the federal case against Denka's chloroprene-emitting elastomer plant in LaPlace
- In June 2025, the Fifth Circuit reversed a lower court dismissal, allowing residents to pursue a lawsuit targeting "a longstanding pattern and practice of racially discriminatory land-use decisions"

[source: Human Rights Watch - Cancer Alley](https://www.hrw.org/report/2024/01/25/were-dying-here/fight-life-louisiana-fossil-fuel-sacrifice-zone) | [source: Columbia Climate School](https://news.climate.columbia.edu/2024/03/18/the-endless-shame-of-louisianas-cancer-alley/) | [source: HRW - Appeal Victory](https://www.hrw.org/news/2025/06/02/cancer-alley-residents-win-petrochemical-suit-appeal-opportunity-justice)

#### East Palestine, Ohio: Train Derailment

On February 3, 2023, a Norfolk Southern freight train derailed in East Palestine, Ohio, spilling thousands of gallons of vinyl chloride, butyl acrylate, and other hazardous chemicals. The controlled burn of vinyl chloride released hydrogen chloride and phosgene into the atmosphere.

**Key facts:**
- East Palestine is a working-class community of approximately 4,800 people with a median household income well below the national average
- 20 of the derailed cars contained hazardous materials
- Tens of thousands of fish were killed in nearby waterways
- Nearly 220,000 tons of contaminated soil and 74+ million gallons of polluted water have been removed
- Health surveys show 85% of respondents report anxiety, 80% report sleep problems, and 82.5% report muscle pain
- Odds of new pulmonary symptoms decrease 11% for each mile further from the derailment site
- NIEHS committed $10 million over 5 years for community health research
- As of October 2024, EPA identified 14 areas still requiring additional soil remediation

[source: EPA - East Palestine Response](https://www.epa.gov/east-palestine-oh-train-derailment) | [source: NIEHS - Research Response](https://www.niehs.nih.gov/research/programs/east_palestine) | [source: IdeastreamNEWS - Health Impacts](https://www.ideastream.org/health/2025-02-03/2-years-after-train-derailment-east-palestine-residents-still-have-questions-about-their-health)

#### Grants Mining District, New Mexico: Uranium Contamination

The Grants uranium district in northwestern New Mexico produced more uranium ore than any other U.S. mining district during the Cold War (1947-1989). The contamination disproportionately affects Navajo Nation, Laguna Pueblo, and other Indigenous communities.

**Key facts:**
- Over 1,000 abandoned uranium mines and seven massive uranium mill tailings piles remain
- 98% of what was mined remains on-site as toxic waste, overburden, and mill tailings
- Studies found elevated levels of radioactive metals in Navajo women and infants decades after mining ended
- The district has been called a national "sacrifice zone" and a case study in environmental racism and settler colonialism
- A New Mexico lawmaker proposed $50 million for uranium mine cleanup in January 2026
- Indigenous perspectives have been systematically excluded from cleanup planning and execution

[source: EPA - Grants Mining District Legacy](https://www.epa.gov/grants-mining-district/legacy-abandoned-uranium-mines-grants-mining-district-new-mexico) | [source: NM Political Report](https://nmpoliticalreport.com/news/this-has-got-to-stop-indigenous-activists-decry-lingering-contamination-decades-after-the-last-uranium-mines-closed/) | [source: NM In Depth - Toxic Legacy](https://nmindepth.com/2022/the-toxic-legacy-of-uranium-mining-in-new-mexico/)

### [+] Redlining to Pollution Siting

In the 1930s, the federal Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) graded neighborhoods across 239 U.S. cities from A ("Best") to D ("Hazardous"). Neighborhoods with Black, immigrant, and low-income residents were systematically graded D -- "redlined" -- making them ineligible for mortgage lending. Research now shows that these same neighborhoods bear the highest pollution burdens today, nearly a century later.

**The Evidence:**

A landmark 2022 study in *Environmental Science & Technology Letters* analyzed 202 U.S. cities and found a "consistent and nearly monotonic" relationship between HOLC grade and pollution levels. Formerly redlined (Grade D) neighborhoods had:

- **50%+ higher NO2 levels** compared to Grade A neighborhoods
- **Higher pollution burdens** in 77% of redlined tracts vs. 18% of greenlined tracts
- **More noise** in 72% of redlined tracts vs. 18% of greenlined tracts
- **Less vegetation** in 86% of redlined tracts vs. 12% of greenlined tracts
- **Higher temperatures** in 72% of redlined tracts vs. 20% of greenlined tracts

A follow-up California-wide analysis confirmed that formerly redlined neighborhoods have poorer environmental quality across multiple indicators: higher pollution, more noise, less vegetation, and elevated temperatures.

Critically, the researchers found that air pollution disparities associated with redlining were even larger than those associated with race and ethnicity alone -- meaning the maps themselves became self-fulfilling prophecies, channeling industrial development and highway construction into already-disadvantaged communities.

However, racial disparities persist within each HOLC grade, indicating that redlining was one of many discriminatory systems -- alongside racial covenants, white flight, exclusionary zoning, and highway routing -- that shaped today's pollution landscape.

[source: Lane et al., ES&T Letters 2022](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.estlett.1c01012) | [source: PMC - Redlining and Environmental Quality](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10867848/) | [source: UC Berkeley Public Health](https://publichealth.berkeley.edu/articles/spotlight/research/study-shows-more-air-pollution-in-areas-of-historical-redlining)

### [+] Environmental Justice Policy Rollbacks

The federal environmental justice infrastructure built over three decades is being dismantled. Environmental justice as a policy framework originated with Executive Order 12898, signed by President Clinton in 1994, which directed federal agencies to identify and address disproportionate environmental and health effects on minority and low-income populations.

**2025 Actions:**

| Date | Action | Impact |
|------|--------|--------|
| Jan 20, 2025 | Executive Order 14151 signed | Directs agencies to terminate all "environmental justice" offices and positions, classifying EJ as a DEI program |
| Feb 5, 2025 | EJScreen removed from EPA website | Primary public tool for identifying overburdened communities taken offline |
| Mar 12, 2025 | EPA Office of Environmental Justice closed | 168 employees placed on administrative leave; 10 regional EJ offices eliminated |
| Mar 2025 | 400+ EJ grants cancelled | $1.7 billion in grants for lead pipe replacement, air monitoring, green workforce training, and flood protection terminated |
| Mar 7, 2025 | DOJ drops Denka case | Federal lawsuit against chloroprene-emitting plant in Cancer Alley dismissed |
| FY2026 proposal | EPA budget cut 54% | Total EPA funding drops from $9.14 billion to $4.16 billion; EJ program eliminated entirely |

**What Was Lost:**
The cancelled grants funded projects across the country, including: child lead-poisoning prevention, community air quality monitoring, tree planting in urban heat islands, solar energy for low-income households, and tribal community environmental health projects. State agencies, city and county governments, schools, nonprofits, and tribal communities all lost funding.

**Legal Challenges:**
Earthjustice filed a class-action lawsuit on behalf of nonprofits, tribes, and local governments to restore terminated EPA grant programs. The case has reached the appeals stage in 2026.

[source: Congress.gov - CRS Report](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF12922) | [source: EDF - EPA EJ Offices](https://www.edf.org/media/trump-administration-close-epas-environmental-justice-offices) | [source: Stateline - Cancelled Grants](https://stateline.org/2025/04/14/trump-has-canceled-environmental-justice-grants-heres-what-communities-are-losing/) | [source: Earthjustice - Lawsuit](https://earthjustice.org/press/2025/nonprofits-tribes-and-local-governments-sue-trump-administration-for-terminating-epa-grant-programs)

### [+] Cumulative Impacts: When Multiple Hazards Overlap

Environmental justice communities rarely face a single pollution source. They face cumulative impacts -- the combined health effects of multiple pollution exposures layered on top of social vulnerabilities like poverty, lack of healthcare access, and pre-existing health conditions.

**How Cumulative Impact Assessment Works:**

Traditional environmental regulation evaluates one pollutant or one facility at a time. Cumulative impact tools assess the total burden on a community by combining:

- **Pollution burden indicators:** Air quality (PM2.5, ozone, diesel particulates, toxic releases), water quality, proximity to hazardous waste sites, pesticide use, lead exposure
- **Population vulnerability indicators:** Poverty rates, unemployment, educational attainment, linguistic isolation, pre-existing health conditions (asthma, cardiovascular disease)
- **Combined score:** Pollution burden multiplied by population vulnerability produces a relative ranking of community-level risk

**Key Tools:**

- **EJScreen (EPA):** Used 12 environmental indicators and 6 demographic indicators to rank census tracts nationally. Removed from EPA's website on Feb 5, 2025, but preserved by the Public Environmental Data Partners at [screening-tools.com](https://screening-tools.com/epa-ejscreen) and mirrored by Yale's Energy History project
- **CalEnviroScreen (California):** Uses 20 indicators across California's 8,000+ census tracts. Created by the California Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment. Version 4.0 is the most comprehensive state-level cumulative impact tool in the country

**Why This Matters:**

CalEnviroScreen data shows clear racial disparities: communities of color consistently score in the highest cumulative impact percentiles. A 2015 study in the *American Journal of Public Health* using CalEnviroScreen data found stark racial and ethnic disparities in cumulative environmental health impacts across California, confirming that overlapping hazards cluster in communities that are least equipped to cope with them.

[source: CalEnviroScreen - About](https://oehha.ca.gov/calenviroscreen/about-calenviroscreen) | [source: AJPH - Racial Disparities in Cumulative Impacts](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26378826/) | [source: EJScreen Mirror - Screening Tools](https://screening-tools.com/epa-ejscreen) | [source: EDGI - EJScreen Removal](https://envirodatagov.org/epa-removes-ejscreen-from-its-website/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

The elimination of federal environmental justice infrastructure does not make the underlying problem disappear -- it removes the tools used to measure and address it. Without EJScreen, communities lose the ability to demonstrate quantitatively that they bear disproportionate pollution burdens. Without the Office of Environmental Justice, there is no federal advocate for communities facing cumulative exposures. Without the $1.7 billion in cancelled grants, lead pipes stay in the ground, air monitors go offline, and contaminated sites remain unremediated.

The pattern is consistent across every case study: communities with less political power receive more pollution. Flint's water was poisoned under a state-appointed emergency manager who overrode local democratic governance. Cancer Alley's petrochemical plants were sited on former plantation lands where the descendants of enslaved people still live. Uranium mines poisoned Navajo land that the federal government itself had designated for mining. East Palestine's working-class residents had no say in the hazardous materials routed through their town.

Environmental justice is not a niche issue -- it is a test of whether environmental law protects all Americans equally. The current data says it does not.

### Who's Affected

- **Black Americans:** Experience 21% higher PM2.5 exposure than the national average; face the highest pollution-related mortality rates at the intersection of race and class
- **Hispanic/Latino Americans:** Disproportionately exposed to industrial emissions, agricultural pesticides, and traffic-related pollution
- **Indigenous communities:** Bear the legacy of uranium mining, oil and gas extraction, and toxic waste siting on tribal lands; the Navajo Nation alone has 500+ abandoned uranium mines
- **Low-income communities of all races:** 77% of formerly redlined neighborhoods still have above-average pollution burdens; working-class towns like East Palestine face industrial hazards with minimal regulatory protection
- **Children:** More vulnerable to lead, air pollution, and chemical exposures; children in high-pollution census tracts have higher rates of asthma, developmental delays, and learning disabilities
- **73 million Americans** live within 3 miles of a Superfund site
- **100+ million Americans** have PFAS in their drinking water

### Attend Local Zoning and Permitting Hearings

Industrial facilities, waste sites, and chemical plants require local permits. Your voice at these hearings matters:

1. **Check your city/county planning commission calendar** for upcoming zoning hearings
2. **Look for permit applications** from industrial facilities near residential areas
3. **Show up and speak** during public comment periods -- even 2 minutes of testimony is entered into the public record
4. **Connect with neighbors** -- coordinated testimony from multiple residents carries more weight

### Get Involved Locally

- **Join or start a community air monitoring program** -- Low-cost PurpleAir sensors (~$250) can document pollution in your neighborhood
- **Request environmental review** of proposed industrial projects near your community
- **Engage your local health department** on environmental health concerns -- request cumulative impact assessments for overburdened areas
- **Document pollution incidents** with photos, dates, and descriptions -- this creates a record for future legal or regulatory action
- **Attend city council meetings** when industrial zoning changes are proposed

### Advocate for Cumulative Impact Laws

Only a handful of states have adopted cumulative impact assessment in permitting. Push your state to join them:
- **New Jersey** passed the first state environmental justice law requiring cumulative impact analysis (2020)
- **California** uses CalEnviroScreen to direct 25% of cap-and-trade revenue to disadvantaged communities
- Contact your state legislators to support similar laws in your state
