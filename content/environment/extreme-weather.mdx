# Extreme Weather & Climate Attribution

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Climate attribution science now lets researchers quantify how much climate change worsened a specific hurricane, heat wave, wildfire, or flood — and the toll keeps mounting. A **historic heat dome baked the eastern U.S. from June 28 to July 8, 2026**, putting roughly **180 million people** under major or extreme heat risk and killing **at least 44** (Atlantic City hit 106°F; NYC and Boston topped 100°F for the first time since 2012), with follow-on storms causing 1.3 million power outages. The U.S. experienced 23 billion-dollar weather disasters in 2025 costing $115 billion, the third-highest year on record. Meanwhile, FEMA has lost one-third of its workforce and $3 billion in resilience grants have been canceled.

The three hottest years in recorded history are 2023, 2024, and 2025 — all in a row.

### [+] Attribution Science Explained

**What is climate attribution?** A field of science that uses weather observations and climate models to determine how much human-caused climate change contributed to a specific extreme weather event. Researchers run climate models with and without the added greenhouse gases from human activity, then compare the results statistically. [source: NOAA Climate.gov - Extreme Event Attribution](https://www.climate.gov/news-features/understanding-climate/extreme-event-attribution-climate-versus-weather-blame-game)

**How it works (four steps):**

1. **Measure** the magnitude and frequency of the event using observed data
2. **Simulate** the event with climate models that include human-caused warming
3. **Simulate again** with a baseline having no climate change (pre-industrial conditions)
4. **Compare** the two simulations statistically to quantify the difference

**What attribution can tell us:**

- "This heat wave was made X times more likely by climate change"
- "This hurricane produced Y% more rainfall due to warming"
- "This event was effectively impossible without climate change"

**What it cannot tell us:**

- Attribution does not claim climate change "caused" an event in a yes-or-no sense
- Natural variability still plays a role in every weather event
- Some event types (heat waves, heavy rainfall) are easier to attribute than others (tornadoes, drought)

**Key organizations:** The [World Weather Attribution](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/) group, founded in 2014, has conducted more than 100 rapid attribution studies on heat waves, rainfall, floods, wildfires, and cold spells worldwide. Their studies are peer-reviewed and published within days to weeks of an event. [source: World Weather Attribution](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/)

The National Academies of Sciences has published comprehensive assessments of attribution methodology. [source: National Academies - Attribution of Extreme Weather Events](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/21852/attribution-of-extreme-weather-events-in-the-context-of-climate-change)

### [+] Hurricanes & Tropical Storms

**Rapid intensification is increasing:** Offshore areas within 400 km of coastlines saw rapid intensification events triple from 1980 to 2020. Atlantic tropical cyclones were 29% more likely to undergo rapid intensification from 2001-2020 compared to 1971-1990. [source: Nature Communications - Tropical Cyclone Rapid Intensification](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-023-40605-2)

**Hurricane Milton (October 2024):** Strengthened from Category 1 to Category 5 in just 18 hours — the second-fastest intensification in recorded Atlantic history. The abnormally high sea surface temperatures that fueled Milton's intensification were made 400-800 times more likely by climate change. Without climate change, Milton would likely have made landfall as a Category 2 instead of a Category 3 storm. [source: Climate Central - Hurricane Milton](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-shift-index-alert/hurricane-milton-october-2024) | [source: World Weather Attribution - Hurricane Milton](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/yet-another-hurricane-wetter-windier-and-more-destructive-because-of-climate-change/)

**Hurricane Erin (August 2025):** Exploded from Category 1 to Category 5 in approximately 24 hours, becoming one of the fastest-strengthening Atlantic hurricanes on record. Warmer-than-usual sea surface temperatures and elevated atmospheric moisture — both driven by climate change — fueled the rapid intensification. [source: Yale Climate Connections - Rapidly Intensifying Hurricanes](https://yaleclimateconnections.org/2025/08/climate-change-brings-more-rapidly-intensifying-hurricanes-noaa-cuts-makes-forecasting-them-harder/)

**Hurricane Melissa (October 2025):** Made landfall in Jamaica as a Category 5 storm, tying the record for the Atlantic's strongest cyclone. [source: World Weather Attribution - 2025 Studies](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/2025/)

**Why warming intensifies hurricanes:** Warmer ocean surface temperatures provide more energy to storms. Warmer air holds more moisture, producing heavier rainfall. Sea level rise amplifies storm surge. Climate models project continued increases in the proportion of Category 4-5 hurricanes. [source: GFDL - Global Warming and Hurricanes](https://www.gfdl.noaa.gov/global-warming-and-hurricanes/)

**Forecasting under threat:** NOAA budget cuts and workforce reductions are degrading the hurricane forecasting capabilities that protect coastal communities. [source: Yale Climate Connections](https://yaleclimateconnections.org/2025/08/climate-change-brings-more-rapidly-intensifying-hurricanes-noaa-cuts-makes-forecasting-them-harder/)

### [+] Heat Waves

**Frequency surge:** In U.S. cities, the average rate of extreme heat events increased from 2 per year in the 1960s to 10 per year between 2010 and 2020. The average heat-wave season in the U.S. has lengthened by 46 days since the 1960s. [source: C2ES - Heat Waves and Climate Change](https://www.c2es.org/content/heat-waves-and-climate-change/)

**Accelerating duration:** Research shows the lengthening of heat waves accelerates with each additional fraction of a degree of warming. The longest heat waves show the greatest acceleration, and the frequency of the most extreme heat waves increases the most. [source: UCLA - Heat Wave Duration Accelerating](https://newsroom.ucla.edu/releases/duration-of-heat-waves-accelerating-faster-than-global-warming)

**Heat deaths rising:** Between 2000 and 2025, heat-related deaths in the United States increased by more than 50%. [source: World Weather Attribution - Escalation of Global Extreme Heat](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/wp-content/uploads/Report_-Climate-Change-and-the-Escalation-of-Global-Extreme-Heat-Heat-Action-Day-2025.pdf)

**2025-2026 attribution studies:**

- **March 2025 Western North America heat wave:** Record-shattering temperatures found to be "virtually impossible without climate change." [source: World Weather Attribution](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/record-shattering-march-temperatures-in-western-north-america-virtually-impossible-without-climate-change/)
- **January 2026 Australia heat wave:** Nighttime temperatures made approximately 2 degrees C hotter and 33 times more likely due to human-caused climate change. [source: World Weather Attribution - 2025 Extreme Weather](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/unequal-evidence-and-impacts-limits-to-adaptation-extreme-weather-in-2025/)
- **Some 2025 heat waves were 10 times more likely** than they would have been just a decade ago. About half of the world's land surface experienced more days than usual with dangerous heat stress in 2025. [source: Carbon Brief](https://www.carbonbrief.org/state-of-the-climate-2025-in-top-three-hottest-years-on-record-as-ocean-heat-surges/)

### [+] Wildfires

**Los Angeles Wildfires (January 2025):** The costliest wildfire event in world history at over $61 billion in damages — twice the previous record (2018 wildfire season). World Weather Attribution found that fire-weather conditions were **35% more likely and 6% more intense** due to 1.3 degrees C of global warming. The dry season has lengthened by approximately 23 days, increasingly overlapping with Santa Ana wind events. Low rainfall from October to December is now 2.4 times more likely than in the pre-industrial climate. [source: World Weather Attribution - LA Wildfires](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/climate-change-increased-the-likelihood-of-wildfire-disaster-in-highly-exposed-los-angeles-area/) | [source: Yale Climate Connections](https://yaleclimateconnections.org/2025/01/climate-change-made-deadly-los-angeles-wildfires-35-more-likely-new-attribution-study/)

**Future projections:** If the world warms by another 1.3 degrees C by 2100, fire-prone conditions in Southern California will grow another 35% more likely. [source: UCLA Sustainable LA](https://sustainablela.ucla.edu/2025lawildfires)

**Long-term U.S. trends:** Since 2000, an annual average of 70,025 wildfires have burned an average of 7.0 million acres per year — more than double the average burned in the 1990s. [source: C2ES - Wildfires and Climate Change](https://www.c2es.org/content/wildfires-and-climate-change/)

**2026 off to record start:** Through March 27, 2026, the U.S. has already experienced over 15,000 wildfire starts consuming more than 1.5 million acres — 127% more acreage than the 10-year average for that period. [source: Inside Climate News](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/27032026/us-wildfires-already-setting-records-this-year/)

**Season lengthening:** Wildfires outside the traditional fire season are becoming more common as rising temperatures lead to earlier snowmelt and later fall and winter precipitation. Climate-driven drought, extreme heat, and drying vegetation have lengthened fire seasons across the West. [source: NOAA Wildfire Climate Connection](https://www.noaa.gov/noaa-wildfire/wildfire-climate-connection)

**Global pattern:** WWA found that climate change made weather conditions fueling wildfires across Portugal and Spain approximately 40 times more likely in 2025. [source: World Weather Attribution - 2025 Assessment](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/unequal-evidence-and-impacts-limits-to-adaptation-extreme-weather-in-2025/)

### [+] Floods & Drought

**2025 — a record flood year:** More than 5,000 flash flood warnings were issued across all U.S. weather-forecasting offices — the first time the country crossed the 5,000-warning threshold in a single year. [source: EarthSky](https://earthsky.org/earth/flash-floods-us-extreme-2025/)

**Texas Hill Country Floods (July 2025):** Catastrophic flash flooding killed at least 138 people, making it one of the deadliest inland floods in U.S. history. Torrential rains from a slow-moving thunderstorm complex triggered rapid river rises that inundated campgrounds and cabins on the Fourth of July. [source: CNN - Summer of Flooding](https://www.cnn.com/2025/07/20/climate/summer-of-flooding)

**The climate connection:** Warmer air holds approximately 7% more moisture per degree Celsius of warming (Clausius-Clapeyron relation), increasing the likelihood of heavy downpours. Some eastern states saw 50% more precipitation than normal in 2025. A warmer ocean evaporates more water, and extraordinary levels of atmospheric moisture were directed into storm systems. [source: C2ES - Extreme Precipitation and Climate Change](https://www.c2es.org/content/extreme-precipitation-and-climate-change/)

**Disaster cost trend:** The average annual number of billion-dollar disasters has surged from approximately 3 events per year in the 1980s to 20 events per year in 2016-2025, with average annual costs rising from $18 billion to $120 billion (inflation-adjusted). [source: Climate Central](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-services/billion-dollar-disasters) | [source: Yale Climate Connections](https://yaleclimateconnections.org/2026/01/earth-was-hit-by-55-billion-dollar-weather-disasters-in-2025/)

**Global flooding in 2025:** Eastern Mexico experienced three days of heavy rainfall in October causing widespread flooding and landslides. Cyclone Ditwah struck Sri Lanka in November causing the worst flooding since the early 2000s. [source: World Weather Attribution](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/2025/)

### [+] Economic & Insurance Impact

**National disaster costs:** The 23 billion-dollar disasters in 2025 cost $115 billion, with severe storms alone accounting for $51 billion — one of the highest totals ever for that hazard type. 21 billion-dollar severe storm events set a new record. [source: Climate Central - 2025 in Review](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-matters/2025-in-review) | [source: Time](https://time.com/7344568/2025-billion-dollar-climate-disasters/)

**Insurance premiums surging:** Homeowner insurance premiums increased nationally by 10.4% in 2024 and another 8% in 2025. Disaster-prone states face increases over 20% for some carriers. Florida homeowners pay the highest rates in the nation — an average of $15,000/year. [source: NPR - Home Insurance](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/09/nx-s1-5734455/home-insurance-risk-disasters-climate-change-extreme-weather)

**Insurer withdrawals:** Seven out of 12 major carriers have left or reduced coverage in California since 2022. After the LA fires, State Farm requested an interim 22% rate hike. The Excess and Surplus (E&S) market — typically for hard-to-insure risks — accounted for 16% of policies in California, Florida, and Texas by December 2025, up from under 2% in 2023. [source: Matic - 2026 Insurance Predictions](https://matic.com/blog/2026-home-insurance-predictions/)

**Who pays when federal support shrinks:** State and local governments will bear enormous costs if the federal government steps back from its 40-year role in disaster recovery. Gulf South and mid-Atlantic states are expected to be hardest hit. [source: Carnegie Endowment](https://carnegieendowment.org/emissary/2025/03/fema-disaster-recovery-budget-cuts-state-impact?lang=en)

**NOAA disaster database transferred:** The NOAA Billion Dollar Weather and Climate Disasters database was retired in 2025. Climate Central now manages and maintains the dataset. [source: NESDIS Notice of Changes](https://www.nesdis.noaa.gov/about/documents-reports/notice-of-changes/2025-notice-of-changes/billion-dollar-weather-and-climate-disasters)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- **Attribution science has closed the debate.** Scientists can now quantify climate change's role in individual events within days. The LA wildfires were 35% more likely. Hurricane Milton's ocean fuel was 400-800 times more likely. Some heat waves are "virtually impossible" without warming. The question is no longer if climate change worsens extreme weather — it is by how much.

- **Disasters are outpacing our capacity to respond.** Billion-dollar disasters have increased from 3 per year to 20 per year in four decades, while FEMA is being systematically weakened — losing staff, funding, and programs at the exact moment they are needed most.

- **The insurance market is signaling systemic risk.** When major insurers withdraw from entire states and premiums double, it means the private sector has determined that climate risk exceeds what markets can absorb. This threatens home values, mortgage markets, and the broader economy.

- **Adaptation and mitigation are not optional.** Every fraction of a degree of warming accelerates these trends. The cancellation of $3 billion in BRIC resilience grants means communities that could have been fortified against future disasters will not be.

### Who's Affected

- **Coastal communities:** Rising seas, stronger storm surge, and more intense hurricanes threaten millions of Americans along the Atlantic and Gulf coasts. Hurricane rapid intensification gives less time to evacuate.
- **Low-income households:** Cannot afford rising insurance premiums, lack resources to evacuate or rebuild, and live in housing most vulnerable to extreme heat, flooding, and wind damage. Disaster recovery often deepens existing inequality.
- **Rural communities:** Wildfires threaten homes at the wildland-urban interface. Agricultural communities face crop losses from drought, flooding, and extreme heat. Rural areas often have fewer emergency response resources.
- **Elderly and health-vulnerable populations:** Heat deaths in the U.S. have increased over 50% since 2000. Elderly residents, outdoor workers, people with chronic conditions, and those without air conditioning face the greatest risk.
- **Taxpayers:** As federal disaster response is cut, state and local governments must absorb costs — meaning higher taxes or reduced services in other areas.
- **Future homeowners:** Insurance market instability, rising premiums, and insurer withdrawals make homeownership more expensive and risky in disaster-prone regions.

### Build Your Disaster Preparedness Kit

Every household should be prepared for extreme weather. FEMA recommends supplies for at least 72 hours:

- **Water:** One gallon per person per day for at least three days
- **Food:** Three-day supply of non-perishable food
- **Battery-powered or hand-crank radio** for emergency alerts
- **Flashlight, first aid kit, extra batteries**
- **Important documents** in a waterproof container (insurance policies, IDs, bank records)
- **Medications:** At least a seven-day supply of prescriptions
- **Plan your evacuation route** and establish family meeting points

Full checklist at [ready.gov/kit](https://www.ready.gov/kit).

### Sign Up for Weather Alerts

- **Wireless Emergency Alerts** are automatic on most smartphones
- **NOAA Weather Radio** — dedicated receivers available at most electronics stores
- **Weather.gov** ([weather.gov](https://www.weather.gov)) — Official NWS forecasts and warnings
- **Ready.gov alerts** ([ready.gov/alerts](https://www.ready.gov/alerts)) — How to receive emergency alerts

### Build Community Resilience
- **Map your neighborhood's vulnerable residents** — Elderly, disabled, and non-English-speaking neighbors need help during disasters. Create a neighborhood contact list.
- **Organize a preparedness block party** — FEMA's Ready Campaign has free templates ([ready.gov](https://www.ready.gov))
- **Push your city for cooling centers, warming shelters, and emergency water distribution plans**
- **Support urban tree planting** — Trees reduce heat island effects by 10-15°F and reduce flood risk

### Become a Weather Spotter
- **SKYWARN** ([weather.gov/skywarn](https://www.weather.gov/skywarn)) — Free NWS training to become a storm spotter. Your reports improve forecasts and save lives.
- **CoCoRaHS** ([cocorahs.org](https://www.cocorahs.org)) — Report daily precipitation measurements from your backyard
- **mPING** ([mping.ou.edu](https://mping.ou.edu)) — NOAA app to report what weather is actually happening at your location in real-time
