# The Food System and the Environment

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Agriculture accounts for **10.5% of US greenhouse gas emissions**, **80–90% of US consumptive freshwater use**, and occupies **roughly half of all US land**. Industrial livestock operations are a leading source of water and air pollution, disproportionately sited near communities of color. A single pound of beef requires an average of **1,800 gallons of water** to produce. The Farm Bill — a roughly **$1.5 trillion, 10-year package** — largely subsidizes commodity crops and industrial agriculture through price supports and crop insurance, with conservation programs receiving a fraction of that spending. **30–40% of the US food supply** is wasted, making food the single largest category in landfills. The next Farm Bill is a critical opportunity for reform.

## Facts

### [+] Agriculture's Climate Footprint

EPA's 2024 Inventory of US Greenhouse Gas Emissions and Sinks found that agriculture accounted for **10.5%** of all US greenhouse gas emissions in 2022, totaling **663.6 million metric tons CO2-equivalent**. The breakdown:

- **46.6%** from nitrous oxide (primarily fertilizer application and manure)
- **41.7%** from methane (primarily enteric fermentation from livestock and manure management)
- **11.6%** from carbon dioxide

Globally, agriculture and land use together account for approximately **24% of all greenhouse gas emissions**. [source: EPA GHG Inventory 2024, Chapter 5](https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2024-04/us-ghg-inventory-2024-chapter-5-agriculture.pdf) | [source: EWG Agriculture and GHG Emissions](https://www.ewg.org/news-insights/news/2024/10/will-agriculture-be-americas-leading-source-greenhouse-gas-emissions)

### Water: Agriculture's Largest User

USGS data consistently show that agriculture — primarily irrigation — accounts for **80–90% of all consumptive freshwater use** in the United States. In many western states, this figure exceeds **90%**. Irrigation is among the largest freshwater withdrawals in the US, second only to thermoelectric power plant cooling in total withdrawals, but first by far in **consumptive** use (water that doesn't return to waterways).

The consequence: rivers in the West are over-appropriated, aquifers like the Ogallala (which underlies 8 Great Plains states) are being drawn down far faster than natural recharge rates, and many rivers no longer reach the ocean in dry years. [source: USGS Irrigation Water Use](https://www.usgs.gov/water-science-school/science/irrigation-water-use) | [source: USDA Water Use in Agriculture](https://ers.usda.gov/sites/default/files/_laserfiche/publications/41964/30286_wateruse.pdf)

### Land Use: Half of America

Agriculture occupies approximately **50% of all land in the contiguous United States**. This makes farming and ranching the single largest land use category — and the leading driver of habitat loss and biodiversity decline nationwide. Land conversion for agriculture is responsible for the loss of grasslands, wetlands, and forests that would otherwise sequester carbon and provide wildlife habitat.

### [+] The Water Footprint of Meat

Producing animal protein requires dramatically more water than plant protein. Using Water Footprint Network methodology:

| Food | Water per pound |
|------|----------------|
| Beef | ~1,800 gallons |
| Pork | ~576 gallons |
| Chicken | ~468 gallons |
| Tofu | ~302 gallons |
| Wheat | ~138 gallons |
| Rice | ~299 gallons |
| Vegetables | 39–100 gallons |

The **1,800 gallons per pound of beef** figure (Water Footprint Network) represents an average across production systems. Of that water, approximately 98% goes to producing the feed crops cattle consume over their lifetime. [source: Water Footprint Calculator](https://watercalculator.org/footprint/water-footprint-beef-industrial-pasture/)

### [+] The Farm Bill: Subsidizing the Status Quo

The Farm Bill is the primary vehicle for US agricultural policy, reauthorized roughly every five years. The current legislative cycle has been contentious, with the 2018 Farm Bill extended through 2024. The projected 10-year package is approximately **$1.51 trillion**.

**How the money is allocated:**
- **Nutrition programs (SNAP):** Approximately 74% of total spending
- **Commodity support programs** (ARC, PLC, Dairy Margin Coverage, livestock disaster): Projected **$69 billion over 2024–2033**
- **Conservation programs:** Approximately **7% of non-nutrition mandatory spending**
- **Crop insurance:** A major and growing line item, with subsidies primarily benefiting large commodity producers

Conservation programs are chronically **oversubscribed** — farmers who want to participate in programs like EQIP (Environmental Quality Incentives Program) and CSP (Conservation Stewardship Program) often cannot get funding. The Inflation Reduction Act added $19.5 billion in climate-smart conservation funding, but much of that has been frozen or redirected under the current administration.

[source: USDA ERS Farm Bill Spending](https://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/farm-economy/farm-commodity-policy/farm-bill-spending) | [source: National Sustainable Agriculture Coalition Farm Bill](https://sustainableagriculture.net/our-work/campaigns/fbcampaign/what-is-the-farm-bill/)

### [+] Factory Farms, Pollution, and Environmental Justice

The United States has approximately **19,000 large confined animal feeding operations (CAFOs)** — industrial facilities housing thousands to hundreds of thousands of animals. CAFOs are:

**Environmental hazards:**
- Generate massive volumes of animal waste stored in open-air "lagoons" that can leak, overflow, and emit ammonia and hydrogen sulfide
- CAFOs are a leading source of nutrient pollution in US waterways, driving hypoxic "dead zones" including the Gulf of Mexico dead zone (approximately 6,000 square miles annually)
- A leading source of agricultural antibiotic use, contributing to antibiotic-resistant bacteria

**Disproportionately sited near communities of color:**
- A 2014 North Carolina study found that African American, Latino, and Native American communities were **1.5 times more likely** to live near a CAFO than white communities
- In Tulare County, CA, over **93% of dairy CAFOs** pose water quality threats in a community that is **67% Hispanic/Latino**
- Communities near hog CAFOs in North Carolina have higher rates of infant mortality, anemia, kidney disease, and respiratory illness

[source: Friends of the Earth - Factory Farms and Environmental Justice](https://foe.org/projects/factory-farms-environmental-justice/) | [source: Civil Eats - CAFO Communities of Color](https://civileats.com/2022/10/26/can-a-court-compel-the-epa-to-protect-communities-of-color-from-cafo-pollution/)

### [+] Soil Degradation

Industrial monoculture farming depletes topsoil through erosion, compaction, and the loss of organic matter. USDA estimates the US loses **1.7 billion tons of topsoil per year** to erosion. Healthy topsoil is not a renewable resource on human timescales — it takes approximately **500 years** to form one inch of topsoil naturally.

Soil health matters for climate: healthy soil can sequester significant quantities of carbon. Degraded soil does not. Practices like cover cropping, reduced tillage, and crop rotation can restore soil health and sequester carbon while maintaining yields.

### [+] Pesticides: The Dirty Dozen

The Environmental Working Group publishes annual "Dirty Dozen" and "Clean Fifteen" lists identifying which fruits and vegetables have the highest and lowest pesticide residues. The **2024 Dirty Dozen** (highest pesticide residues) includes strawberries, spinach, kale/collard/mustard greens, peaches, pears, nectarines, apples, grapes, bell/hot peppers, cherries, blueberries, and green beans.

Choosing organic for Dirty Dozen produce reduces exposure significantly. The **Clean Fifteen** (lowest pesticide residues) includes avocados, sweet corn, pineapples, onions, papaya, asparagus, frozen peas, honeydew, kiwi, cabbage, mushrooms, mangoes, sweet potatoes, watermelon, and carrots. [source: EWG Dirty Dozen 2024](https://www.ewg.org/foodnews/dirty-dozen.php)

### [+] Food Waste: 30–40% of the Food Supply

USDA estimates that **30–40% of the US food supply** is wasted, totaling approximately **133 billion pounds** and **$161 billion** annually at the retail and consumer levels. The average American family of four loses **$1,500 per year** to uneaten food.

At the global scale, the UN Food and Agriculture Organization estimates **1.3 billion tons** of food are wasted annually. The environmental footprint of that wasted food — the water, land, and energy used to grow it — is a major and largely invisible driver of environmental degradation. [source: USDA Food Loss and Waste](https://www.usda.gov/foodwaste/faqs)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- US agricultural policy, through the Farm Bill, has for decades prioritized large-scale commodity production (corn, soybeans, wheat, cotton) over environmental sustainability, small farms, and food system resilience.
- The environmental costs of industrial agriculture — water pollution, soil degradation, habitat loss, greenhouse gas emissions — are largely **externalized**: they don't show up in the price of food, but they are paid by communities, ecosystems, and future generations.
- Factory farms impose their heaviest costs on low-income communities and communities of color who have the least political power to oppose them.
- Reforming the Farm Bill to redirect even a fraction of commodity subsidies toward conservation programs would be one of the most impactful environmental policy levers available.
- Individual dietary choices — especially reducing beef consumption — have measurable impact on water use and greenhouse gas emissions, but systemic change requires policy reform.

### Who's Affected

- **Rural communities near CAFOs:** Air and water pollution, health impacts, reduced property values
- **Communities of color:** Disproportionately sited near industrial animal operations
- **Farmers:** Family farms compete against heavily subsidized industrial operations; conservation-minded farmers are often shut out of oversubscribed programs
- **Downstream communities:** Nutrient runoff from farms pollutes rivers, lakes, and coastal fisheries
- **Taxpayers:** Subsidize commodity agriculture and crop insurance, including for farms that repeatedly flood or experience drought
- **Everyone who eats:** Food system resilience, food safety, and food access are all downstream of agricultural policy

### Comment on CAFO Permits in Your State

When new or expanding CAFOs apply for water discharge permits, there is usually a public comment period. To participate:
1. Search "[your state] CAFO permit public comment" or contact your state environmental agency
2. Organizations like Earthjustice and the Environmental Integrity Project track open comment periods
3. Comments expressing concern about water quality and community health are entered into the public record and must be responded to

### Support Local Farmers Markets and CSAs

- Find farmers markets near you at [ams.usda.gov/local-food-directories](https://www.ams.usda.gov/local-food-directories)
- Join a Community Supported Agriculture (CSA) share from a local farm
- Buying directly from small farms keeps more money with the farmer and often supports more sustainable practices

### Reduce Beef Consumption

Beef production requires far more land, water, and generates more greenhouse gases per calorie than any other common food. Even one or two fewer beef meals per week has a measurable impact:
- Swap beef for chicken, pork, legumes, or plant proteins
- When you do eat beef, look for grass-fed or regeneratively raised options
- Use [EWG's Meat Eater's Guide](https://www.ewg.org/meateatersguide/) to compare environmental impacts

### Grow Food at Home or in a Community Garden

- Visit [communitygarden.org](https://www.communitygarden.org) to find community gardens
- Even a small container garden produces food with zero transportation emissions
- Composting kitchen scraps for your garden closes the waste loop

### Check EWG's Dirty Dozen Before Shopping

- The [EWG Dirty Dozen list](https://www.ewg.org/foodnews/dirty-dozen.php) identifies produce with the highest pesticide residues
- Prioritizing organic for these items reduces exposure while keeping costs manageable
- The Clean Fifteen list identifies low-residue produce where conventional is fine

- **Community Food Systems** via **USDA Agricultural Marketing Service** ([ams.usda.gov](https://www.ams.usda.gov)) — Grants and resources for local food systems and farmers markets

### Push for State CAFO Regulations

Many states have authority to regulate CAFOs more strictly than federal minimums. Contact your state environmental agency and state legislators asking for:
- Mandatory CAFO siting buffers from schools, homes, and waterways
- Independent water quality testing near CAFOs
- Stricter manure management requirements
- Environmental justice review for new CAFO permits

### Advocate at Your School or Workplace

- Push for meatless days in school cafeteria menus
- Advocate for local food purchasing at your institution
- Support food rescue programs that redirect surplus food to food banks
