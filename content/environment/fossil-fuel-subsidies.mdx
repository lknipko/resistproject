# Fossil Fuel Subsidies

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The U.S. federal government provides an estimated **$35 billion per year** in direct subsidies to the fossil fuel industry through tax breaks, below-market royalty rates, and direct spending. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (2025) added nearly **$40 billion in new subsidies** over 10 years. When including externalized costs like health damages and climate impacts, the IMF estimates U.S. fossil fuel subsidies exceed **$600 billion annually**. Meanwhile, the same legislation cut clean energy tax credits from the Inflation Reduction Act. The five largest oil companies earned over $100 billion in profits in 2024 alone.

These subsidies flow to one of the most profitable industries in history, distort energy markets, and lock in fossil fuel dependence at the expense of taxpayers and the climate.

## Facts

### Key Points

- The U.S. provides roughly **$35 billion/year** in federal fossil fuel subsidies, more than double the amount in 2017
- The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (July 2025) added **$39.7 billion** in new fossil fuel subsidies over the next decade
- The same bill **cut clean energy tax credits** from the Inflation Reduction Act
- Federal oil and gas royalty rates were **rolled back to 12.5%** (set in 1920) from the IRA's 16.67% rate
- Coal royalty rates on federal land were **slashed from 12.5% to 7%** through 2034
- The **End Polluter Welfare Act of 2025** (Sanders/Omar) would eliminate $190 billion in subsidies over 10 years but has not advanced
- The IMF estimates global fossil fuel subsidies (including externalized costs) at **$7.4 trillion** annually

[source: Oil Change International - U.S. Fossil Fuel Subsidies](https://oilchange.org/news/us-fossil-fuel-subsidies/) | [source: Yale E360 - Spending Bill Fossil Fuel Subsidies](https://e360.yale.edu/digest/republican-spending-bill-fossil-fuel-subsidies)

### [+] Direct Tax Subsidies

These are explicit tax provisions that reduce the fossil fuel industry's tax burden. Most have been in the tax code for decades, some for over a century.

| Provision | Description | Est. Annual Cost |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Intangible Drilling Costs (IDC) Expensing** | Oil/gas companies immediately deduct drilling labor, fuel, site prep, and supplies rather than spreading costs over asset life. In place since 1913. Independent producers deduct 100%; majors deduct 70%. | ~$2.4 billion |
| **Percentage Depletion Allowance** | Oil/gas producers deduct 15% of gross income from wells, even after recovering all capital costs. Other industries use cost depletion, which stops when costs are recovered. | ~$1.2 billion |
| **Publicly Traded Partnership Exception** | Oil and gas partnerships (MLPs) avoid corporate income tax while trading on public stock exchanges. Other industries must incorporate to go public. | ~$2.7 billion |
| **Last-In, First-Out (LIFO) Accounting** | Fossil fuel companies value inventory using LIFO, reducing taxable income during price increases. | ~$1.7 billion |
| **Foreign Tax Credit for Royalties** | Companies recharacterize foreign royalty payments as income taxes to claim U.S. foreign tax credits, effectively making taxpayers subsidize overseas extraction. | ~$3.0 billion |
| **Carbon Capture Tax Credits (45Q)** | Expanded under the OBBBA to send over $1.4 billion/year to oil/gas companies for enhanced oil recovery labeled as "carbon capture." | ~$1.4 billion |
| **Domestic Manufacturing Deduction (Section 199)** | Fossil fuel companies claim a manufacturing deduction despite extracting raw materials. | ~$1.0 billion |

Total direct tax subsidies: estimated **$13-15 billion/year** from these provisions alone, with additional state-level subsidies bringing totals higher.

[source: EESI Fact Sheet - Fossil Fuel Subsidies](https://www.eesi.org/papers/view/fact-sheet-fossil-fuel-subsidies-a-closer-look-at-tax-breaks-and-societal-costs) | [source: Tax Policy Center - Energy Tax Incentives](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/what-tax-incentives-encourage-energy-production-fossil-fuels)

### [+] Below-Market Federal Leasing

The federal government leases public lands and waters for oil, gas, and coal extraction at rates far below market value.

**Oil and Gas:**
- The federal onshore royalty rate was set at **12.5% in 1920** and went unchanged for 102 years
- The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) raised it to **16.67%** and increased minimum bids
- The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (2025) **reverted the rate to 12.5%** for all new leases
- By comparison, Texas charges **25%** and North Dakota charges **18.75%** on state lands
- Federal minimum lease bids are just **$2/acre** (far below fair market value)

**Coal:**
- The OBBBA reduced federal coal royalties from **12.5% to 7%** through September 2034
- Congress mandated BLM to make **4 million acres** of public land available for coal leasing
- In Wyoming alone, the royalty cut will reduce federal revenue by **~$100 million/year**

**Revenue losses:** Taxpayers for Common Sense estimates the federal government loses **billions annually** by charging below-market rates for extraction on public lands.

[source: Taxpayers for Common Sense - Federal Leasing Revenue Loss](https://www.taxpayer.net/by-the-numbers-federal-onshore-oil-and-gas-leasing-revenue-loss/) | [source: BLM - One Big Beautiful Bill Act](https://www.blm.gov/press-release/interior-advances-energy-dominance-through-one-big-beautiful-bill-act)

### [+] Indirect Subsidies & Externalized Costs

Beyond direct tax breaks and below-market leasing, fossil fuels receive enormous implicit subsidies through costs imposed on society but not paid by the industry.

**The IMF's comprehensive estimate (2025 update):**
- Global fossil fuel subsidies: **$7.4 trillion/year** (explicit + implicit)
- Explicit subsidies (direct government spending): **$725 billion** globally
- Implicit subsidies (unpriced externalities): **$6.7 trillion** globally
- The U.S. is the **second-largest subsidizer** globally, behind China
- The U.S. implicit subsidy estimate exceeds **$600 billion/year**

**What "implicit subsidies" include:**
- **Air pollution health costs:** PM2.5 and other emissions cause ~100,000-200,000 premature American deaths annually, costing hundreds of billions in healthcare, lost productivity, and early death
- **Climate change damages:** Each ton of CO2 causes estimated damages of $50-200+ (social cost of carbon), which fossil fuel producers do not pay
- **Water contamination:** Groundwater and surface water pollution from extraction, processing, and spills
- **Infrastructure damage:** Roads, bridges, and rail damaged by extraction and transport activity
- **Military spending:** Significant U.S. military expenditure protects global oil supply chains

[source: IMF - Fossil Fuel Subsidies 2025 Update](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/wp/issues/2025/12/20/underpriced-and-overused-fossil-fuel-subsidies-data-2025-update-572729) | [source: IMF - Fossil Fuel Subsidies Surged to $7 Trillion](https://www.imf.org/en/blogs/articles/2023/08/24/fossil-fuel-subsidies-surged-to-record-7-trillion)

### [+] Who Benefits

Fossil fuel subsidies disproportionately flow to the largest and most profitable corporations.

**Top beneficiaries include:**
- **ExxonMobil** -- Earned $33.7 billion profit in 2024. Extracts 31% of oil/gas domestically but pays only ~10% of total taxes in the U.S. Reported paying nearly 5x as much tax to the UAE as to the U.S. in 2023-2024.
- **Chevron** -- Enjoys single-digit effective tax rates, lower than many nurses or firefighters pay
- **ConocoPhillips, Shell, BP** -- Together with ExxonMobil and Chevron, the five largest oil companies earned over **$100 billion in combined profits** in 2024
- **Independent producers** -- Benefit most from percentage depletion (which is limited to independent producers) and IDC expensing (100% deduction vs. 70% for majors)

**Industry lobbying:**
- The oil and gas industry spent **$124.4 million** on federal lobbying in 2024
- Industry political contributions heavily favor candidates who oppose subsidy reform

[source: Earth Track - ExxonMobil Subsidies](https://www.earthtrack.net/document/exxonmobil%E2%80%99s-real-quid-pro-quo-government) | [source: FracTracker - Fossil Fuel Subsidy Myth](https://www.fractracker.org/2025/03/fossil-fuel-subsidies-free-market-myth/)

### [+] International Comparison

The United States is an outlier among wealthy nations in the scale of its fossil fuel subsidies.

| Country/Region | Annual Direct Subsidies | Key Context |
|---------------|------------------------|-------------|
| **United States** | ~$35 billion | Second-largest global subsidizer; OBBBA added $40B more over 10 years |
| **China** | ~$50 billion | Largest subsidizer, but also largest renewable energy investor |
| **European Union** | Varies by member | EU committed to phasing out fossil fuel subsidies by 2025 (incomplete) |
| **Canada** | ~$4.8 billion CAD | Pledged phase-out by 2023, extended to 2025 |
| **G20 Commitments** | N/A | Pledged in 2009 to phase out "inefficient" fossil fuel subsidies; little progress |

The U.S. is the only G7 nation to **increase** fossil fuel subsidies in 2025 while simultaneously cutting clean energy incentives.

[source: Our World in Data - Fossil Fuel Subsidies](https://ourworldindata.org/how-much-subsidies-fossil-fuels) | [source: IEA Fossil Fuel Subsidies Database](https://www.iea.org/data-and-statistics/data-product/fossil-fuel-subsidies-database)

### [+] Legislative History

Efforts to end fossil fuel subsidies have been introduced repeatedly in Congress but have never succeeded.

| Year | Legislation | Sponsor | Outcome |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|
| 2012 | End Polluter Welfare Act (original) | Sen. Sanders | Did not advance |
| 2015 | End Polluter Welfare Act (reintro) | Sen. Sanders | Did not advance |
| 2021 | End Polluter Welfare Act | Sen. Sanders / Rep. Omar | Did not advance |
| 2022 | Inflation Reduction Act | Sen. Manchin / Sen. Schumer | Passed -- raised royalty rates, did NOT end tax subsidies |
| 2025 | End Oil and Gas Tax Subsidies Act (H.R.383) | House Democrats | Introduced 119th Congress |
| 2025 | End Polluter Welfare Act (S.2444 / H.R.4714) | Sen. Sanders / Rep. Omar | Introduced 119th Congress; would save $190B over 10 years |
| 2025 | One Big Beautiful Bill Act | House Republicans | **Passed** -- added $40B in new fossil fuel subsidies |

The End Polluter Welfare Act of 2025 (S.2444) would eliminate all fossil fuel tax preferences, update below-market royalty rates, prohibit taxpayer-funded fossil fuel R&D, recoup offshore drilling royalties, and end support for international oil/gas/coal projects. It is co-sponsored by Senators Warren, Merkley, Welch, Van Hollen, Markey, Booker, and 20+ House members.

[source: S.2444 - End Polluter Welfare Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2444) | [source: H.R.383 - End Oil and Gas Tax Subsidies Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/383/text) | [source: Sen. Sanders - End Polluter Welfare Act](https://www.sanders.senate.gov/press-releases/end-polluter-welfare-act/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- **The playing field is rigged.** Fossil fuels receive over a century of embedded tax advantages, while clean energy tax credits from the IRA are being phased out. This is not a "free market" -- it is a market systematically tilted toward fossil fuels by government policy.
- **Taxpayers pay twice.** Once through direct subsidies to the industry, and again through the health and climate costs the industry externalizes onto the public.
- **Subsidies lock in fossil fuel dependence.** By making fossil fuels artificially cheap, subsidies slow the transition to clean energy and make it harder for renewables to compete, even when renewables are cheaper on an unsubsidized basis.
- **The OBBBA represents a major escalation.** Adding $40 billion in new subsidies while cutting clean energy incentives reverses years of incremental progress and signals long-term commitment to fossil fuel infrastructure.
- **Reform is politically viable but blocked.** The End Polluter Welfare Act would save taxpayers $190 billion over 10 years, but the fossil fuel industry's lobbying prevents it from advancing.

### Who Pays

- **All taxpayers** fund direct subsidies through reduced federal revenue ($35+ billion/year)
- **Communities near extraction sites** bear pollution, water contamination, and health impacts
- **Environmental justice communities** -- low-income and communities of color are disproportionately located near refineries, pipelines, and fossil fuel infrastructure
- **Future generations** bear the costs of climate change driven by subsidized fossil fuel production
- **Renewable energy companies and workers** face an unlevel playing field created by preferential fossil fuel tax treatment
- **State and local governments** lose revenue from below-market federal leasing rates, while bearing infrastructure and health costs
