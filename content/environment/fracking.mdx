# Fracking: Risks & Regulation

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Hydraulic fracturing ("fracking") injects millions of gallons of chemical-laden water underground at extreme pressure to crack rock and release oil and gas. Since 2005, the "Halliburton Loophole" has exempted fracking from the Safe Drinking Water Act, meaning EPA cannot regulate what chemicals are injected underground or require monitoring of drinking water impacts. From 2014 to 2021, fracking companies used more than 282 million pounds of hazardous chemicals with no federal oversight. 62-73% of fracking jobs each year use at least one chemical categorized as harmful under the Safe Drinking Water Act.

EPA's own 2016 study found scientific evidence that fracking can and does contaminate drinking water. Fracking wastewater injection has triggered thousands of earthquakes in Oklahoma and other states. Health studies document elevated cancer risk, respiratory illness, and birth defects in communities near fracking operations. Yet the industry remains largely exempt from the laws designed to protect public health. Recent state-level fights split in opposite directions: in July 2026, a Republican effort to **overturn the Delaware River Basin's long-standing fracking ban** via a federal water bill **failed**, preserving protections for the four-state watershed — but an Ohio commission **approved fracking beneath 10,000+ acres of state land** in June over activists' objections.

## Facts

### How Fracking Works

Hydraulic fracturing involves:

1. **Drilling** a well vertically (often 5,000-10,000+ feet deep), then horizontally through the target rock formation
2. **Injecting** millions of gallons of fluid (water mixed with sand and chemicals) at pressures of 500-20,000+ psi
3. **Fracturing** the rock to create pathways for oil or gas to flow to the wellbore
4. **Recovering** a fraction of the injected fluid as "flowback" -- the rest remains underground
5. **Disposing** of wastewater, often through deep injection wells

Each well can require 1.5 to 16 million gallons of water, with per-well water usage increasing up to 770% since 2011 as wells grow longer and target tighter formations. Over 200,000 fracking jobs have been documented in the FracFocus disclosure system alone.

[source: EPA - Hydraulic Fracturing Study](https://www.epa.gov/hfstudy) | [source: NIEHS - Hydraulic Fracturing & Health](https://www.niehs.nih.gov/health/topics/agents/fracking)

### [+] The Halliburton Loophole

The 2005 Energy Policy Act exempted hydraulic fracturing from regulation under the Safe Drinking Water Act's Underground Injection Control (UIC) program. The provision was championed by then-Vice President Dick Cheney, former CEO of Halliburton -- the company that invented modern fracking.

**What the exemption means in practice:**
- EPA **cannot regulate** the chemical composition of fracking fluids injected underground
- EPA **cannot require** groundwater monitoring near fracking operations
- EPA **cannot require** disclosure of chemicals used (some states require partial disclosure, but trade secrets are claimed in over 80% of FracFocus disclosures)
- Fracking is also exempt from portions of the **Clean Air Act**, **Clean Water Act**, and **CERCLA** (Superfund law)

From 2014 through 2021, 62% to 73% of reported fracking jobs each year used at least one chemical regulated under the Safe Drinking Water Act. A total of more than 282 million pounds of hazardous chemicals were injected into underground sources of drinking water with no federal oversight.

[source: Earthworks - The Halliburton Loophole](https://earthworks.org/issues/inadequate_regulation_of_hydraulic_fracturing/) | [source: PMC - Outcomes of the Halliburton Loophole](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10187986/)

### [+] Water Contamination Evidence

**EPA's 2016 Assessment:**
EPA's final report -- integrating approximately 1,200 cited sources -- concluded there is "scientific evidence that hydraulic fracturing activities can impact drinking water resources under some circumstances." The agency documented impacts at every stage of the fracking water cycle, ranging from temporary water quality changes to contamination that made private drinking water wells unusable. EPA reported 457 spills in 11 states between 2006 and 2012, with 324 reaching soil, surface water, or groundwater.

[source: EPA - Final Hydraulic Fracturing Assessment](https://cfpub.epa.gov/ncea/hfstudy/recordisplay.cfm?deid=332990)

**Pavillion, Wyoming:**
A Stanford University study confirmed that fracking contaminated groundwater in Pavillion, Wyoming, where residents had reported changes in water taste, smell, and color. Researchers found fracking chemicals including methanol, ethanol, and diesel compounds in wells near fracking operations.

[source: Stanford - Pavillion Fracking Water Study](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2016/03/pavillion-fracking-water-032916)

**Dimock, Pennsylvania:**
In 2009, methane from fracking operations contaminated well water in Dimock, PA, with at least one water well exploding. The state ordered the drilling company to provide replacement water supplies. This case became one of the most documented examples of fracking-related water contamination in the country.

**Mechanisms of contamination:**
- Well casing failures allowing methane and fluids to migrate into aquifers
- Surface spills of fracking fluids and wastewater
- Underground migration of fracking fluids through fractures
- Improper disposal of flowback water
- A high percentage of injected fluid is never recovered and its fate underground is unknown

[source: EPA - Questions and Answers on Fracking Study](https://www.epa.gov/hfstudy/questions-and-answers-about-epas-hydraulic-fracturing-drinking-water-assessment)

### [+] Induced Earthquakes

Wastewater disposal from fracking operations has caused a dramatic increase in earthquakes across the central United States:

- **Oklahoma** went from an average of fewer than 2 earthquakes of magnitude 3.0+ per year (1978-2008) to **hundreds per year** in 2014-2017
- The USGS confirmed that 98% of Oklahoma's induced earthquakes are caused by wastewater disposal wells, with 2% linked directly to fracking operations
- Wastewater injected underground changes pore pressure in rock formations, which can trigger slip on pre-existing faults
- Even when injection stops, earthquake activity can persist for years as pressure continues to diffuse through rock
- The 2011 Prague, Oklahoma earthquake (magnitude 5.7) damaged homes and was linked to wastewater injection

Other states with documented induced seismicity from oil and gas wastewater injection include Kansas, Texas, Ohio, Arkansas, and Colorado.

[source: USGS - Does Fracking Cause Earthquakes?](https://www.usgs.gov/faqs/does-fracking-cause-earthquakes) | [source: USGS - Oklahoma Earthquakes](https://www.usgs.gov/faqs/oklahoma-has-had-a-surge-earthquakes-2009-are-they-due-fracking)

### [+] Air Quality and Health Impacts

Fracking operations release a mix of air pollutants:

- **Methane** -- a greenhouse gas 80x more potent than CO2 over 20 years. Fracking has likely caused 40% of the total increase in atmospheric methane this century
- **Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs)** -- benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, xylenes; cause respiratory illness, nervous system damage, and cancer
- **Particulate matter (PM2.5)** -- from diesel equipment, dust, and flaring
- **Hydrogen sulfide** -- toxic gas causing respiratory failure at high concentrations
- **Ground-level ozone** -- formed when VOCs react with sunlight; worsens asthma and lung disease

**Health studies document:**
- Increased risk of childhood cancer near fracking sites in Pennsylvania
- Higher rates of respiratory illness, including asthma exacerbation
- Elevated risk of preterm birth and low birth weight
- Neurological symptoms, nosebleeds, and skin rashes in nearby residents
- Cardiovascular impacts from chronic exposure to air pollution

[source: FracTracker - Pennsylvania Health Study](https://www.fractracker.org/2023/08/pennsylvania-health-study-results/) | [source: NRDC - Fracking Fumes](https://www.nrdc.org/sites/default/files/fracking-air-pollution-IB.pdf)

### [+] Setback Distances

How close fracking can get to homes, schools, and hospitals varies wildly by state:

- **Expert recommendation:** At least 1,320 feet (quarter mile) from any occupied building; 6,600 feet from schools, daycares, hospitals, and nursing homes
- **Reality in most states:** Setbacks range from 150 to 1,000 feet -- far below health-protective levels
- **Colorado** adopted a 2,000-foot setback in 2019 after a ballot initiative, but enforcement has been inconsistent
- Some states have **no mandatory setback** at all

16 out of 18 experts consulted in a peer-reviewed study concluded that setbacks should be at least a quarter mile to protect public health.

[source: Environmental Health Project - Setback Distances](https://www.environmentalhealthproject.org/post/renewing-the-call-for-increased-setback-distances) | [source: EHN - Pennsylvania Fracking Too Close](https://www.ehn.org/pennsylvania-fracking-too-close-to-homes)

### [+] State Bans and Moratoriums

Six states have banned fracking:

| State | Year | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| **Vermont** | 2012 | First state to ban; no known gas reserves |
| **New York** | 2014 (moratorium), 2020 (ban) | Expanded in 2024 to include liquid CO2-based extraction |
| **Maryland** | 2017 | Second state with proven reserves to ban |
| **Washington** | 2019 | Statewide ban |
| **Oregon** | 2019 | Moratorium (expired 2025) |
| **California** | 2024 | Banned new permits; existing wells can continue |

Meanwhile, major fracking states -- Texas, Pennsylvania, Ohio, North Dakota, Oklahoma, and Colorado -- continue to expand operations.

[source: Climate XChange - Fracking Bans Policy Explainer](https://climate-xchange.org/2024/08/policy-explainer-drilling-down-on-state-efforts-to-ban-fracking/) | [source: FracTracker - How Fracking Bans Protect Communities](https://www.fractracker.org/2024/08/fracking-bans/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The Halliburton Loophole means there is **no federal cop on the beat** for fracking's impact on drinking water -- communities must rely on state regulators who are often underfunded, understaffed, or politically aligned with the industry
- Without mandatory chemical disclosure, residents near fracking wells often cannot determine what is being injected underground near their homes
- The connection between fracking wastewater and earthquakes is scientifically established, yet regulation remains voluntary in many states
- Health impacts are documented but proving causation is difficult precisely because the Halliburton Loophole prevents the baseline monitoring that would generate the evidence
- This is a circular problem: no monitoring → no data → "insufficient evidence" → no regulation

### Who's Affected

- **17+ million Americans** live within one mile of an active oil or gas well
- **Rural communities** on private wells with no alternative water supply
- **Children and pregnant women** -- the most vulnerable to fracking-related chemicals and air pollution
- **Low-income communities** who cannot afford to move or purchase bottled water
- **Indigenous communities** -- tribal lands in the western U.S. face expanding fracking with limited regulatory authority
- **Communities in earthquake zones** created by wastewater injection -- Oklahoma, Kansas, Texas, Ohio

### Attend State Oil & Gas Commission Hearings

Your state oil and gas commission (or equivalent regulatory body) holds public hearings on:
- New well permits near homes, schools, and water sources
- Setback distance regulations
- Wastewater disposal well permits
- Industry compliance and enforcement

**How to find hearings:**
- Search "[your state] oil and gas commission public hearings" or check your state agency website
- Sign up for email alerts from your state regulatory agency
- Contact your state representative's office for hearing schedules

**How to participate:**
- Public comment periods are usually limited to 3-5 minutes -- prepare a concise, factual statement
- Bring water testing results, health documentation, or seismic data if relevant
- Coordinate with neighbors to present a united community voice

### Request Air Quality Monitoring

If you live near fracking operations:
1. **Contact your state environmental agency** to request ambient air monitoring in your area
2. **Install a PurpleAir sensor** (~$250) to monitor particulate matter near your home
3. **Document air quality events** -- odors, visible emissions, flaring -- with dates, times, and photos
4. **Report violations** to your state air quality agency and EPA's enforcement tip line: [epa.gov/enforcement/report-environmental-violations](https://www.epa.gov/enforcement/report-environmental-violations)

### File a FOIA Request

Get information about fracking operations near you:

1. **Federal FOIA to EPA:** [epa.gov/foia](https://www.epa.gov/foia) -- Request well inspection reports, enforcement actions, and water quality data
2. **Federal FOIA to BLM:** [doi.gov/foia](https://www.doi.gov/foia) -- For fracking on federal and tribal lands
3. **State public records requests** -- Request well permits, chemical disclosures, spill reports, and complaint records from your state oil and gas commission
4. **Useful requests:** Spill reports within X miles of your address, inspection records for specific well pads, air monitoring data near fracking operations

### Document Health Impacts

If you or your family experience health symptoms near fracking operations:
1. **See your doctor** and specifically mention proximity to oil and gas operations
2. **Get baseline blood work** -- request testing for benzene metabolites and heavy metals
3. **Keep a health diary** -- log symptoms, dates, and any correlation with nearby operations (flaring, truck traffic, well completions)
4. **Report to your state health department** -- formal reports create a record that can trigger community health assessments
5. **Connect with** [Physicians for Social Responsibility](https://psr.org) and the [Environmental Health Project](https://www.environmentalhealthproject.org) for guidance

### Support Fracking Setback Measures

If your state is considering setback legislation or ballot measures:
- Testify at legislative hearings with personal experience or health data
- Support ballot initiatives for health-protective setback distances (2,500+ feet minimum)
- Contact your state legislators directly about setback requirements

### Community Organizing

- **Form a local coalition** with neighbors near fracking operations -- shared documentation is more powerful than individual complaints
- **Invite experts to community meetings** -- FracTracker and the Environmental Health Project offer free community presentations
- **Build relationships with local elected officials** -- city councils and county commissions can pass local ordinances on setbacks, road use, and noise
- **Document truck traffic, noise, light pollution, and road damage** -- these "quality of life" impacts often have stronger local legal standing than health claims
- **Connect with state-level coalitions** already fighting for stronger regulation

### Water Testing and Monitoring

- **Get baseline water testing BEFORE fracking begins** near your property -- this is critical for proving contamination later
- **Test quarterly** if fracking is active within 1 mile of your water well
- **Test for:** methane (dissolved gas), BTEX compounds (benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, xylenes), chloride, barium, strontium, and total dissolved solids
- **Use a state-certified lab** -- results from uncertified labs may not be accepted in legal proceedings
- **Keep all lab reports** in a safe place -- they are legal evidence

### Push for State-Level Protection

Even with the Halliburton Loophole in place, states can act:
- **Support state-level fracking bans or moratoriums** -- six states have already banned fracking
- **Advocate for mandatory chemical disclosure** without trade secret exemptions at the state level
- **Push for health-protective setbacks** of at least 2,500 feet from occupied buildings
- **Support state bonding requirements** -- require companies to post bonds large enough to cover well abandonment and remediation costs
- **Advocate for water well testing programs** funded by the industry within buffer zones around wells

### Consumer Actions

- **Know your energy sources** -- if your utility generates electricity from natural gas, ask about the source and advocate for renewable alternatives
- **Support renewable energy** in your community through utility programs, community solar, or home solar
- **Reduce natural gas usage** where possible -- electric alternatives for heating, cooking, and water heating reduce demand for fracked gas
- **Invest responsibly** -- check whether your retirement accounts hold oil and gas stocks; consider fossil-free investment options
