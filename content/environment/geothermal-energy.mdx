# Geothermal Energy

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Geothermal energy -- power generated from the Earth's internal heat -- provides reliable, near-zero-carbon baseload electricity with a smaller land footprint than any other energy source. The US has just 4 GW of installed geothermal capacity, but the DOE estimates over 100 GW of potential -- 40 times current capacity. Utah ranks among the top four states for geothermal potential and hosts the nation's premier research site, Utah FORGE, near Milford. Breakthroughs in Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) are making it possible to generate geothermal power nearly anywhere, not just in volcanic regions.

The first commercial-scale EGS power plant is coming online in 2026 in Utah. Geothermal enjoys rare bipartisan support, but funding for research, permitting reform, and workforce development are all at risk.

## Facts

### What Is Geothermal Energy?

The Earth's interior is extremely hot -- temperatures exceed 1,000 degrees F just a few miles below the surface. Geothermal energy taps this heat to generate electricity or directly heat buildings. There are three main technology types:

**Conventional Hydrothermal:** Taps naturally occurring underground reservoirs of hot water and steam. Used commercially since 1960 (The Geysers, California). Limited to areas with specific geological conditions.

**Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS):** Creates engineered reservoirs by injecting water into hot, dry rock and fracturing it to create pathways. This is the breakthrough technology that could make geothermal available almost anywhere. Uses techniques adapted from oil and gas hydraulic fracturing.

**Direct Use and Ground-Source Heat Pumps:** Uses shallow geothermal heat (50-60 degrees F year-round just 10 feet below the surface) for heating and cooling buildings. Available everywhere, not just in geologically active areas.

[source: DOE Geothermal Technologies Office](https://www.energy.gov/hgeo/geothermal/forge) | [source: EIA - Enhanced Geothermal Systems](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67204)

The US is the world's largest producer of geothermal electricity, but geothermal represents less than 0.5% of US electricity generation. Most existing capacity is in California and Nevada. The DOE's GeoVision study found that with technology improvements, geothermal could reach 60 GW by 2050 -- enough to power 60 million homes.

### Utah FORGE: The Nation's Geothermal Laboratory

Utah FORGE (Frontier Observatory for Research in Geothermal Energy) is a dedicated underground field laboratory near Milford in Beaver County, Utah's Renewable Energy Corridor. Managed by the University of Utah's Energy and Geoscience Institute with over $200 million in DOE funding, FORGE is where scientists develop, test, and accelerate EGS breakthroughs.

**Key achievements:**
- Drilled two directional wells to approximately 8,000 feet depth into hot crystalline granite
- Successfully created engineered fracture networks connecting the wells
- Achieved a critical milestone: recovered more than two-thirds of injected water through the fracture system, proving EGS heat extraction is viable
- Generated data used by dozens of research teams worldwide

**Current status:** FORGE funding extends through July 2025, with a proposed extension through 2030 submitted to DOE. The project's continuation is not guaranteed and depends on congressional appropriations.

**Commercial interest:** In September 2023, Fervo Energy leased land adjacent to FORGE and announced plans for Cape Station, a 400 MW EGS power plant beginning operation in 2026.

[source: Utah FORGE](https://utahforge.com/) | [source: DOE - Utah FORGE Breakthrough](https://www.energy.gov/eere/geothermal/articles/utah-forges-literal-breakthrough-enhanced-geothermal-systems-community) | [source: University of Utah - FORGE Milestone](https://attheu.utah.edu/science-technology/supported-with-200-million-from-doe-university-of-utah-research-peo/)

### Utah's Geothermal Resources

Utah's geothermal potential for electricity production ranks among the top four states nationally, behind California, Nevada, and Oregon:

- **Blundell Power Plant** (Roosevelt Hot Springs, Beaver County): Utah's first geothermal plant, operating since 1984. Currently 34 MW capacity.
- **Cove Fort** (Millard/Beaver County): 25 MW geothermal plant
- **Thermo Hot Springs:** 14 MW capacity
- **20-year development potential:** 620 MW -- approximately 12% of Utah's electricity consumption
- Located in Utah's "Renewable Energy Corridor" in the state's southwest

[source: Utah Geological Survey - Geothermal Energy](https://geology.utah.gov/energy-minerals/geothermal/) | [source: DOE - Six Utah Plants](https://www.energy.gov/eere/geothermal/articles/six-utah-plants-help-fuel-rise-geothermal-projects)

### Fervo Energy and Commercial EGS

Fervo Energy is building the world's first commercial-scale Enhanced Geothermal System, demonstrating that EGS can move from laboratory to grid:

- **Cape Station Phase I** (Utah, adjacent to FORGE): 100 MW, scheduled to deliver power to the grid in 2026
- **Cape Station Phase II:** Additional 400 MW planned by 2028
- **Total funding raised:** Approximately $1.5 billion since 2017, including backing from Google
- **$421 million non-recourse project loan** (March 2026) -- a milestone showing lender confidence in EGS as a proven technology
- **Thermal recovery factors:** 50-60%, tripling useful thermal energy compared to conventional approaches
- Recognized as one of TIME's 100 Most Influential Companies of 2025

[source: Fervo Energy - Cape Station](https://fervoenergy.com/fervo-secures-new-financing-to-accelerate-development/) | [source: Nature Reviews - EGS Innovation in 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44359-025-00143-0)

### Cost and Performance

| Metric | Geothermal | Solar | Wind | Natural Gas |
|--------|-----------|-------|------|-------------|
| LCOE ($/kWh) | $0.05-0.10 | $0.02-0.04 | $0.03-0.06 | $0.05-0.08 |
| Capacity factor | ~85% | ~25% | ~35% | ~55% |
| Carbon emissions | Near zero | Zero | Zero | ~450g CO2/kWh |
| Land footprint | Very small | Large | Large | Medium |
| Baseload capable | Yes | No | No | Yes |

Geothermal's higher LCOE is offset by its 85% capacity factor -- it runs 24/7 regardless of weather, time of day, or season. Modern geothermal plants emit less than 5% of the CO2 produced by coal plants. EGS costs are projected to decline 20% by 2035 with new drilling techniques.

The global weighted average LCOE for new geothermal projects fell 22% between 2021 and 2022, to $0.056/kWh.

[source: IRENA - Geothermal LCOE](https://www.thinkgeoenergy.com/irena-reports-22-lower-lcoe-of-geothermal-projects-in-2022/) | [source: SLB - Beyond LCOE for Geothermal](https://www.slb.com/insights/beyond-lcoe-what's-the-true-value-of-geothermal-energy)

### International Success Stories

**Iceland:** Geothermal provides 27% of electricity and 66% of primary energy. Ninety percent of homes are heated by geothermal. Iceland demonstrates what a fully developed geothermal economy looks like -- energy independence, low electricity costs, and a thriving geothermal tourism industry.

**New Zealand:** One of the world's largest geothermal electricity producers, with geothermal providing over 15% of electricity. Pioneered industrial geothermal at Wairakei in 1958.

**Kenya, El Salvador, Philippines, Costa Rica:** All generate more than 15% of electricity from geothermal, proving the technology works across diverse geological and economic contexts.

[source: Government of Iceland - Geothermal](https://www.government.is/topics/business-and-industry/energy/geothermal/) | [source: Wikipedia - Geothermal Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geothermal_power)

### Policy Landscape

Geothermal enjoys rare bipartisan support in Congress, but faces significant policy challenges:

**Current support:**
- DOE announced $171.5 million funding opportunity for next-generation geothermal field tests (February 2026)
- DOE merged geothermal office with fossil energy, creating the Hydrocarbons and Geothermal Energy Office (HGEO)
- Trump administration supports geothermal growth, with 1.2 GW planned by end of term
- 13 states launched a geothermal accelerator program through the National Association of State Energy Officials (December 2025)

**Challenges:**
- Federal permitting for geothermal on public lands takes 4-7 years on average -- far longer than comparable oil and gas permits
- H.R. 5576 (Enhancing Geothermal Production on Federal Lands Act) would streamline exploration permits
- The residential 30% tax credit for ground-source heat pumps expired December 31, 2025 under OBBBA changes
- Commercial geothermal tax credits were largely preserved
- Utah FORGE funding extension through 2030 is pending

[source: Global Energy Monitor - Geothermal Policy](https://globalenergymonitor.org/report/trump-administrations-policies-support-rapid-growth-of-geothermal-power-in-the-united-states-with-1-2-gw-planned-by-end-of-term/) | [source: Clean Air Task Force - Geothermal Permitting](https://www.catf.us/2025/12/thoughtful-permitting-reform-can-help-expand-next-generation-geothermal/) | [source: Foley Hoag - Federal Geothermal Support](https://foleyhoag.com/news-and-insights/blogs/energy-and-climate-counsel/2026/march/new-daylight-for-geothermal-how-federal-support-and-bipartisan-permitting-reform-are-fueling-a-geot/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Geothermal is the only renewable energy source that provides 24/7 baseload power comparable to fossil fuels
- EGS technology breakthroughs mean geothermal is no longer limited to volcanic regions -- it could work almost anywhere
- Utah is at the center of the geothermal revolution, with FORGE, Fervo's Cape Station, and existing plants
- The combination of bipartisan political support and commercial momentum creates a rare window of opportunity
- However, slow federal permitting, funding uncertainty, and the expiration of residential tax credits are headwinds
- Geothermal workforce development is critical -- the industry needs trained drillers, engineers, and technicians

### Who's Affected

- **Utah communities:** Direct economic benefits from FORGE, Cape Station, and existing geothermal plants in Beaver and Millard Counties
- **Energy consumers:** Geothermal baseload power stabilizes grids and reduces dependence on volatile natural gas prices
- **Oil and gas workers:** Geothermal drilling uses similar skills, offering a transition pathway for fossil fuel workers
- **Rural communities:** Geothermal plants bring jobs and tax revenue to rural areas near geothermal resources
- **Homeowners:** Ground-source heat pumps can cut heating/cooling costs by 50% but face high upfront costs
- **Ratepayers nationally:** Diversifying the energy mix with geothermal reduces exposure to fuel price shocks

### Engage With Your Utility's Energy Planning (30 minutes)

Electric utilities periodically file Integrated Resource Plans (IRPs) that determine the energy mix for the next 10-20 years. These plans are open for public comment:

1. **Find your utility's IRP:** Search "[your utility name] integrated resource plan" or check your state public utility commission's website
2. **Submit a comment** asking the utility to evaluate geothermal baseload power as an alternative to natural gas
3. **Attend public hearings** on energy planning -- these shape billions of dollars in infrastructure investment
4. **In Utah:** Rocky Mountain Power (PacifiCorp) files IRPs with the Utah Public Service Commission. Comment at [psc.utah.gov](https://psc.utah.gov)

### Explore Ground-Source Heat Pumps for Your Home

Ground-source (geothermal) heat pumps use the Earth's constant underground temperature to heat and cool your home with 50-70% less energy than conventional HVAC:

- **How it works:** A loop of pipes buried underground circulates fluid that absorbs heat in winter and deposits heat in summer
- **Cost:** $15,000-$40,000 installed (horizontal loops are less expensive than vertical)
- **Savings:** $800-$2,000/year in energy costs; 50% less heating energy, 50% less cooling energy
- **Payback:** 5-15 years depending on climate, energy costs, and system size
- **Lifespan:** Underground loop lasts 50+ years; indoor components last 20-25 years
- **Federal tax credit:** The 30% residential tax credit expired December 31, 2025 -- check your state for remaining incentives
- **Get a quote:** Find certified installers through the [International Ground Source Heat Pump Association](https://igshpa.org/)

### Support Community Energy Choice

Many states allow communities to aggregate their electricity purchasing power:

- **Community Choice Aggregation (CCA):** Ask your local government about CCA programs that let communities choose cleaner energy sources, including geothermal
- **Green power programs:** Check if your utility offers a geothermal or renewable energy option
- **Local energy cooperatives:** Rural electric cooperatives can choose to invest in geothermal baseload power -- attend co-op board meetings and advocate for clean energy procurement

### Advocate for Permitting Reform

The single biggest barrier to geothermal deployment is slow federal permitting:

- Contact your representatives about H.R. 5576 (Enhancing Geothermal Production on Federal Lands Act)
- Support the Geothermal Cost-Recovery Authority Act (H.R. 398) to improve agency capacity
- Push for geothermal to receive the same expedited permitting that oil and gas enjoys on federal lands
- Advocate for categorical exclusions for small-scale geothermal test wells
- Support state-level geothermal permitting streamlining

### Workforce Development

The geothermal industry needs skilled workers, and many geothermal skills overlap with oil and gas:

- **Career opportunities:** Browse the [Geothermal Rising Job Board](https://geothermal.org/resources/job-board)
- **DOE careers:** Explore positions at the [Office of Geothermal](https://www.energy.gov/hgeo/geothermal/office-geothermal-careers)
- **Skills transfer:** Drilling, reservoir engineering, geology, and project management skills from oil and gas translate directly to geothermal
- **Education:** Support geothermal curriculum at your local university or community college
