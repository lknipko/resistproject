# Green Infrastructure: Nature's Solutions to Urban Flooding and Heat

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Green infrastructure uses natural systems — trees, rain gardens, permeable pavement, green roofs, wetlands — to manage stormwater, reduce urban heat, and improve air quality. Philadelphia's decision to build green instead of gray saved an estimated **$6.5 billion** in construction costs. The EPA estimates that about **850 billion gallons** of raw sewage overflow into US waterways each year from cities with outdated combined sewer systems. Urban tree canopy can reduce local air temperatures by up to **9°F**. Yet neighborhoods that are majority people of color have, on average, **33% less tree canopy** than majority-white neighborhoods — making green infrastructure an environmental justice issue.

This page explains what green infrastructure is, why it matters, and how you can push for it in your community.

## Facts

### What Is Green Infrastructure?

Green infrastructure is a broad category of practices that use natural processes to manage water, moderate temperature, improve air quality, and support biodiversity. It works alongside — or in place of — conventional "gray" infrastructure like pipes, culverts, and concrete channels.

**Common types:**

- **Rain gardens** — Shallow planted depressions that capture and slowly absorb stormwater runoff from roofs and pavement
- **Bioswales** — Vegetated channels that slow and filter stormwater before it enters storm drains or streams
- **Permeable pavement** — Porous surfaces (gravel, permeable asphalt, concrete, or pavers) that allow rainwater to seep into the ground rather than run off
- **Green roofs** — Rooftops covered with vegetation that absorb rainfall, provide insulation, reduce heat, and extend roof life
- **Urban trees and tree canopy** — The most cost-effective form of green infrastructure; trees intercept rain, provide shade, reduce energy use, and sequester carbon
- **Urban wetlands** — Restored or preserved wetland areas in urban settings that absorb floodwater and filter pollutants
- **Living shorelines** — Coastal protection using natural materials (marsh grass, oyster reefs, sand) rather than hard barriers like concrete seawalls

### [+] The Combined Sewer Overflow Problem

About **700 US municipalities** — mostly older cities in the Northeast and Midwest — have combined sewer systems that carry both stormwater and raw sewage in the same pipes. When heavy rain overwhelms these systems, **untreated sewage overflows directly into rivers, lakes, and coastal waters**.

In EPA's last comprehensive estimate, **approximately 850 billion gallons** of contaminated combined sewer overflow (CSO) is discharged into US waterways each year. This creates:
- Beach closures and public health hazards
- Fish kills and aquatic dead zones
- Drinking water contamination risks
- Violations of the Clean Water Act

Traditional solutions — building larger underground pipes and tanks — cost tens of billions of dollars and can take decades to build. Green infrastructure can capture stormwater before it ever enters the combined sewer, dramatically reducing overflow at a fraction of the cost.

[source: EPA — Combined Sewer Overflows](https://www.epa.gov/npdes/combined-sewer-overflows-csos)

### [+] Philadelphia: The Landmark Case Study

Philadelphia has the largest combined sewer system in the US, serving 1.5 million residents. When EPA required the city to reduce sewage overflows, Philadelphia faced two options:

- **Gray infrastructure:** Build massive new underground tunnels, tanks, and pipes — estimated cost **$8 billion to $10 billion**
- **Green infrastructure:** Implement green stormwater infrastructure across 10,000+ acres of paved surfaces — estimated cost **$2.1 billion over 25 years**

Philadelphia chose green. The result is **Green City, Clean Waters** — a 25-year plan launched in 2011 in agreement with EPA and the Pennsylvania DEP.

**Results so far:**
- Exceeded the 10-year pollution reduction goal
- Nearly **3 billion gallons** of stormwater kept out of waterways
- Estimated **$6.5 billion** in savings compared to gray infrastructure
- **$60 million** added to local economies through green infrastructure installation and maintenance jobs
- Hundreds of new parks, green spaces, and tree plantings across the city

[source: Philadelphia Water Department — Green City, Clean Waters](https://water.phila.gov/green-city/) | [source: Great Lakes — Philadelphia GSI Case Study](https://greatlakes.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/09/AGL_GSI_CaseStudy_Philadelphia_FIN.pdf)

### Urban Heat Islands and Tree Canopy

Cities are significantly hotter than surrounding rural areas — the "urban heat island" effect. Asphalt, concrete, and rooftops absorb and re-radiate solar heat; the lack of vegetation eliminates evaporative cooling. The health consequences are severe:

- **More than 3.3 million children** and **2.6 million seniors** live in US urban neighborhoods experiencing more than **9°F of additional warming** due to the built environment
- Heat is the deadliest weather hazard in the US, killing more Americans each year than hurricanes, tornadoes, or floods combined
- Urban trees can reduce local air temperatures by **2–9°F** through shade and evapotranspiration
- Trees shade buildings and reduce air conditioning energy consumption

[source: EPA — Urban Heat Island Effect](https://www.epa.gov/green-infrastructure/heat-island-effect) | [source: PNAS — Tree Canopy and Urban Heat](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1817561116)

### [+] The Tree Equity Gap

Research from American Forests reveals a stark racial and economic disparity in who benefits from urban tree canopy:

- **Majority-nonwhite neighborhoods** have on average **33% less tree canopy** than majority-white neighborhoods
- **Neighborhoods with the fewest residents of color** have **four times more tree cover per person** than neighborhoods with the most residents of color
- **Lowest-poverty neighborhoods** have double the tree cover per person of highest-poverty neighborhoods
- These disparities trace directly to **redlining** — the HOLC's 1930s racial grading system for neighborhoods correlates strongly with current tree canopy patterns
- More than **11% of Asian Americans** in urban areas nationally live in neighborhoods experiencing more than 9°F of additional urban warming

The Tree Equity Score tool from American Forests maps canopy coverage for **over 200,000 neighborhoods** and **260 million people** across every US urban area, paired with income, race, health, and climate risk data. [source: American Forests — Tree Equity Score](https://www.americanforests.org/tools-research-reports-and-guides/tree-equity-score/)

### [+] Green Infrastructure Co-Benefits

Green infrastructure delivers a wide range of benefits beyond stormwater management:

**Public Health:**
- Reduced urban heat → lower heat mortality
- Trees filter particulate air pollution
- Green spaces are associated with lower rates of depression, anxiety, and stress
- Physical activity increases when walkable green spaces are available

**Carbon Sequestration:**
- Urban trees sequester carbon; green roofs reduce building energy consumption
- Restored urban wetlands can sequester significant amounts of soil carbon

**Property Values:**
- Studies consistently show that street trees and parks increase adjacent property values
- Green stormwater infrastructure often beautifies neighborhoods and increases community investment

**Biodiversity:**
- Urban trees, rain gardens, and green corridors provide habitat for birds, insects, and native plants
- Native plantings in rain gardens and bioswales support pollinators

**Economic Development:**
- Green infrastructure installation and maintenance creates local jobs that cannot be outsourced
- Philadelphia's program added $60 million to local economies in its first decade

### [+] Federal and State Funding Opportunities

Green infrastructure has been explicitly supported by several major federal programs:

**Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA / "Bipartisan Infrastructure Law"):**
- Section 221 — $50 million for a competitive green infrastructure grant program
- Clean Water State Revolving Fund (CWSRF) — Can fund green stormwater infrastructure projects
- Drinking Water State Revolving Fund — Can fund lead pipe replacements alongside green infrastructure

**Inflation Reduction Act (IRA):**
- Environmental and climate justice block grants include green infrastructure
- Urban and community forestry funding through USDA Forest Service

**EPA Programs:**
- Water Infrastructure Finance and Innovation Act (WIFIA) — Low-interest loans for large water infrastructure projects including green
- Combined sewer overflow Long-Term Control Plans (LTCPs) — EPA increasingly accepts green infrastructure as CSO compliance strategy

Contact your local water utility or public works department to ask whether they are leveraging these funding streams.

## Analysis

### What This Means

Green infrastructure is not a fringe concept — it is increasingly the mainstream choice for cities confronting aging sewer systems, urban heat, and flooding. The economics are compelling: Philadelphia avoided up to $8 billion in costs by choosing green. The health case is clear: tree canopy and cool surfaces save lives. And the equity case is urgent: the communities with the least canopy and the most heat exposure are the same communities that have historically been excluded from public investment.

The current administration has moved to rescind IRA funding and cut EPA green infrastructure programs. Local and state advocacy is now the primary lever for protecting and expanding this investment.

### Who's Affected

- **Residents of older cities with combined sewers:** CSO discharges contaminate swimming, fishing, and drinking water sources
- **Low-income and majority-nonwhite urban neighborhoods:** Least tree canopy, most urban heat, most flooding — and least resourced to adapt individually
- **Coastal communities:** Living shorelines and restored wetlands are more resilient and less expensive than seawalls
- **Property owners near green infrastructure:** Studies show property value increases near parks and street trees
- **Municipalities with aging infrastructure:** Facing billions in gray pipe replacement — green infrastructure offers a fiscally responsible alternative
- **Everyone who breathes city air:** Trees filter particulate pollution and cool summer air

### What Stands in the Way

- **Zoning codes** in many cities still require parking lots to use impermeable pavement and prohibit rooftop vegetation
- **Fragmented responsibility** — stormwater, parks, street trees, and planning often fall under different agencies that don't coordinate
- **Maintenance gaps** — green infrastructure requires consistent maintenance; cities that install it without funding maintenance see poor results
- **Redevelopment pressure** — in gentrifying neighborhoods, green infrastructure investment can accelerate displacement if not paired with housing affordability policy
- **Federal funding rollbacks** — IRA and IIJA green infrastructure funding is under threat from the current administration

### Find Your Neighborhood's Tree Equity Score

Look up your neighborhood's Tree Equity Score to see how your canopy compares to similar neighborhoods and how your area ranks for need:

1. Visit [treeequityscore.org](https://www.treeequityscore.org)
2. Enter your city or neighborhood
3. Share your results with your city council member and local planning department
4. Advocate for tree planting investments in the lowest-scoring neighborhoods

### Plant a Rain Garden or Native Plants at Home

You can implement green infrastructure on a small scale at home:

- **Rain garden:** A shallow planted depression below a downspout that captures roof runoff. Materials: native plants, gravel, and amended soil. Cost: $3–$30 per square foot depending on size and plants.
- **Native plantings:** Replace lawn with native groundcovers that absorb more rain and support pollinators
- **Disconnect your downspout:** Redirect roof runoff to a rain barrel or rain garden instead of into the storm drain

Many utilities and municipalities offer rebates or free plants for homeowners installing green stormwater features. Check your local water utility's website.

### Attend Local Planning and Zoning Meetings

Green infrastructure is implemented through local policy — zoning codes, building ordinances, capital budgets. The most direct way to advance it is to show up:

1. Find your city or county planning board meeting schedule
2. Comment in support of green stormwater requirements for new development
3. Ask about the status of your city's stormwater management plan
4. Request that the city require permeable surfaces in new parking lots

### Push for Street Tree Planting in Under-Canopied Neighborhoods

Contact your city's urban forestry department or parks department:
- Ask about their street tree planting program and waitlist
- Request trees for your block, especially if you live in a low-canopy neighborhood
- Support budget allocations for tree maintenance (tree mortality from deferred maintenance is a major problem)

### Submit Public Comments on EPA Green Infrastructure Programs

EPA and state agencies regularly accept comments on stormwater regulations and combined sewer overflow (CSO) long-term control plans. These decisions directly shape whether cities invest in green or gray infrastructure:

- Visit [regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov) and search "combined sewer overflow" or "stormwater management" for open dockets
- Your state environmental agency also runs state revolving fund (SRF) processes — search for your state's "Clean Water SRF intended use plan" comment period
- Comments supporting green infrastructure as an EPA-accepted CSO compliance strategy help establish that communities want nature-based solutions

### Volunteer for Urban Tree Planting and Stewardship

Tree mortality from lack of maintenance (watering, mulching) is one of the biggest failures of urban forestry programs. Volunteer stewardship dramatically improves survival rates:

- **Million Trees programs** — Many cities have tree planting volunteer programs; search your city name + "million trees" or "urban forestry volunteer"
- **American Forests Community Tree Programs** ([americanforests.org/programs](https://www.americanforests.org/programs/)) — Coordinates tree planting in urban areas prioritizing equity gaps
- **Local land trusts and park conservancies** — Often organize greenway restoration, bioswale planting, and rain garden installation volunteer events

### Participate in Stormwater Monitoring (Citizen Science)

- **iNaturalist Water Quality Projects** ([inaturalist.org](https://www.inaturalist.org)) — Document aquatic life in local streams; healthy macroinvertebrate populations indicate effective stormwater management
- **Waterkeeper Alliance local groups** ([waterkeeper.org](https://waterkeeper.org)) — Many local Waterkeeper organizations run volunteer water testing programs to document CSO impacts in your watershed
- **Creek or river monitoring programs** — Contact your city's environmental or public works department; many partner with citizen scientists for ongoing water quality sampling

## Sustained Actions

### Organizations to Support

- **American Forests** ([americanforests.org](https://www.americanforests.org)) — Created the Tree Equity Score; advocates for urban forestry investment; runs tree planting programs in underserved communities
- **Trust for Public Land** ([tpl.org](https://www.tpl.org)) — Creates parks and green space in urban areas; focuses on equity in green space access; produces ParkScore equity data
- **Green Infrastructure Leadership Forum** ([gilforum.org](https://www.gilforum.org)) — National network of municipalities and utilities advancing green infrastructure; resources for local advocates
- **Waterkeeper Alliance** ([waterkeeper.org](https://waterkeeper.org)) — Network of local waterway advocates; fights combined sewer pollution and supports green infrastructure policy
- **Groundwork USA** ([groundworkusa.org](https://www.groundworkusa.org)) — Network of community organizations working on environmental justice in urban areas; urban greening and stormwater projects

### Engage Your Local Utility

Municipal water and wastewater utilities are often the entities most directly responsible for stormwater management and CSO compliance. They have budgets and regulatory drivers that make them natural allies for green infrastructure:

- Attend public utility board meetings
- Ask about the utility's Long-Term Control Plan for combined sewer overflows
- Ask whether the utility offers incentive programs for private green infrastructure installation
- Request that the utility report on tree canopy and green infrastructure equity metrics

### Advocate for Green Infrastructure in State Policy

- Push your state legislature to require green infrastructure in state construction projects
- Advocate for state revolving fund (SRF) set-asides specifically for green infrastructure
- Support state legislation requiring climate vulnerability assessments that include urban heat and stormwater
