# Greenwashing: How Corporations Exploit Environmental Marketing

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Over **50% of all green claims** made by businesses are vague, misleading, or based on unfounded information, according to the European Commission. A 2023 investigation by The Guardian found that **94% of rainforest carbon offset credits** certified by the world's leading standard-setter were likely "phantom credits" representing no real emissions reduction. BP spent millions marketing itself as "Beyond Petroleum" while **96% of its annual capital expenditure went to fossil fuels**. And the term "greenwashing" itself was coined in the 1980s when Chevron launched ads claiming its operations were environmentally friendly — while fighting environmental regulations in court.

Greenwashing isn't just deceptive marketing. It delays the real action needed to address the climate crisis by allowing companies and consumers to feel progress is being made when it isn't. With federal regulatory capacity to police these claims weakened and the FTC's Green Guides still lacking enforcement teeth, public scrutiny and legal action remain the most effective checks.

## Facts

### What Is Greenwashing?

Greenwashing refers to the practice of making false, misleading, or unsubstantiated environmental claims to gain marketing advantage, deflect regulatory pressure, or improve public perception — without making corresponding real-world environmental improvements.

Common greenwashing tactics include:
- **Vague claims:** "Eco-friendly," "green," "sustainable," "natural" — with no specific meaning or verification
- **Hidden trade-offs:** Highlighting one environmental benefit while ignoring others (e.g., "recycled packaging" for a product with a massive carbon footprint)
- **Irrelevant claims:** Emphasizing compliance with regulations as if it were a voluntary achievement (e.g., "CFC-free" decades after CFCs were banned)
- **False certifications:** Using made-up logos or self-certified labels that imply third-party verification
- **Carbon offset laundering:** Claiming "carbon neutrality" through the purchase of offsets that don't represent real emissions reductions

### [+] The FTC Green Guides

The U.S. Federal Trade Commission's "Green Guides" (16 C.F.R. Part 260) provide guidance on what environmental marketing claims are deceptive under the FTC Act. First issued in 1992 and last revised in 2012, the Green Guides are the primary federal framework for regulating environmental marketing.

- The FTC announced a review of the Green Guides in December 2022, specifically asking for public input on carbon offsets, renewable energy claims, and net-zero marketing [source: FTC](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2022/12/ftc-seeks-public-comment-potential-updates-its-green-guides-use-environmental-marketing-claims)
- Environmental organizations including the Sierra Club urged the FTC to strengthen the guides and add specific rules on carbon offset claims [source: Sierra Club](https://www.sierraclub.org/press-releases/2023/04/ftc-urged-tackle-corporate-greenwashing-new-green-guides)
- **Key weakness:** The Green Guides are currently guidance documents, not binding rules — the FTC cannot seek civil penalties for violations. The FTC has been considering whether to make them enforceable regulations.
- In recent litigation, courts have allowed plaintiffs to use the Green Guides as evidence of what constitutes deceptive marketing: in *Dorris v. Danone Waters of America*, a judge allowed plaintiffs to cite the Green Guides to support "carbon neutral" greenwashing claims [source: National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/environmental-marketing-claims-regulatory-and-litigation-outlook)

### [+] The Carbon Offset Problem

Carbon offsets are credits that companies purchase to claim they've "offset" their emissions by funding emissions reductions elsewhere — typically in forests or renewable energy projects in the developing world. The market has exploded as companies rush to claim "carbon neutrality" or "net zero" — but the credits often don't reflect real reductions.

**The Guardian/Verra Investigation (January 2023)**

In a landmark nine-month joint investigation, The Guardian, Die Zeit, and SourceMaterial found:

- **94% of rainforest offset credits** certified by Verra — the world's largest carbon offset standard-setter — were likely "phantom credits" representing no genuine carbon reductions [source: EcoWatch](https://www.ecowatch.com/phantom-credits-verra.html)
- The threat of deforestation had been overstated by approximately **400% on average** in Verra-certified projects, meaning forests were receiving credit for protecting trees that were never actually at risk
- Major buyers of these phantom offsets included Gucci, Salesforce, BHP, Shell, easyJet, and Pearl Jam [source: Carbon Herald](https://carbonherald.com/the-guardian-investigation-of-verra-carbon-offsets-shows-90-are-worthless/)
- Verra disputed the findings but the investigation sparked a global reassessment of voluntary carbon markets

**Why Offsets Fail**

- **Permanence:** A forest protected today can burn tomorrow — California's own forest carbon credit buffer pool has been partially depleted by wildfires
- **Additionality:** Credits should only be issued for emissions reductions that wouldn't have happened anyway; many projects would have protected forests without the credit revenue
- **Leakage:** Protecting one patch of forest may simply push logging to another area
- **Verification:** On-the-ground monitoring is expensive and infrequent; satellite analysis often reveals credits were issued for forests that were never threatened

### [+] Landmark Greenwashing Cases

**BP's "Beyond Petroleum" Campaign**

- BP rebranded itself as "Beyond Petroleum" in 2000, emphasizing its investments in solar energy and positioning itself as an oil company transitioning to clean energy
- Throughout this campaign, **96% of BP's annual capital expenditure remained in fossil fuels** [source: ClientEarth](https://www.clientearth.org/latest/press-office/press-releases/lawyers-take-action-against-bp-s-climate-greenwashing-advertising-campaign/)
- BP also popularized the concept of the individual "carbon footprint" in a 2004 advertising campaign — a framing that shifted responsibility from fossil fuel producers to individual consumers [source: Green is the New Black](https://greenisthenewblack.com/carbon-footprint-bp/)
- **September 2024:** A Hamburg Regional Court ruled that BP could not advertise certain products as "climate neutral" after BP justified the claim by purchasing forest protection offsets in Zambia [source: Peters & Peters](https://www.petersandpeters.com/case/german-environmental-ngo-files-greenwashing-claim-against-bp/)
- ClientEarth filed legal action against BP's advertising in the UK, arguing the ads were misleading about BP's actual climate commitments

**The Origin of Greenwashing: Chevron's "People Do" Campaign**

- The term "greenwashing" traces to the 1980s, when Chevron launched its "People Do" advertising campaign claiming that the company's business was environmentally friendly — while simultaneously fighting environmental regulations and funding habitat destruction [source: The Simple Environmentalist](https://www.thesimpleenvironmentalist.com/blog-1/ux6spk7rts2frf179zb4qii23s6qp2)
- A U.S. congressional investigation later revealed internal communications among lobbyists at Shell, Chevron, and ExxonMobil questioning the companies' own public environmental commitments [source: Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/2022/09/23/shell-bp-exxon-seized-emails-reveal-deceptive-climate-tactics-and-greenwashing)

**H&M's "Conscious Collection"**

- H&M marketed its "Conscious" clothing line as a sustainable alternative, using vague language about environmentally better materials
- A Norwegian Consumer Authority investigation found the sustainability claims lacked adequate product-level data to substantiate them
- H&M reached a **$3 million settlement** in a U.S. class action lawsuit over the Conscious Collection's unsupported sustainability claims [source: Clark Hill](https://www.clarkhill.com/news-events/news/cracking-down-on-deceptive-green-marketing-claims-recent-developments-in-the-us-and-eu/)
- In 2024, the UK Competition and Markets Authority found similar problems with ASOS, Boohoo, and George at Asda — their sustainability terms lacked sufficient clarity and could mislead consumers

**The Plastic Industry's "Keep America Beautiful" Campaign**

One of the earliest documented examples of industry-funded greenwashing is the "Keep America Beautiful" campaign, launched in 1953. Funded by beverage and packaging companies, the campaign promoted anti-littering messages that successfully shifted public attention from producer responsibility and packaging reduction to individual consumer behavior — helping industry defeat bottle deposit laws and other packaging regulations for decades.

### [+] How to Spot Greenwashing

**Red Flags:**

- **No numbers:** Claims like "reduced emissions" or "lower carbon" with no specific percentage, baseline, or timeframe
- **Self-certification:** Environmental logos or labels not issued by a recognized independent third party
- **Offsetting ≠ reducing:** "Carbon neutral" or "net zero" claims based entirely on offset purchases rather than operational emissions reductions
- **Scope cherry-picking:** Companies claiming they're "carbon neutral" for Scope 1 (direct) emissions while ignoring Scope 3 (supply chain and product use) emissions — which are often 90%+ of total impact
- **Aspirational without accountability:** "Our goal is net zero by 2050" with no interim targets, no current progress data, and no independent verification
- **Irrelevant credentials:** Claiming environmental leadership on one narrow issue while ignoring much larger impacts
- **Suing for the right to pollute:** Companies that publicly tout environmental commitments while simultaneously lobbying against environmental regulations or suing agencies to weaken standards

**Third-Party Certifications That Carry Weight:**

- **B Corp Certification** ([bcorporation.net](https://www.bcorporation.net)) — Rigorous third-party assessment of environmental and social performance; requires recertification every 3 years
- **Energy Star** ([energystar.gov](https://www.energystar.gov)) — EPA-backed certification for energy efficiency in appliances and buildings
- **UL Environment** ([ul.com](https://www.ul.com)) — Independent testing and certification for specific environmental claims
- **Fairtrade** ([fairtrade.net](https://www.fairtrade.net)) — Supply chain standards for commodities including food and textiles
- **Forest Stewardship Council (FSC)** ([fsc.org](https://www.fsc.org)) — Credible third-party certification for sustainably managed forests

**Certifications to Approach Skeptically:**

- Any logo designed by the company itself with no third-party verification
- Vague terms like "eco," "green," "clean," "natural," or "sustainable" without specific standards
- "Recyclable" claims without evidence that recycling infrastructure actually exists for the product in your area

### [+] The EU's Approach vs. U.S. Weak Regulation

**European Union:**

The EU has moved aggressively to legally define and prohibit greenwashing, with two major directives:

- **Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition Directive (2024):** Amended the EU's Unfair Commercial Practices Directive to explicitly ban generic environmental claims without evidence ("eco-friendly," "green," "natural"), claims based solely on carbon offsetting, and sustainability labels not backed by approved certification schemes [source: Latham & Watkins](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/eu-sustainability-state-of-play-greenwashing-and-consumer-protection)
- **EU Green Claims Directive:** Would have required companies to scientifically substantiate specific environmental claims before making them and submit them for third-party verification. However, as of June 2025 the European Commission announced it would withdraw the proposal due to concerns about administrative burden — a significant setback for greenwashing enforcement [source: Morgan Lewis](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2024/05/two-eu-directives-aim-to-shape-european-legal-framework-for-green-claims-and-greenwashing)
- Even without the Green Claims Directive, EU member states are actively enforcing against misleading green marketing under existing consumer protection law

**United States:**

- The FTC Green Guides remain non-binding guidance, not enforceable regulations
- No federal law specifically prohibits greenwashing — enforcement relies on the FTC's general deceptive practices authority
- The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) proposed climate disclosure rules in 2022 that would have required companies to verify and disclose climate-related claims; these rules were significantly weakened and their implementation is uncertain under the Trump administration
- Private class action litigation has become the primary U.S. mechanism for greenwashing accountability — and judges are increasingly allowing these cases to proceed

## Analysis

### What This Means

- When companies claim "carbon neutral," "net zero," or "sustainable" without rigorous third-party verification, those claims are almost certainly misleading — and should be treated as such
- Carbon offset markets as currently structured allow companies to maintain or increase their actual emissions while claiming climate leadership; this is not a path to solving the climate crisis
- The EU-U.S. regulatory gap means that some companies face legal liability in Europe for claims they make freely in the United States
- Individual consumer choices matter less than systemic change — but consumers who actively call out greenwashing and support accountability create real pressure on corporate behavior
- Private litigation is increasingly filling the enforcement gap left by weak U.S. regulation; supporting organizations that file greenwashing cases is one of the most effective things individuals can do

### Who's Affected

- **All consumers:** Pay premiums for products marketed as green without getting the environmental benefits claimed
- **Legitimate sustainable businesses:** Lose competitive advantage when greenwashers claim the same labels without doing the underlying work
- **Communities bearing pollution burdens:** When greenwashing lets polluters claim environmental responsibility, it reduces pressure for actual emissions reductions in frontline communities
- **Climate action broadly:** Resources and political will get diverted toward the perception of action rather than actual reductions

## Quick Actions

### Report Greenwashing to the FTC (10 minutes)

File a consumer complaint with the Federal Trade Commission when you spot misleading environmental marketing. The FTC uses complaint data to identify enforcement targets.

**How to file:**
1. Go to [ftc.gov/complaint](https://reportfraud.ftc.gov/)
2. Select "Other" as the complaint type, then describe the greenwashing
3. Include: the company name, the specific claim made, where you saw it, and why it's misleading
4. Include screenshots or links where possible

### Check Corporate CDP Climate Scores

The CDP (formerly Carbon Disclosure Project) is the most rigorous public database of corporate environmental performance. Companies disclose actual emissions, water use, and deforestation risk — and receive letter grades.

- Go to [cdp.net](https://www.cdp.net/en/companies) and search for any publicly traded company
- Companies that refuse to disclose receive an "F" — a red flag that their green claims lack substance
- Grades are publicly available and free to access

### Use Good On You for Fashion

The fashion industry is one of the worst greenwashing offenders. [Good On You](https://goodonyou.eco) rates fashion brands on worker welfare, environmental impact, and animal welfare on a 1–5 scale using publicly verifiable data — cutting through vague sustainability marketing.

### Divest Personal Finances

- Move investments and retirement accounts away from fossil fuel companies using greenwashing to maintain social license
- Look for investment funds screened for genuine ESG performance (not self-reported), or use tools like [Fossil Free Funds](https://fossilfreefunds.org) to check whether your 401(k) or IRA invests in fossil fuel companies
- Consider community development financial institutions (CDFIs) or credit unions as alternatives to large banks that finance fossil fuel expansion

### Expose Greenwashing on Social Media

When you identify a specific greenwashing claim — with documentation — sharing it publicly creates accountability and educates others. Effective public exposure has directly contributed to companies pulling misleading campaigns.

**Effective format:**
- Quote or screenshot the specific claim
- Link to evidence that contradicts it (CDP data, investigative journalism, legal filings)
- Tag the company and relevant journalists or advocacy accounts
- Use #greenwashing and #climateaccountability
