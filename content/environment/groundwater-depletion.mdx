# Groundwater Depletion

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** America's aquifers are being pumped dry. The Ogallala Aquifer -- which irrigates 30% of U.S. cropland -- could lose 70% of its usable water in the Texas Panhandle within two decades. In California's Central Valley, groundwater depletion has accelerated by 31% since 2019, and the land is literally sinking -- costing $1.87 billion in lost home values. The Colorado River Basin has lost groundwater equal to an entire Lake Mead since 2003, with depletion accelerating 3 times faster in the last decade. Approximately 71% of U.S. groundwater withdrawals go to agriculture, and in many states, pumping remains largely unregulated. When these aquifers run out, they cannot be refilled on any human timescale.

## Facts

### The Ogallala Aquifer: America's Underground Ocean

The Ogallala (High Plains) Aquifer stretches beneath eight states from South Dakota to Texas, supplying water to nearly one-third of all U.S. irrigated farmland. It took millions of years to fill. We are emptying it in decades.

- **Annual recharge rate:** Less than 1 inch per year in most areas -- extraction rates are 10-100 times faster
- **Kansas 2024-2025:** Aquifer levels fell **1.52 feet** in a single year, accelerating from the prior year's 1.43-foot decline
- **Texas Panhandle:** Researchers at the University of Texas project that up to **70% will become unusable** within 20 years at current pumping rates
- **Kansas:** **2.6 million acres** of land no longer have enough groundwater to support large-scale agriculture
- **Nebraska:** In 2025, 62% of nearly 5,000 monitored wells showed declining water levels, with the largest declines exceeding 10 feet in the Panhandle
- **Economic impact:** A 2025 USDA Economic Research Service analysis projected a **$50 billion drop** in regional agricultural output over the next decade if current trends persist

The Ogallala supports approximately **$20 billion per year** in agricultural production, including corn, wheat, cotton, and cattle.

[source: NOAA - Ogallala Aquifer](https://www.climate.gov/news-features/featured-images/national-climate-assessment-great-plains%E2%80%99-ogallala-aquifer-drying-out) | [source: Kansas Aquifer Decline](https://farmpolicynews.illinois.edu/2025/01/ogallala-aquifer-dropped-more-than-a-foot-in-kansas-in-2024/) | [source: USGS Groundwater Decline](https://www.usgs.gov/special-topics/water-science-school/science/groundwater-decline-and-depletion)

### [+] California's Central Valley: Sinking Land, Shrinking Future

California's Central Valley produces 25% of America's food on just 1% of its farmland. It depends heavily on groundwater, especially during droughts -- and the consequences are becoming irreversible.

- **Depletion acceleration:** Since 2019, groundwater depletion has accelerated by **31%** compared to recent droughts, and has increased by nearly **5 times** compared to the 60-year average
- **Land subsidence:** Parts of the San Joaquin Valley have sunk by **over one foot per year** since 2006. This sinking is permanent -- once the ground compacts, aquifer storage capacity is lost forever.
- **Infrastructure damage:** Subsidence has reduced the Friant-Kern Canal's flow capacity by **60%**, and restricted State Water Project deliveries by **3%** annually
- **Home values:** Homes in subsiding areas lost **2.4-5.8%** of their sale value, totaling **$1.87 billion** in aggregate housing value losses across the Central Valley
- **SGMA deadlines:** California's 2014 Sustainable Groundwater Management Act requires critically over-drafted basins to achieve sustainability by **2040** -- but compliance is uncertain and politically contentious

[source: Stanford - San Joaquin Valley Subsidence](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2024/11/groundwater-pumping-drives-rapid-sinking-in-california) | [source: Nature Communications - Central Valley Depletion](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-35582-x) | [source: California DWR Subsidence Study](https://water.ca.gov/News/Blog/2025/May-25/Study-Finds-That-Subsidence-Groundwater-Over-Pumping-Could-Limit-Future-Water-Deliveries)

### [+] Colorado River Basin: The Hidden Crisis Underground

While headlines focus on Lake Mead and Lake Powell, the real crisis is underground. As surface water shrinks, farmers and cities are pumping more groundwater -- draining reserves that took thousands of years to accumulate.

- Since 2003, the Colorado River Basin has lost groundwater equivalent to **an entire Lake Mead** (approximately 27.8 million acre-feet)
- Groundwater is disappearing **2.4 times faster** than surface water in the basin
- Depletion has accelerated **3 times faster** in the last decade compared to the decade before
- The Lower Basin already relies on groundwater for **40%** of its water supply -- a number expected to rise
- Large-scale industrial farming in Arizona is closely tied to major depletion, with groundwater pumping **largely unregulated** in much of the state

[source: AGU - Colorado River Basin Groundwater](https://news.agu.org/press-release/colorado-river-basin-has-lost-water-equal-to-lake-mead-due-to-climate-change/) | [source: Geophysical Research Letters](https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2025GL115593)

### [+] The National Picture

Groundwater depletion is not limited to a few aquifers. It is a nationwide crisis:

- **71% of U.S. groundwater** is used for agriculture -- primarily irrigation
- **45% of monitored wells** nationally show statistically significant declines since 1980
- **4 in 10** monitoring sites reached record-low water levels during the past decade
- The Mississippi Embayment aquifer system (Arkansas, Mississippi, Louisiana) is also declining rapidly from rice and soybean irrigation
- Domestic wells are going dry in rural communities across the West and Great Plains, forcing families to haul water or drill deeper at costs of **$20,000-50,000+**

[source: USGS Groundwater Depletion](https://www.usgs.gov/special-topics/water-science-school/science/groundwater-decline-and-depletion) | [source: USGS Aquifer Productivity Impacts](https://www.usgs.gov/publications/aquifer-depletion-and-potential-impacts-long-term-irrigated-agricultural-productivity)

### [+] Regulatory Gaps

Groundwater regulation in the U.S. is a patchwork with enormous gaps:

- **Texas:** Rule of capture -- landowners can pump unlimited groundwater regardless of impact on neighbors
- **Arizona:** No regulation of agricultural pumping outside designated "Active Management Areas," allowing industrial farms to drain aquifers freely
- **Kansas:** Local Groundwater Management Districts exist but have limited enforcement power
- **California:** SGMA (2014) was the first statewide groundwater law, but sustainability deadlines extend to 2040-2042 and compliance is challenging
- **Nebraska:** Natural Resources Districts manage groundwater with moratoriums in some areas, but significant depletion continues
- **Most states** have no comprehensive groundwater management framework, treating underground water as a private resource rather than a public trust

[source: California SGMA](https://water.ca.gov/programs/groundwater-management/sgma-groundwater-management)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Aquifer depletion is effectively **irreversible** on human timescales -- the Ogallala took millions of years to fill, and recharge rates are a fraction of withdrawal rates
- Food prices will rise as irrigated farmland loses water access -- the affected regions produce a significant share of U.S. grain, cotton, dairy, and beef
- Rural communities face well failures, water hauling, and potential displacement as domestic wells go dry
- Land subsidence causes **permanent** loss of aquifer storage capacity, infrastructure damage (canals, pipelines, roads, buildings), and flooding risk
- As surface water becomes scarcer due to climate change, pressure on groundwater will only increase, accelerating depletion
- The lack of federal groundwater regulation means solutions depend on state-by-state action, which has been slow and politically difficult

### Who's Affected

- **Farmers and ranchers:** Livelihoods depend on irrigation water that is running out; many face impossible choices between reducing production and depleting their water supply
- **Rural communities:** Domestic wells are going dry in Kansas, Arizona, California, and other states, with replacement costs of $20,000-50,000+
- **Homeowners in subsidence zones:** Permanent property damage and value loss from sinking ground
- **Tribal nations:** Many rely on springs and wells fed by the same aquifers being depleted by industrial agriculture
- **All Americans:** Food security depends on irrigated agriculture; the Ogallala region alone produces 30% of U.S. irrigated crops
- **Future generations:** The water being pumped now will not be available for our children and grandchildren

### Find Out Where Your Water Comes From (10 minutes)

1. **Look up your water utility:** Visit your city or county water provider's website and find their annual Consumer Confidence Report (required by law)
2. **Check if you depend on groundwater:** Many communities -- especially rural ones -- rely entirely on well water. Your CCR will identify the source.
3. **Check your water level trends:** If you have a private well, the [USGS Groundwater Watch](https://groundwaterwatch.usgs.gov/) tracks water levels at thousands of monitoring wells
4. **Test your well:** If you have a private well, get it tested annually -- your county health department can help

### Attend a Local Water Board Meeting (1-2 hours)

Water decisions are made at the local level, and meetings are often poorly attended -- your voice carries significant weight:

- **Groundwater management districts** (Kansas, Nebraska, Texas) -- search "[your county] groundwater management district"
- **Groundwater sustainability agencies** (California) -- find yours at [sgma.water.ca.gov](https://sgma.water.ca.gov/portal/)
- **County water boards and irrigation districts** -- search "[your county] water board meeting schedule"
- **City council meetings** on water rate increases, conservation ordinances, or water reuse programs

Bring one fact from this page and ask: "What is our plan for groundwater sustainability?"

### Reduce Your Water Footprint (Ongoing)

Most personal water use is **indirect** -- embedded in the food you eat and products you buy:

- **Reduce beef consumption:** A single pound of beef requires approximately **1,800 gallons** of water to produce, much of it from irrigation
- **Choose water-efficient foods:** Chicken requires ~500 gallons/lb, vegetables ~40-100 gallons/lb
- **Buy local and seasonal produce** -- shorter supply chains often mean less irrigation-intensive farming
- **Fix leaks:** A dripping faucet wastes 3,000+ gallons per year
- **Use water-efficient landscaping** -- replace lawn with native plants adapted to your climate (xeriscaping)

### Support Water Conservation Ballot Measures

When water conservation measures appear on your ballot -- fund them. These may include:

- Water infrastructure bonds for pipe replacement and water recycling
- Conservation easement funding for agricultural land
- Water reuse and recycling program funding
- Aquifer recharge project bonds

## Sustained Actions

### Support Groundwater Organizations

- **The Groundwater Foundation** ([groundwater.org](https://groundwater.org)) -- Community-based groundwater education and protection. Their **Groundwater Guardian** program supports 420 volunteer entities nationwide. Great for starting local awareness.
- **American Farmland Trust** ([farmland.org](https://farmland.org)) -- Works with farmers on water conservation and aquifer recharge, including a $10 million NRCS partnership in California's San Joaquin Valley for on-farm groundwater recharge
- **National Ground Water Association** ([ngwa.org](https://www.ngwa.org)) -- Professional organization that also provides consumer resources on well maintenance and groundwater science
- **Waterkeeper Alliance** ([waterkeeper.org](https://waterkeeper.org)) -- 300+ local waterkeeper organizations advocating for both surface and groundwater protection
- **Western Resource Advocates** ([westernresources.org](https://westernresourceadvocates.org)) -- Policy advocacy for sustainable water use across the Interior West

### Advocate for Policy Change

**At the state level:**
- Push for **comprehensive groundwater management laws** in states that lack them (Texas, Arizona outside AMAs, many others)
- Support **agricultural water efficiency incentive programs** -- subsidized drip irrigation, soil moisture sensors, and deficit irrigation research
- Advocate for **aquifer recharge programs** that capture flood and stormwater for underground storage
- Support **groundwater monitoring network funding** to track depletion in real time

**At the federal level:**
- Support full funding for the **USGS groundwater monitoring network** -- the primary source of national groundwater data
- Advocate for **USDA EQIP and CSP** water conservation programs that help farmers adopt efficient irrigation
- Support **Bureau of Reclamation** aquifer storage and recovery (ASR) projects
- Push for federal **water recycling and reuse** grants

### Participate in Citizen Science

- **Monitor your well:** If you have a private well, measure and record water levels seasonally. The [USGS](https://www.usgs.gov/special-topics/water-science-school/science/groundwater-decline-and-depletion) provides guidance on measurement techniques.
- **Report dry wells:** Contact your state water agency if your well goes dry or water levels drop significantly -- this data helps track depletion
- **Join a watershed group:** Local watershed organizations often monitor groundwater-surface water interactions. Find one through the [Waterkeeper Alliance](https://waterkeeper.org)
- **Document subsidence:** If you observe cracked foundations, tilting structures, or sinking land, report it and photograph it with GPS coordinates

### Support Farmers Transitioning to Water Efficiency

- **Buy from farms using efficient irrigation** -- drip irrigation uses 30-50% less water than flood irrigation
- **Support regenerative agriculture** that builds soil health and water retention (cover crops, no-till, composting)
- **Advocate for crop diversification** away from water-intensive crops in depleting aquifer regions
- **Support local food systems** -- farmers markets, CSAs, and farm-to-table programs reduce reliance on heavily irrigated commodity crops

### File FOIA Requests for Water Data

Request information on water use in your region:

- **Bureau of Reclamation:** Water delivery records for federal irrigation projects -- [usbr.gov/foia](https://www.usbr.gov/foia/)
- **USDA Farm Service Agency:** Crop and irrigation data by county
- **State water agencies:** Permitted groundwater withdrawal data, well drilling permits, and compliance records
- **Army Corps of Engineers:** Water supply studies and aquifer assessments
