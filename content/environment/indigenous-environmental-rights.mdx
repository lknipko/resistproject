# Indigenous Environmental Rights

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Indigenous communities have been stewards of this land for millennia and are disproportionately impacted by environmental degradation -- from pipelines crossing treaty lands to uranium mining contaminating water. In 2025, the Interior Department rolled back NEPA regulations that protected tribal consultation rights, and a coalition of 228 organizations opposed the changes. Meanwhile, the historic Bears Ears co-management plan -- the first tribal-federal collaborative management of a national monument -- was finalized in January 2025 but faces threats from monument reduction reviews. Indigenous-led environmental organizations are among the most effective defenders of land and water.

Supporting Indigenous environmental rights is not charity -- it is justice, and it is effective conservation.

## Facts

### Treaty Rights and Environmental Protection

Tribal nations hold treaty rights -- legally binding agreements with the U.S. government -- that often include rights to water, hunting, fishing, and gathering on ancestral lands. These rights carry the force of federal law:

- **Reserved rights doctrine:** Treaties reserved specific rights for tribes, including water rights, which take priority over later appropriations in many Western states
- **Trust responsibility:** The federal government has a legal obligation to protect tribal resources
- **Tribal sovereignty:** Tribes are sovereign nations with authority to manage their own environmental regulations, often stricter than state or federal standards
- **EPA tribal delegations:** Over 100 tribes have been delegated authority to implement Clean Water Act or Clean Air Act programs on their lands

When the federal government fails to consult with tribes or weakens environmental protections, it can directly violate treaty obligations.

[source: EPA Tribal Programs](https://www.epa.gov/tribal) | [source: DOI Indian Trust Responsibilities](https://www.doi.gov/tribes)

### NEPA Rollback and Tribal Consultation

The National Environmental Policy Act requires environmental review before major federal actions, including consultation with affected tribal nations. In 2025:

- The Interior Department **removed most NEPA procedures from the Code of Federal Regulations**, shifting them to an internal handbook that can be changed without public notice
- **228 conservation, environmental justice, and tribal organizations** submitted a joint comment letter opposing the changes
- Tribal nations warned the rollback would **weaken consultation and reduce protections** for cultural resources, sacred places, and tribal communities
- The FCC's approach to tribal consultation was criticized as a "listening session" where attendees were hidden from one another, rather than meaningful dialogue
- The SPEED Act proposals would **further weaken NEPA** and limit the scope of reviews, reducing opportunities for tribal input

[source: Tribal Business News - NEPA Rollback](https://tribalbusinessnews.com/sections/energy/15512-interior-nepa-rollback-shifts-procedures-to-internal-handbook-raising-tribal-consultation-concerns) | [source: ABA - Native Nations and Public Lands](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/environment_energy_resources/resources/natural-resources-environment/2026-winter/native-nations-public-lands-trump-20-era/)

### Bears Ears: A Model Under Threat

On January 14, 2025, the Bureau of Land Management and U.S. Forest Service finalized the Bears Ears National Monument Resource Management Plan -- the **first tribal-federal collaborative management plan** for a national monument in U.S. history.

The five Commission Tribes -- Hopi Tribe, Navajo Nation, Ute Mountain Ute Tribe, Zuni Tribe, and Ute Indian Tribe of Uintah and Ouray Reservation -- co-developed the plan with federal agencies. The Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition received Harvard's Honoring Nations Award for groundbreaking Indigenous stewardship.

However, Executive Order 14174 directed review of all national monuments designated since 1996 for potential reduction or elimination. Bears Ears (1.36 million acres of culturally sacred land in Utah) is a primary target.

[source: Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition](https://www.bearsearscoalition.org/final-bears-ears-resource-management-plan/) | [source: DOI Press Release](https://www.doi.gov/pressreleases/blm-forest-service-and-five-tribes-bears-ears-commission-commit-historic-co-management)

### [+] Pipeline Fights and Sacred Sites

Indigenous communities have led the most visible environmental resistance of the past decade:

**Standing Rock / Dakota Access Pipeline (2016-present):**
- The Standing Rock Sioux Tribe opposed the pipeline crossing under Lake Oahe, the reservation's primary water source
- Despite mass protests, the pipeline was completed and operates today
- Federal courts found the Army Corps of Engineers violated NEPA by not conducting a full environmental review
- Multiple oil spills have occurred since operation began

**Line 3 Replacement (2021):**
- Enbridge replaced Line 3 pipeline across Anishinaabe treaty territory in Minnesota
- Indigenous-led resistance highlighted treaty rights to wild rice waters and hunting lands
- The pipeline crosses over 200 waterways

**Mountain Valley Pipeline (2023-present):**
- 303-mile natural gas pipeline through Appalachia, crossing tribal cultural sites
- Authorized by Congress after courts blocked it for environmental violations
- Sets precedent for legislative overrides of environmental review

These pipeline fights demonstrate a pattern: fossil fuel infrastructure is repeatedly routed through Indigenous lands and treaty territories, with inadequate consultation and environmental review.

[source: Indigenous Environmental Network 2025 Year in Review](https://www.ienearth.org/2025-year-in-review/) | [source: NARF - Bears Ears](https://narf.org/cases/bears-ears/)

### [+] Environmental Justice on Tribal Lands

Indigenous communities face disproportionate environmental burdens:

- **Uranium legacy:** Over 500 abandoned uranium mines on Navajo Nation land, contaminating water and causing elevated cancer rates for decades
- **Coal ash and power plants:** Four Corners Power Plant and Navajo Generating Station operated for decades on tribal lands, contributing to respiratory illness
- **PFAS contamination:** Military bases on or near tribal lands have contaminated groundwater
- **Climate impacts:** Alaska Native communities face coastal erosion, permafrost thaw, and loss of subsistence resources; Southwest tribes face severe drought threatening cultural practices
- **Water rights:** Many tribal communities still lack clean drinking water -- the Indian Health Service estimates a $3.5 billion backlog in water infrastructure needs

[source: EPA Environmental Justice](https://www.epa.gov/environmentaljustice) | [source: CEQ Tribal and NEPA](https://ceq.doe.gov/get-involved/tribes-and-nepa.html)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Weakened NEPA consultation requirements mean projects can proceed on or near tribal lands with less tribal input
- The Bears Ears co-management model -- a landmark in Indigenous governance -- could be undone if the monument is reduced
- Pipeline and extraction projects continue to target treaty lands and sacred sites
- As federal environmental enforcement declines, tribes with delegated authority become critical environmental regulators
- Climate change compounds historical injustices, threatening subsistence practices and cultural survival

### Who's Affected

- **574 federally recognized tribal nations** with treaty rights, trust responsibilities, and sovereignty
- **Alaska Native communities:** Facing the fastest climate impacts in the nation
- **Navajo Nation:** Over 500 abandoned uranium mines and ongoing water contamination
- **All Americans:** Indigenous land management protects watersheds, forests, and biodiversity that benefit everyone
- **Future generations:** Sacred sites and cultural landscapes, once destroyed, cannot be restored

### Learn Whose Land You Are On (5 minutes)

Understanding the Indigenous history of the land you live on is a first step toward accountability:

1. **Native Land Digital** ([native-land.ca](https://native-land.ca)) -- Interactive map showing Indigenous territories, treaties, and languages for any location
2. **Learn about the specific tribal nation(s)** whose land you occupy -- their history, current status, and issues they face
3. **Attend local Indigenous cultural events** when welcomed -- many tribes host public powwows, cultural festivals, and educational programs
4. **Acknowledge Indigenous land** at community events and meetings -- not as empty gesture, but as commitment to ongoing accountability

### Support Indigenous-Led Environmental Organizations (10 minutes)

These organizations are led by and for Indigenous communities:

- **Indigenous Environmental Network** ([ienearth.org](https://www.ienearth.org)) -- Grassroots Indigenous peoples working on environmental and economic justice
- **Honor the Earth** ([honorearth.org](https://www.honorearth.org)) -- Founded by Winona LaDuke, focuses on Indigenous environmental justice
- **NDN Collective** ([ndncollective.org](https://www.ndncollective.org)) -- Indigenous-led organization building power in communities
- **Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition** ([bearsearscoalition.org](https://www.bearsearscoalition.org)) -- Coalition of five tribes protecting Bears Ears
- **Native American Rights Fund (NARF)** ([narf.org](https://narf.org)) -- Legal representation for tribal nations

### Buy from Indigenous-Owned Businesses (ongoing)

Economic support strengthens tribal communities' ability to protect their lands:

1. **Beyondbuckskin.com** -- Directory of Native-owned businesses
2. **Native-owned outdoor brands:** Companies like [Natives Outdoors](https://www.nativesoutdoors.com) and [Eighth Generation](https://eighthgeneration.com) (owned by the Snoqualmie Tribe)
3. **Buy directly from tribal enterprises** when available
4. **Choose Indigenous-led tourism** when visiting public lands and national monuments

### Submit Public Comments on Tribal Land Actions

When federal agencies propose actions affecting tribal lands or sacred sites:

- **BLM ePlanning** ([eplanning.blm.gov](https://eplanning.blm.gov)) -- Environmental assessments for BLM projects
- **Regulations.gov** ([regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov)) -- Federal rulemaking affecting tribal consultation
- **FERC** ([ferc.gov](https://www.ferc.gov)) -- Pipeline and energy project proposals
- Comments mentioning treaty obligations, trust responsibility, and specific tribal impacts carry legal weight

## Sustained Actions

### Volunteer and Participate

- **Support land back efforts:** The Land Back movement advocates for returning public lands to Indigenous stewardship -- learn about campaigns in your area
- **Volunteer with Indigenous-led conservation:** Many tribal conservation programs accept volunteers for habitat restoration, water monitoring, and archaeological preservation
- **Attend tribal environmental public hearings** when they are open to the public -- your presence shows solidarity
- **Support tribal water rights settlements** -- many are pending in Congress and need public advocacy

### Push for Policy Change

- Advocate for **codifying tribal consultation** in federal law (not just executive orders that can be reversed)
- Support **co-management expansion** -- the Bears Ears model should be replicated for other culturally significant lands
- Push for **abandoned uranium mine cleanup** on Navajo Nation and other tribal lands
- Support **tribal water infrastructure funding** to address the $3.5 billion backlog
- Advocate for **free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC)** as the standard for projects affecting tribal lands

### Educate Yourself and Others

- **Read Indigenous voices:** Follow Indigenous journalists, scholars, and activists -- they tell their own stories best
- **Share resources:** Amplify Indigenous-led organizations on social media
- **Support Indigenous media:** Outlets like Indian Country Today, High Country News (Indigenous Affairs desk), and tribal newspapers
- **Learn treaty history:** Understanding the legal framework helps you advocate effectively
