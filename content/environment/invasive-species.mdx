# Invasive Species: Silent Destroyers of Ecosystems

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Invasive species are the **second-leading cause of species extinction** after habitat loss, contributing to the decline of **42% of imperiled species** in the United States. They cost the U.S. economy an estimated **$120 billion per year** in damages. In the West, invasive cheatgrass now blankets an estimated **100 million acres**, fueling wildfires that burn **3–10 times more frequently** than natural fire cycles. In Utah, quagga mussels — already established in Lake Powell — pose an existential threat to the state's water delivery infrastructure if they spread further.

Every outdoor recreationist, gardener, and boater can take direct action. The most effective prevention tool in Utah and the Colorado River basin costs nothing: Clean. Drain. Dry.

## Facts

### Key Facts

- Invasive species cost the U.S. an estimated **$120 billion per year** in damages [source: Science of the Total Environment — Economic Costs of Biological Invasions in the US](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0048969721063968)
- Non-native species are the primary cause of decline in **42% of imperiled U.S. species** [source: USDA NIFA — Invasive Species Affect Everyone](https://www.nifa.usda.gov/about-nifa/blogs/invasive-species-affect-everyone)
- Quagga mussels were confirmed in **Lake Powell in 2012** and remain the greatest aquatic invasive species threat to Utah's water systems [source: Utah DWR — What Are Aquatic Invasive Species](https://stdofthesea.utah.gov/ais/what-are-they/)]
- Invasive cheatgrass covers an estimated **100 million acres** of the western U.S. and burns **4 times more frequently** than native vegetation [source: PNAS — Invasive Grasses Increase Fire Occurrence](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1908253116)
- The emerald ash borer has killed **tens of millions of ash trees** and threatens all **8.7 billion ash trees** across North America [source: USDA Forest Service — Emerald Ash Borer](https://www.fs.usda.gov/detail/r9/home/?cid=FSEPRD1000085)

### [+] The Quagga Mussel Threat to Utah

Quagga mussels (*Dreissena bugensis*) — native to Ukraine — arrived in the Great Lakes in ballast water in the 1980s and reached Lake Mead by 2007. They were confirmed in Lake Powell in 2012. Quagga mussels reproduce explosively: a single female can produce **1 million eggs per year**, and mussels can reach densities of **700,000 per square meter** on hard surfaces.

**Why they are so destructive:**
- Clog water intake pipes, power plant cooling systems, irrigation equipment, and boat engines
- Each mussel filters up to **1 liter of water per day**, stripping out phytoplankton and disrupting food webs
- Damage boat hulls and dock infrastructure, costing individual boaters thousands of dollars in repairs
- Eliminate native mussel species through competitive displacement

**The water infrastructure risk:** Utah's water delivery systems — serving millions of people — draw from the Colorado River basin. If quagga mussels spread beyond Lake Powell into canals, reservoirs, and delivery pipes, the annual cost of removal and management would run into the **tens of millions of dollars**, directly raising water utility bills for Utah residents.

Utah now requires all boaters to complete a mandatory Mussel-Aware Boater course before launching on certain waters, and decontamination stations are operating at Lake Powell and other high-risk access points.

[source: Utah DWR — Mussel-Aware Boater Program](https://dwrcitizen.utah.gov/mabs/) | [source: Gephardt Daily — Utah Boaters and Invasive Species](https://gephardtdaily.com/local/dwr-reminds-boaters-in-utah-to-take-mandatory-mussel-aware-course/)

### [+] Cheatgrass and the Western Fire Crisis

Cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum*) is a winter annual grass native to Eurasia that arrived in contaminated crop seed in the 1890s. It now dominates an estimated **100 million acres** of the western United States — the largest invasive plant footprint in North American history.

**The grass-fire cycle:** Cheatgrass grows rapidly in early spring, dries out completely by June, and creates a continuous carpet of dry fuel across the landscape. Areas dominated by cheatgrass:
- Burn **3–10 times more frequently** than they would under natural fire cycles
- Experience fire return intervals of **3–10 years** instead of the natural **50–100 years** for sagebrush steppe
- Become even more cheatgrass-dominated after fire (native vegetation doesn't recover fast enough)
- Release stored carbon and accelerate regional warming and drying

Cheatgrass-fueled fires are now the primary driver of range fire expansion in the Great Basin, and according to Bureau of Land Management data, range fires now burn more acres than forest fires in most recent years.

[source: PNAS — Invasive Grasses and Fire](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1908253116) | [source: USDA Forest Service — Cheatgrass-Wildfire Cycle](https://www.fs.usda.gov/rm/pubs_int/int_gtr276/int_gtr276_011_018.pdf)

### [+] Other High-Impact Invasive Species

**Feral hogs (*Sus scrofa*):**
- An estimated **6–9 million feral hogs** occupy 35+ U.S. states
- Cause **$1.6 billion per year** in agricultural losses from rooting, wallowing, and crop destruction
- Destroy native plant communities, spread disease (brucellosis, swine fever), and kill ground-nesting birds
- Nearly impossible to eradicate once established; populations can double in 4 months

[source: USDA NIFA — Feral Swine](https://www.nifa.usda.gov/about-nifa/blogs/invasive-species-affect-everyone)

**Asian carp (Silver, Bighead, and Grass carp):**
- Filter up to **40% of their body weight in plankton per day**, starving native fish
- Can jump **10 feet** out of the water when disturbed by boat motors, injuring boaters
- Reached the Illinois River just miles from Lake Michigan before intensive containment efforts
- Would devastate the $7 billion Great Lakes fishery if established

**Purple loosestrife (*Lythrum salicaria*):**
- A single plant produces **2.7 million seeds per year**
- Colonizes wetlands and river margins, eliminating native marsh vegetation that waterfowl depend on
- Now present in all 48 contiguous states; designated a noxious weed in most

**Buffelgrass (*Cenchrus ciliaris*) in the Southwest:**
- Invading Sonoran Desert and increasingly appearing in southern Utah
- Transforms fire-free desert into fire-prone grassland — saguaro cacti cannot survive fire
- Climate warming is expanding its range northward

[source: National Invasive Species Information Center — Wildland Fire](https://www.invasivespeciesinfo.gov/subject/wildland-fire)

### [+] Pathways of Introduction and Spread

Understanding how invasive species arrive and spread is key to prevention:

**Ballast water:** Commercial ships take on seawater as ballast in one port and discharge it in another, releasing any organisms in the water. This pathway brought quagga and zebra mussels from the Black Sea to the Great Lakes in the 1980s. The U.S. now requires ballast water treatment on commercial vessels, but enforcement is inconsistent.

**Live animal and plant trade:** Exotic pets (Burmese pythons in Florida, red-eared slider turtles nationally), aquarium releases (Asian carp, various fish), and ornamental plants (English ivy, burning bush, Bradford pear) are major pathways. Many invasive plants are still sold in nurseries.

**Contaminated firewood:** Moving firewood across state lines or long distances spreads bark beetles, emerald ash borer, and fungal pathogens like oak wilt. "Buy it where you burn it" is now a federal recommendation for all campers.

**Recreational equipment:** Boats, kayaks, waders, and fishing gear carry aquatic plants, mussels, mudsnails, and pathogens. A single boat can move invasive species between watersheds hundreds of miles apart in a single afternoon.

**Climate change:** Warming winters are enabling many invasive species to expand north into previously inhospitable ranges. Kudzu, the "vine that ate the South," is now established in Ohio and Michigan. Buffelgrass is moving north through Utah.

[source: National Invasive Species Information Center — Pathways](https://www.invasivespeciesinfo.gov/)

### [+] Federal and State Response

**USDA APHIS (Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service)** coordinates federal invasive species response, including the National Invasive Species Council (NISC) and its management plan. Budget cuts have repeatedly reduced monitoring and rapid response capacity.

**Utah Division of Wildlife Resources** operates the Clean Watercraft Inspection Program with checkpoints at major boat ramps, particularly at Lake Powell, Flaming Gorge, Jordanelle, and Utah Lake. The program decontaminated significantly more boats in 2025 than in previous years.

**USFS and BLM** manage cheatgrass treatment programs using herbicide (imazapic), seeding with native grasses, and targeted grazing — but the scale of infestation far exceeds available funding.

[source: Utah DWR — Aquatic Invasive Species Program](https://stdofthesea.utah.gov/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Invasive species are not a background problem — they are actively restructuring western ecosystems, with cheatgrass-driven fires increasing in frequency and severity every year
- Lake Powell's quagga mussel infestation is a warning: every other Utah waterbody is one contaminated boat away from the same outcome
- The pet trade and horticultural industry continue to sell invasive species; consumer choices matter
- Federal prevention budgets are small relative to the damage costs — public participation in monitoring and reporting provides data that guides where limited management dollars go
- Climate change is accelerating range expansion, meaning species that couldn't survive Utah winters may be able to within a decade

### Who's Affected

- **Utah boaters and anglers:** Mandatory decontamination inspection programs, risk of boat engine damage, and potential water bill increases if mussels spread
- **Western ranchers and farmers:** Cheatgrass reduces forage quality for livestock, accelerates fire cycles that destroy rangeland, and competes with crops
- **Wildfire-adjacent communities:** Cheatgrass is a direct driver of fire risk in sagebrush country; faster fire cycles mean more homes in the fire zone
- **Fishermen and water-dependent recreation:** Invasive plants and animals degrade water quality, reduce native fish populations, and harm the recreational fishing economy
- **Homeowners with ash trees:** Emerald ash borer has killed hundreds of millions of ash trees across the East and Midwest and continues to spread west
- **Hunters:** Chronic Wasting Disease, spread partly through infected deer movement, affects deer and elk populations in 31 states including Utah

## Quick Actions

### Clean, Drain, Dry Your Watercraft (Every Single Time)

This is the single most important action Utah boaters can take to prevent quagga mussel spread to uninfested waters:

1. **Clean** all visible mud, plants, fish, and animals from your boat, trailer, motor, and equipment before leaving any water body
2. **Drain** all water from bilge, livewells, ballast tanks, motor, and any other water-holding compartment
3. **Dry** all equipment completely — minimum 5–7 days in warm, dry conditions, or use a high-pressure hot water wash at an inspection station

**Utah law requires** all boaters to complete a **Mussel-Aware Boater Safety course** before launching on designated waters. The free course takes about 30 minutes at [dwrcitizen.utah.gov/mabs](https://dwrcitizen.utah.gov/mabs).

### Report Invasive Species Sightings via EDDMapS (5 minutes)

**EDDMapS** ([eddmaps.org](https://www.eddmaps.org)) is a free, expert-verified database of invasive species observations, operated by the University of Georgia's Center for Invasive Species and Ecosystem Health.

- Download the free EDDMapS app for iOS or Android
- Photograph the species, note the GPS location, and submit
- Reports are reviewed by expert verifiers before going into the public database
- Your observations guide where limited management funding is spent

Also report to your state: Utah invasive species reports go to [utah.gov/invasivespecies](https://ag.utah.gov/plants-pests/invasive-species/)

### Don't Move Firewood

Firewood carries the emerald ash borer, oak wilt fungus, and other tree-killing pathogens. The rule is simple: **Buy it where you burn it.** Don't transport firewood across county or state lines. This single behavior change has been credited with significantly slowing the spread of the emerald ash borer in some regions.

Look for the certified heat-treated firewood seal when buying packaged firewood.

[dontmovefirewood.org](https://www.dontmovefirewood.org)

### Audit Your Garden for Invasive Plants

Many common landscape plants are invasive. The following are particularly problematic and are still sold in nurseries in many states:
- **English ivy** (*Hedera helix*) — outcompetes native groundcover and climbs trees
- **Burning bush** (*Euonymus alatus*) — spreads from ornamental plantings into woodlands
- **Bradford/Callery pear** (*Pyrus calleryana*) — escapes cultivation and forms thorny thickets
- **Japanese barberry** (*Berberis thunbergii*) — creates tick habitat and displaces native shrubs
- **Yellow flag iris** (*Iris pseudacorus*) — invades wetlands and waterways

Replace these with native alternatives. Contact your state's noxious weed list or the **Native Plant Society** for regionally appropriate replacements.

### Support Native Plant Restoration

Native plants are the biological defense against invasive colonization — healthy, diverse native plant communities are harder for invasives to penetrate:
- Buy native plants from reputable nurseries (ask for locally sourced seed stock)
- Participate in native seed collection and restoration projects through your local land trust
- Advocate for native plantings in public parks, roadsides, and restoration projects on public lands

### Advocate for Stronger Nursery and Pet Trade Regulations

Many invasive species are still legally sold in garden centers and pet stores. Contact your state legislature and USDA APHIS to:
- Expand the list of prohibited invasive plants that cannot be sold
- Require invasive species disclosure on plant tags
- Strengthen restrictions on live aquatic animal sales
- Increase penalties for releasing invasive species
