# Marine Mammals Under Threat

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972 established the legal foundation for protecting ocean-going mammals in US waters — but key protections are being weakened. The **North Atlantic right whale** population sits at just **384 individuals**, with entanglement in fishing gear and vessel strikes as the leading killers. A proposed vessel speed rule that would have protected more whales was **withdrawn in January 2025**, and further rollbacks are now proposed. The **Cook Inlet beluga whale** has declined **80% since 1979**, to roughly 331 animals. **Southern Resident killer whales** number only **73** and are starving due to Chinook salmon collapse. PFAS "forever chemicals" bioaccumulate through marine food chains into top predators. Navy sonar has been linked to beaked whale mass strandings.

These are not distant or hypothetical threats. Several populations are at real risk of extinction within decades.

## Facts

### North Atlantic Right Whale: On the Brink

The North Atlantic right whale is one of the most endangered large mammals on Earth. The 2024 population estimate is **384 individuals**, a modest 2.1% increase from 2023's estimate of 376 — the result of intensive conservation efforts and some years of reduced mortality. [source: New England Aquarium - Right Whale Population](https://www.neaq.org/about-us/press-room/press-releases/north-atlantic-right-whale-population-continues-slow-growth/)

Despite this fragile uptick, the population remains critically low. An "Unusual Mortality Event" has been declared since 2017, during which more than 30 whales have died or been seriously injured. In 2024 alone: **five deaths, 16 entanglements** (10 with attached fishing gear), and **eight vessel strikes**. [source: NOAA Unusual Mortality Event](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/national/marine-life-distress/2017-2025-north-atlantic-right-whale-unusual-mortality-event)

**Entanglement in fishing gear** is the #1 killer. Rope from lobster and crab pot lines runs vertically through the water column; right whales swim through it and become trapped. A single entanglement can cause a whale to drag gear for months, leading to exhaustion, infection, and death. **Vessel strikes** are the second leading cause — a whale struck by a ship at speed rarely survives.

**The speed rule rollback:** NOAA proposed a rule that would have required vessels **35 feet or longer** to slow to 10 knots when whales were expected nearby — expanding the existing rule that only applies to vessels 65 feet or longer. That proposal was **formally withdrawn in January 2025**. [source: New England Aquarium - Speed Rule Withdrawal](https://www.neaq.org/about-us/press-room/press-releases/formal-withdrawal-of-vessel-speed-rule-is-a-serious-setback-for-the-critically-endangered-north-atlantic-right-whale/)

Now, as of March 2026, NOAA is considering further **deregulatory action** to replace seasonal speed restrictions with "technology-based strike-avoidance measures" — a proposal conservation scientists say is untested and inadequate. [source: Federal Register ANPRM March 2026](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/03/04/2026-04260/advance-notice-of-proposed-rulemaking-to-amend-the-north-atlantic-right-whale-vessel-strike)

### [+] Cook Inlet Beluga: Alaska's Forgotten Whale

The **Cook Inlet beluga whale** is a distinct, endangered population found only in Cook Inlet, Alaska. The population has declined approximately **80% since 1979**, from roughly 1,300 whales to a 2022 survey estimate of **331 individuals**. NOAA listed them as endangered under the ESA in 2008 and designated Critical Habitat in 2011. [source: NOAA Cook Inlet Beluga](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/feature-story/new-abundance-estimate-endangered-cook-inlet-beluga-whales/)

Despite decades of protection, the population has not recovered. Threats include prey fish depletion (particularly eulachon smelt), noise and vessel disturbance in an increasingly busy inlet, pollution, and the effects of a warming ocean on prey availability. Long-term surveys show belugas now occupy only a fraction of their historic seasonal range. [source: NOAA - Beluga Range Decline](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/feature-story/long-term-surveys-show-endangered-beluga-population-inhabiting-just-portion-historic)

A 2024 advocacy victory halted a commercial eulachon smelt fishery in Cook Inlet that threatened the belugas' critical prey. But the underlying recovery problem — a population that refuses to grow despite nominal ESA protections — remains unresolved. [source: Alaska Wildlife Alliance - Beluga Victory](https://www.akwildlife.org/news/2024/3/18/victory-for-endangered-beluga-whales-cook-inlet-smelt-fishery)

### [+] Southern Resident Killer Whales: Starving in the Salish Sea

The **Southern Resident killer whales** — the J, K, and L pods that live in the inland waters of Washington State and British Columbia — number just **73 individuals** as of the most recent census. Between 2023 and 2024, the population dropped from 75 to 73; K pod hit its lowest count since surveys began, with just 14 animals. [source: Puget Sound Info - SRKW Vital Signs](https://vitalsigns.pugetsoundinfo.wa.gov/VitalSignIndicator/Detail/32)

Southern Residents feed almost exclusively on **Chinook salmon**, their preferred prey — fat-rich, large, older fish. Chinook stocks throughout the Pacific Northwest are far below historic levels due to dams, habitat loss, and warming rivers. **Prey limitation is the primary constraint on Southern Resident recovery**, and a science panel has found that government initiatives on both sides of the US-Canada border are insufficient. [source: National Wildlife Federation - SRKW](https://blog.nwf.org/2025/04/on-the-hunt-southern-resident-killer-whales-hunger-for-vanishing-salmon/)

A malnourished female orca cannot carry a pregnancy to term — "peanut head" syndrome, visible fat depletion behind the skull, marks starving whales. The population showed no sign of recovery in the 2025 annual survey.

### [+] PFAS "Forever Chemicals" in Marine Mammals

**Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS)** — synthetic chemicals used in everything from non-stick cookware to firefighting foam — are now found in marine mammals worldwide. Because PFAS do not break down in the environment and are fat-soluble, they **bioaccumulate** up the food chain. Marine mammals at the top of the food chain — dolphins, orcas, seals, porpoises — accumulate the highest concentrations.

Research consistently finds PFAS in livers and kidneys of marine mammals at concentrations linked to:
- Immune system suppression
- Reproductive failure and developmental problems in offspring
- Elevated autoimmune responses and tissue toxicity

Harbor porpoises, white-sided dolphins, gray seals, and harbor seals consistently show high PFAS accumulation. Bottlenose dolphin studies document chronic exposure effects at the population level. [source: NIH - PFAS Bioaccumulation Patterns](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10228145/) | [source: Frontiers - PFAS in Marine Mammals](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/environmental-chemistry/articles/10.3389/fenvc.2024.1403728/full)

Because PFAS sources are land-based (industrial discharge, firefighting foam, consumer products), reducing marine mammal exposure requires action far from the ocean — EPA regulation of PFAS at the source. The Trump administration has moved to narrow EPA's PFAS cleanup obligations.

### [+] Navy Sonar and Beaked Whale Strandings

The US Navy's **mid-frequency active sonar (MFAS)**, used for antisubmarine warfare, has been linked to **mass strandings of beaked whales** — a connection established by multiple independent research teams. Beaked whales are deep divers sensitive to acoustic disturbance; evidence suggests sonar causes them to surface too rapidly, causing hemorrhaging and gas bubble formation similar to decompression sickness.

A study in the Bahamas found 17 animals — including 14 beaked whales — stranded on three islands within 36 hours of US Navy destroyer sonar exercises nearby. Research on strandings in the Mariana Islands found a significant correlation between naval sonar use and beaked whale strandings. [source: Royal Society - Beaked Whale Strandings and Sonar](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspb.2020.0070)

The Marine Mammal Protection Act requires the Navy to obtain **Incidental Take Authorizations** from NOAA for marine mammals harmed by sonar. Critics argue these authorizations are too permissive and do not adequately protect sensitive beaked whale populations. As of March 2026, NOAA has received a new Navy request for MMPA authorization for Surveillance Towed Array Sensor System sonar operations across the North Pacific through 2033. [source: Federal Register - Navy SURTASS 2026](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/03/10/2026-04668/takes-of-marine-mammals-incidental-to-specified-activities-taking-marine-mammals-incidental-to-us)

### [+] The Marine Mammal Protection Act: History and Rollback Threats

The MMPA (1972) was landmark legislation that banned the killing or harassment of all marine mammals in US waters and by US citizens, regardless of conservation status. It established a framework of science-based management and prohibited the import of marine mammals or products taken in violation of the Act.

The MMPA has delivered real results: some populations have recovered substantially. But the Act faces ongoing pressure:

- **Incidental take loopholes:** The fishing and Navy incidental take authorization system allows significant harm with limited public accountability
- **Bycatch:** Hundreds of thousands of marine mammals are caught and killed or injured in fishing gear annually — a "take" authorized as incidental to commercial fishing
- **Enforcement capacity:** NOAA's marine mammal enforcement budget has faced cuts
- **International loopholes:** The MMPA prohibits imports of seafood from countries that cannot demonstrate comparable marine mammal bycatch standards — a provision that has been difficult to enforce

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Multiple marine mammal populations are at or near critical thresholds where small changes in mortality rates can tip them toward extinction
- Regulatory rollbacks — the vessel speed rule withdrawal, potential MMPA incidental take expansions — remove the buffers that allowed modest population recovery
- PFAS contamination and prey collapse are systemic threats that cannot be addressed through single-species protection alone; they require economy-wide policy changes
- The US is legally obligated under the MMPA to protect these species in US waters — rollbacks may be legally challengeable

### Who's Affected

- **Marine mammals themselves:** Right whales, beaked whales, orcas, belugas, porpoises, seals, dolphins — populations that took decades to partially recover face new pressure
- **Commercial fishing industry:** Entanglement gear modifications, speed restrictions, and bycatch requirements impose real costs — but so do species listings and litigation
- **Shipping industry:** Vessel speed restrictions reduce fuel efficiency and cargo throughput — a genuine economic tradeoff
- **Coastal tourism and whale watching:** These industries depend on healthy whale populations; a right whale extinction would end the New England whale-watching industry
- **Indigenous and subsistence communities:** Alaska Native communities have cultural and food security ties to beluga whales and other marine mammals
- **Scientists and future generations:** Some species, like beaked whales, remain poorly understood — losing them would eliminate irreplaceable knowledge

### Submit Public Comments on the Right Whale Speed Rule

NOAA issued an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in March 2026 to amend the vessel speed rule. This comment period is an opportunity to put your voice on record:

- Go to [regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov)
- Search for docket **NOAA-NMFS-2026-0043** or the right whale speed rule ANPRM
- Submit a comment explaining why strong speed restrictions are necessary
- Personal stories, local connections, and specific science references make comments more effective

### Choose Sustainable Seafood (Ongoing)

Your seafood choices directly affect the demand for fisheries that use entanglement-prone gear. Use the **Monterey Bay Aquarium Seafood Watch** to identify ocean-friendly options:

- Download the free [Seafood Watch app](https://www.seafoodwatch.org/) or use the website
- Look for "Best Choice" or "Good Alternative" ratings
- Ask restaurants and grocery stores what their sourcing standards are — consumer pressure works
- Choose trap-free, line-caught, or aquaculture options where available

### Report Marine Mammal Sightings and Strandings

Your observations help scientists track populations and respond to distress:

- **NOAA Marine Mammal Stranding Hotline:** 1-866-755-6622 — call immediately if you see a stranded, entangled, or injured marine mammal
- **Whale Alert app** ([whalealert.org](https://www.whalealert.org)) — report right whale sightings in real time; reports go directly to mariners and NOAA
- Do not approach, touch, or attempt to free entangled animals — contact professionals

### Support the Whale Entanglement Response Network

When a right whale is found entangled, trained response teams attempt disentanglement at sea — a dangerous, specialized operation. You can support this work:

- Donate to the **Center for Coastal Studies** ([coastalstudies.org](https://www.coastalstudies.org)), which runs the NOAA-authorized large whale disentanglement team
- Share information about the entanglement hotline so more fishers know to call rather than cut lines

### Advocate Against Navy Sonar Expansion in Sensitive Habitats

When NOAA accepts public comment on Navy Incidental Take Authorization requests, submit comments urging stronger beaked whale protections:

- Monitor [regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov) for NMFS/Navy sonar incidental take dockets
- Support organizations litigating Navy sonar cases (NRDC, Earthjustice)
- Contact the Senate Armed Services Committee to request stronger environmental oversight of Navy sonar training

### Urge EPA to Strengthen PFAS Regulation

Marine mammal PFAS contamination comes from land-based sources. Reducing it requires upstream action:

- Contact the EPA directly via [epa.gov/pfas](https://www.epa.gov/pfas) to comment on PFAS regulatory proceedings
- Ask your senators to support the PFAS Action Act to establish comprehensive cleanup standards
- Support local efforts to eliminate PFAS-containing firefighting foam (AFFF) use at airports and military bases near coastal watersheds
