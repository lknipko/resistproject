# Microplastics Everywhere

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Microplastics -- tiny plastic fragments smaller than 5mm -- have infiltrated every corner of our world. An estimated 170 trillion plastic particles float in the ocean's surface waters alone. Humans may ingest up to 5 grams of plastic per week (roughly the weight of a credit card), and microplastics have now been detected in human blood, lungs, placentas, and brains. A landmark 2024 study found that people with microplastics in their arterial plaque had over 4x the risk of heart attack, stroke, or death. Despite this, the U.S. has no federal regulations on microplastics. In April 2026, EPA added microplastics to its drinking water contaminant watch list for the first time -- but binding rules may be years away.

## Facts

### Key Points

- **170 trillion** plastic particles circulate in the ocean's surface waters, weighing roughly 2.3 million metric tons [source: 5 Gyres / PLoS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0281596)
- **92%** of all ocean plastic is microplastics (smaller than 5mm) [source: Our World in Data](https://ourworldindata.org/plastic-pollution)
- Over **400 million tonnes** of new plastic are produced globally each year [source: UN Environment Programme](https://www.unep.org/topics/chemicals-and-pollution-action/pollution-action/plastics)
- Microplastics have been found in human **blood, lungs, placentas, brains, and arterial plaque** [source: Stanford Medicine](https://med.stanford.edu/news/insights/2025/01/microplastics-in-body-polluted-tiny-plastic-fragments.html)
- In April 2026, EPA designated microplastics as a **priority contaminant group** for drinking water for the first time [source: EPA Press Release](https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-takes-bold-action-ensure-drinking-water-safe-microplastics-pharmaceuticals-and)

### [+] Sources of Microplastics

Microplastics enter the environment through four main pathways:

**1. Synthetic Clothing Fibers (35% of ocean microplastics)**

Every load of laundry sheds over 1,900 microfibers per garment. Fleece garments shed 170% more fibers than other synthetic fabrics. Between 200,000 and 500,000 tonnes of textile microfibers enter the global marine environment each year.

[source: European Environment Agency](https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/analysis/publications/microplastics-from-textiles-towards-a-circular-economy-for-textiles-in-europe) | [source: The Nature Conservancy](https://www.nature.org/content/dam/tnc/nature/en/documents/210322TNCBain_Pre-ConsumerMicrofiberEmissionsv6.pdf)

**2. Tire Wear (28% of ocean microplastics)**

Tires are composed partly of synthetic styrene-butadiene rubber and shed microplastic particles as they wear. U.S. tires alone produce about 1.8 million tons of microplastics annually.

[source: Pew Charitable Trusts](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2021/03/30/microplastics-are-a-big-and-growing-part-of-global-pollution)

**3. Plastic Packaging Breakdown**

Under sunlight, wind, and waves, larger plastic items degrade into microplastic fragments (0.001-5mm). Single-use packaging -- bottles, bags, food containers, wrappers -- is the single largest category of plastic waste reaching the ocean. Plastic pellets ("nurdles") used in manufacturing also enter ecosystems through industrial spills.

[source: ITRC Microplastics](https://mp-1.itrcweb.org/introduction/)

**4. Personal Care Products & Industrial Sources**

Microbeads in exfoliating scrubs and cosmetics (now banned in some countries), paint chips, agricultural plastic mulch, and construction materials also contribute microplastic pollution.

[source: Wikipedia - Microplastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microplastics)

### [+] In Our Bodies

Microplastics have been detected in virtually every human organ tested:

**Blood (2022)**

A Dutch study in *Environment International* detected microplastics in **77% of blood samples** from 22 healthy volunteers. PET plastic (used in bottles and food packaging) was the most common type, found in 50% of samples. Polystyrene (disposable food containers) was found in over a third.

[source: Environment International - Discovery in Human Blood](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0160412022001258)

**Brain (2025)**

University of New Mexico researchers found microplastics in human brains at concentrations **higher than in any other organ** tested. The median concentration in 2024 brain samples was nearly 5,000 micrograms per gram -- almost 0.5% plastic by weight. Concentrations have **increased 50% in just eight years**, doubling roughly every 10-15 years. People who had been diagnosed with dementia had up to 10x more plastic in their brains than others.

[source: Nature Medicine - Bioaccumulation in Human Brains](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03453-1) | [source: UNM Health Sciences](https://hscnews.unm.edu/news/hsc-newsroom-post-microplastics-human-brains)

**Placenta (2021)**

Microplastics were found in **all ten human placentas** examined in a 2021 Italian study, including in intracellular compartments. A landmark 2020 study ("Plasticenta") first documented microplastics on both fetal and maternal sides of the placenta. Studies in mice show nanoplastics can cross the placental barrier and enter fetal organs, including the brain.

[source: Environment International - Plasticenta](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0160412020322297) | [source: MDPI - Intracellular Microplastics in Placentas](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/19/18/11593)

**Lungs, Arteries, and Other Organs**

Microplastics have been confirmed in human lung tissue, liver, kidney, testes, and breast milk. A March 2024 study published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* found microplastics and nanoplastics in the arterial plaque of patients undergoing carotid surgery. Patients with plastics in their plaque had a **4.5x higher risk** of heart attack, stroke, or death over the following 34 months.

[source: NEJM - MNPs and Cardiovascular Events](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2309822)

### [+] In Our Water & Food

**Bottled vs. Tap Water**

A 2024 Columbia University study found an average of **240,000 plastic particles per liter** of bottled water -- 10 to 100 times more than previous estimates. About 90% were nanoplastics (smaller than 1 micrometer). Bottled water contains roughly **3x more nanoplastics** than treated tap water.

[source: NIH - Plastic Particles in Bottled Water](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/nih-research-matters/plastic-particles-bottled-water) | [source: Ohio State University](https://news.osu.edu/some-bottled-water-worse-than-tap-for-microplastics-study-shows/)

**Human Ingestion Estimates**

People who drink only bottled water may ingest an additional **90,000 microplastic particles per year** compared to about 4,000 for those who drink tap water. A 2019 WWF/University of Newcastle analysis estimated people could ingest up to 5 grams of plastic weekly -- roughly the weight of a credit card -- though this estimate has been debated by other researchers.

[source: WWF - Plastic Ingestion Study](https://wwf.panda.org/wwf_news/?348337%2FRevealed-plastic-ingestion-by-people-could-be-equating-to-a-credit-card-a-week=)

**Food Contamination**

Microplastics have been detected in seafood, honey, salt, beer, fruits, vegetables, and ultra-processed foods. Foods packaged in plastic and ultra-processed foods tend to contain higher microplastic concentrations.

[source: World Economic Forum](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/02/how-microplastics-get-into-the-food-chain/)

### [+] In the Ocean

An estimated **75 to 199 million tons** of plastic waste currently exists in the oceans, with approximately 8-10 million metric tons added each year. Roughly **170 trillion plastic particles** float on the ocean's surface, and an additional **11 million metric tons** of plastic has been deposited on the seafloor.

Microplastics are now found in every marine environment tested -- from the deepest ocean trenches to Arctic sea ice. They are ingested by marine organisms at every level of the food chain, from plankton to whales, and a 2026 study found that microplastics are undermining the ocean's ability to absorb carbon dioxide.

[source: Condor Ferries - Ocean Plastic Statistics](https://www.condorferries.co.uk/plastic-in-the-ocean-statistics) | [source: ScienceDaily - Microplastics and Carbon Absorption](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/01/260116035322.htm) | [source: Plastic Pollution Coalition](https://www.plasticpollutioncoalition.org/blog/2025/6/26/how-microplastics-are-changing-the-oceans)

### [+] Regulatory Status

**Federal (United States)**

- **April 2, 2026:** EPA designated microplastics as a **priority contaminant group** on the draft Contaminant Candidate List 6 (CCL 6) -- the first step toward potential drinking water regulation. Actual binding standards could still be years away. [source: EPA - CCL 6 Announcement](https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-takes-bold-action-ensure-drinking-water-safe-microplastics-pharmaceuticals-and)
- **April 2026:** HHS Secretary announced the **STOMP initiative** (Systematic Targeting of MicroPlastics) -- a $144 million program to measure microplastics in the human body and research health effects. [source: Chemical & Engineering News](https://cen.acs.org/environment/water/microplastics-water-epa-hhs-sdwa-ccl/104/web/2026/04)
- **July 2025:** Bipartisan bill **H.R. 4486** introduced, directing HHS to study and report on human health impacts of microplastics in food and water. [source: National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/microplastics-2025-regulatory-trends-and-updates)
- **2025:** Rep. Haley Stevens (D-MI) introduced **H.R. 4903**, requesting $10 million/year through 2030 for plastic exposure health research. [source: Bergeson & Campbell](https://www.lawbc.com/microplastics-in-2025-regulatory-trends-and-updates/)
- There are currently **no federal limits** on microplastics in drinking water, food, or consumer products.

**State Level**

California has led state-level action, becoming the first state to require microplastic testing of drinking water (2020) and developing standardized testing methods. Several states have banned microbeads in personal care products.

**International**

The UN Global Plastics Treaty negotiations aim to create a binding international agreement to reduce plastic pollution, including microplastics. The EU has restricted intentionally added microplastics in products.

[source: Chemical Processing - Microplastics Regulation](https://www.chemicalprocessing.com/voices/compliance-advisor/article/55328532/microplastics-regulation-revs-up-in-2025-more-action-expected-in-2026)

## Analysis

### What This Means

The science on microplastics is where asbestos research was decades ago -- we can see the contamination is everywhere and early evidence points to serious health consequences, but the full picture of harm is still emerging.

What we know:
- Microplastics cause **inflammation** in the respiratory and gastrointestinal systems and **disrupt hormonal regulation** (endocrine disruption)
- They carry harmful chemicals (plasticizers, flame retardants, heavy metals) deep into the body
- Nanoplastics cross the **blood-brain barrier** and the **placental barrier**
- The cardiovascular evidence (NEJM 2024) is the strongest human health signal yet -- but is observational, not proof of causation
- Brain concentrations are **increasing over time**, suggesting cumulative bioaccumulation

What we don't know:
- The threshold at which microplastic exposure causes disease
- Long-term effects of chronic low-level exposure over a lifetime
- Whether microplastics directly cause neurodegenerative disease or cancer in humans
- Which types and sizes of plastic particles are most harmful
- How nanoplastics interact with medications, nutrients, and other chemicals in the body

The regulatory landscape is moving, but slowly. The EPA's CCL 6 designation is a first step, not a regulation. Even if microplastics are formally listed, binding drinking water standards could be 5-10 years away under normal regulatory timelines.

### Who's Affected

- **Everyone:** Microplastics are in the air, water, and food supply globally -- no one is unexposed
- **Infants and children:** May face higher risks due to developing organs, hand-to-mouth behavior, and exposure through breast milk and formula
- **Pregnant women:** Microplastics cross the placenta and enter fetal circulation
- **Communities dependent on bottled water:** May have 20x higher exposure than tap water drinkers
- **Coastal and fishing communities:** Higher seafood consumption means higher microplastic ingestion
- **Workers in plastic manufacturing and textile industries:** Occupational exposure at much higher levels

### Reduce Your Personal Exposure

These steps won't eliminate exposure, but can meaningfully reduce it:

1. **Switch from bottled to filtered tap water** -- Use a reverse osmosis or carbon block filter. This alone could reduce your annual microplastic ingestion from ~90,000 to ~4,000 particles. [source: Beyond Plastics](https://www.beyondplastics.org/fact-sheets/microplastics-exposure)
2. **Don't microwave food in plastic** -- Heat causes plastics to shed particles into food. Use glass or ceramic containers. [source: Fortune Well](https://fortune.com/well/2025/03/13/microplastics-reduce-exposure/)
3. **Install a washing machine microfiber filter** -- External filters capture 52-99% of microfibers from laundry. Brands like PlanetCare and Filtrol are available. [source: PlanetCare](https://planetcare.org/)
4. **Choose natural fiber clothing when possible** -- Cotton, wool, linen, and hemp don't shed microplastics. When you do wash synthetics, use cold water and lower spin speeds.
5. **Reduce plastic food packaging** -- Store food in glass, metal, or ceramic. Avoid ultra-processed foods, which tend to contain more microplastics. [source: PIRG](https://pirg.org/articles/how-to-protect-yourself-from-microplastics/)
6. **Dust and vacuum regularly** -- Indoor dust is a major source of microplastic inhalation. Use a vacuum with a HEPA filter.

### Push for Local Plastic Bans

Your city or county can ban the biggest sources of microplastic pollution:

- **Plastic bag bans** -- Over 500 US municipalities have them. If yours doesn't, attend a city council meeting and advocate for one.
- **Polystyrene (Styrofoam) bans** -- These break into microplastics rapidly. Push for bans on food service polystyrene.
- **Microfiber filter mandates** -- France requires washing machine filters by 2025. Push your state to follow.

### Test Your Water

- **Request your utility's Consumer Confidence Report** -- required annually, shows what's been tested
- **Independent testing:** [Tap Score](https://mytapscore.com) offers microplastic-specific water testing ($150-200)
- **Check EWG's Tap Water Database** ([ewg.org/tapwater](https://www.ewg.org/tapwater)) for contaminants in your zip code

- **International Coastal Cleanup** ([oceanconservancy.org/trash-free-seas](https://oceanconservancy.org/trash-free-seas/international-coastal-cleanup/)) -- September each year, 100+ countries

### Do a Personal Plastic Audit

Track every piece of single-use plastic you touch for one week. Most people are shocked.

- Photograph each item before discarding
- Identify the top 5 sources and find reusable alternatives
- Share your results on social media to normalize the conversation
