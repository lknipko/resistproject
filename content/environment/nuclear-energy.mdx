# Nuclear Energy

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Nuclear energy generates 18% of US electricity and 47% of America's zero-emissions power -- more than utility-scale wind and solar combined. The US fleet of 94 reactors avoids 437 million metric tons of CO2 per year, equivalent to removing 95 million cars from the road. Nuclear has the lowest death rate per unit of energy of any major power source, including wind and solar. But real tradeoffs exist: no permanent waste repository has been built despite 80+ years of nuclear power, new plants cost far more than renewables, and uranium mining has left a toxic legacy on Indigenous lands. Public support is at historic highs (72%), and both parties support nuclear expansion. Small modular reactors (SMRs) are advancing through NRC approval but won't deploy until the early 2030s.

This page presents the data on both sides. Nuclear energy's role in climate action is a legitimate scientific and policy question -- not a culture war.

## Facts

### Nuclear's Role in US Electricity

The United States operates 94 commercial nuclear reactors at 54 plants across 28 states -- the largest nuclear fleet in the world. In 2025, nuclear power provided approximately 18% of all US electricity.

**Climate impact:**
- Nuclear reactors produce zero carbon dioxide during operation
- Lifecycle emissions are approximately 3.0 gCO2e/kWh -- comparable to wind and lower than solar
- The US nuclear fleet avoids 437 million metric tons of CO2 emissions per year
- Nuclear provides 47% of America's zero-emissions electricity -- more than utility-scale wind and solar combined

For context, replacing the existing nuclear fleet with natural gas would add roughly 437 million metric tons of CO2 annually -- equivalent to the emissions of 95 million passenger vehicles.

[source: Nuclear Energy Institute - Climate](https://www.nei.org/advantages/climate) | [source: EIA - Nuclear Power and the Environment](https://www.eia.gov/energyexplained/nuclear/nuclear-power-and-the-environment.php)

[source: Lifecycle Emissions Study (2025)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/jiec.70008) | [source: C2ES - Nuclear Energy](https://www.c2es.org/content/nuclear-energy/)

### Safety Record: What the Data Shows

Nuclear energy has the lowest death rate per unit of energy produced of any major power source. This includes all deaths from accidents, air pollution, and occupational hazards.

**Deaths per terawatt-hour of electricity (Our World in Data):**

| Energy Source | Deaths per TWh |
|--------------|---------------|
| Brown coal | 32.7 |
| Coal | 24.6 |
| Oil | 18.4 |
| Natural gas | 2.8 |
| Wind | 0.04 |
| Nuclear | 0.03 |
| Solar | 0.02 |

From 1970 to 1992, there were 39 on-the-job deaths at nuclear power plants worldwide, compared to 6,400 at coal plants and 1,200 at natural gas plants during the same period. Coal power plants kill an estimated 24,000 Americans per year from air pollution.

**Major accidents:**
- **Three Mile Island (1979):** Partial meltdown in Pennsylvania. No deaths. No measurable health effects in follow-up studies. TMI Unit 1 (undamaged) continued operating until 2019 and is now being restarted.
- **Chernobyl (1986):** Explosion and fire at a Soviet RBMK reactor (a design never used in the US). 31 immediate deaths. WHO estimates up to 4,000 eventual cancer deaths. Led to global safety reforms.
- **Fukushima (2011):** Three meltdowns after a magnitude 9.0 earthquake and tsunami in Japan. One confirmed radiation death. ~2,200 deaths attributed to the evacuation itself. Japanese government investigation found the accident was "man-made" due to regulatory failures.

No nuclear accident in an OECD country has resulted in 5 or more prompt deaths. The risk of a nuclear accident with more than 100 fatalities is at least 10 times lower than from coal, oil, gas, or hydropower.

[source: Our World in Data - Safest Energy Sources](https://ourworldindata.org/safest-sources-of-energy) | [source: World Nuclear Association - Safety](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/safety-and-security/safety-of-plants/safety-of-nuclear-power-reactors)

### The Waste Problem: Unsolved After 80 Years

Nuclear power produces spent fuel that remains radioactive for thousands of years. The US has no permanent repository for high-level nuclear waste.

**Current status:**
- Approximately 90,000 metric tons of spent fuel are stored at reactor sites in 30+ states
- Fuel is held in cooling pools and dry cask storage -- both designed as temporary solutions
- **Yucca Mountain** (Nevada) was designated as the permanent repository in 1987 but has been politically blocked since 2010. No serious legislative effort to restart it has succeeded.
- Two private-sector interim storage facilities have been proposed in New Mexico and Texas. The Texas facility received NRC approval in 2021, but faces state-level opposition.
- Spent fuel continues to accumulate at roughly 2,000 metric tons per year

**The scale in context:**
- All the spent fuel ever produced by US nuclear plants would fit on a single football field stacked less than 10 yards high
- By contrast, a single coal plant produces millions of tons of CO2, ash, and particulate matter per year -- waste that goes directly into the atmosphere and water

The waste problem is real and politically intractable. But it is a storage and political problem, not an unsolvable engineering problem. Finland's Onkalo repository, scheduled to begin operations in 2025, demonstrates that permanent geologic disposal is technically feasible.

[source: Congressional Research Service - Civilian Nuclear Waste](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/RL33461) | [source: GAO - Nuclear Waste Disposal Challenges](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-11-731t)

### Cost: Nuclear's Biggest Challenge

New nuclear construction is significantly more expensive than wind, solar, or natural gas on a levelized cost basis.

**Levelized Cost of Energy (Lazard, June 2025):**

| Source | LCOE Range ($/MWh) |
|--------|-------------------|
| Utility-scale solar | $38 - $212 |
| Onshore wind | $37 - $86 |
| Offshore wind | $72 - $140 |
| Nuclear (new build) | $141 - $220 |
| Combined cycle gas | $39 - $101 |

However, LCOE comparisons have important limitations:
- Nuclear provides **baseload, dispatchable** power -- it runs 24/7 regardless of weather
- Wind and solar require **battery storage or gas backup** for reliability, which adds cost not captured in LCOE
- Existing nuclear plants (already built and paid for) produce electricity at roughly $30/MWh -- among the cheapest sources available
- Nuclear plants typically operate for 60-80 years, spreading capital costs over decades

The real cost question is not "new nuclear vs. new solar" but rather: "What is the total system cost of reliable, zero-carbon electricity?"

[source: Lazard LCOE+ June 2025](https://www.lazard.com/media/eijnqja3/lazards-lcoeplus-june-2025.pdf) | [source: World Nuclear Association - Economics](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/economic-aspects/economics-of-nuclear-power)

### The Aging Fleet and Plant Restarts

The average US nuclear reactor is approaching 50 years old. The NRC has approved license renewals for most plants from 40 to 60 years, and four subsequent license renewals (to 80 years) have been approved.

**Recent developments:**
- The NRC approved 13 reactor license renewals in 2025
- **Palisades** (Michigan): On track to restart in early 2026 -- the first US nuclear plant ever to resume operation after decommissioning. Michigan provided $300 million and the federal government a $1.5 billion loan. Restart was delayed by cracked steam generator tubes requiring repair.
- **Three Mile Island Unit 1** (Pennsylvania): Constellation is spending an estimated $1.6 billion to recommission the plant, with a $1 billion federal loan. Expected to take approximately four years.
- Both restarts are driven by massive electricity demand from AI data centers

**Executive Order (May 2025):** The Trump administration ordered reform of the Nuclear Regulatory Commission and announced a goal to expand US nuclear capacity from 100 GW to 400 GW by 2050.

[source: NRC License Renewal Status](https://www.nrc.gov/reactors/operating/licensing/renewal/subsequent-license-renewal) | [source: Palisades Restart](https://www.michiganpublic.org/environment-climate-change/2025-12-17/palisades-nuclear-plant-restart-plans-pushed-back-to-early-2026)

[source: TMI Restart Loan](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/18/trump-nuclear-three-mile-island-crane-loan-constellation-ceg.html)

### Small Modular Reactors (SMRs)

SMRs are factory-built reactors with lower power output (typically 50-300 MW vs. 1,000+ MW for conventional plants). They promise lower upfront costs, faster construction, and the ability to be sited in more locations.

**NuScale Power** -- the most advanced US SMR developer:
- First and only SMR design to receive NRC approval (462 MW plant design approved May 2025)
- 12 power modules in production with manufacturing partner Doosan
- TVA signed a nonbinding agreement to deploy up to 6 GW of NuScale SMRs across seven states
- A proposed 462 MW plant in Romania could reach final investment decision in 2026
- First deployments expected in the early 2030s
- NuScale ended 2025 with $1.3 billion in cash and investments

**Other SMR developers:** Kairos Power, X-energy, TerraPower (Bill Gates-backed), GE Hitachi, Holtec

**Challenges:**
- No SMR has been built in the US yet -- costs and timelines remain uncertain
- NuScale's first project (at Idaho National Laboratory) was canceled in 2023 due to cost overruns
- Regulatory approval takes years even after design certification
- Critics argue SMRs will never achieve cost parity with renewables

[source: NRC Approves NuScale Design](https://www.energy.gov/ne/articles/nrc-approves-nuscale-powers-uprated-small-modular-reactor-design) | [source: TVA-NuScale Agreement](https://www.ans.org/news/2025-09-03/article-7342/tva-and-entra1-to-deploy-6-gw-of-nuscale-smrs/)

### Uranium Mining: A Toxic Legacy

Nuclear energy's fuel cycle begins with uranium mining, which has caused severe environmental and health damage -- particularly on Indigenous lands.

- Over 500 abandoned uranium mines remain on and near Navajo Nation from Cold War-era extraction (1944-1986)
- Nearly 30 million tons of uranium ore were extracted from Navajo lands
- Water on parts of Navajo Nation contains up to 700 micrograms per liter of uranium -- the EPA safe limit is 30
- Approximately 30% of Navajo households lack piped water, forcing reliance on potentially contaminated unregulated wells
- Health studies show increased rates of lung cancer, kidney disease, birth defects, and chronic respiratory illness among Navajo communities
- Only 219 of roughly 520 abandoned mines have been funded for remediation
- One in five uranium mines nationally is located within 10 km of a Native American reservation

Any expansion of nuclear energy must address the fuel cycle's environmental justice implications, including responsible mining practices, adequate remediation funding, and meaningful consent from affected communities.

[source: EPA - Navajo Nation Uranium Cleanup](https://www.epa.gov/navajo-nation-uranium-cleanup/aum-cleanup) | [source: Environmental Health Perspectives - Uranium Exposure](https://ehp.niehs.nih.gov/doi/full/10.1289/EHP7537)

### Public Opinion

Public support for nuclear energy is at or near historic highs:

- **Bisconti Research (2025):** 72% favor nuclear energy
- **Gallup (2025):** 61% support -- one point below the all-time high
- **Pew Research (2025):** 59% favor more nuclear power plants, up from 43% in 2020
- Support has grown among both Democrats and Republicans
- 87% support license renewal for existing plants
- Americans still prefer expanding solar (77%) and wind (68%) over nuclear (59%)

[source: Bisconti Research 2025 Survey](https://www.bisconti.com/blog/public-opinion-2025) | [source: Pew Research - Nuclear Support](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/10/16/support-for-expanding-nuclear-power-is-up-in-both-parties-since-2020/)

[source: Gallup - Nuclear Support](https://news.gallup.com/poll/659180/nuclear-energy-support-near-record-high.aspx)

## Analysis

### The Case for Nuclear in Climate Action

- Nuclear is the only proven, scalable, zero-carbon power source that provides reliable baseload electricity 24/7
- Closing existing nuclear plants has historically led to increased fossil fuel use and higher emissions (e.g., Germany's coal increase after nuclear phase-out, Vermont Yankee replacement with gas)
- Meeting net-zero goals likely requires keeping the existing fleet running while rapidly expanding renewables
- The IPCC and IEA include nuclear in most pathways to limiting warming to 1.5 degrees C
- Nuclear can complement renewables by providing firm power when the sun isn't shining and wind isn't blowing

### The Case for Caution

- New nuclear construction is expensive and slow -- capital would reduce more emissions if invested in solar, wind, and storage in the near term
- The waste problem remains politically unsolved after eight decades
- Uranium mining has caused severe environmental injustice, particularly for Indigenous communities
- Catastrophic accidents, while extremely rare, have long-lasting consequences
- Nuclear proliferation concerns exist with certain reactor designs and fuel cycles
- Water use for cooling is substantial -- a concern as droughts intensify

### Who's Affected

- **Communities near nuclear plants:** Benefit from high-paying jobs (average plant employs 500-700 people at $100K+ salaries) but bear proximity risks
- **Indigenous communities:** Disproportionately affected by uranium mining contamination
- **Climate-vulnerable populations:** Benefit from nuclear's zero-carbon electricity
- **Ratepayers:** Existing nuclear is cheap; new nuclear is expensive
- **Energy workers:** Nuclear provides some of the highest-paid, most stable jobs in the energy sector

## Quick Actions

### Learn About Your Nearest Nuclear Plant (5 Minutes)

1. Find your nearest reactor at [NRC Info Finder](https://www.nrc.gov/info-finder/reactors/)
2. Check its inspection reports and safety performance
3. Review its license status -- is a renewal or subsequent renewal pending?
4. Sign up for NRC notifications about that plant

### Attend NRC Public Meetings on License Renewals

The NRC holds public meetings for all license renewal applications. These are your opportunity to ask questions, raise concerns, and provide input.

1. Check the [NRC Public Meetings Calendar](https://www.nrc.gov/public-involve/public-meetings) for upcoming meetings
2. Review the [license renewal application status](https://www.nrc.gov/reactors/operating/licensing/renewal/subsequent-license-renewal) for plants near you
3. Attend in person or virtually -- meetings are typically held in communities near the plant
4. You can also submit written comments during formal public comment periods published in the Federal Register

### Engage in Local Energy Planning

Your state and local government make decisions about the energy mix that powers your community:

1. **Attend Public Utility Commission hearings** -- these bodies approve power plant construction, closures, and rate changes
2. **Participate in Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) processes** -- utilities must publish long-term plans for public comment
3. **Join community energy committees** -- many cities and counties have advisory committees on energy and sustainability
4. **Weigh in on energy siting and permitting** -- new energy projects (nuclear, solar, wind) require public review
5. **Support state clean energy standards** that are technology-inclusive (include nuclear alongside renewables)

## Sustained Actions

### Stay Informed with Balanced Sources

The nuclear debate is polarized. These organizations provide evidence-based analysis from different perspectives:

- **Union of Concerned Scientists** ([ucsusa.org](https://www.ucsusa.org/nuclear-power)) -- Independent safety and policy analysis; neither pro- nor anti-nuclear
- **Clean Air Task Force** ([catf.us](https://www.catf.us)) -- Supports nuclear as part of a diverse clean energy portfolio
- **Nuclear Energy Institute** ([nei.org](https://www.nei.org)) -- Industry trade group; comprehensive data on nuclear fleet performance
- **Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists** ([thebulletin.org](https://thebulletin.org)) -- In-depth analysis of nuclear policy, safety, and proliferation
- **World Nuclear Association** ([world-nuclear.org](https://world-nuclear.org)) -- International data and analysis on nuclear energy

### Advocate for Environmental Justice in the Nuclear Fuel Cycle

- Support full federal funding for abandoned uranium mine remediation on Navajo Nation and other affected Indigenous lands
- Advocate for meaningful community consent requirements before new uranium mining or waste storage projects
- Push for environmental justice analysis in all nuclear siting decisions
- Support organizations like the **Southwest Research and Information Center** ([sric.org](https://www.sric.org)) working on uranium contamination cleanup

### Engage in Community Forums and Public Processes

- Attend or watch NRC public meetings -- they are open to everyone and cover safety, licensing, and environmental reviews
- Participate in state energy planning processes when nuclear plants or new energy projects are under consideration
- Join local energy advisory committees to ensure nuclear is honestly assessed alongside other options
- Engage in public comment periods for NRC license renewals, environmental impact statements, and DOE funding decisions

### Push for a Spent Fuel Solution

The waste problem will not solve itself. Advocate for:
- Congressional action on permanent geologic repository or consent-based siting
- Adequate interim storage funding so communities near closed plants are not indefinite waste hosts
- Research into advanced fuel cycles that could reduce waste volume and radioactivity
