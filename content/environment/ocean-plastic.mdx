# Ocean Plastic Pollution

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** An estimated 171 trillion plastic particles -- weighing 2.3 million tons -- are floating in the world's oceans, and the amount is doubling every six years. The Great Pacific Garbage Patch alone covers 1.6 million square kilometers (three times the size of France). Only 5-6% of US plastic is actually recycled. Every year, 11 million metric tons of new plastic enters the ocean, killing over 1 million seabirds and 100,000 marine mammals. Microplastics have been found in human blood, placentas, and brains.

The solutions are clear: reduce production, ban the worst single-use items, hold producers responsible, and clean up what's already there.

## Facts

### The Scale of Ocean Plastic

A 2023 study published in PLOS ONE by researchers at the 5 Gyres Institute analyzed nearly 12,000 ocean sampling stations across the Atlantic, Pacific, Indian Oceans, and Mediterranean Sea from 1979 to 2019. They found:

- **171 trillion plastic particles** afloat in the world's oceans, weighing approximately **2.3 million tons**
- A "rapid and unprecedented" increase since 2005
- Ocean plastic concentration is **doubling roughly every 6 years**
- Without policy action, the rate of plastic entering oceans could increase **2.6 times by 2040**

[source: Eriksen et al. 2023 - PLOS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0281596)

### The Great Pacific Garbage Patch

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch (GPGP) is the largest accumulation of ocean plastic in the world:

- **1.6 million square kilometers** in area -- three times the size of France
- Contains an estimated **1.8 trillion plastic pieces** weighing **79,000+ tons**
- **94% of pieces** are microplastics (smaller than 0.5 cm), but they account for only 8% of the mass
- **Fishing nets and ropes** make up 47% of the plastic mass
- The patch has grown **10-fold each decade** since 1945
- There are five major garbage patches worldwide -- the Pacific, North Atlantic, South Atlantic, Indian Ocean, and South Pacific

[source: The Ocean Cleanup](https://theoceancleanup.com/great-pacific-garbage-patch/) | [source: Lebreton et al. 2018 - Nature Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-018-22939-w)

### Wildlife Impacts

Plastic pollution kills marine animals through ingestion, entanglement, and habitat destruction:

- **1 million+ seabirds** die from plastic annually
- **100,000+ marine mammals** die from ingestion or entanglement each year
- **300,000 whales, dolphins, and porpoises** die annually from ghost fishing gear entanglement
- **81 of 123 marine mammal species** are known to have eaten or been trapped in plastic
- **All 7 sea turtle species** are affected -- one in five risks death from ingesting a single piece
- Over **800 marine and coastal species** are affected by plastic pollution through ingestion, entanglement, or habitat damage

[source: Ocean Conservancy](https://oceanconservancy.org/work/plastics/) | [source: Center for Biological Diversity](https://www.biologicaldiversity.org/campaigns/ocean_plastics/) | [source: WWF - Plastic in Oceans](https://wwf.org.au/blogs/plastic-in-our-oceans-is-killing-marine-mammals/)

### Where It Comes From

The vast majority of ocean plastic originates from land-based sources:

| Source | Share of Ocean Plastic |
|--------|----------------------|
| Single-use packaging (bottles, wrappers, bags) | ~40% |
| Fishing gear (nets, ropes, lines) | ~20% |
| Microfibers from synthetic clothing | ~15% |
| Tire wear particles | ~7% |
| Microbeads and pellets | ~2% |
| Other sources | ~16% |

The top items found in the 2024 International Coastal Cleanup (486,000 volunteers, 7.4 million pounds collected): food wrappers (1.4 million), beverage bottles (1.2 million), and cigarette butts (1.2 million).

[source: Ocean Conservancy - ICC](https://oceanconservancy.org/work/plastics/cleanups-icc/)

### The Recycling Myth

The US plastic recycling rate has **collapsed from 9.5% in 2014 to roughly 5-6% today** -- meaning over 94% of plastic produced in the US is landfilled, incinerated, or leaked into the environment.

- Only **a fifth** of the most common plastic types (bottles, jugs, food containers) are actually recyclable
- The plastic industry spent decades promoting recycling as a solution while knowing it was not economically viable at scale
- Global plastic production continues to climb and is **projected to triple by 2050**
- Major brands like Coca-Cola, Unilever, and Nestle have quietly retracted sustainability commitments

[source: Greenpeace - Merchants of Myth](https://www.greenpeace.org/usa/merchants-of-myth/) | [source: Beyond Plastics](https://www.beyondplastics.org/publications/us-plastics-recycling-rate)

### Microplastics in the Human Body

Plastic doesn't disappear -- it breaks down into microplastics (under 5mm) and nanoplastics (under 1 micrometer) that enter our food, water, and air:

- **Microplastics found in human brains** at concentrations higher than liver or kidneys, with levels **increasing 50% over the past eight years** (University of New Mexico, 2024)
- **Microplastics in arterial plaque** linked to higher risk of heart attack, stroke, and death (New England Journal of Medicine, 2024)
- Detected in **placentas, breast milk, blood, semen, and newborn meconium**
- Can cross the **blood-brain barrier** and accumulate in the brain's myelin sheath
- Linked to **inflammation, immune impairment, endocrine disruption, and cell damage** in laboratory studies

[source: Nature Medicine - Microplastics in Human Brains](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-024-03453-1) | [source: Stanford Medicine - Microplastics and Health](https://med.stanford.edu/news/insights/2025/01/microplastics-in-body-polluted-tiny-plastic-fragments.html)

### Policy Landscape

**Global Plastics Treaty:** The UN Intergovernmental Negotiating Committee (INC) has been negotiating a legally binding global plastics treaty since 2022. Talks at INC-5.1 (Busan, December 2024) and INC-5.2 (Geneva, August 2025) both ended without agreement. Nations are split between ~100 countries supporting production limits and oil-producing nations (Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the US) opposing production caps. Negotiations are expected to resume at INC-5.4 later in 2026.

**US State Plastic Bag Bans:** 12 states have banned single-use plastic bags: California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Hawaii, Maine, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, and Washington. California's SB 1053 (2026) closed the "thicker reusable bag" loophole.

**Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR):** Seven states have passed EPR laws for packaging (California, Colorado, Maine, Maryland, Minnesota, Oregon, Washington), requiring producers to fund recycling and waste management. Oregon launched the first program in July 2025.

**Polystyrene Foam Bans:** Banned in California, Delaware, Oregon, Rhode Island, and most of Hawaii. Virginia banning food vendor polystyrene containers effective July 2026. New York expanded its ban to cold storage containers in 2026.

[source: UNEP - INC Plastic Pollution](https://www.unep.org/inc-plastic-pollution) | [source: Earth911 - State of Plastic Bans](https://earth911.com/business-policy/the-state-of-plastic-bans-in-the-united-states/)

### Cleanup Technology

**The Ocean Cleanup** deployed System 03 (a 2,250-meter floating barrier) to the Great Pacific Garbage Patch in 2023. In 2025, the organization removed a record **25 million kilograms** of trash from oceans and rivers worldwide, bringing their total to over 45 million kg. Their goal: clean 90% of floating ocean plastic by 2040.

However, cleanup alone cannot solve the crisis. An estimated 11 million tons of new plastic enters the ocean every year -- far outpacing removal capacity. Stopping plastic at the source remains essential.

[source: The Ocean Cleanup - 2025 Year in Review](https://theoceancleanup.com/updates/2025-in-review-the-ocean-cleanup/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Ocean plastic is **accelerating**, not declining -- doubling every 6 years despite awareness campaigns
- Recycling has failed as a primary solution -- the 5% US recycling rate proves the system is broken
- Microplastics are now inside every human being tested, with unknown long-term health consequences
- The global plastics treaty is stalled because major producers (including the US) resist production limits
- **Source reduction** -- producing less plastic -- is the only strategy that can reverse the trend
- Municipal and state bans on specific items (bags, foam, straws) are the fastest-moving policy lever

### Who's Affected

- **Coastal communities:** Bear the brunt of ocean plastic washing ashore, harming tourism and fisheries
- **Fishing industry:** Ghost gear entangles target species and damages equipment
- **Seafood consumers:** Microplastics are found in fish, shellfish, and sea salt consumed worldwide
- **Everyone:** Microplastics are in drinking water (tap and bottled), food, and the air we breathe
- **Pacific Island nations:** Small island developing states are disproportionately impacted despite producing minimal plastic
- **Marine ecosystems:** Coral reefs, sea grass beds, and deep ocean habitats are all contaminated

### Do a Personal Plastic Audit (1 week)

Track every piece of single-use plastic you encounter for one week. Then target your biggest sources:

| Common Plastic | Swap To | Savings |
|---------------|---------|---------|
| Plastic water bottles | Reusable bottle (stainless steel or glass) | ~150 bottles/year |
| Plastic grocery bags | Reusable tote bags | ~500 bags/year |
| Plastic wrap | Beeswax wraps or silicone lids | ~100 ft/year |
| Plastic produce bags | Mesh produce bags or skip the bag | ~200 bags/year |
| Disposable razors | Safety razor (metal) | ~12 razors/year |
| Liquid soap in plastic bottles | Bar soap or refillable dispensers | ~6 bottles/year |
| Plastic straws | No straw, or reusable metal/glass | ~500 straws/year |
| Single-serve coffee pods | French press, pour-over, or compostable pods | ~365 pods/year |
| Plastic toothbrush | Bamboo toothbrush | ~4 toothbrushes/year |
| Shampoo/conditioner bottles | Shampoo bars or refill stations | ~6 bottles/year |

**Refill options:** Look for refill stores near you at [litterless.com/refillery-directory](https://www.litterless.com/refillery-directory).

### Join a Beach or Waterway Cleanup

Cleanups remove plastic before it breaks into microplastics and generate data on pollution sources.

- **Ocean Conservancy International Coastal Cleanup (ICC)** -- The world's largest cleanup, held annually in September. 486,000 volunteers collected 7.4 million pounds in 2024. Find a cleanup near you at [oceanconservancy.org/trash-free-seas/international-coastal-cleanup](https://oceanconservancy.org/trash-free-seas/international-coastal-cleanup/)
- **Surfrider Foundation** -- Local chapters organize regular beach cleanups year-round. Find your chapter at [surfrider.org/chapters](https://www.surfrider.org/chapters)
- **American Rivers** -- National river cleanup program. Find events at [americanrivers.org/make-an-impact/national-river-cleanup](https://www.americanrivers.org/make-an-impact/national-river-cleanup/)
- **Waterkeeper Alliance** -- 300+ local waterkeeper organizations host regular cleanups. Find one at [waterkeeper.org](https://waterkeeper.org)
- **Trash Free Seas Alliance** -- Industry and NGO partnership with local cleanup events

Can't find one? **Start your own** using Ocean Conservancy's [cleanup toolkit](https://oceanconservancy.org/trash-free-seas/international-coastal-cleanup/start-a-cleanup/).

### Push for Local Plastic Bans

Your city or county can ban single-use plastics even if your state hasn't:

1. **Research what's already banned** in your area -- check your city/county ordinances
2. **Identify the target** -- plastic bags, polystyrene foam, plastic straws, and single-use utensils are the most common bans
3. **Contact your city council member** or county commissioner and ask them to introduce an ordinance
4. **Attend a council meeting** and speak during public comment in support of a ban
5. **Build a coalition** -- local businesses, restaurants, and environmental groups can all advocate together

Roughly one-third of Americans already live in a jurisdiction with some plastic bag policy. If your community doesn't have one, be the person who changes that.

### Advocate for Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)

EPR laws require producers to pay for managing the waste their products create. Seven states have passed EPR for packaging. If yours hasn't:

1. Check if your state has pending EPR legislation at your state legislature's website
2. Contact your state legislators to voice support
3. Join a state-level advocacy coalition (Beyond Plastics tracks state campaigns)
4. Support campaigns to strengthen existing EPR laws if your state has one

### Reduce Plastic in Your Daily Life

Beyond individual swaps, make systemic changes:

- **Choose stores that minimize packaging** -- co-ops, farmers markets, bulk stores
- **Support businesses with refill programs** -- cleaning products (Blueland, Grove), personal care (Plaine Products), food (bulk bins)
- **Bring your own containers** to restaurants for leftovers
- **Choose natural fibers** for clothing when possible (synthetic fabrics shed microfibers)
- **Use a microfiber-catching laundry filter** like Cora Ball or Guppyfriend bag to reduce microfiber pollution from washing
