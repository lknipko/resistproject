# Oil Spills: History, Risk, and Response

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Oil spills remain one of the most destructive environmental disasters, and the risk is growing. The U.S. has over 2.6 million miles of pipelines -- many decades old -- and pipeline incidents occur at a rate of more than one per day. Offshore drilling is expanding dramatically, with up to 34 new lease sales planned across 1.27 billion acres of ocean. Meanwhile, safety regulations enacted after the Deepwater Horizon disaster have been rolled back, and cleanup technology still recovers only 10-15% of spilled oil in real-world conditions.

The Exxon Valdez spill (1989) still has toxic oil in Alaska's shorelines. Deepwater Horizon (2010) is still damaging Gulf ecosystems 15 years later. The Keystone pipeline has ruptured 23 times. The question is not whether the next major spill will happen -- it is when.

## Facts

### Key Points

- **Pipeline incidents average 1.45 per day** across the U.S. (530 incidents in 2024), with 37% occurring in Texas alone [source: PHMSA Pipeline Incident Data](https://www.phmsa.dot.gov/data-and-statistics/pipeline/pipeline-incident-20-year-trends)
- **Only 10-15% of spilled oil is typically recovered** in real-world conditions, despite industry claims [source: Mechanical Recovery Effectiveness Study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0025326X20309668)
- **34 new offshore lease sales planned** across 1.27 billion acres, the largest expansion in decades [source: DOI 11th Leasing Program](https://www.doi.gov/pressreleases/interior-launches-expansive-11th-national-offshore-leasing-program-advance-us-energy)
- **Post-Deepwater Horizon safety rules weakened** -- third-party certification of blowout preventers eliminated, returning to industry self-inspection [source: BSEE Rule Rollback - Harvard EELP](https://eelp.law.harvard.edu/tracker/bsee-blowout-preventer-and-well-control-rule/)
- **Endangered species at risk** -- the Rice's whale, with fewer than 100 individuals remaining, lost an estimated 22% of its population from Deepwater Horizon [source: Oceana - Deepwater Horizon 15 Years Later](https://usa.oceana.org/the-deepwater-horizon-disaster-15-years-later/)

### [+] Major Spills and Long-Term Damage

| Spill | Year | Oil Released | Long-Term Impact |
|-------|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Exxon Valdez** (Alaska) | 1989 | 11 million gallons | Oil still present in shoreline sediments 35+ years later. Herring fishery closed for 20+ of 35 years since spill. |
| **Deepwater Horizon** (Gulf of Mexico) | 2010 | 210 million gallons | Largest marine oil spill in history. 700+ square miles of deep-sea habitat damaged. | **Keystone Pipeline** (multiple states) | 2010-2025 | 23 spills total | Most recent: April 2025, 147,000 gallons in North Dakota farmland. GAO found preventable construction issues caused spills more frequently than other pipelines. Four ruptures in North Dakota alone in six years. |
| **Taylor Energy** (Gulf of Mexico) | 2004-present | Ongoing for 20+ years | Platform damaged by Hurricane Ivan. Leaked continuously for two decades -- one of the longest-running oil spills in U.S. history. |

[source: NOAA Exxon Valdez Assessment](https://darrp.noaa.gov/oil-spills/exxon-valdez) | [source: NOAA Deepwater Horizon Timeline](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/national/habitat-conservation/noaas-work-after-deepwater-horizon-oil-spill-timeline) | [source: PHMSA Keystone Updates](https://www.phmsa.dot.gov/news/south-bow-keystone-pipeline-updates)

**Exxon Valdez -- Still Not Recovered:**

The 1989 Exxon Valdez spill killed an estimated 250,000 seabirds, 2,800 sea otters, 300 harbor seals, 250 bald eagles, up to 22 killer whales, and billions of salmon and herring eggs. As of 2014, two species were listed as "not recovered," seven species and the intertidal ecosystem as "recovering," and only 11 species as "recovered" or "most likely recovered." Spilled oil lingered in subsurface patches, and some of it retained its original toxicity.

[source: USGS Wildlife Recovery Study](https://www.usgs.gov/news/national-news-release/wildlife-recovery-following-exxon-valdez-oil-spill-was-highly-variable) | [source: Exxon Valdez Trustee Council - Lingering Oil](https://evostc.state.ak.us/status-of-restoration/lingering-oil/)

**Deepwater Horizon -- The Damage Continues:**

A 2024 study found "severe" or "moderate" biodiversity damage across an area nearly nine times larger than previously reported. The Gulf Long-term Follow-up Study, which tracked 32,608 cleanup workers for 10 years, found increased respiratory disease, cardiovascular impacts, asthma, and heart attacks. A 2025 study found U.S. Coast Guard members involved in cleanup experienced long-term endocrine and metabolic conditions.

[source: Frontier Group - Gulf Still Affected](https://frontiergroup.org/resources/the-deepwater-horizon-oil-spill-is-still-wreaking-havoc-on-the-gulf-of-mexico/) | [source: NIH Health Impacts Study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7038885/)

### [+] Current Infrastructure and Risk

The U.S. has approximately **2.6 million miles** of oil and gas pipelines -- enough to circle the Earth more than 100 times. Many of these pipelines are decades old and deteriorating.

**By the numbers:**

- **530 pipeline incidents** reported in 2024 (1.45 per day average) [source: FracTracker - Pipeline Incidents](https://www.fractracker.org/2025/04/pipeline-incidents-are-a-daily-occurrence/)
- **25 serious incidents** in 2024 alone [source: PHMSA National Pipeline Performance](https://www.phmsa.dot.gov/data-and-statistics/pipeline/national-pipeline-performance-measures)
- **37% of 2024 incidents** occurred in Texas [source: PHMSA Source Data](https://www.phmsa.dot.gov/data-and-statistics/pipeline/source-data)
- **Aging infrastructure** is a major risk factor -- pipelines made of cast iron, wrought iron, and bare steel pose the highest risk [source: PHMSA Pipeline Replacement](https://www.phmsa.dot.gov/data-and-statistics/pipeline-replacement/pipeline-replacement-background)

**Keystone pipeline** is a case study in construction-related failure. The GAO found it had preventable construction defects at higher rates than comparable pipelines, and it has ruptured 23 times since entering service, including four times in North Dakota in just six years.

[source: Bold Nebraska - Keystone Spill History](https://boldnebraska.org/keystone-pipeline-spill-history/) | [source: US News - Keystone Troubled History](https://www.usnews.com/news/us/articles/2025-04-08/the-latest-leak-in-the-keystone-oil-pipeline-continues-its-troubled-history)

### [+] Offshore Drilling Expansion

The current administration has launched the most aggressive offshore drilling expansion in U.S. history:

**11th National Offshore Leasing Program:**

- **34 potential lease sales** across 21 of 27 existing planning areas
- **1.27 billion acres** of ocean opened to drilling -- Atlantic, Pacific, Gulf, and Arctic
- **New planning areas created**, including the eastern Gulf off Florida and the high Arctic
- **30 lease sales mandated** by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) in the Gulf alone

[source: DOI Press Release](https://www.doi.gov/pressreleases/interior-launches-expansive-11th-national-offshore-leasing-program-advance-us-energy) | [source: NRDC - Selling Off Our Ocean](https://www.nrdc.org/media/trump-administration-wants-sell-our-ocean-under-new-offshore-oil-and-gas-leasing-plan)

**Timeline:**

| Date | Action |
|------|--------|
| April 2025 | 45-day public comment period on leasing in all federal waters |
| December 2025 | First Gulf lease sale held under OBBBA -- first sale since December 2023 |
| January 2026 | 60-day comment period on draft schedule closed |
| March 2026 | Cook Inlet (Alaska) lease sale -- approximately 1 million acres |
| October 2026 | Final 11th Leasing Program expected |

Over 250,000 Americans submitted letters opposing the expansion during the public comment period.

[source: BOEM Lease Sales](https://www.boem.gov/newsroom/press-releases/boem-advances-first-two-obbba-offshore-lease-sales) | [source: Earth.org - Legal Backlash](https://earth.org/trump-administration-faces-legal-and-public-backlash-over-massive-offshore-drilling-expansion/)

**Endangered Species Exemption:**

The administration convened the "God Squad" (Endangered Species Committee) to exempt Gulf oil and gas drilling from Endangered Species Act protections -- directly affecting the Rice's whale (fewer than 100 individuals) and other protected marine species.

[source: PBS - Endangered Species Exemption](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/science/trumps-endangered-species-committee-exempts-oil-and-gas-drilling-in-the-gulf-from-rules)

### [+] Response Capability Decline

**Safety Regulations Weakened:**

The Blowout Preventer Systems and Well Control Rule -- enacted after Deepwater Horizon to prevent future disasters -- has been significantly weakened:

- **Third-party certification eliminated** -- oil companies can now inspect their own blowout preventers instead of independent inspectors
- **Well design and well control standards loosened** -- requirements for casing, cementing, and real-time monitoring reduced
- **Subsea containment requirements relaxed** -- the very systems meant to prevent another Deepwater Horizon

BSEE's own safety experts were overruled in the rulemaking process.

[source: Harvard EELP - Well Control Rule Tracker](https://eelp.law.harvard.edu/tracker/bsee-blowout-preventer-and-well-control-rule/) | [source: Earthjustice - Safety Experts Muzzled](https://earthjustice.org/experts/chris-eaton/trump-administration-muzzles-own-safety-experts-on-offshore-drilling-rule) | [source: Ocean Conservancy](https://oceanconservancy.org/blog/2019/05/14/trump-administration-weakens-offshore-drilling-safety-rules/)

**Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund:**

The Coast Guard's oil spill response activities are funded through the Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund, which receives $24.5 million annually for prevention and cleanup. With offshore drilling expanding into new, remote areas (Arctic, deep Atlantic), response infrastructure has not kept pace.

[source: USCG FY2026 Budget](https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/2025-06/25_0613_uscg_fy26-congressional-budget-justificatin.pdf)

### [+] Cleanup Reality

**What the industry claims vs. what actually happens:**

| Metric | Industry Claim | Reality |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| Oil recovered | "State of the art response" | **10-15%** in typical conditions |
| Best case mechanical recovery | "Effective containment" | **Maximum ~40%** under ideal conditions |
| Skimmer recovery | "Advanced oleophilic technology" | **Up to 20%** in practice |
| Deepwater Horizon recovery | "Unprecedented response" | **~16% recovered** by all methods combined |

[source: ScienceDirect - Mechanical Recovery Effectiveness](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0025326X20309668) | [source: Seaside Sustainability - Cleanup Technology](https://www.seasidesustainability.org/post/oil-spills-under-control-the-science-and-promise-of-advanced-cleanup-technologies)

**Why cleanup is so limited:**

- **Oil spreads rapidly** -- forms a thin film across the water surface within hours
- **Boom systems fail above 0.7-1 knot tow speeds** -- oil escapes containment in anything but calm conditions
- **Weather and waves** make skimming operations impossible during storms -- exactly when spills are most likely
- **Dispersants** (like Corexit used in Deepwater Horizon) break oil into smaller droplets but do not remove it -- they push it below the surface where it damages deep-sea ecosystems
- **Advanced sorbent technologies** work for small spills but are not scalable to major disasters
- **In the Arctic**, ice, darkness, and extreme cold make response virtually impossible for much of the year

[source: NOAA Oil Spills Education](https://www.noaa.gov/education/resource-collections/ocean-coasts/oil-spills) | [source: UNEP - Managing Oil Spill Damage](https://www.unep.org/news-and-stories/story/how-manage-damage-oil-spills)

## Analysis

### What This Means

The U.S. is simultaneously expanding offshore drilling into new areas, weakening the safety regulations designed to prevent spills, and failing to improve the cleanup technology that would mitigate damage when spills occur. This is the same pattern that preceded the Deepwater Horizon disaster.

**The math of risk:**

- More drilling in more places = more potential spill sites
- Weaker safety rules = higher probability of equipment failure
- Same limited cleanup technology = same devastating outcomes when spills happen
- New drilling in remote areas (Arctic, deep Atlantic) = longer response times and harsher conditions

The Oil Pollution Act of 1990, passed after the Exxon Valdez, was supposed to prevent future catastrophes. The post-Deepwater Horizon safety rules were supposed to prevent another blowout. Both sets of protections are being weakened while drilling expands to areas with even greater risks and fewer response capabilities.

[source: NOAA - Oil Pollution Act 30 Years](https://response.restoration.noaa.gov/oil-pollution-act-1990-30-years-spill-response-and-restoration)

### Who's Affected

- **Coastal communities:** Fishing, tourism, and property values devastated for years after major spills. Gulf Coast communities are still experiencing economic impacts from Deepwater Horizon.
- **Commercial fisheries:** The Exxon Valdez destroyed Prince William Sound's herring fishery for 20+ years. Deepwater Horizon closed vast areas of Gulf fishing grounds. Fisheries that depend on clean water face existential risk.
- **Marine wildlife:** Deepwater Horizon killed an estimated 1 million seabirds, 5,000 marine mammals, and 1,000 sea turtles. The Rice's whale, with fewer than 100 individuals, could face extinction from a single major Gulf spill.
- **Tourism economies:** Coastal tourism generates over $300 billion annually in the U.S. A major spill in the Atlantic or Pacific could devastate communities that have never experienced oil contamination.
- **Cleanup workers:** The 32,608 workers tracked after Deepwater Horizon showed elevated rates of respiratory disease, cardiovascular problems, and endocrine disorders. These health effects persist for decades.
- **Indigenous communities:** Alaska Native communities that depend on subsistence hunting and fishing face disproportionate harm from Arctic drilling and spills, as the Exxon Valdez demonstrated.

### The Liability Gap

Companies pay a fraction of the true damage they cause:

- **Exxon Valdez:** Exxon paid approximately $4.3 billion total (fines, cleanup, settlements). The Supreme Court reduced punitive damages from $5 billion to $507.5 million. Oil is still in the shoreline 35 years later. [source: EPA - Exxon Settlement](https://www.epa.gov/archive/epa/aboutepa/exxon-pay-record-one-billion-dollars-criminal-fines-and-civil-damages-connection-alaskan.html)
- **Deepwater Horizon:** BP paid approximately $65 billion -- the largest environmental settlement in history. Yet deep-sea corals are still degrading, dolphin populations remain impaired, and cleanup workers continue to develop chronic diseases. [source: EPA Deepwater Horizon](https://www.epa.gov/enforcement/deepwater-horizon-bp-gulf-america-oil-spill) | [source: NOAA - Where the Money Went](https://www.noaa.gov/explainers/deepwater-horizon-oil-spill-settlements-where-money-went)
- **Keystone Pipeline:** Despite 23 spills from documented construction defects, the pipeline continues operating with no fundamental redesign required.

No amount of money restores a destroyed ecosystem. The Exxon Valdez proved that some damage is permanent. Deepwater Horizon proved that even $65 billion cannot undo the harm. The cost of prevention is always less than the cost of cleanup -- and far less than the cost of what can never be cleaned up.

[source: NOAA - Who Pays for Oil Spills](https://response.restoration.noaa.gov/about/media/who-pays-oil-spills.html)

### Report a Spill

If you see oil, sheen, or chemical discharge in waterways:

- **National Response Center:** Call **1-800-424-8802** (24/7 hotline operated by the U.S. Coast Guard)
- **EPA Spill Reporting:** [epa.gov/report-spill](https://www.epa.gov/report-spill)
- **State environmental agency:** Contact your state's Department of Environmental Quality
- **Document it:** Take photos and video with timestamps and GPS coordinates if safe to do so

### Comment on Offshore Lease Sales

When new drilling is proposed on public waters, you can comment:

- **BOEM lease sale notices** at [boem.gov/oil-gas-energy/leasing](https://www.boem.gov/oil-gas-energy/leasing)
- **NEPA environmental reviews** -- Comment on Environmental Impact Statements for new drilling areas
- Your comment doesn't need to be long -- even "I oppose this lease sale because of spill risk" is counted

### Monitor Your Local Waterways

- **Report sheens, odors, or dead wildlife** to the **National Response Center**: **1-800-424-8802** (24/7)
- Join a local **Waterkeeper** patrol ([waterkeeper.org](https://www.waterkeeper.org)) -- Citizen patrols that monitor waterways for pollution
- If you live near pipelines, know the **Pipeline Marker** locations and watch for signs of leaks (dead vegetation, unusual odors, hissing sounds)

### Reduce Oil Dependence

The most effective way to prevent spills is to reduce demand:

- **Drive less** -- Combine trips, carpool, bike, transit
- **Choose fuel-efficient or electric vehicles** when replacing your car
- **Reduce single-use plastics** -- Most plastics are made from petroleum
- **Support local food** -- Less transportation = less fuel demand

### Volunteer for Spill Response Training

- **NOAA Disaster Preparedness Program** offers training for citizen responders
- Local Audubon chapters train volunteers for **oiled bird rescue**
- **Volunteer with Surfrider Foundation** for coastal monitoring
