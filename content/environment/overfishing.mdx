# Overfishing: Emptying the Ocean

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** According to the UN Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), **35.5% of assessed global fish stocks are now overfished** — and that proportion has been rising by roughly 1% per year. Industrial fishing fleets receive over **$30 billion in annual government subsidies**, much of it flowing to the most destructive operations. Every year, bottom trawling disturbs an area of ocean floor **twice the size of the United States**, crushing coral, destroying habitat, and producing **23% discarded bycatch**.

The Atlantic cod — once so abundant they could be scooped from the ocean in baskets — collapsed in the 1990s and has never recovered. That collapse cost 40,000 jobs and devastated coastal communities. It was preventable. The same trajectory is playing out across dozens of species today.

## Facts

### Global Stock Status

The FAO's most recent State of World Fisheries and Aquaculture report found:

- **35.5% of assessed stocks are overfished** (biologically unsustainable levels)
- 64.5% of stocks are within biologically sustainable levels
- The proportion of overfished stocks has increased from 10% in 1974 to 35.5% today
- Overfishing has been rising at roughly **1% per year on average**

These figures cover only *assessed* stocks. Many of the world's fisheries — particularly in developing nations — are not assessed at all, meaning the true picture is likely worse.

[source: FAO State of World Fisheries 2024](https://www.fao.org/newsroom/detail/fao-releases-the-most-detailed-global-assessment-of-marine-fish-stocks-to-date/en)

### U.S. Fisheries: Progress and Remaining Gaps

The U.S. has made genuine progress under the Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (MSA):

- In 2023, NOAA reported a record low for fish stocks subject to overfishing: **94% of U.S. stocks are not subject to overfishing** and **82% are not overfished**
- The MSA requires NOAA to set annual catch limits and rebuild overfished stocks
- However, roughly **45 U.S. stocks** remain overfished or subject to overfishing as of recent reports

Progress in U.S. waters makes the global contrast starker: strong management works, but it requires political will and enforcement.

[source: NOAA - 2024 Overfishing Report](https://phys.org/news/2024-05-noaa-overfishing.html) | [source: NOAA Magnuson-Stevens Act](https://www.noaa.gov/explainers/magnuson-stevens-act-unique-charge-for-sustainable-seafood)

### Bycatch: The Hidden Toll

Bycatch is any marine life caught unintentionally by fishing gear. The scale is staggering:

- Trawlers discard an average of **23% of their catch** as bycatch
- Bycatch includes juvenile fish of target species, sea turtles, marine mammals, seabirds, sharks, and non-commercial fish
- Bottom trawling in the Mediterranean Sea alone caused an estimated **172,000 sea turtle deaths** between 2000 and 2020
- Deep-sea trawling bycatch includes **70% of species with no commercial value**

Bycatch is not just a waste problem — discarding dead or dying animals disrupts food webs, depletes non-target populations, and removes the ecological services those species provide.

[source: NOAA - Understanding Bycatch](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/insight/understanding-bycatch)

### [+] Bottom Trawling: Bulldozing the Ocean Floor

Bottom trawling — dragging heavy nets and weighted gear across the seafloor — is one of the most destructive fishing methods in use:

- Every year, bottom trawling disturbs **1.5% of the ocean floor** — an area **twice the size of the United States**
- Trawling resuspends **1.5 gigatons of sediment** annually, burying filter feeders, clogging fish gills, and blocking sunlight
- Rocky reef habitats: "Rockhopper" gear crushes cold-water corals thousands of years old on contact; sponge gardens are reduced to rubble, with recovery potentially taking centuries
- Recovery from trawling disturbance takes 6 months to 6 years in sandy habitats — and far longer in complex reef or coral environments

Despite these impacts, **60% of 727 formally designated Marine Protected Areas in Atlantic European waters were commercially trawled** in a recent assessment, raising questions about whether MPAs offer meaningful protection.

[source: Fauna & Flora - Bottom Trawling Impact](https://www.fauna-flora.org/explained/bottom-trawling-impact/) | [source: BirdLife - Bottom Trawling in MPAs](https://www.birdlife.org/news/2023/03/23/the-impacts-of-bottom-trawling-in-marine-protected-areas/) | [source: Earth.org - Bottom Trawling](https://earth.org/the-deep-scars-of-bottom-trawling-a-silent-crisis-on-the-ocean-floor/)

### [+] The Atlantic Cod Collapse: A Warning

The collapse of Canadian Atlantic cod in the late 1980s and early 1990s is the most documented fishery disaster in history:

- Cod had been fished in the Northwest Atlantic for **500 years** before industrial trawling drove the population to near-extinction
- The Canadian government declared a moratorium on cod fishing in **July 1992** — but by then the stock had collapsed below 1% of historical levels
- The collapse eliminated **40,000 jobs** in Newfoundland and Labrador virtually overnight
- **More than 30 years later, the stock has not recovered**

The collapse was predicted by NOAA scientists years in advance. Politicians and industry ignored the warnings. The same pattern — early warnings dismissed, short-term economic interests prevailing — is playing out in fisheries around the world today.

[source: IERE - Atlantic Cod Collapse](https://iere.org/what-is-a-real-example-of-overfishing/)

### [+] Illegal, Unreported, and Unregulated (IUU) Fishing

IUU fishing — fishing outside legal frameworks — accounts for an estimated **20% of global catch**, rising to **50% or more** in some developing nations:

- IUU fishing undermines conservation limits set by legitimate fishery management bodies
- It is especially prevalent in high-value species like bluefin tuna, toothfish, and sharks
- Bluefin tuna is among the 13 species identified by NOAA as most vulnerable to IUU fishing and seafood fraud
- **Shark finning** — removing shark fins and discarding the living animal — is driven largely by IUU operations; an estimated 73–100 million sharks are killed per year
- NOAA announced an action plan in November 2024 to strengthen the Seafood Import Monitoring Program (SIMP) and bolster seafood traceability

[source: NOAA - IUU Fishing](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/insight/understanding-illegal-unreported-and-unregulated-fishing) | [source: Congress.gov - IUU Fishing CRS Report](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48215)

### [+] Fishing Subsidies: Paying to Overfish

Governments worldwide pay over **$30 billion in annual subsidies** to fishing fleets. Much of this money goes to the operations causing the most harm:

- Subsidies pay for fuel, vessel construction, gear, and port infrastructure for industrial fleets
- The most harmful subsidies — those that increase fishing capacity and effort — are estimated at **$22 billion per year** globally
- A 2022 WTO agreement to reform harmful fishing subsidies was a first step, but implementation and enforcement remain weak
- China operates the world's largest distant-water fishing fleet, with substantial state subsidies enabling it to fish in waters far from home, including off West African and South American coasts

[source: Our World in Data - Fish and Overfishing](https://ourworldindata.org/fish-and-overfishing)

### [+] Climate Change as a Multiplier

Overfishing and climate change interact in ways that accelerate population collapse:

- **Ocean warming** shifts species distributions poleward, disrupting ecosystems and catch areas
- **Ocean acidification** threatens shellfish and the plankton at the base of the marine food web
- **Deoxygenation** creates expanding "dead zones" where fish cannot survive
- **Coral bleaching** destroys the nursery habitats juvenile fish depend on

Fisheries already under stress from overfishing are far less resilient to climate-driven shocks. Management margins that might have been adequate in a stable climate are now insufficient.

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The global trajectory is clear: more stocks overexploited each year, subsidies sustaining the fleets doing the damage, and enforcement lagging behind the rate of collapse.
- The U.S. example proves recovery is possible — when management is science-based, enforced, and politically sustained. The Magnuson-Stevens Act has genuinely rebuilt overfished U.S. stocks. The question is whether that model can be scaled globally.
- Current rollbacks to environmental oversight in the U.S. — including reduced NOAA staffing and budget cuts — risk eroding the progress made under decades of fisheries management.
- Without strong Marine Protected Areas (with real enforcement), bycatch reduction requirements, and an end to harmful subsidies, the baseline will continue declining.

### Who's Affected

- **Coastal and island communities:** Billions of people globally depend on wild-caught fish as their primary protein source. Fishery collapse means food insecurity.
- **Commercial fishing workers:** The same fleets that deplete stocks ultimately destroy the livelihoods of fishermen — as the cod collapse showed.
- **Indigenous communities:** Subsistence and cultural fishing practices are threatened when stocks collapse.
- **Marine ecosystems:** Removing top predators and keystone species cascades through entire food webs.
- **Future generations:** Recovery from overfishing takes decades — and some stocks may never return.

## Quick Actions

### Check Seafood Watch Before You Buy (2 minutes)

The Monterey Bay Aquarium's Seafood Watch program rates seafood sustainability:

- Visit [seafoodwatch.org](https://www.seafoodwatch.org) or download the free app
- Look for "Best Choice" or "Good Alternative" ratings
- Avoid "Avoid" listed species — particularly wild-caught shrimp, imported swordfish, and farmed salmon from some regions
- Ask your grocery store, restaurant, or sushi bar where their seafood comes from

### Participate in Citizen Science Fish Surveys

- **REEF Fish Survey Project** ([reef.org](https://www.reef.org)) — Volunteer underwater survey program; training available for divers and snorkelers
- **iNaturalist** ([inaturalist.org](https://www.inaturalist.org)) — Document fish sightings from shore, pier, or boat
- **NOAA Citizen Science** ([fisheries.noaa.gov/contact/office-science-and-technology](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/contact/office-science-and-technology)) — Various programs for anglers and recreational fishers

### Support Marine Protected Area Expansion

- Contact your senators about expanding National Marine Sanctuaries and Marine National Monuments
- Support the "30x30" initiative to protect 30% of U.S. lands and waters by 2030
- Oppose any rollbacks to existing MPAs or monument boundaries

### Submit Public Comments on NOAA Fisheries Regulations (5 minutes)

NOAA Fisheries regularly opens comment periods on fishery management plans and annual catch limit updates. Public comments from non-industry stakeholders are important counterweights to fishing industry lobbying:

- Visit [regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov) and search "NOAA Fisheries" or "Magnuson-Stevens" for open dockets
- Key dockets to watch: annual catch limit updates for overfished species, fishery management plan amendments, and any NOAA proposals to weaken catch limits
- Comments supporting science-based limits and stock rebuilding timelines carry real weight in the administrative record

### Volunteer for Beach and Ocean Monitoring

- **NOAA Marine Debris Program** ([marinedebris.noaa.gov](https://marinedebris.noaa.gov)) — Organize or join a beach cleanup; data collected is submitted to NOAA's national database
- **Oceana Wavemakers** ([act.oceana.org](https://act.oceana.org)) — Volunteer network for ocean advocacy events and coastal monitoring
- **Audubon Society Seabird Monitoring** — Seabird populations are direct indicators of fishery health; volunteer programs in many coastal states

### Boycott the Most Harmful Seafood Sources

Make consumer choices that directly reduce market demand for the most destructive fishing practices:

- Avoid **wild-caught farmed Atlantic salmon** unless ASC-certified (high bycatch and habitat impact)
- Avoid **imported shrimp** from Southeast Asia without certification (often trawl-caught with extreme bycatch)
- Avoid **orange roughy** and **Chilean sea bass** (unless MSC-certified) — extremely slow-reproducing deep-sea species
- Ask restaurants where their fish comes from — establishments that can't answer are likely sourcing irresponsibly
