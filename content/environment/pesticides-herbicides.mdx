# Pesticides & Herbicides

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The United States applies over 1.1 billion pounds of pesticides annually -- roughly 22% of global use. Glyphosate (Roundup), the most widely used herbicide in history, is classified as "probably carcinogenic" by the WHO's International Agency for Research on Cancer, and Bayer faces 65,000+ lawsuits and over $11 billion in settlements. Neonicotinoid insecticides are driving pollinator collapse -- the EU banned three key neonics in 2018, but the US has not. The organophosphate chlorpyrifos, linked to brain damage in children, was briefly banned then reinstated for some food crops. Meanwhile, farmworkers suffer elevated rates of cancer, neurological disorders, and reproductive harm, and 75% of non-organic produce contains pesticide residues.

You can reduce your exposure, support safer farming, and push for stronger regulation -- starting today.

## Facts

### Scale of US Pesticide Use

The US is the world's largest pesticide user, applying over 1.1 billion pounds of conventional pesticides per year. Herbicides account for nearly 60% of total use, with glyphosate alone applied to over 280 million acres annually. US expenditures on pesticides exceed $14 billion per year at the user level.

Pesticide use has increased dramatically since World War II -- from roughly 500 million pounds in the 1930s to a peak of nearly 1.5 billion pounds in the late 1970s, settling at around 1.1 billion pounds today.

[source: EPA Pesticide Sales and Usage Reports](https://www.epa.gov/pesticides/pesticides-industry-sales-and-usage-2008-2012-market-estimates) | [source: USDA NIFA Pesticide Trends](https://www.nifa.usda.gov/sites/default/files/resources/Pesticide%20Trends.pdf)

### [+] Glyphosate (Roundup): The Most-Used Herbicide in History

Glyphosate is the active ingredient in Roundup and hundreds of other herbicide products. It is applied on roughly 90% of US corn, soybean, and cotton acres, and is widely used in home gardens, parks, and along roadsides.

**Health concerns:**
- The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified glyphosate as "probably carcinogenic to humans" (Group 2A) in 2015
- A June 2025 long-term animal study linked glyphosate exposure to leukemia and osteosarcoma in rats, supporting the IARC classification
- A key safety paper from 2000, widely cited by industry, was retracted due to ghostwriting by Monsanto employees and undisclosed financial payments to listed authors
- EPA maintains that glyphosate is "not likely to be carcinogenic to humans" -- a position at odds with IARC and under review in 2026

**Litigation:**
- As of January 2026, approximately 65,000 Roundup lawsuits remain pending
- Bayer has paid over $11 billion in settlements and jury verdicts
- In 2025, a Georgia jury ordered Bayer to pay $2.1 billion to a single plaintiff who developed non-Hodgkin lymphoma
- In February 2026, Bayer proposed a $7.25 billion class settlement, with preliminary court approval granted in March 2026
- Bayer's CEO warned the company could be forced to pull glyphosate off the market if litigation is not contained

EPA's 15-year registration review for glyphosate is due for completion in 2026, with an updated human health risk assessment expected.

[source: EPA - Glyphosate](https://www.epa.gov/ingredients-used-pesticide-products/glyphosate) | [source: IARC Monograph on Glyphosate](https://monographs.iarc.who.int/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/mono112-10.pdf)

[source: Bayer $7.25B Settlement](https://cen.acs.org/environment/pesticides/bayer-roundup-glyphosate-cancer-class-action-lawsuit-settlement/104/web/2026/03) | [source: $2.1B Georgia Verdict](https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/healthcare/articles/2-billion-bayer-verdict-roundup-194517794.html)

### [+] Neonicotinoids: Pollinator Collapse

Neonicotinoids (neonics) are the most widely used insecticides in the world. They are neurotoxins that attack the central nervous system of insects. Three key neonics -- imidacloprid, clothianidin, and thiamethoxam -- are applied as seed coatings on most US corn, soybean, and canola crops.

**Impact on pollinators:**
- Chronic exposure causes neurological dysfunction, reproductive failure, and colony collapse in honeybees and wild bees
- EPA confirmed that neonicotinoid insecticides threaten extinction for more than 200 endangered species
- Pollinator-dependent crops account for roughly one-third of the US food supply, valued at over $15 billion annually

**Regulatory status:**
- The European Union banned outdoor use of imidacloprid, clothianidin, and thiamethoxam in 2018, and will prohibit imports of products containing even trace amounts by 2026
- The US has not banned neonicotinoids -- EPA issued limited restrictions and is conducting registration reviews expected in 2025-2026
- States including Illinois, New York, Rhode Island, Maine, Vermont, and Connecticut have passed their own neonicotinoid restrictions
- Canada restricted some neonic uses but has not imposed a full ban

[source: EPA Pollinator Protection Actions](https://www.epa.gov/pollinator-protection/epa-actions-protect-pollinators) | [source: Center for Food Safety - EPA Confirms Neonic Threats](https://www.centerforfoodsafety.org/press-releases/6814/epa-confirms-bee-killing-neonicotinoid-insecticides-threaten-extinction-for-more-than-200-endangered-species)

[source: NRDC - Neonicotinoids 101](https://www.nrdc.org/stories/neonicotinoids-101-effects-humans-and-bees) | [source: Wiley - Overview of Neonic Regulation](https://scijournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ps.70126)

### [+] Chlorpyrifos: A Nerve Agent on Your Food

Chlorpyrifos is an organophosphate insecticide -- the same chemical class as nerve agents like sarin. It has been used on fruits, vegetables, and grain crops since 1965.

**Health effects:**
- Prenatal exposure is associated with lower IQ, reduced working memory, attention disorders, and delayed motor development in children
- Scientists found that even small exposures cause irreparable damage to brain regions controlling language, memory, emotion, and behavior
- EPA's own scientists concluded in 2016 that no safe level of chlorpyrifos exists for children

**Regulatory whiplash:**
- 2017: EPA Administrator Scott Pruitt reversed a planned ban on chlorpyrifos
- 2021: EPA banned all food uses of chlorpyrifos
- 2023: The 8th Circuit Court of Appeals overturned the ban
- 2025: Earthjustice sued EPA for allowing chlorpyrifos back on fruits and vegetables including apples, cherries, citrus, and wheat
- Several states (California, Hawaii, New York, Maryland) have enacted their own bans

[source: Earthjustice - Chlorpyrifos](https://earthjustice.org/feature/chlorpyrifos-what-you-need-to-know) | [source: EWG - Chlorpyrifos Timeline](https://www.ewg.org/news-insights/news/2024/12/how-states-and-epa-acted-ban-brain-damaging-pesticide-chlorpyrifos)

### [+] Atrazine in Drinking Water

Atrazine is the second most commonly used herbicide in the US, applied primarily on corn. It is a frequent surface water and groundwater contaminant in agricultural regions.

- EPA's maximum contaminant level (MCL) for atrazine in drinking water is 3 micrograms per liter
- Atrazine is an endocrine disruptor linked to adverse reproductive effects in wildlife and under study for cancer risks
- Private wells and rural water systems in agricultural areas are most at risk -- they are not covered by federal drinking water standards
- The EU banned atrazine in 2004 due to persistent groundwater contamination; the US continues to allow it

[source: EPA - Atrazine](https://www.epa.gov/ingredients-used-pesticide-products/atrazine) | [source: EWG Tap Water Database - Atrazine](https://www.ewg.org/tapwater/reviewed-atrazine.php)

### [+] Farmworker Health Impacts

An estimated 2-3 million farmworkers in the US face routine pesticide exposure through mixing, applying, and working in treated fields. Pesticides can be absorbed through skin, inhaled, or carried home on clothing to expose workers' families.

- Farmworkers experience elevated rates of cancer (especially non-Hodgkin lymphoma, leukemia, and brain cancer), neurological disorders, and endocrine disruption
- Reproductive impacts include low birth weight, preterm delivery, stillbirth, and congenital abnormalities
- Farmworkers are not covered by OSHA's general duty clause protections -- they are among the only US workers without full federal safety coverage
- Pesticide drift from agricultural fields has caused illness at over 100 schools located within 200 feet of agricultural operations in Washington state alone

[source: EPA - Farmworker Safety](https://www.epa.gov/pesticide-worker-safety/protecting-safety-our-farmworkers) | [source: Farmworker Justice - Exposed and Ignored](https://kresge.org/sites/default/files/Exposed-and-ignored-Farmworker-Justice-KF.pdf)

### [+] What's in Your Food: The 2026 Dirty Dozen

EWG's 2026 Shopper's Guide found that 75% of conventionally grown produce contains pesticide residues. More than 95% of "Dirty Dozen" produce tested positive. For the first time, PFAS "forever chemical" pesticide residues were detected on 63% of all Dirty Dozen samples.

**2026 Dirty Dozen** (highest pesticide contamination):
1. Spinach
2. Strawberries
3. Grapes
4. Nectarines
5. Peaches
6. Cherries
7. Apples
8. Blackberries
9. Pears
10. Potatoes
11. Blueberries

**2026 Clean Fifteen** (lowest contamination -- OK to buy conventional):
Pineapple, sweet corn, avocados, papaya, onions, frozen sweet peas, asparagus, cabbage, cauliflower, watermelon, mangoes, bananas, carrots, mushrooms, kiwi. Nearly 60% of Clean Fifteen samples had no detectable residues.

[source: EWG 2026 Shopper's Guide](https://www.ewg.org/foodnews/) | [source: EWG 2026 Press Release](https://www.ewg.org/news-insights/news-release/2026/03/ewgs-2026-shoppers-guide-pesticides-producetm-finds-widespread)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- EPA's 2026 glyphosate review will determine the regulatory future of the most-used herbicide in history -- and the outcome will be shaped by industry pressure
- Without a federal neonicotinoid ban, pollinator decline will continue, threatening food security and agricultural productivity
- The chlorpyrifos regulatory cycle -- ban, overturn, reinstate -- shows how difficult it is to remove even well-documented neurotoxins from the food supply
- Farmworkers bear a disproportionate health burden while lacking basic occupational safety protections
- Children are uniquely vulnerable due to developing brains and bodies, higher intake relative to body weight, and proximity to treated surfaces
- State-level action has become the primary pathway for stronger pesticide regulation as federal policy stalls

### Who's Affected

- **Everyone who eats:** 75% of conventional produce contains pesticide residues
- **Farmworkers and their families:** 2-3 million workers with elevated cancer, neurological, and reproductive risks
- **Children:** Greater vulnerability to neurodevelopmental harm; schools near agricultural fields face drift exposure
- **Rural communities:** Private wells not covered by federal drinking water standards; atrazine and other contaminants in groundwater
- **Pollinators and ecosystems:** Colony collapse, endangered species, cascading food web effects
- **Environmental justice communities:** Low-income and immigrant farmworker communities bear the greatest exposure burden

## Quick Actions

### Buy Organic for the Dirty Dozen (Next Grocery Trip)

The simplest way to reduce your pesticide exposure is to buy organic for the items that matter most. Focus your organic budget on the Dirty Dozen -- spinach, strawberries, grapes, nectarines, peaches, cherries, apples, blackberries, pears, potatoes, and blueberries. Save money by buying conventional for the Clean Fifteen.

Look up your produce at [EWG's Shopper's Guide](https://www.ewg.org/foodnews/).

### Switch to IPM Gardening at Home (This Weekend)

Integrated Pest Management (IPM) reduces or eliminates pesticide use in your yard and garden:

1. **Identify before you spray** -- Most "pests" are harmless or beneficial. Use [iNaturalist](https://www.inaturalist.org) to ID insects before acting
2. **Attract beneficial insects** -- Plant native flowers to support ladybugs, lacewings, and parasitic wasps that eat pests
3. **Use physical barriers** -- Row covers, mulch, and hand-picking are effective for many garden pests
4. **Choose resistant varieties** -- Disease-resistant plant varieties reduce the need for fungicides
5. **If you must treat** -- Use targeted, least-toxic options: insecticidal soap, neem oil, Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis), or diatomaceous earth
6. **Never use neonicotinoid-treated plants** -- Ask your garden center if plants were treated with neonics before purchase

[source: EPA IPM Principles](https://www.epa.gov/safepestcontrol/integrated-pest-management-ipm-principles)

### Check Your Water for Pesticides (5 Minutes)

1. Look up your water system at [EWG's Tap Water Database](https://www.ewg.org/tapwater/) -- search by zip code
2. Check specifically for atrazine, glyphosate, and other herbicides
3. If you have a private well in an agricultural area, get it tested through your state health department (~$30-150)
4. Consider an activated carbon or reverse osmosis filter if contaminants are detected

### Petition Your School District for Pesticide-Free Grounds (15 Minutes)

Children spend hours on school grounds treated with herbicides and insecticides. Thirty-six states have some school pesticide regulations, but most lack comprehensive protections.

1. Contact your school board or facilities director and ask what pesticides are used on school grounds
2. Request adoption of an IPM policy that eliminates cosmetic pesticide use on playgrounds, athletic fields, and school lawns
3. Reference Connecticut's ban on synthetic herbicides at K-8 schools and New York's Child Safe Playing Fields Act as models
4. Connect with [Beyond Pesticides](https://www.beyondpesticides.org/programs/children-and-schools/overview) for template policies and organizing guides

## Sustained Actions

### Support Local Organic Agriculture

- Join a **Community Supported Agriculture (CSA)** program to buy directly from local organic farms
- Shop at **farmers markets** and ask growers about their pest management practices -- many small farms use IPM or organic methods even without certification
- Support the **Organic Farming Research Foundation** ([ofrf.org](https://ofrf.org)) -- funds research on organic farming methods
- Advocate for organic options in school lunch programs

### Engage in Local Pesticide Policy

- **Attend city council and county commission meetings** when pesticide policies are on the agenda
- Push for **pesticide-free parks and public spaces** -- many cities have adopted IPM policies for public grounds
- Support **pollinator-friendly habitat ordinances** that restrict neonic use in municipal landscaping
- Advocate for **buffer zones** between agricultural fields and schools, homes, and waterways
- Get involved in your state's pesticide regulatory process -- most states accept public comment on pesticide registration decisions

### Join Advocacy Organizations

- **Pesticide Action Network (PAN)** ([panna.org](https://www.panna.org)) -- Science-based pesticide policy advocacy and farmworker protection
- **Beyond Pesticides** ([beyondpesticides.org](https://www.beyondpesticides.org)) -- IPM resources, school pesticide policies, and community organizing
- **Environmental Working Group (EWG)** ([ewg.org](https://www.ewg.org)) -- Dirty Dozen list, tap water database, pesticide research
- **Xerces Society** ([xerces.org](https://www.xerces.org)) -- Pollinator conservation, neonic-free habitat guides
- **Farmworker Justice** ([farmworkerjustice.org](https://www.farmworkerjustice.org)) -- Legal and policy advocacy for farmworker health

### Citizen Science: Document and Monitor

- Report suspected pesticide drift or illegal application to your state environmental or agricultural agency
- Use [iNaturalist](https://www.inaturalist.org) to track pollinator populations in your area
- Join the **Great Sunflower Project** or **Bumble Bee Watch** to contribute to pollinator monitoring research
- Test your water and share results with your community -- building a local evidence base supports policy change
