# PFAS: Forever Chemicals in Your Water

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) -- a class of over 15,000 synthetic chemicals that never break down in the environment -- contaminate the drinking water of an estimated 176 million Americans. PFAS have been detected in the blood of 99% of Americans, including newborn babies. Exposure is linked to cancer, thyroid disease, immune suppression, and reproductive harm. In April 2024, EPA finalized the first-ever national drinking water standards for six PFAS compounds, but the current administration has rolled back protections for four of the six chemicals and delayed compliance deadlines to 2031.

There are 9,728 confirmed PFAS contamination sites across all 50 states. The chemicals accumulate in your body for years and the contamination is expanding, not shrinking.

## Facts

### Key Points

- 176 million Americans drink tap water contaminated with PFAS -- up from 158 million in earlier testing rounds [source: EWG March 2026 Report](https://www.ewg.org/news-insights/news-release/2026/03/new-data-shows-176m-exposed-forever-chemicals-trump-epa-rolls)
- 9,728 confirmed PFAS contamination sites have been identified across all 50 states [source: EWG PFAS Contamination Map](https://www.ewg.org/interactive-maps/pfas_contamination/)
- CDC has detected PFAS in the blood of 99% of Americans tested, including newborn babies [source: ATSDR PFAS Health Effects](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/pfas/about/health-effects.html)
- In May 2025, EPA kept drinking water limits for PFOA and PFOS (4 parts per trillion) but moved to rescind standards for four other PFAS compounds (PFHxS, PFNA, GenX, and the Hazard Index mixture) [source: EPA Announcement](https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-announces-it-will-keep-maximum-contaminant-levels-pfoa-pfos)
- Compliance deadline extended from 2029 to 2031, giving water systems two additional years before they must meet even the remaining standards [source: Holland & Knight EPA Analysis](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/10/epas-pfas-rulemaking-trajectory-key-updates)
- PFAS contamination in drinking water imposes estimated annual social costs of at least $8 billion in medical care, long-term health impacts, and reduced lifetime earnings [source: University of Arizona Research](https://news.arizona.edu/news/new-research-links-health-impacts-related-forever-chemicals-billions-economic-losses)
- Nearly 350 PFAS bills were introduced across 39 states in 2025 as states move to fill federal regulatory gaps [source: NCEL State Action Report](https://www.ncelenviro.org/articles/confronting-forever-chemicals-states-continue-to-lead-the-way-in-2025/)

### [+] What Are PFAS?

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are a family of over 15,000 synthetic chemicals that have been manufactured since the 1940s. They are built around chains of carbon atoms bonded to fluorine atoms -- the carbon-fluorine bond is the strongest covalent bond in organic chemistry, which is why these chemicals essentially never break down.

**Why they persist:**

- The carbon-fluorine bond is one of the shortest and strongest bonds known in chemistry -- resistant to heat, water, oil, and biological degradation [source: ITRC Physical and Chemical Properties](https://pfas-1.itrcweb.org/4-physical-and-chemical-properties/)
- PFAS resist hydrolysis, oxidation, photolysis, and microbial breakdown -- the normal processes that degrade industrial chemicals in the environment [source: Green Science Policy Institute](https://greensciencepolicy.org/harmful-chemicals/pfas/)
- Scientists cannot estimate an environmental half-life because PFAS products remain in the environment for hundreds to over 1,000 years [source: Yale Sustainability Explainer](https://sustainability.yale.edu/explainers/yale-experts-explain-pfas-forever-chemicals)
- In the human body, PFOA has a half-life of approximately 3.5 years and PFOS approximately 4.8 years -- meaning it takes 3-5 years for your body to eliminate just half of the amount present [source: Environmental Research Meta-Analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0013935123025471)
- PFAS accumulate primarily in blood and liver tissue and are cleared extremely slowly through urine [source: ATSDR Toxicokinetics](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/toxprofiles/tp200-c3.pdf)

**Common names you may recognize:**

- **PFOA** (perfluorooctanoic acid) -- formerly used to make Teflon
- **PFOS** (perfluorooctane sulfonic acid) -- formerly used in Scotchgard and firefighting foam
- **GenX** -- a replacement chemical marketed as safer, but also linked to health effects
- **PFHxS, PFNA, PFBS** -- other PFAS compounds found in drinking water

### [+] Where They Come From: Contamination Sources

PFAS enter the environment through multiple pathways. The largest sources are military installations, industrial facilities, and firefighting foam.

**Military bases and firefighting foam (AFFF):**

- The Department of Defense has identified 723 military installations where PFAS may have been used or released [source: EWG Military PFAS Map](https://www.ewg.org/research/mapping-pfas-chemical-contamination-206-us-military-sites)
- Since the 1970s, DOD has used aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) containing PFOS to fight fuel fires at bases, airfields, and shipyards [source: VA PFAS Exposures](https://www.publichealth.va.gov/exposures/pfas.asp)
- AFFF training exercises released massive quantities of PFAS directly into soil and groundwater near fire training areas [source: Clean Water Action](https://cleanwater.org/pfas-containing-firefighting-foams)

**Industrial and manufacturing sites:**

- Chemical manufacturing plants, chrome plating facilities, electronics manufacturers, and textile/paper producers release PFAS through waste streams, air emissions, and spills [source: EPA PFAS Overview](https://www.epa.gov/pfas/our-current-understanding-human-health-and-environmental-risks-pfas)
- Wastewater treatment plants cannot remove PFAS -- they pass through into rivers and drinking water supplies
- When PFAS-contaminated sewage sludge is applied as fertilizer on farmland, it contaminates soil, groundwater, and crops [source: NIEHS PFAS Page](https://www.niehs.nih.gov/health/topics/agents/pfc)

**Consumer products:**

PFAS are used in thousands of everyday products for their water-repellent and non-stick properties:

- Non-stick cookware (Teflon and similar coatings)
- Stain-resistant carpet and upholstery treatments
- Water-repellent clothing (especially outdoor/rain gear)
- Grease-resistant food packaging (fast food wrappers, microwave popcorn bags, pizza boxes)
- Cosmetics and personal care products
- Cleaning products, paints, and sealants

[source: EPA Consumer Product Exposure](https://www.epa.gov/pfas/our-current-understanding-human-health-and-environmental-risks-pfas)

### [+] Health Impacts

Scientific evidence links PFAS exposure to a wide range of serious health effects. The National Toxicology Program has concluded that PFOA and PFOS suppress the antibody response and are a hazard to immune system function in humans.

**Cancer:**

- Kidney cancer and testicular cancer have the strongest associations with PFOA exposure [source: NCI PFAS and Cancer](https://dceg.cancer.gov/research/what-we-study/pfas)
- A 2024 study in the Journal of Exposure Science found PFAS in drinking water associated with cancers of the oral cavity, lung, digestive system, brain, urinary system, and thyroid [source: Nature - PFAS Cancer Incidence Study](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41370-024-00742-2)
- The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified PFOA as a Group 1 carcinogen (carcinogenic to humans) in 2023

**Immune system suppression:**

- PFAS exposure reduces vaccine effectiveness -- children with higher PFAS blood levels produce fewer antibodies after vaccination [source: ATSDR Health Effects](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/pfas/about/health-effects.html)
- The National Toxicology Program confirmed PFOA and PFOS suppress the immune response in humans [source: NIEHS](https://www.niehs.nih.gov/health/topics/agents/pfc)

**Thyroid disease:**

- PFAS disrupt thyroid hormone production, which is critical for metabolism, brain development, and growth [source: EPA Health Risks](https://www.epa.gov/pfas/our-current-understanding-human-health-and-environmental-risks-pfas)
- Pregnant women and developing fetuses are particularly vulnerable to thyroid disruption

**Reproductive and developmental harm:**

- Associated with pre-eclampsia during pregnancy, low birth weight, and developmental delays [source: ATSDR Health Effects](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/pfas/about/health-effects.html)
- Linked to decreased fertility in both men and women
- Studies show associations with accelerated puberty in children

**Other health effects:**

- Elevated cholesterol levels [source: NIEHS](https://www.niehs.nih.gov/health/topics/agents/pfc)
- Liver damage and liver disease [source: ATSDR Health Effects](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/pfas/about/health-effects.html)
- Ulcerative colitis
- Endocrine disruption

### [+] Regulatory Status

**The 2024 Biden-era PFAS drinking water rule:**

In April 2024, EPA finalized the first-ever National Primary Drinking Water Regulation for six PFAS compounds:

| Chemical | Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL) |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| PFOA | 4 parts per trillion (ppt) |
| PFOS | 4 ppt |
| PFHxS | 10 ppt |
| PFNA | 10 ppt |
| HFPO-DA (GenX) | 10 ppt |
| Hazard Index (mixture of PFHxS, PFNA, GenX, PFBS) | 1.0 (unitless) |

[source: EPA PFAS Drinking Water Rule](https://www.epa.gov/sdwa/and-polyfluoroalkyl-substances-pfas)

**Trump administration rollbacks (2025-2026):**

- **May 2025:** EPA Administrator Zeldin announced EPA will keep PFOA and PFOS limits (4 ppt) but rescind standards for PFHxS, PFNA, GenX, and the Hazard Index mixture [source: EPA Announcement](https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-announces-it-will-keep-maximum-contaminant-levels-pfoa-pfos)
- **October 2025:** EPA proposed extending compliance deadline from 2029 to 2031 [source: BBK Law Analysis](https://bbklaw.com/resources/la-051525-epa-announces-plan-to-modify-pfas-drinking-water-rule-and-extend-compliance-deadline)
- EPA's PFAS research budget has been cut, and funding to states for PFAS testing and remediation reduced
- CERCLA (Superfund) designation of PFOA and PFOS as hazardous substances is under review [source: EPA PFAS Cleanup Announcement](https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/trump-epa-announces-next-steps-regulatory-pfoa-and-pfos-cleanup-efforts-provides)

[source: Earthjustice Response](https://earthjustice.org/press/2025/epa-announces-illegal-plan-to-eliminate-restrictions-for-toxic-pfas-in-drinking-water) | [source: NRDC Analysis](https://www.nrdc.org/press-releases/epa-seeks-roll-back-pfas-drinking-water-rules-keeping-millions-exposed-toxic-forever)

**State-level action:**

Several states have enacted their own PFAS drinking water standards, some stricter than federal limits:

| State | PFOA Standard | PFOS Standard | Notes |
|-------|--------------|--------------|-------|
| New Jersey | 14 ppt | 13 ppt | First state to set enforceable limits |
| Michigan | 8 ppt | 16 ppt | Also regulates PFNA (6 ppt) |
| New Hampshire | 12 ppt | 15 ppt | Covers 4 PFAS compounds |
| Vermont | 20 ppt (combined) | 20 ppt (combined) | Covers 5 PFAS as a group |
| New York | 10 ppt | 10 ppt | Set in 2020 |
| Massachusetts | 20 ppt (combined) | 20 ppt (combined) | Covers 6 PFAS as a group |

[source: BCLP State-by-State Standards](https://www.bclplaw.com/en-US/events-insights-news/pfas-drinking-water-standards-state-by-state-regulations.html) | [source: MultiState 2026 PFAS Analysis](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/1/22/forever-chemicals-face-sweeping-bans-as-states-pass-pfas-laws-in-2025)

In 2025, nearly 350 PFAS-related bills were introduced in 39 states, covering drinking water, product bans, and cleanup liability. [source: Safer States 2026 Analysis](https://www.saferstates.org/resource/2026-analysis-of-state-policy-addressing-toxic-chemicals-and-plastics/pfas-forever-chemicals-policies-lead-in-2026/)

### [+] How to Protect Yourself: Water Testing and Filtration

**Step 1: Find out what's in your water**

- **EWG Tap Water Database:** Enter your ZIP code at [ewg.org/tapwater](https://www.ewg.org/tapwater/) to see contaminants detected in your water system, including PFAS
- **EWG PFAS Contamination Map:** Check the [interactive map](https://www.ewg.org/interactive-maps/pfas_contamination/) for known contamination sites near you
- **USGS PFAS Dashboard:** View nationwide tap water PFAS data at the [USGS interactive dashboard](https://www.usgs.gov/tools/pfas-us-tapwater-interactive-dashboard)
- **Home water test:** Order a certified PFAS test kit ($100-300) through your state health department or a certified lab

**Step 2: Choose the right filter**

Not all water filters remove PFAS. Look for NSF/ANSI certification for PFAS reduction:

| Filter Type | NSF Standard | Effectiveness | Cost |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|------|
| **Reverse osmosis (RO)** | NSF/ANSI 58 | Removes 90-99% of PFAS | $150-600 (under-sink) |
| **Activated carbon (granular)** | NSF/ANSI 53 | Removes many PFAS, varies by compound | $20-200 (pitcher/faucet) |
| **Ion exchange** | NSF/ANSI 53 | Effective for long-chain PFAS | $200-500 (whole-house) |

[source: EPA Filter Certification Guide](https://www.epa.gov/water-research/identifying-drinking-water-filters-certified-reduce-pfas) | [source: NSF Filtration Standards](https://www.nsf.org/knowledge-library/forever-chemicals-advancement-filtration-standards)

**Critical:** Filters only work if maintained on schedule. Replace cartridges according to manufacturer instructions -- an expired filter can actually release trapped contaminants back into your water. [source: EPA Water Filter Fact Sheet](https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2024-04/water-filter-fact-sheet.pdf)

**Step 3: Reduce other PFAS exposure**

- Avoid non-stick cookware -- use cast iron, stainless steel, or ceramic instead
- Choose PFAS-free food packaging when possible
- Avoid stain-resistant fabric treatments
- Check cosmetics for PFAS ingredients (look for "fluoro" in ingredient lists)

## Analysis

### What This Means

The federal government established the first-ever PFAS drinking water standards in 2024 after decades of inaction -- then immediately began weakening them. By rescinding standards for four of six regulated PFAS and extending compliance deadlines, the current EPA is leaving millions of Americans exposed to chemicals the agency itself acknowledged are dangerous at any detectable level.

The core problem is one of accountability. The companies that manufactured and profited from PFAS -- primarily 3M and DuPont -- knew about health risks for decades before the public learned of them. Now taxpayers and water utilities are bearing the multi-billion dollar cost of cleanup. The delay to 2031 means water systems that have already detected dangerous PFAS levels can legally continue serving contaminated water for five more years.

State-level action is filling some gaps, but creates a patchwork of protections where your safety depends on which state you live in. Communities near military bases and industrial sites -- often low-income and communities of color -- bear the worst contamination and have the fewest resources to install filtration.

### Who's Affected

- **Military families and veterans:** 723 DOD installations have known or suspected PFAS contamination. Service members, their families, and surrounding communities have experienced decades of exposure from AFFF firefighting foam used in training exercises. Many military communities have PFAS blood levels several times higher than the national average.

- **Communities near industrial sites:** People living near PFAS manufacturing plants, chemical facilities, and airports face the highest contamination levels. Parkersburg, West Virginia (DuPont Washington Works) and Fayetteville, North Carolina (Chemours Fayetteville Works) are among the most heavily contaminated communities in the country.

- **Low-income communities and communities of color:** Environmental justice communities are disproportionately located near contaminated industrial sites and military bases. These communities often lack the resources to install home filtration systems or purchase bottled water, and are least likely to have political influence to demand cleanup.

- **Rural communities on private wells:** Unlike public water systems, private wells are not required to test for PFAS. An estimated 23 million Americans rely on private wells, many of which may be contaminated without the owner's knowledge.

- **Children and pregnant women:** PFAS cross the placenta and are found in breast milk. Children are particularly vulnerable to immune suppression (reduced vaccine effectiveness) and developmental effects from early-life exposure.

- **Firefighters:** Both military and civilian firefighters who have used AFFF foam have among the highest occupational PFAS exposure. Studies show elevated rates of cancer among firefighters linked to PFAS exposure.

### Check If Your Water Is Contaminated (2 minutes)

1. **Search the EWG Tap Water Database:** Go to [ewg.org/tapwater](https://www.ewg.org/tapwater/) and enter your ZIP code
2. **Check the PFAS contamination map:** Visit [EWG's interactive PFAS map](https://www.ewg.org/interactive-maps/pfas_contamination/) to see known contamination sites near you
3. **Check the USGS dashboard:** View federal testing data at the [USGS PFAS tap water dashboard](https://www.usgs.gov/tools/pfas-us-tapwater-interactive-dashboard)
4. **Contact your water utility:** Call your local water provider and ask if they have tested for PFAS and what the results were

### Test Your Water

If your area shows PFAS contamination -- or if you are on a private well -- get your water tested:

- Contact your **state health department** for a list of certified PFAS testing labs
- Home PFAS test kits cost **$100-300** and test for 20-40 PFAS compounds
- If PFAS are detected, install an **NSF/ANSI 58 certified reverse osmosis filter** (most effective) or **NSF/ANSI 53 certified activated carbon filter**
