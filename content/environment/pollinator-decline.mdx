# Pollinator Decline

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The 2024-2025 season saw the highest honey bee colony losses ever recorded -- 56% of commercial colonies lost, 1.6 million hives gone, $600 million in damages. Monarch butterflies have declined 80% in the East and 96% in the West. The rusty patched bumble bee has lost 87% of its range. Meanwhile, neonicotinoid pesticides -- banned in the EU since 2018 -- remain in widespread use across U.S. agriculture, and EPA's registration review has dragged on for over a decade.

Pollinators are responsible for one in three bites of food we eat and contribute $34 billion annually to U.S. agriculture. This is not just an environmental crisis -- it is a food security emergency.

## Facts

### Record Honey Bee Colony Losses (2024-2025)

The 2024-2025 season produced the worst honey bee colony losses since tracking began in 2010. Commercial beekeepers lost an average of 56-62% of colonies between June 2024 and March 2025 -- approximately 1.6 million colonies with an estimated financial impact of $600 million.

USDA researchers identified viruses transmitted by Varroa destructor mites as the primary driver of the 2024-2025 collapse. Critically, all mites sampled showed genetic resistance to amitraz, the most widely used mite control treatment, leaving beekeepers with fewer management options.

[source: USDA ARS - Cause of Recent Colony Collapses](https://www.ars.usda.gov/news-events/news/research-news/2025/usda-researchers-find-viruses-from-miticide-resistant-parasitic-mites-are-cause-of-recent-honey-bee-colony-collapses/) | [source: Auburn University - Highest Colony Losses](https://agriculture.auburn.edu/feature/u-s-beekeeping-survey-reveals-highest-honeybee-colony-losses-during-2024-2025/)

### [+] Monarch Butterfly Collapse

| Population | Historic Baseline | Current Status | Decline |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|---------|
| Eastern monarchs | ~1 billion (1990s) | Near 30-year low | ~80% |
| Western monarchs | ~1.2 million (1990s) | 12,260 counted (2025-26) | ~99% |

The western monarch population crashed from 233,394 in 2023 to just 9,119 in 2024, a 96% single-year decline. The Xerces Society's 2025-26 count found only 12,260 butterflies across 249 sites. Scientists estimate a 98-99% probability of western monarch extinction within 60 years under current conditions.

The single biggest driver is the disappearance of milkweed -- the only plant monarch caterpillars can eat. Herbicide-tolerant crops have eliminated milkweed from agricultural landscapes where it once grew abundantly between rows of corn and soybeans.

In December 2024, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service proposed listing the monarch as threatened under the Endangered Species Act. A final decision was due December 2025 but remains pending.

[source: Xerces Society - Western Monarch Decline](https://xerces.org/press/western-monarch-butterfly-population-declines-to-near-record-low) | [source: NWF - Western Monarch Count](https://monarchs.nwf.org/western-monarch-population-count-underscores-need-for-endangered-species-listing/)

### [+] Native Bee Species at Risk

The pollinator crisis extends far beyond honey bees and monarchs:

- **Rusty patched bumble bee** -- First bee listed as federally endangered (2017). Population declined 87%, range reduced to 8% of historical extent. Once common across 28 states, now found in only 13 states. USFWS proposed 1.6 million acres of critical habitat in 2024.
- **American bumble bee** -- Declined 89% in the last 20 years, petitioned for ESA listing
- **Franklin's bumble bee** -- Not seen since 2006, possibly extinct
- **Yellow-banded bumble bee** -- Declined across much of its range in the eastern U.S.
- **Illinois** added 12 bee species to its state endangered/threatened list in 2025

[source: USFWS - Rusty Patched Bumble Bee](https://www.fws.gov/species/rusty-patched-bumble-bee-bombus-affinis) | [source: Federal Register - Critical Habitat Designation](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/11/26/2024-27316/endangered-and-threatened-wildlife-and-plants-designation-of-critical-habitat-for-the-rusty-patched-bumble-bee)

### [+] Neonicotinoid Pesticides: The Chemical Connection

Neonicotinoids are the world's most widely used insecticides, applied as seed coatings on the majority of U.S. corn, soybeans, cotton, and canola. They are systemic -- absorbed by the plant and present in pollen, nectar, and water droplets that pollinators consume.

**Regulatory status:**
- **European Union:** Banned outdoor use of imidacloprid, clothianidin, and thiamethoxam since 2018
- **United States:** Still in widespread use. EPA's registration review has been ongoing since 2012 with no final decisions
- **Canada:** Partial restrictions, with further reevaluation ongoing

A Center for Food Safety report found that neonicotinoid seed treatments are unnecessary in most cases -- studies show they provide little or no yield benefit for many crops while exposing pollinators to lethal and sublethal doses.

[source: EPA - Neonicotinoid Registration Review Schedule](https://www.epa.gov/pollinator-protection/schedule-review-neonicotinoid-pesticides) | [source: Center for Food Safety - Seed Treatments Unnecessary](https://www.centerforfoodsafety.org/issues/304/pollinators-and-pesticides/press-releases/3000/new-report-widely-used-neonicotinoid-seed-treatments-are-unnecessary-in-most-cases)

### [+] Food Security at Stake

| Statistic | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Share of global food crops requiring animal pollination | 35% (1 in 3 bites) |
| Annual value of pollination to U.S. agriculture | $34 billion |
| Global value of pollination services | $235-800 billion |
| Crop species pollinated by bees (of top 100 food crops) | 71 |

Key U.S. crops dependent on pollinators include almonds (100% bee-pollinated), apples, blueberries, cherries, watermelons, squash, pumpkins, and alfalfa (which feeds the dairy and beef industries). California's $6 billion almond industry alone requires 2.7 million managed honey bee colonies each year -- more than exist in the entire country.

[source: USFWS - Pollinators Benefit Agriculture](https://www.fws.gov/initiative/pollinators/pollinators-benefit-agriculture) | [source: USFWS - Pollinators Benefit Economies](https://www.fws.gov/initiative/pollinators/pollinators-benefit-economies)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Record colony losses plus mite resistance to the primary treatment creates a compounding crisis with no quick fix
- The monarch ESA listing decision has stalled, leaving the species without federal protection during its most vulnerable period
- EPA's decade-plus neonicotinoid review allows continued widespread use while pollinators decline
- The 10-for-1 deregulation mandate (EO 14192) makes new pesticide restrictions nearly impossible at the federal level
- Without pollinators, many crops would require hand pollination or produce dramatically reduced yields -- some crops would become commercially unviable
- The economic consequences would cascade through food prices, agricultural jobs, and export markets

### Who's Affected

- **Farmers and ranchers:** Crop yields and livestock feed depend on pollination. Beekeepers face unsustainable losses.
- **Consumers:** Pollinator decline drives up prices for fruits, vegetables, and nuts -- the healthiest foods
- **Rural economies:** Agricultural communities dependent on pollinator-reliant crops
- **Ecosystems:** 85% of flowering plants depend on animal pollinators for reproduction
- **Everyone who eats:** One in three bites of food depends on pollination

### Plant a Pollinator Garden (This Weekend)

You do not need acres of land -- even a few square feet of native plants makes a difference. The key is choosing plants native to your region that bloom across the full growing season (spring through fall).

**Native Plants by Region:**

| Region | Spring | Summer | Fall |
|--------|--------|--------|------|
| **Northeast** | Columbine, Virginia bluebells | Bee balm, purple coneflower, milkweed | New England aster, goldenrod |
| **Southeast** | Coral honeysuckle, wild indigo | Butterfly weed, black-eyed Susan, bee balm | Ironweed, blazing star |
| **Midwest** | Wild lupine, golden alexanders | Common milkweed, purple coneflower, prairie phlox | Stiff goldenrod, heath aster |
| **Mountain West** | Pasqueflower, Rocky Mountain penstemon | Showy milkweed, blanketflower, hyssop | Rabbitbrush, prairie sunflower |
| **Pacific Northwest** | Red flowering currant, Oregon grape | Showy milkweed, Douglas aster, fireweed | Oregon sunshine, goldenrod |
| **Southwest** | Desert marigold, penstemon | Desert milkweed, globemallow, agave | Sacred datura, desert zinnia |

**Find your exact ecoregion planting guide:** [Pollinator Partnership Planting Guides](https://pollinator.org/guides) -- enter your zip code for a free, downloadable guide with species specific to your area.

**Milkweed is critical** -- it is the only host plant for monarch caterpillars. Common milkweed (*Asclepias syriaca*) grows in most of the eastern U.S.; showy milkweed (*Asclepias speciosa*) in the West; butterfly weed (*Asclepias tuberosa*) adapts broadly. Never plant tropical milkweed (*Asclepias curassavica*) -- it can disrupt monarch migration and spread disease.

[source: Xerces Society - Pollinator-Friendly Plant Lists](https://xerces.org/pollinator-conservation/pollinator-friendly-plant-lists) | [source: USFWS - Top Plants for Pollinator Gardens](https://www.fws.gov/story/top-plants-your-pollinator-garden)

### Stop Using Neonicotinoids in Your Yard (Today)

- **Check your products:** Look for active ingredients imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam, dinotefuran, or acetamiprid on insecticide labels
- **Avoid "systemic" insecticides** for ornamental plants -- these are typically neonicotinoids
- **Ask your garden center:** Many plants sold at nurseries are pre-treated with neonicotinoids. Ask if plants are "neonic-free" before purchasing
- **Use alternatives:** Neem oil, insecticidal soap, beneficial insects (ladybugs, lacewings), and integrated pest management

### Buy Organic to Reduce Pesticide Demand

- Organic farming prohibits neonicotinoids and most synthetic pesticides
- Prioritize organic for the crops most heavily treated: corn, soybeans, cotton products, and stone fruits
- Look for **Bee Better Certified** products -- a Xerces Society program certifying pollinator-friendly farming
- Support local farmers' markets where you can ask growers directly about their practices

### Join Bumble Bee Watch Citizen Science (10 minutes)

1. Visit [bumblebeewatch.org](https://www.bumblebeewatch.org) or download the mobile app
2. Take a photo of any bumble bee you see
3. Upload with location data -- experts verify the species
4. Your observations fill critical knowledge gaps, especially for rare species
5. Over 75,000 observations have been submitted, helping track species like the rusty patched bumble bee

[source: Bumble Bee Watch](https://www.bumblebeewatch.org)

### Push for Local Pesticide Restrictions

Cities and counties across the U.S. are banning cosmetic pesticide use on public and private land:

- **Montgomery County, MD** -- Banned lawn pesticides in 2015 (upheld by state's highest court)
- **Portland, ME** -- Banned neonicotinoids, glyphosate, and combo fertilizer-pesticides (2018)
- **Baltimore, MD** -- Prohibited neonicotinoids on lawns, playgrounds, and playing fields (2022)
- **Seattle, WA** -- Banned city use of neonicotinoids (2014)
- **Boulder, CO** -- Restricted bee-toxic pesticides on city property (2015)

**How to start in your community:**
1. Research whether your municipality has preemption laws (some states block local pesticide regulation)
2. Contact your city council member and present the case for a pollinator-friendly ordinance
3. Build a coalition with local beekeepers, gardeners, and environmental groups
4. Model your ordinance on successful examples above

## Sustained Actions

### Support Pollinator Conservation Organizations

- **Xerces Society** ([xerces.org](https://xerces.org)) -- Leading invertebrate conservation org. Has implemented 120,000+ acres of pollinator habitat. Runs Bumble Bee Watch and Bee Better Certified.
- **Pollinator Partnership** ([pollinator.org](https://pollinator.org)) -- Regional planting guides, National Pollinator Week, agricultural pollinator programs
- **Monarch Joint Venture** ([monarchjointventure.org](https://monarchjointventure.org)) -- Federal-state-NGO partnership for monarch conservation. Free native seed mixes and milkweed plugs through the Monarchs and More program.
- **The Bee Conservancy** ([thebeeconservancy.org](https://thebeeconservancy.org)) -- Sponsors hive installations, educational programs, and citizen science
- **Local native plant societies** -- Find yours through the [National Wildlife Federation Plant Finder](https://www.nwf.org/nativeplantfinder/)

### Join or Start a Community Pollinator Project

- **Pollinator Pathway** ([pollinator-pathway.org](https://www.pollinator-pathway.org)) -- Networks of pollinator-friendly gardens connecting fragmented habitat in neighborhoods
- **Community gardens:** Advocate for dedicating sections to native pollinator plants
- **School gardens:** Help local schools plant pollinator gardens -- many grant programs exist
- **Church and civic grounds:** Offer to convert mowed lawn areas to native wildflower meadows
- **"No-mow May":** Advocate for your city to adopt No-Mow May, letting early-season wildflowers bloom for hungry pollinators

### Become a Beekeeper or Support Local Beekeepers

- Find your local beekeeping association through the [American Beekeeping Federation](https://www.abfnet.org)
- Many clubs offer beginner courses and mentorship programs
- If you cannot keep bees yourself, buy local honey -- it supports beekeepers in your area
- Consider sponsoring a hive through a local beekeeper or the Bee Conservancy

### Advocate for Agricultural Change

- Support the **Saving America's Pollinators Act** (reintroduced in Congress) to suspend neonicotinoid registrations pending EPA review
- Push for expanding USDA Conservation Reserve Program (CRP) pollinator habitat incentives
- Advocate for state-level neonicotinoid restrictions -- 11 states have enacted some form of restriction
- Support farm bill provisions that fund pollinator habitat on working agricultural lands

### Emergency Preparedness for Beekeepers

- Stay informed on Varroa mite resistance developments through [Bee Informed Partnership](https://beeinformed.org)
- Diversify mite treatment strategies -- do not rely solely on amitraz
- Report unusual colony losses to your state apiarist and the [Bee Informed Partnership annual survey](https://beeinformed.org/citizen-science/loss-and-management-survey/)
