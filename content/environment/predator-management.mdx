# Predator Management: The War on Wolves, Bears, and Cougars

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The USDA's Wildlife Services program killed **375,045 native animals** in 2023 at taxpayer expense — including **305 gray wolves**, **430 black bears**, **235 mountain lions**, and **68,562 coyotes** — primarily to benefit the livestock industry. [source: Center for Biological Diversity - 2023 Kill Data](https://biologicaldiversity.org/w/news/press-releases/375000-native-animals-killed-by-federal-program-in-2023-2024-04-03/) Science is unambiguous: apex predators like wolves, bears, and cougars regulate ecosystems from the top down. Their removal causes cascading ecological collapse. Utah has eliminated all limits on mountain lion hunting — anyone with a general hunting license can kill them year-round with no permit. Mexican gray wolf recovery is making slow progress but faces continued illegal killing and political opposition. Grizzly bears remain under threat of delisting. Killing contests for coyotes remain legal in most Western states.

The war on predators is not science-based wildlife management. It is a taxpayer subsidy to the livestock industry at the expense of functional ecosystems.

## Facts

### USDA Wildlife Services: The Federal Predator Killing Program

The USDA's **Wildlife Services** program is one of the least-known and most consequential federal wildlife programs. In fiscal year 2023, Wildlife Services killed **375,045 native animals**, primarily at the request of agricultural interests. [source: Center for Biological Diversity](https://biologicaldiversity.org/w/news/press-releases/375000-native-animals-killed-by-federal-program-in-2023-2024-04-03/)

**Key 2023 kill numbers:** [source: Project Coyote - 2023 Wildlife Services Report](https://projectcoyote.org/media-release-2023-wildlife-services-report-taxpayer-dollars-used-to-kill-over-70000-wild-carnivores/)
- **68,562 coyotes**
- **305 gray wolves**
- **430 black bears**
- **235 mountain lions**
- **469 bobcats**
- **2,122 foxes** (red and gray)
- **24,603 beavers**

Wildlife Services operates largely without public scrutiny, using methods including aerial gunning, leg-hold traps, poison bait stations (M-44 "cyanide bombs"), and snares. M-44 devices — spring-loaded tubes that spray sodium cyanide when triggered — have killed dogs, injured humans, and killed non-target wildlife, yet remain in use. [source: NPR - Wildlife Services Investigation](https://www.npr.org/2024/10/10/g-s1-26426/wildlife-services-usda-wild-animals-killed-livestock)

**The math on justification is striking:** Over three years in Montana, Wildlife Services killed 71 wolves at just five locations. During the same period, wolves at those locations were documented harming 61 cattle and sheep. Montana has approximately 2.5 million cattle and sheep. The program killed 6% of the state's wolf population to protect against documented predation on 0.002% of Montana's livestock. [source: NPR - Wildlife Services Investigation](https://www.npr.org/2024/10/10/g-s1-26426/wildlife-services-usda-wild-animals-killed-livestock)

The program kills more than 22 million animals per decade — the vast majority birds and rodents, but tens of thousands of carnivores annually.

### [+] The Science of Trophic Cascades

The ecological case against predator removal is now overwhelming. **Trophic cascades** — the ripple effects of top predator presence or absence through entire ecosystems — have been documented worldwide.

The most studied example is Yellowstone. Wolves were extirpated from Yellowstone by 1926. They were reintroduced in 1995 and 1996. Thirty years of subsequent research, synthesized in work by Dr. William Ripple and colleagues at Oregon State University, documents the cascade effects: [source: Trophic Cascades in Yellowstone - Ripple & Beschta](https://digitalcommons.usu.edu/aspen_bib/7382/)

- **Elk behavior changed:** Elk avoided riverbanks and open areas where wolves could hunt them, allowing streamside vegetation to recover
- **Willows and aspens recovered:** 24 studies found increases in plant height, stem diameter, establishment, canopy cover, or recruitment in riparian areas
- **Beavers returned:** More woody plants meant more beaver food; beaver dams stabilized stream flows
- **Songbirds increased:** Recovering riparian vegetation provided nesting habitat
- **Stream morphology changed:** Beaver dams and stabilized banks altered river channels themselves

This "landscape of fear" — where elk presence is governed by predator risk, not just food availability — is absent wherever predators have been eliminated. Without wolves, elk overgraze stream banks, destabilizing waterways and reducing biodiversity. [source: Trophic Cascades from Wolves to Alders - Ripple et al.](https://trophiccascades.forestry.oregonstate.edu/sites/default/files/Ripple_alder2015.pdf)

Similar trophic cascade research documents the effects of cougar removal on deer, vegetation, and stream ecology across the American West.

### [+] Utah: Open Season on Mountain Lions

In 2023, the Utah Legislature, in a **last-minute amendment to a wildlife bill**, eliminated permit requirements for mountain lion hunting. Now, anyone with a general hunting license can **shoot, trap, or snare mountain lions with no limit, year-round**. No permit. No quota. No season. [source: Deseret News - Utah Year-Round Cougar Hunting](https://www.deseret.com/the-west/2026/02/02/utah-year-round-cougar-hunting-impact-mule-deer-sheep-herds-wildlife-management/)

Going further, Utah is paying hunters and trappers to kill **as many mountain lions as possible** in six of its 30 hunting units as part of a state-funded predator removal study, running continuously through **2029**, funded in part by sportsmen's groups. The stated goal: determine whether killing cougars increases mule deer and bighorn sheep populations for hunters. [source: High Country News - Utah's War on Cougars](https://www.hcn.org/articles/utahs-new-study-aims-to-kill-as-many-cougars-as-possible/)

In 2024–2025, 371 cougars were killed statewide — and the study units are actively seeking higher numbers. Wildlife advocates note that mountain lions naturally regulate deer populations, reducing deer-vehicle collisions and the spread of Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD) by removing sick animals. Removing cougars may worsen the very problems the state says it wants to solve.

### [+] Gray Wolf Recovery and Backlash

Gray wolves were reintroduced to the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem in 1995 after 70 years of absence. The recovery has been one of the most successful — and most politically contested — in Endangered Species Act history.

**Current population:** Approximately 2,797 gray wolves in the western US as of a 2022 USFWS report — roughly 1,000 in Idaho, 1,000 in Montana, fewer than 400 in Wyoming, and around 200 across Oregon and Washington. [source: Idaho Fish & Game - Wolf Delisting FAQ](https://idfg.idaho.gov/press/frequently-asked-questions-gray-wolf-delisting)

**Delisting and hunt expansion:** Following delisting from ESA protections in Idaho and Montana, both states immediately implemented aggressive hunt programs. Idaho's 2023–2028 Wolf Management Plan targets reducing the state's population from ~1,200 to **500 animals** — a 58% reduction. Idaho authorized unlimited wolf kills, night hunting, trapping, and snaring. [source: Idaho Wolf Management Plan 2023-2028](https://idfg.idaho.gov/sites/default/files/idaho-gray-wolf-management-plan-2023-2028.pdf)

In December 2025, the US House of Representatives voted to delist gray wolves nationally from ESA protections. [source: Daily Montanan - House Votes to Delist Wolves](https://dailymontanan.com/2025/12/18/house-votes-to-delist-gray-wolves-from-endangered-species-list/)

**Non-lethal coexistence tools** — guardian dogs (livestock guard dogs), fladry (flagging that deters wolves), range riders who patrol grazing allotments, and shed placement — have been documented to reduce livestock losses significantly. They are chronically underfunded relative to lethal control.

### [+] Grizzly Bear Delisting Controversy

Greater Yellowstone grizzly bears were listed as threatened in 1975, with only ~150 bears remaining. By 2016, the population had recovered to over 700 bears and USFWS proposed delisting. Lawsuits by conservation groups successfully overturned two delisting attempts, in 2007 and 2017.

As of early 2025, the Biden administration's final position was to maintain ESA protections while proposing a new rule clarifying the geographic scope of protections across all six grizzly recovery zones as a single Distinct Population Segment. [source: USFWS Grizzly Bear ESA Proposal - January 2025](https://www.fws.gov/press-release/2025-01/usfws-proposes-update-grizzly-bear-esa-listing-management)

However, the **Grizzly Bear State Management Act of 2025** (HR 281), introduced by Rep. Harriet Hageman (R-WY) with Montana's congressional delegation, would legislatively delist the Greater Yellowstone population — bypassing ESA scientific review entirely. A House committee approved this approach in July 2025. [source: Daily Montanan - House Committee Signs Off on Delisting](https://dailymontanan.com/2025/07/17/house-committee-signs-off-on-delisting-grizzly-bear/)

State wildlife agencies in Wyoming, Montana, and Idaho have already developed grizzly hunting quotas contingent on delisting. Conservation groups note that once hunting begins, the likelihood of litigation-triggered relisting has historically been high, creating regulatory uncertainty.

### [+] Mexican Gray Wolf: Slow Recovery Under Political Pressure

The **Mexican gray wolf** (*Canis lupus baileyi*) is the most genetically distinct and historically smallest subspecies of gray wolf. Extirpated from the wild by the 1970s, a US-Mexico recovery program has reintroduced captive-born wolves into Arizona and New Mexico since 1998.

As of 2025, the US population stands at **319 wolves** — the ninth consecutive year of growth, and an 11% increase from 2024's count of 286. [source: NY Wolf Center - Mexican Gray Wolf 2025](https://nywolf.org/2026/02/wild-mexican-gray-wolf-population-increases-to-319-wolves-in-southwest/) This approaches the interim recovery target of 320 wolves, which under the contested 2022 recovery plan would trigger consideration for downlisting from Endangered to Threatened.

**Ongoing challenges:**
- **Illegal killing** is the leading cause of mortality — poaching remains a significant and underreported problem
- Wolves are **geographically confined** to the Blue Range Wolf Recovery Area; those that disperse north of I-40 in Arizona are rounded up and removed, limiting range expansion essential for long-term genetic viability
- Conservation groups argue wolves must be allowed to establish populations north of the Grand Canyon, and the 2022 recovery plan's geographic restrictions are too limiting

### [+] Killing Contests: Coyotes and Foxes

Wildlife killing contests — competitions where participants win prizes for killing the most animals in a set time — remain legal in most Western states, including Utah. These events typically target coyotes and foxes and have no ecological basis. Scientific agencies including USDA itself acknowledge that lethal coyote control does not reduce coyote populations long-term; coyotes respond to lethal pressure by increasing litter sizes and expanding ranges.

California banned wildlife killing contests in 2019. New Mexico banned them in 2023. Federal legislation, the **Prohibit Wildlife Killing Contests Act of 2024** (HR 8492), was introduced in the 118th Congress but did not advance. [source: Congress.gov - Prohibit Wildlife Killing Contests Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/8492)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The taxpayer-funded Wildlife Services program operates as a subsidy to the livestock industry with minimal public accountability or scientific justification
- Removing apex predators creates cascading ecological dysfunction — overgrazed landscapes, destabilized streams, CWD spread, and vegetation loss
- State-level predator management (Utah cougars, Idaho/Wyoming wolves) is often driven by hunting industry politics rather than science-based population management
- Non-lethal coexistence tools work and are chronically underfunded — a policy choice, not an inevitability
- Several populations (Mexican gray wolf, gray wolf in Wyoming) remain vulnerable to political extinction even after biological recovery

### Who's Affected

- **Ecosystems:** Predator removal causes measurable cascading harm to rivers, vegetation, prey species, and biodiversity across landscapes
- **Livestock producers:** Real losses do occur, but are often overstated relative to actual documentation; coexistence tools can reduce losses with adequate support
- **Hunters:** Paradoxically, predator removal can harm hunting by destabilizing prey populations; CWD spread in deer and elk is linked to lack of predation pressure
- **Taxpayers:** Wildlife Services' budget of approximately $100 million/year funds predator killing that primarily benefits private agricultural interests on public lands
- **Rural communities near Yellowstone:** Wolf recovery has created a thriving wolf-watching tourism economy — Yellowstone wolf tours generate an estimated $35 million annually
- **Utah residents:** The state's cougar management sets a precedent for eliminating all wildlife management science guardrails
- **Indigenous communities:** Wolves, bears, and cougars hold cultural significance for many tribes whose relationship with these animals predates European settlement

- **Governor Spencer Cox:** [governor.utah.gov/contact](https://governor.utah.gov/contact/) — Ask him to support restoring science-based mountain lion management

### Submit a FOIA Request for Wildlife Services Kill Data

Wildlife Services' detailed kill data — by county, method, and species — is technically public but not proactively published in useful formats. You can request it:

- Submit a FOIA request to USDA via [efoia.usda.gov](https://efoia.usda.gov)
- Request: "Wildlife Services Program Data Management System kill records, [state name], [year range], by species, county, and control method"
- Sharing the data publicly through local media or social networks builds public awareness of the program's scope

### Support Non-Lethal Coexistence on Your Public Lands

Livestock grazing on public lands is a major driver of Wildlife Services predator control. You can push for coexistence:

- Contact the **Bureau of Land Management** ([blm.gov](https://www.blm.gov)) when grazing permits come up for renewal in your region — these are public processes
- Ask BLM to include **non-lethal conflict prevention requirements** in grazing permit conditions
- Support ranchers who are voluntarily adopting coexistence practices through organizations like Keystone Conservation

### Attend or Watch Yellowstone Wolf Watching

Yellowstone's wolves are one of the most observable wild wolf populations in the world, and wolf-watching tourism generates an estimated **$35 million annually** for gateway communities. Your presence contributes to the economic case for wolf conservation:

- The Lamar Valley in Yellowstone's northeastern corner offers the best wolf visibility, especially at dawn and dusk in winter
- **Yellowstone Forever** ([yellowstonefforever.org](https://www.yellowstoneforum.org)) offers guided wolf-watching tours with proceeds supporting park science
- Share wolf sighting photos and stories — the economic and emotional case for living with wolves matters in Western political debates

### Oppose Killing Contests in Your State

Wildlife killing contests — competitive events for killing coyotes, foxes, and other predators — remain legal in most states:

- Contact your state wildlife agency and legislature to support banning killing contests
- **Project Coyote** ([projectcoyote.org](https://projectcoyote.org)) tracks state-by-state legislation and provides model bills
- If in Utah: Contact state wildlife officials to oppose coyote killing contests

### Volunteer with Wolf or Bear Monitoring Programs

Citizen science contributions to predator monitoring are valuable and relatively accessible:

- **Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife Wolf Program** ([dfw.state.or.us/wolves](https://www.dfw.state.or.us/wildlife/living_with/wolves/)) accepts public wolf sighting reports
- **Defenders of Wildlife Grizzly Bear Coexistence** ([defenders.org](https://www.defenders.org)) offers volunteer opportunities and ambassador programs
- **Yellowstone Wolf Project** tracks every wolf in the park — public wolf sighting reports contribute to their data
