# Sea Level Rise

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Global sea levels are rising at accelerating rates. NASA satellite data shows the rate of rise doubled from 2.1 mm/year in 1993 to 4.5 mm/year by 2024. NOAA projects 10-12 inches of rise along the US coastline by 2050 -- as much in 30 years as the entire previous century. Approximately 129 million Americans (40% of the population) live in coastal counties that account for less than 10% of the nation's land mass.

Coastal flooding is expected to occur more than 10 times as often as today within the next 30 years. Property insurance markets are already retreating from high-risk areas, military readiness is threatened, and communities are beginning to confront the reality of managed retreat.

## Facts

### Current Rate of Rise

NASA satellite measurements from the Sentinel-6 Michael Freilich mission show that the rate of global sea level rise has doubled over the past three decades:

| Period | Rate of Rise | Source |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| 1993 | ~2.1 mm/year | NASA satellite altimetry |
| 2024 | ~4.5 mm/year | NASA satellite altimetry |
| Acceleration | 0.077 mm/year per year | NASA Earthdata |

In 2024, global mean sea level rose by 0.59 cm (0.23 inches) -- an unexpectedly large amount. In 2025, a mild La Nina temporarily slowed the rate to 0.08 cm, but the long-term acceleration trend continues.

Since 1880, global mean sea level has risen approximately 8-9 inches (21-24 cm). The rate is accelerating, not linear.

[source: NASA - Rate of Sea Level Rise Doubled](https://www.earthdata.nasa.gov/learn/data-in-action/rate-global-sea-level-rise-doubled-during-past-three-decades) | [source: NASA - Sea Level Rise in 2024](https://www.nasa.gov/missions/jason-cs-sentinel-6/sentinel-6-michael-freilich/nasa-analysis-shows-unexpected-amount-of-sea-level-rise-in-2024/)

### [+] Ice Sheet Contributions

Mass loss from the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets has quadrupled since the 1990s and now represents the dominant source of sea level rise from the cryosphere:

- **Greenland:** Losing approximately 270 billion metric tons of ice per year
- **Antarctica:** Losing approximately 150 billion metric tons per year
- **Combined contribution:** Nearly 1.2 mm of sea level rise per year from ice sheets alone
- **West Antarctic Ice Sheet (WAIS):** Considered the most vulnerable, with marine ice cliff instability potentially accelerating loss dramatically

Even if warming stabilizes at the current +1.2 degrees C, research indicates several meters of committed sea level rise over coming centuries from ice sheet dynamics already set in motion.

[source: NOAA - Climate Change: Global Sea Level](https://www.climate.gov/news-features/understanding-climate/climate-change-global-sea-level) | [source: Nature - Warming of +1.5C Too High for Polar Ice Sheets](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43247-025-02299-w)

### [+] Projections

**NOAA 2022 Technical Report (US Coastline):**

| Timeframe | Projected Rise | Context |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| By 2050 | 10-12 inches (0.25-0.30 m) | Equal to entire previous century |
| By 2100 (low emissions) | ~2 feet (0.6 m) | If emissions are sharply reduced |
| By 2100 (high emissions) | ~7 feet (2.2 m) | If current trajectory continues |
| By 2150 (high emissions) | Up to 13 feet (3.9 m) | Long-term commitment |

**IPCC AR6 Global Projections:**

| Scenario | By 2050 | By 2100 |
|----------|---------|---------|
| SSP1-1.9 (best case) | 0.15-0.23 m (6-9 in) | 0.28-0.55 m (11-22 in) |
| SSP5-8.5 (worst case) | 0.20-0.29 m (8-11 in) | 0.63-1.01 m (25-40 in) |

Sea level rise lags behind temperature changes by decades. Even under aggressive emission cuts, rise will continue accelerating through at least 2050. Decisions made now determine post-2050 trajectories.

[source: NOAA 2022 Sea Level Rise Technical Report](https://earth.gov/sealevel/us/resources/2022-sea-level-rise-technical-report/) | [source: IPCC AR6 Sea Level Projection Tool](https://sealevel.nasa.gov/data_tools/17)

### [+] Most Threatened US Cities

Many US coastal cities face compounding threats: rising seas, land subsidence (sinking), and intensifying storms.

**Miami, FL:** Low-lying limestone geology means water rises through the ground, not just over seawalls. Already experiences regular "sunny day flooding" during high tides. $2-3.5 trillion in assets at risk by 2070.

**Norfolk/Virginia Beach, VA:** Seas rising at twice the global average due to land subsidence and ocean current changes. Land sinking at more than 0.13 inches per year. Home to the largest naval installation in the world.

**Charleston, SC:** Among the fastest-sinking cities on the East Coast, subsiding approximately 0.16 inches per year. Flooding events have tripled in the past two decades.

**Houston/Galveston, TX:** Parts of the region have subsided more than 6 feet over the past century due to groundwater extraction. High social vulnerability population in threatened areas.

**New York City:** Has the greatest number of people on threatened land of any US city. Storm surge funneled into narrow harbor. $19 billion in damage from Hurricane Sandy (2012) previewed future risks.

[source: Climate Central - US Cities Most Vulnerable](https://www.climatecentral.org/report/us-cities-most-vulnerable-major-coastal-flooding-sea-level-rise-21748) | [source: Nature - Disappearing Cities on US Coasts](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07038-3)

### [+] Military Base Threats

The Department of Defense identified sea level rise as a direct threat to military readiness:

- **Naval Station Norfolk, VA:** The world's largest naval base faces a "tipping point" at 1.5 feet of rise that would dramatically increase infrastructure damage risk. Nine major floods in the past decade have damaged ship repair equipment. The five dry docks are among the most vulnerable facilities.
- **Tyndall Air Force Base, FL:** Hurricane Michael (2018) destroyed much of the base, including F-22 stealth fighters -- a preview of combined sea level rise and storm surge threats.
- **128 military bases** valued at approximately $100 billion are threatened by 3 feet of sea level rise.

[source: Union of Concerned Scientists - Naval Station Norfolk](https://www.ucs.org/resources/front-lines-rising-seas-naval-station-norfolk-virginia) | [source: Navy Times - 128 Military Bases Threatened](https://www.navytimes.com/news/your-navy/2016/07/29/rising-oceans-threaten-to-submerge-128-military-bases-report/)

### [+] Insurance Market Retreat

Flood insurance is becoming unaffordable or unavailable in high-risk areas:

- The National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) is transitioning to risk-based pricing under **Risk Rating 2.0**
- National average annual premium: **$926** (2025), but coastal properties can exceed $2,800
- 9% of policyholders will see increases of **more than 300%** to reach full-risk rates
- Premiums can increase up to **18% per year** for primary residences, **25% per year** for other properties
- The NFIP is currently operating under an extension expiring **September 30, 2026**
- Private insurers are withdrawing from coastal markets in Florida, Louisiana, and other high-risk states
- Total flood insurance claims projected to increase 25-130% by 2100

[source: FEMA Risk Rating 2.0](https://www.fema.gov/flood-insurance/risk-rating) | [source: GAO - Flood Insurance Reform](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977)

### [+] Managed Retreat: Communities Already Moving

Some US communities have begun relocating away from rising waters:

- **Isle de Jean Charles, LA:** A Biloxi-Chitimacha-Choctaw tribal community that lost 98% of its land mass (from 35 square miles in 1955 to half a square mile today). Received $48.3 million from HUD to relocate 33 households inland.
- **Oakwood Beach, Staten Island, NY:** After Hurricane Sandy, residents organized a grassroots buyout campaign and returned the neighborhood to coastal wetlands.
- **Quinault Indian Nation, WA:** Received $25 million federal grant to move residents to higher ground.
- **Soldiers Grove, WI** and **Valmeyer, IL:** Early examples of entire communities relocating after repeated flooding.

[source: Issues in Science and Technology - True Stories of Managed Retreat](https://issues.org/true-stories-managed-retreat-rising-waters-pinter/) | [source: UC Press - Lost History of Managed Retreat](https://online.ucpress.edu/elementa/article/9/1/00036/118392/The-lost-history-of-managed-retreat-and-community)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Sea level rise is not a future problem -- it is measurable today and accelerating
- The 10-12 inches projected by 2050 is largely "locked in" regardless of emission cuts, due to thermal lag
- After 2050, the trajectory depends heavily on emission decisions made now
- Coastal flooding frequency will increase 10x within 30 years
- Property values in flood-prone areas are beginning to decline as insurance becomes unaffordable
- Infrastructure designed for historical flood levels is becoming inadequate
- The compounding effect of subsidence (sinking land) doubles the problem in many cities

### Who's Affected

- **129 million Americans** in coastal counties (40% of the population)
- **Homeowners in flood zones:** Facing rising insurance costs and declining property values
- **Military families and national security:** 128 bases threatened
- **Low-income and minority communities:** Disproportionately located in flood-prone areas with fewer resources to relocate
- **Renters:** Often lack flood insurance and have less control over resilience investments
- **Small businesses:** Coastal tourism, fishing, and maritime industries face existential threats
- **Taxpayers:** Federal disaster assistance costs are rising -- FEMA's budget is increasingly strained

## Quick Actions

### Check Your Flood Risk (5 minutes)

Your first step is understanding your personal exposure:

1. **FEMA Flood Map:** Look up your property at [msc.fema.gov/portal/search](https://msc.fema.gov/portal/search) -- enter your address to see your flood zone designation
2. **NOAA Sea Level Rise Viewer:** Visualize 1-10 feet of rise on an interactive map at [coast.noaa.gov/slr](https://coast.noaa.gov/slr/)
3. **Climate Central Surging Seas:** Explore your risk at [sealevel.climatecentral.org](https://sealevel.climatecentral.org/maps/risk-zone)
4. **NOAA Sea Level Calculator:** View historical and projected changes at your location at [coast.noaa.gov/sealevelcalculator](https://coast.noaa.gov/sealevelcalculator/)

### Review Your Flood Insurance (15 minutes)

- **Do you have flood insurance?** Standard homeowner's policies do NOT cover flood damage
- **Are you in a flood zone?** Even if not, 25% of flood claims come from outside high-risk zones
- **Compare NFIP vs. private:** Private flood insurance may offer better rates -- get quotes from both
- **Check your coverage limits:** NFIP caps at $250,000 for buildings and $100,000 for contents
- **Understand Risk Rating 2.0:** Your premiums may be increasing -- review your latest NFIP statement

### Attend Local Coastal Planning Meetings

Your local government makes critical decisions about flood risk:

- **Comprehensive plan updates:** Most cities update their plans every 5-10 years -- these shape zoning and building codes for decades
- **Floodplain management meetings:** Your city or county floodplain administrator holds public meetings on flood map updates
- **Stormwater management:** Local stormwater utility boards set policy on drainage infrastructure
- **Building code reviews:** Push for updated building codes that require elevation, flood-proofing, or setbacks
- **Community Rating System (CRS):** Ask if your community participates in FEMA's CRS program, which reduces flood insurance premiums through proactive floodplain management

Search "[your city] floodplain management" or "[your county] comprehensive plan update" to find upcoming meetings.

### Support Living Shoreline Projects

Living shorelines use natural materials -- plants, oyster reefs, sand, and rock -- instead of concrete seawalls. They are more resilient during storms, less expensive to maintain, and provide habitat for fish and wildlife.

- Ask your local government about living shoreline ordinances
- Contact your state coastal management agency about restoration grants
- Volunteer with local land trusts or conservation organizations doing shoreline restoration
- Learn more at [NOAA Living Shorelines](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/insight/understanding-living-shorelines)

## Sustained Actions

### Build Personal and Community Resilience

- **Prepare a flood emergency plan:** Know your evacuation routes and have supplies ready
- **Elevate critical utilities:** Move electrical panels, HVAC, and water heaters above projected flood levels
- **Install a sump pump and backflow prevention valves** if in a flood-prone area
- **Document your property:** Maintain a photo/video inventory for insurance purposes
- **Talk to your neighbors:** Community preparedness is more effective than individual action

### Engage in Managed Retreat Discussions

Managed retreat is politically difficult but increasingly necessary. You can help by:

- Learning about successful retreat examples (Isle de Jean Charles, Oakwood Beach, Soldiers Grove)
- Attending local planning meetings and asking about long-term flood risk projections
- Supporting buyout programs that help willing homeowners relocate from high-risk areas
- Advocating for zoning changes that prevent new development in flood zones
- Pushing for "build back better" policies after flood events (rebuilding in safer locations)
- Reviewing the [Georgetown Climate Center Managed Retreat Toolkit](https://georgetownclimate.org/adaptation/toolkits/managed-retreat-toolkit/)

- **Your local land trust** -- Search the [Land Trust Alliance](https://www.landtrustalliance.org/find-land-trust) to find conservation organizations near you

### Push for State and Local Action

- **Updated flood maps:** Push your state to adopt flood maps that include future sea level rise projections, not just historical data
- **Building codes:** Advocate for freeboard requirements (building above the base flood elevation)
- **Disclosure laws:** Support laws requiring sellers to disclose flood history and risk to buyers
- **Green infrastructure:** Support stormwater management that uses natural systems (rain gardens, permeable pavement, wetland restoration)
- **Coastal setback lines:** Push for development setbacks that account for future erosion and inundation
