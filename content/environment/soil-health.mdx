# Soil Health: The Foundation We're Losing

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The United States loses an estimated **1.7 billion tons of topsoil every year** to erosion — a rate nearly double what the USDA considers sustainable. One-third of the Corn Belt has lost its entire topsoil layer. The economic cost is at least **$12.75 billion per year** in lost productivity. Earth's soils hold **2,500 gigatons of carbon** — more than three times the amount in the atmosphere — making soil restoration one of the most powerful, shovel-ready tools we have for addressing climate change.

The Dust Bowl was not just a historical catastrophe; it was a preview of what happens when soil health fails at scale. The same conditions — drought, bare ground, fragile ecosystems — are re-emerging across the Great Plains and Southwest.

## Facts

### Key Facts

- The U.S. loses **1.7 billion tons of topsoil** per year, at an average rate of **4.63 tons per acre annually** [source: PNAS — Extent of Soil Loss Across the US Corn Belt](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7923383/)
- Soil erosion rates in the Midwest average **1.9 mm per year** — nearly double the USDA's sustainable threshold of 1 mm per year [source: PNAS — US Corn Belt Soil Loss](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1922375118)
- **One-third of the Corn Belt** has lost its entire A horizon — the organic-matter-rich topsoil layer that took thousands of years to form [source: PNAS — US Corn Belt Soil Loss](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1922375118)
- Earth's soils hold approximately **2,500 gigatons of carbon** — more than **3 times** the amount in the atmosphere and **4 times** the amount in all living plants and animals [source: Nature Scitable — Soil Carbon Storage](https://www.nature.com/scitable/knowledge/library/soil-carbon-storage-84223790/)
- Topsoil loss costs the U.S. at minimum **$12.75 billion per year** in agricultural productivity losses [source: FAS — Pathways to Net-Zero Soil Loss](https://fas.org/publication/pathways-to-net-zero-soil-loss-by-2050/)

### [+] What Topsoil Is and Why It Takes So Long to Form

Topsoil — the A horizon — is the living layer of soil. It typically extends **6–18 inches** below the surface and contains the vast majority of soil organic matter, microbial life, and nutrients that support plant growth. It takes an estimated **500–1,000 years for nature to build 1 inch of topsoil** from bedrock and organic material. When we lose it to erosion, compaction, or chemical degradation, it is effectively gone on any human timescale.

What makes healthy topsoil:
- **Organic matter:** Decomposed plant and animal material, humus — stores water, nutrients, and carbon
- **Microbial community:** 1 teaspoon of healthy topsoil contains **more microorganisms than there are people on Earth** — bacteria, fungi, protozoa, and nematodes that cycle nutrients and support plant health
- **Soil structure:** Aggregates of mineral particles, organic matter, and fungal networks create pore spaces for water infiltration and root growth
- **Earthworms and macro-invertebrates:** Mix organic matter downward, create drainage channels, and process nutrients

Industrial agriculture disrupts all of these through deep tillage, bare-soil periods between crops, heavy machinery compaction, and synthetic chemical inputs that reduce microbial diversity.

[source: USDA NRCS — Soil Health](https://www.nrcs.usda.gov/conservation-basics/natural-resource-concerns/soil/soil-health)

### [+] Soil Carbon: The Climate Connection

Soil is the planet's largest terrestrial carbon reservoir. When soil is disturbed — by tillage, erosion, compaction, or development — stored carbon is released as CO₂. When soil organic matter is built up through cover crops, no-till, and diverse rotations, carbon is pulled from the atmosphere and stored.

**The numbers:**
- Global soils hold **~2,500 gigatons of carbon**, compared to ~860 gigatons in the atmosphere and ~560 gigatons in all living plants and animals
- Scientists estimate that improving agricultural soil management could sequester **over 1 billion additional tons of carbon per year** globally
- This represents a significant fraction of the carbon reduction needed to meet climate targets
- Every 1% increase in soil organic matter in a single acre of 6-inch-deep soil stores approximately **27 lbs of additional carbon**

This is not theoretical. Farms practicing no-till and cover cropping have documented measurable increases in soil organic matter and carbon over 5–10 year periods.

[source: MIT Climate Portal — Soil-Based Carbon Sequestration](https://climate.mit.edu/explainers/soil-based-carbon-sequestration) | [source: Columbia Climate School — Can Soil Help Combat Climate Change](https://news.climate.columbia.edu/2018/02/21/can-soil-help-combat-climate-change/)

### [+] The Dust Bowl: A Historical Warning

The Dust Bowl of the 1930s — when severe drought combined with bare, pulverized soil from a decade of aggressive tillage — displaced an estimated **3.5 million people** from the Great Plains in the largest internal migration in U.S. history. Dust storms buried farms, fouled livestock water, and created respiratory illness ("dust pneumonia") across the region. The 1935 "Black Sunday" storm moved an estimated **300 million tons of topsoil** in a single day.

The federal response created the Soil Conservation Service (now USDA NRCS) and established the framework for federal soil conservation programs that still exist today. Crop yields in severely eroded areas dropped by **50–70%** and took decades to partially recover.

Today, the same regions face new pressures: aquifer depletion (the Ogallala Aquifer is being drawn down 10–50 times faster than it recharges), drought intensification from climate change, and consolidation of farms that reduces the economic incentive to invest in soil health.

[source: USDA — Historical Soil Conservation Programs](https://www.usda.gov/sites/default/files/documents/USDA_Durability_WhitePaper_01_14.pdf)

### [+] Industrial Agriculture Practices That Degrade Soil

**Conventional tillage:** Turning soil with a plow or disc breaks up soil aggregates, buries organic matter, exposes bare soil to wind and water erosion, and kills the fungal networks (mycorrhizae) that connect plants to nutrients. Deep tillage can compact soil into a "plow pan" that prevents root penetration and water infiltration.

**Monoculture:** Growing a single crop over hundreds of acres for years in a row depletes specific soil nutrients, reduces microbial diversity, and removes the year-round root systems and organic matter inputs that healthy soil requires. Monoculture also requires more pesticides and fertilizers, which further reduce soil microbial life.

**Bare soil periods:** After harvest and before planting, conventional fields are left with no living vegetation. Bare soil is vulnerable to erosion by wind and rain, loses moisture rapidly, heats up to temperatures that kill surface microbes, and accumulates no organic matter. A single inch of rain on bare sloped ground can carry away tons of topsoil.

**Synthetic nitrogen:** High-application synthetic fertilizer increases crop yields in the short term but depresses microbial activity, acidifies soil over time, and reduces the incentive for plants to invest energy in root networks and symbiotic fungi. Excess nitrogen runs off into waterways, creating dead zones in rivers and coastal areas.

**Heavy machinery:** Modern farm equipment can weigh 50–100 tons when loaded. Repeated passes compact soil to depths of 20+ inches, reducing pore space, water infiltration, and root penetration. Compaction is one of the hardest soil degradation problems to reverse.

[source: USDA NRCS — Soil Health Principles](https://www.nrcs.usda.gov/conservation-basics/natural-resource-concerns/soil/soil-health)

### [+] Soil Health Practices That Work

**No-till and reduced tillage:** Leaving soil undisturbed dramatically reduces erosion, preserves soil structure, maintains fungal networks, and builds organic matter over time. No-till fields infiltrate water faster, need less irrigation, and show measurably higher organic matter after 5–10 years. No-till adoption has grown significantly in the U.S. but still represents a minority of cropland.

**Cover crops:** Planting winter rye, crimson clover, radishes, or other cover crops after cash crop harvest keeps soil covered, adds organic matter when terminated, suppresses weeds, and feeds soil biology through the winter. Cover crops can reduce erosion by **90%** on slopes compared to bare fields.

**Diverse rotations:** Rotating between corn, soybeans, small grains, and perennial forages breaks pest cycles, diversifies root architecture and organic matter inputs, and reduces synthetic input requirements.

**Composting:** Adding compost supplies diverse microorganisms, humic acids, and slow-release nutrients that build soil organic matter over time. Municipal compost programs, farm-scale composting, and even backyard composting contribute to soil health at different scales.

**Regenerative grazing:** Managed rotational grazing mimics the movement of wild herds across grasslands, allowing rest periods for plant recovery, stimulating root growth and organic matter deposition, and building soil carbon. Well-managed grazing can increase soil organic matter on degraded rangeland.

[source: USDA NRCS — Cover Crops](https://www.farmers.gov/blog/ask-expert-score-multiple-field-goals-with-cover-crops-q-with-stephanie-mclain) | [source: USDA NRCS — Programs and Initiatives](https://www.nrcs.usda.gov/programs-initiatives)

### [+] USDA Programs and Farm Bill Provisions

**USDA NRCS (Natural Resources Conservation Service)** administers the primary federal soil conservation programs:

- **Environmental Quality Incentives Program (EQIP):** Pays farmers for specific conservation practices including cover crops, no-till, and nutrient management. The Inflation Reduction Act added an additional $8.45 billion in EQIP funding through 2026.
- **Conservation Stewardship Program (CSP):** Rewards farmers for ongoing conservation performance, including soil health management systems.
- **Conservation Reserve Program (CRP):** Pays farmers to convert highly erodible cropland to perennial grass or tree cover for 10–15 years, preventing erosion and building soil.

**Farm Bill:** Reauthorized approximately every 5 years, the Farm Bill's Title II Conservation programs fund soil health, water quality, and wildlife habitat on agricultural land. Conservation funding has historically been underfunded relative to demand — NRCS maintains a waiting list of farmers seeking EQIP assistance.

[source: USDA NRCS — Farm Bill](https://www.nrcs.usda.gov/farmbill) | [source: Resources for the Future — USDA Conservation Programs](https://www.rff.org/publications/issue-briefs/how-usda-conservation-programs-mitigate-agricultural-greenhouse-gas-emissions/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- At current erosion rates, the U.S. is mining its soil the way it mined its oil: drawing down a capital asset that cannot be easily replenished
- Soil health connects directly to food security, water quality, flood resilience, and climate — it is not a niche agricultural concern
- The solutions are well-documented and practiced by thousands of farmers already — the barrier is economic incentives, not knowledge
- Federal conservation programs are chronically undersubscribed because the demand from farmers who want to adopt soil health practices exceeds available funding
- Cuts to USDA conservation programs directly increase soil erosion rates and carbon emissions from farmland

### Who's Affected

- **Farmers:** Eroding fields lose productivity, require more inputs, and generate less profit over time; soil health practices improve long-term farm economics
- **Rural communities:** Agricultural productivity underpins rural economies; soil degradation reduces farm income and increases commodity price volatility
- **Downstream waterways:** Eroded soil carries nitrogen, phosphorus, and pesticides into rivers and lakes, degrading drinking water and recreation
- **Floodplain communities:** Degraded soil has lower water infiltration capacity, increasing surface runoff and downstream flood peaks
- **Everyone who eats:** Long-term food security depends on maintaining productive agricultural land; the U.S. has already lost a third of its most productive topsoil in the Corn Belt
- **Climate:** Every ton of topsoil lost releases stored carbon; every acre converted to soil-building practices sequesters it

### Start or Expand Composting at Home (Ongoing)

Composting food scraps and yard waste returns organic matter to soil, reduces landfill methane emissions, and builds soil life. **Approximately 30% of U.S. household waste is compostable.**

**Getting started:**
- **Outdoor bin:** Combine "browns" (dry leaves, cardboard, paper) with "greens" (food scraps, grass clippings) in a 3:1 ratio by volume; keep moist; turn occasionally
- **Vermicomposting (worm bin):** Works in apartments; a small bin under the kitchen sink can process 1–2 lbs of food scraps per week
- **Municipal pickup:** Many cities now offer curbside compost pickup or drop-off sites — check [compostnow.org](https://www.compostnow.org) or your city's website

Finished compost added to garden beds, lawn, or trees feeds soil microbes, improves water retention, and builds organic matter.

### Request a Free NRCS Consultation

**Any landowner** — from a backyard gardener to a 1,000-acre farm — can request a free soil health consultation from their local USDA Service Center. NRCS staff can:
- Assess soil health indicators on your land
- Recommend practices suited to your land type, climate, and operation
- Connect you with cost-share programs you may qualify for

Find your local USDA Service Center: [offices.sc.egov.usda.gov](https://offices.sc.egov.usda.gov)

### Conduct a DIY Soil Health Test

A simple soil assessment takes 15 minutes and tells you a lot about what's living in your soil:

**Jar test (soil texture):**
1. Fill a clear jar 1/3 with your soil
2. Add water to fill; shake vigorously; let settle for 48 hours
3. Observe layers: sand settles first (minutes), silt next (hours), clay last (days), organic matter floats

**Earthworm count (biological activity):**
1. Dig a hole 12 inches square, 12 inches deep
2. Count earthworms found in the excavated soil
3. Fewer than 10 = low biological activity; 10–20 = moderate; 20+ = healthy

**Slake test (aggregate stability):**
1. Place a dry soil clod on a piece of window screen over a container of water
2. Good aggregates hold their shape; degraded soil immediately disperses

### Advocate for Mandatory Soil Erosion Reporting

The U.S. requires no mandatory reporting of topsoil erosion losses at the farm level (unlike air and water pollutants). Advocate with your state agriculture department and congressional representatives for:
- Mandatory baseline soil health assessments tied to federal farm program eligibility
- Public reporting of USDA National Resources Inventory data on erosion trends
- Soil carbon accounting in farm-level greenhouse gas reporting
