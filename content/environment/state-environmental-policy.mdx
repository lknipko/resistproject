# State Environmental Policy: When States Lead — or Obstruct

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Under the Clean Air Act, EPA sets a national floor — states can go further, but not lower. With the federal government now gutting those floors, **12 states plus D.C.** have adopted California's zero-emission vehicle mandate, **30 states plus D.C.** have Renewable Portfolio Standards requiring clean electricity, and **11 states** participate in the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative (RGGI) cap-and-trade program. Meanwhile, the Trump administration filed suit against California in March 2026 to kill its Advanced Clean Cars II rules, and DOJ sued four states in April–May 2025 to block climate accountability laws. The battle over who controls environmental standards has moved from Washington D.C. into the courts, and into your state legislature.

State policy decisions made in the next two years will lock in emissions trajectories and pollution protections — or strip them away — for decades.

## Facts

### The Federal Floor: How the System Is Supposed to Work

The federal government sets minimum national environmental standards under laws like the Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, and Endangered Species Act. States are free to adopt stricter standards — they just cannot go lower than the federal floor.

- **Clean Air Act Section 209** bars states from setting their own vehicle emission standards, with one historic exception: California [source: Clean Air Act Text](https://www.epa.gov/clean-air-act-overview/clean-air-act-text)
- **California waiver system:** Because California had its own standards before the federal law was enacted, it retained the right to seek EPA waivers to set stricter limits — and other states can adopt California's standards instead of the federal ones [source: Congressional Research Service](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48168)
- When the federal floor is weakened or eliminated, states with their own standards become the last line of protection — but only if those standards survive federal legal attack

### [+] California's Vehicle Emissions Authority — and the 2026 Fight

California's Advanced Clean Cars II (ACC II) rules require automakers to sell increasing percentages of zero-emission vehicles (ZEVs), starting at 35% of new car sales in model year 2026 and reaching 100% by 2035.

- **EPA granted California the ACC II waiver** in December 2024, allowing the rules to take effect [source: Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/01/06/2024-31128/california-state-motor-vehicle-and-engine-pollution-control-standards-advanced-clean-cars-ii-waiver)
- **Congress passed Congressional Review Act (CRA) resolutions** in early 2025 purporting to rescind the waiver — California challenged this, arguing that EPA waiver decisions are not "rules" subject to the CRA [source: Seyfarth Shaw](https://www.seyfarth.com/news-insights/trump-rescinds-californias-emission-waivers-what-it-means-for-the-future-of-ev-mandates.html)
- **DOJ sued California in March 2026** to block the state from enforcing its ZEV requirements, asking a federal court to declare California's rules unlawful [source: Washington Times](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/mar/12/justice-department-sues-california-zero-emission-vehicle-targets/)
- **12 states plus D.C. adopted ACC II** — seven beginning with model year 2026, five more following with model year 2027 [source: NC Clean Energy Technology Center](https://nccleantech.ncsu.edu/2024/06/14/the-advanced-clean-cars-ii-controversy-where-are-states-adopting-or-blocking-californias-zero-emission-vehicle-rules/)
- California and 10 other states have counter-sued to defend the waiver [source: California Air Resources Board](https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/current-litigation)

### Renewable Portfolio Standards: 30 States Have Clean Energy Mandates

Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS) — also called Renewable Energy Standards — require utilities to generate a minimum percentage of electricity from renewable sources. These are entirely state-level policies, not federal requirements.

- **30 states plus D.C. have binding RPS policies**; 8 more have voluntary renewable energy goals [source: DSIRE Database](https://www.dsireusa.org/)
- States with 100% clean energy targets include California (2045), New York (2040), Colorado (2050), Hawaii (2045), Virginia (2050), and Washington state (2045)
- RPS policies have been one of the most effective drivers of renewable energy buildout in the U.S., helping bring solar and wind costs down by creating stable demand
- States without RPS policies leave clean energy investment to market forces alone — which historically favored the cheapest incumbent fuels

### [+] RGGI: The Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative

The Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative is a cap-and-trade program for power plant carbon emissions covering northeastern and mid-Atlantic states. It is the longest-running carbon pricing system in the U.S.

- **11 states currently participate:** Connecticut, Delaware, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, Vermont, and Virginia [source: RGGI Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_Greenhouse_Gas_Initiative)
- Since its 2009 launch, RGGI has generated **over $6 billion** in auction proceeds invested in clean energy, efficiency, and bill assistance
- Power sector carbon emissions in RGGI states have fallen by more than **50%** since the program began
- **Pennsylvania withdrew from RGGI in November 2025** — Gov. Shapiro agreed to rescind the state's RGGI regulation as part of a deal to resolve a 135-day budget impasse, despite the program generating hundreds of millions per year in state revenue [source: Spotlight PA](https://www.spotlightpa.org/news/2025/11/rggi-climate-program-pennsylvania-budget-deal-environment/)

### [+] The Federal Preemption Offensive: Trump vs. State Climate Laws

The Trump administration has moved aggressively to block state-level climate policies it views as interfering with fossil fuel development.

- **April 2025 Executive Order** — "Protecting American Energy From State Overreach" directed the attorney general to identify state laws "burdening" domestic energy development that may be preempted by federal law [source: Jones Day](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2025/12/the-trump-administrations-actions-against-state-climate-laws-and-lawsuits)
- **May 2025 DOJ lawsuits** — The federal government sued New York and Vermont over their Climate Superfund Acts (which require fossil fuel companies to pay billions toward climate damages), and preemptively sued Hawaii and Michigan to stop them from filing their own climate cases against oil companies [source: CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2025/05/02/climate/trump-climate-lawsuits-hawaii-michigan-new-york-vermont)
- **New York's Climate Change Superfund Act** mandates that fossil fuel companies collectively pay **$75 billion** into a state-managed fund for climate adaptation
- The federal government argues these state laws are preempted by the Clean Air Act and interfere with foreign affairs — legal arguments most environmental scholars view as weak

### State AGs Fighting Back: The Pro-Environment Coalition

When the federal government retreats, state attorneys general have increasingly stepped into the breach as enforcers of environmental law.

- A coalition of **25 attorneys general plus 10 cities and counties**, led by California AG Rob Bonta, filed suit in the D.C. Circuit challenging the EPA's February 2026 rescission of the Endangerment Finding — the legal basis for all federal climate regulation [source: California DOJ](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/president-trump-ignores-climate-science-law-will-hold-him-accountable-california)
- State AGs in California, Minnesota, Hawaii, Delaware, New Jersey, and Michigan are pursuing or have filed independent lawsuits against fossil fuel companies for climate damages [source: Center for Climate Integrity](https://climateintegrity.org/lawsuits)
- The Sabin Center for Climate Change Law tracks all state AG environmental actions at [climate.law.columbia.edu](https://climate.law.columbia.edu/content/state-attorneys-general-environmental-actions)

### [+] Anti-ESG Movement and State Investment Policy

A separate battle is playing out over how state pension funds and government investments are managed.

- The "anti-ESG" movement, led by Republican state legislatures and attorneys general, pushes to prohibit state pension funds from considering environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors in investment decisions
- **Utah, Texas, Florida, and 15+ other states** have passed legislation restricting ESG investing by state entities, preventing pension managers from prioritizing climate risk in their investment decisions
- Critics argue anti-ESG policies actually harm retirees by forcing fund managers to ignore financial risks from climate change, stranded fossil fuel assets, and regulatory exposure
- Proponents argue ESG investing is politically motivated and constitutes a breach of fiduciary duty to maximize returns
- BlackRock, the world's largest asset manager, has been the primary corporate target of these campaigns; multiple states have withdrawn hundreds of millions in pension assets from BlackRock in protest

### [+] Blue State vs. Red State: Diverging Enforcement Realities

Even with identical federal laws on paper, enforcement varies dramatically by state.

- States with their own environmental agencies and strong enforcement cultures (California, New York, Massachusetts) continue vigorous enforcement even as the federal EPA retreats
- States with weaker agencies or politically hostile governors have long relied on federal enforcement as a backstop — that backstop is now largely gone
- The EPA's own civil enforcement cases dropped to a **20-year low** in 2025, with 71% of new cases resulting in zero penalties — meaning state enforcement is increasingly the only check on polluters [source: Environmental Integrity Project](https://environmentalintegrity.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/EIP_Report_2025EnvironmentalEnforcement_2.5.26.pdf)
- States that have not adopted their own clean vehicle standards, strong water quality rules, or emissions limits are now largely without protection against pollution rollbacks

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The federal "floor" for environmental protection is eroding in real time. States without their own standards have fewer protections than at any time in recent decades.
- States that adopted California vehicle standards are now in a legal battle with the federal government — the outcome will determine whether they can keep those standards or are forced to revert to weaker federal rules.
- RGGI's success in reducing power plant emissions demonstrates that state-level carbon pricing works — Pennsylvania's exit is a policy setback, but the program continues for the remaining 11 states.
- The Trump preemption offensive creates legal uncertainty for states pursuing climate accountability — but courts have not yet ruled that federal law actually blocks these efforts.
- State legislatures and governors are making decisions right now that will determine emissions trajectories and pollution exposure for millions of people for decades.

### Who's Affected

- **Clean air and vehicle emissions:** Residents of the 12 ACC II states benefit from stronger standards — if those standards survive litigation
- **Pension holders in anti-ESG states:** Retirees in Utah, Texas, Florida, and other states may face higher long-term investment risk as fund managers are barred from considering climate financial risks
- **Residents in states without RPS policies:** Pay no direct cost of renewable mandates but forgo the air quality, jobs, and price stability benefits of renewable buildout
- **Communities near power plants in non-RGGI states:** No longer have cap-and-trade pressure on plant operators to reduce emissions
- **Taxpayers in climate-impact states:** States like Hawaii, with its 2023 Maui wildfire disaster, are absorbing climate costs that the Climate Superfund laws would partly shift to the fossil fuel industry

### Track Your State's Bills at OpenStates.org

Visit [openstates.org](https://openstates.org) to search bills in your state legislature by keyword (try "environment," "emissions," "clean energy," "ESG"). Sign up for email alerts on bills you care about. This is the best free tool for tracking state-level environmental legislation in real time.

### Contact Your Governor's Office

Governors control state agency leadership, enforcement priorities, and whether to join interstate climate coalitions. Contact your governor's office directly at your state's official government website and urge the governor to:

- Appoint strong environmental advocates to lead state agencies
- Join or maintain participation in the RGGI or Western Climate Initiative
- Defend state vehicle standards against federal preemption

### Find Your State Legislature's Environmental Committee

Most state legislatures have an environment, natural resources, or energy committee that holds public hearings. Find your state's committee at [ncsl.org](https://www.ncsl.org) (National Conference of State Legislatures) and request to testify on pending environmental bills. Public testimony from constituents carries significant weight in state legislatures.

### Sign Up for State Agency Comment Periods

State environmental agencies regularly accept public comments on proposed rules — just like the federal process. Find your state's environmental agency (e.g., "California Air Resources Board," "New York DEC," "Utah DEQ") and sign up for their mailing list to be notified of comment periods.

### Attend State Environmental Board Meetings

State environmental quality boards, air quality boards, and water boards hold regular public meetings that are open to the public. Most now offer virtual attendance. These are decision-making bodies where your presence and comments matter.

### Check Your State's Air Quality in Real Time (Citizen Science)

Monitoring local air quality creates concrete, shareable data about pollution that makes state environmental policy debates tangible:

- **AirNow** ([airnow.gov](https://www.airnow.gov)) — EPA's official real-time air quality map by ZIP code; shows whether your community has "Good," "Moderate," or "Unhealthy" air
- **PurpleAir** ([map.purpleair.com](https://map.purpleair.com)) — Crowd-sourced sensor network with higher geographic resolution than AirNow; run by community volunteers
- When your state is considering weakening clean air standards, screenshots of your local AirNow data during bad days are powerful testimony at public hearings
- Consider installing a PurpleAir sensor at your home or school — sensors cost ~$250 and data feeds into the public network

### Choose Energy Providers and Products That Align with State Standards

In deregulated electricity markets (Texas, much of the Northeast), you can often choose your electricity supplier:

- Search "[your state] choose renewable electricity" or "[your state] green power" to find options
- Look for suppliers that offer 100% renewable energy certificates verified through the DSIRE database
- In states with RPS policies, your utility is already meeting a minimum — but you can usually choose to go above that minimum
- Use [EnergySage](https://www.energysage.com) to get solar installation quotes and compare them to your current utility costs
