# Superfund Sites

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** The EPA's Superfund program manages cleanup of the nation's most contaminated toxic waste sites -- currently 1,340 active sites on the National Priorities List. Cleanup funding has plummeted from $2.6 billion in 1999 to roughly $537 million, and the FY2026 budget proposes further cuts. EPA enforcement is down sharply, cleanup timelines are stretching to decades, and 73 million Americans live within 3 miles of a Superfund site. Environmental justice communities -- disproportionately low-income and communities of color -- bear the heaviest burden.

Every American has the right to know what is contaminating their community and to participate in cleanup decisions.

## Facts

### What Is the Superfund Program?

The Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA), commonly known as Superfund, was enacted in 1980 to clean up sites contaminated with hazardous substances. The National Priorities List (NPL) is the list of the most serious sites identified for long-term cleanup. As of March 2025, the NPL includes **1,340 active sites** across all 50 states and U.S. territories.

The program operates on the "polluter pays" principle: responsible parties (companies that contaminated the site) are legally required to pay for cleanup. When no responsible party can be identified or is bankrupt, the federal government pays using the Superfund trust fund.

[source: EPA Superfund NPL](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/superfund-national-priorities-list-npl) | [source: EPA Superfund Overview](https://www.epa.gov/superfund)

### Funding Collapse

| Year | Superfund Appropriation |
|------|------------------------|
| FY1999 | ~$2.6 billion |
| FY2010 | ~$1.3 billion |
| FY2024 | ~$537 million |
| FY2026 (proposed) | Further cuts proposed |

The Superfund trust fund, originally financed by taxes on the petroleum and chemical industries, expired in 1995 and was not reinstated until the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022. The reinstated tax faces potential repeal under current budget proposals. With fewer dollars, cleanups slow or stall -- the average NPL site takes 12-15 years to complete remediation.

[source: GAO Superfund Report](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-108408) | [source: EPA FY2026 Budget in Brief](https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2025-05/fy-2026-epa-bib.pdf)

### [+] Health Impacts of Superfund Contamination

Superfund sites can contain a range of hazardous substances, including heavy metals (lead, arsenic, mercury), volatile organic compounds (trichloroethylene, benzene), pesticides, radioactive materials, and PFAS "forever chemicals." Documented health effects include:

- **Cancer:** Elevated rates of leukemia, bladder cancer, and liver cancer near Superfund sites
- **Birth defects:** Increased rates of low birth weight and congenital abnormalities
- **Neurological damage:** Lead and mercury exposure causes developmental delays in children
- **Respiratory illness:** Airborne contamination from volatile organic compounds
- **Immune system dysfunction:** PFAS exposure linked to weakened vaccine response

A 2023 study in the *American Economic Review* found that Superfund cleanups reduced infant mortality in surrounding areas by 12-20%, demonstrating the direct health benefits of the program.

[source: EPA Superfund Health Studies](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/health-studies-superfund-sites) | [source: ATSDR Public Health Assessments](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/hac/pha/index.asp)

### Environmental Justice and Superfund

Superfund sites are not randomly distributed. Research consistently shows:

- **56% of people living within 3 miles** of NPL sites are people of color
- Low-income communities are more likely to have uncontrolled hazardous waste sites
- Cleanup at sites in majority-minority communities takes **longer** and receives **less funding** than sites in predominantly white communities
- EPA's own analysis shows environmental justice communities face disproportionate exposure

The EPA's Environmental Justice Collaborative Problem-Solving program, which provided grants to community organizations near Superfund sites, has faced significant budget cuts.

[source: EPA Environmental Justice](https://www.epa.gov/environmentaljustice) | [source: CRS Superfund Report](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48575)

### [+] EPA Enforcement Decline

EPA compliance inspections at Superfund sites have been reduced. Staffing cuts of over 1,200 full-time equivalents in the FY2026 budget mean fewer inspectors, fewer enforcement actions, and slower response to new contamination discoveries. The 10-for-1 deregulation mandate (Executive Order 14192) makes it harder to add new sites to the NPL or strengthen cleanup standards.

When enforcement declines, responsible parties face less pressure to complete cleanups on time, and communities bear the consequences.

[source: EPA Administrator FY2026 Testimony](https://www.epa.gov/ocir/administrator-zeldins-52025-fiscal-year-2026-budget-testimony) | [source: Executive Order 14192](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/02/05/2025-02349/unleashing-prosperity-through-deregulation)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Cleanup timelines at existing sites will stretch longer as funding shrinks
- New contamination sites will be slower to investigate and list on the NPL
- Communities near Superfund sites face ongoing health risks from delayed cleanup
- Responsible parties face less enforcement pressure to fund remediation
- The burden falls hardest on environmental justice communities that already bear disproportionate exposure
- State programs may need to fill gaps, but state environmental budgets are also under pressure

### Who's Affected

- **73 million Americans** live within 3 miles of a Superfund site
- **Environmental justice communities:** Disproportionately located near contaminated sites, with slower cleanup timelines
- **Children:** Most vulnerable to lead, mercury, and other developmental toxins
- **Rural communities:** Often lack resources to monitor or advocate for cleanup
- **Property owners:** Home values near Superfund sites are depressed until cleanup is complete
- **Workers:** Cleanup workers face occupational exposure risks

### File a FOIA Request for Cleanup Status (30 minutes)

If you live near a Superfund site, you have the right to request detailed cleanup documents:

1. **Identify your site:** Use the [EPA NPL Site Search](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/search-superfund-sites-where-you-live)
2. **Submit a FOIA request:** [epa.gov/foia](https://www.epa.gov/foia) -- Request site investigation reports, cleanup plans, monitoring data, and enforcement actions
3. **What to request:** Five-Year Review reports, Remedial Investigation/Feasibility Study documents, Record of Decision, and recent monitoring data
4. **Tip:** Be specific about the site name, EPA ID number, and document types to get faster responses

### Request a Health Screening (if near a site)

If you live within 1-3 miles of a Superfund site:

1. **Contact ATSDR:** The Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry ([atsdr.cdc.gov](https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov)) conducts health assessments near Superfund sites
2. **Request a health consultation:** Community members can petition ATSDR to evaluate health risks at their site
3. **Talk to your doctor:** Mention proximity to the specific Superfund site and known contaminants
4. **Get tested:** Blood lead levels, liver function tests, and other screenings may be appropriate depending on site contaminants
5. **Document health patterns:** Keep records of health issues in your household and neighborhood -- this evidence matters for cleanup priority and legal action

### Join or Form a Community Advisory Group

EPA supports Community Advisory Groups (CAGs) at Superfund sites. These are formal groups of community members who participate directly in cleanup decisions:

1. **Check if one exists:** Contact your EPA regional office or search [epa.gov/superfund/superfund-community-advisory-groups](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/superfund-community-advisory-groups)
2. **Join an existing CAG:** Attend meetings (usually open to the public) and apply for membership
3. **Form a new one:** If your site does not have a CAG, community members can request that EPA help establish one
4. **Apply for a Technical Assistance Grant (TAG):** Communities can apply for EPA funding (~$50,000) to hire independent technical advisors to review cleanup plans

[source: EPA Community Advisory Groups](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/superfund-community-advisory-groups) | [source: EPA TAG Grants](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/technical-assistance-grant-tag-program)

## Sustained Actions

### Monitor and Document

- **Attend public meetings:** EPA holds public comment periods for cleanup decisions at every site -- your participation is part of the official record
- **Track Five-Year Reviews:** EPA must review cleanup progress every five years at sites where contamination remains -- request copies and attend public hearings
- **Monitor local water quality:** If near a Superfund site, consider testing well water or tap water annually
- **Report new contamination:** If you discover potential contamination (unusual discharges, odors, dead vegetation), report to EPA's [Environmental Violations hotline](https://echo.epa.gov/report-environmental-violations)

### Support Organizations

- **Center for Health, Environment & Justice** ([chej.org](https://chej.org)) -- Founded by Love Canal activist Lois Gibbs, supports communities near toxic waste sites
- **Earthjustice** ([earthjustice.org](https://earthjustice.org)) -- Free legal representation for communities fighting pollution
- **Coming Clean** ([comingcleaninc.org](https://www.comingcleaninc.org)) -- Collaborative focused on reforming chemical policy
- **Environmental Justice Health Alliance** ([ejhealthalliance.org](http://www.ejha.org)) -- Coalition of environmental justice communities near toxic sites
- **Your state environmental agency** -- Search "[your state] department of environmental quality superfund" for state-managed cleanup sites

### Push for Legal Action

- **Citizen suits:** The Clean Water Act and RCRA allow citizens to sue polluters directly when government enforcement fails
- **Contact legal aid:** Organizations like Earthjustice and local law school environmental clinics provide free legal help
- **Document health impacts:** Community health surveys and medical records strengthen legal cases
- **Support state attorneys general** who file environmental enforcement actions
