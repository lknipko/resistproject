# Utah Mining & Extraction

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Utah's mining legacy spans copper, gold, uranium, coal, and now lithium -- producing enormous economic value alongside severe environmental contamination. Kennecott's Bingham Canyon Mine (the largest open-pit mine in the Western Hemisphere) released 261 million pounds of toxic chemicals in 2023 alone, and its tailings pond may be leaking selenium into Great Salt Lake, threatening millions of migratory birds. The Moab uranium mill tailings remediation is 98.4% complete after decades of work. New lithium extraction proposals are advancing in western Utah. Mine reclamation across the state remains underfunded, leaving contaminated sites for taxpayers to clean up.

What happens in Utah's mines affects Utah's water, air, wildlife, and communities.

## Facts

### Kennecott / Bingham Canyon Mine

The Bingham Canyon Mine, operated by Rio Tinto's Kennecott Utah Copper, is the **largest open-pit mine in the Western Hemisphere** -- over 2.5 miles wide and 0.75 miles deep, visible from space. Located just 20 miles southwest of Salt Lake City, it has operated since 1906.

**Toxic releases:** In 2023, Kennecott released approximately **261 million pounds of toxic chemicals**, including arsenic, lead, mercury, and selenium -- making it Utah's top polluter by a wide margin. Utah jumped two spots nationally in total toxic chemical releases in recent reporting.

**Superfund status:** The Kennecott North Zone is a designated Superfund site (EPA ID: UTD980951057) due to historical contamination of soil and groundwater with heavy metals. Remediation is ongoing.

[source: EPA TRI - Kennecott](https://www.epa.gov/toxics-release-inventory-tri-program) | [source: Salt Lake Tribune - Utah's Top Polluter](https://www.sltrib.com/news/environment/2024/07/05/utah-has-fourth-most-toxic/) | [source: EPA Superfund - Kennecott](https://cumulis.epa.gov/supercpad/SiteProfiles/index.cfm?fuseaction=second.cleanup&id=0800636)

### Tailings Contamination and Great Salt Lake

A critical and developing story: Kennecott's tailings pond has sunk 20 feet or more, raising concerns that contamination is seeping into the neighboring Great Salt Lake.

- More than a century of mining has produced **two groundwater plumes with selenium pollution** that have impacted Great Salt Lake wetlands
- Selenium is toxic to migratory birds -- it weakens eggs and deforms embryos. **Millions of migratory birds** depend on Great Salt Lake
- Regulators detected **elevated selenium levels in migratory bird eggs** collected near the discharge point
- The Utah Division of Water Quality **allowed Kennecott to edit selenium information** out of a recent groundwater permit
- The division also allowed Kennecott to **cancel a study** that would have investigated the tailings pond's connectivity to Great Salt Lake

[source: Salt Lake Tribune - Selenium and Great Salt Lake](https://www.sltrib.com/news/environment/2025/07/14/rising-selenium-great-salt-lake/) | [source: Utah Physicians for Healthy Environment](https://www.uphe.org/2025/07/15/tailings-ponds-pollution-and-great-salt-lake/)

### Moab Uranium Mill Tailings

The Moab Uranium Mill Tailings Remedial Action (UMTRA) Project is one of the largest environmental cleanup projects in U.S. history:

- **16 million tons** of radioactive uranium mill tailings sat on the banks of the Colorado River near Moab for decades
- The U.S. Department of Energy has been relocating the tailings 32 miles north to an engineered disposal cell near Crescent Junction
- As of March 2025, **98.4% of the tailings have been moved**
- Remaining work includes groundwater remediation, sub-pile soil removal, and site verification
- **Site closure is expected in 2029**
- The project also includes active remediation of contaminated groundwater at the mill site

The Moab project demonstrates that cleanup is possible but requires sustained commitment and funding over decades.

[source: DOE Moab UMTRA Project](https://www.energy.gov/em/moab-uranium-mill-tailings-remedial-action-umtra-project) | [source: DOE Moab Major Milestone](https://www.energy.gov/em/articles/moab-project-marks-major-cleanup-milestone)

### [+] Coal Mining in Carbon and Emery Counties

Utah's coal mining industry is concentrated in Carbon and Emery counties in central Utah:

- Utah produced approximately 12 million tons of coal annually in recent years, primarily from underground mines
- Coal mining employment has declined significantly, from thousands of workers to roughly 1,000-1,500
- Abandoned mines create ongoing acid mine drainage and groundwater contamination
- Many mine reclamation bonds are insufficient to cover actual cleanup costs
- The community faces a "just transition" challenge as the coal economy declines
- Federal Abandoned Mine Land (AML) program provides some reclamation funding, but the backlog is large

[source: Utah DOGM Coal Mining](https://naturalresources.utah.gov/oil-gas-mining/coal)

### [+] Lithium Extraction Proposals

Utah is seeing growing interest in lithium extraction, driven by demand for electric vehicle batteries:

- Companies have proposed lithium extraction from brines in western Utah
- Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) technology is less water-intensive than traditional evaporation ponds but remains largely unproven at scale
- Environmental concerns include water consumption in an already arid region, brine disposal, and potential groundwater contamination
- Economic proponents argue lithium extraction supports the energy transition and creates jobs
- Environmental review and permitting processes are underway for several proposals

The tension between supporting clean energy (which requires lithium) and protecting Utah's scarce water and landscapes is real and complex.

[source: Utah DOGM Minerals Program](https://naturalresources.utah.gov/oil-gas-mining/minerals)

### Mine Reclamation Failures

When mining companies finish extraction, they are legally required to reclaim the land -- restore it to a usable condition. In practice:

- **Reclamation bonds** (financial guarantees) are often set too low to cover actual cleanup costs
- When companies go bankrupt, taxpayers are left with the bill
- Utah has hundreds of abandoned mines with no responsible party
- The federal Abandoned Mine Land (AML) reclamation program is chronically underfunded
- Mine waste piles, open pits, and contaminated water persist for decades or centuries without active remediation

[source: Utah DEQ - Mining](https://deq.utah.gov/businesses-facilities/kennecott-utah-copper-llc) | [source: Grand County UMTRA](https://www.grandcountyutah.net/257/Moab-UMTRA-Project)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Kennecott's operations continue to release hundreds of millions of pounds of toxic chemicals annually with limited public accountability
- The selenium contamination of Great Salt Lake wetlands threatens one of the most important migratory bird habitats in the Western Hemisphere
- The regulatory relationship between Kennecott and Utah DEQ raises questions about whether the state can effectively regulate its largest industrial operation
- New lithium extraction must be evaluated carefully -- supporting the energy transition should not mean repeating the environmental mistakes of copper and uranium mining
- Mine reclamation reform is needed to prevent future taxpayer-funded cleanups

### Who's Affected

- **Salt Lake Valley residents:** Live downwind and downstream from the largest toxic chemical emitter in the state
- **Great Salt Lake ecosystem:** Millions of migratory birds, brine shrimp, and the communities that depend on the lake
- **Moab community:** Decades of living adjacent to radioactive tailings on the Colorado River
- **Coal communities:** Carbon and Emery counties face economic transition and abandoned mine legacy
- **Utah taxpayers:** Underfunded reclamation bonds mean the public pays for cleanup when companies walk away
- **Downstream communities:** Colorado River water users across seven states

### Attend Utah DOGM Public Hearings (varies)

The Utah Division of Oil, Gas, and Mining (DOGM) holds public hearings on mining permits, reclamation plans, and bond amounts:

1. **Check the calendar:** [naturalresources.utah.gov/oil-gas-mining](https://naturalresources.utah.gov/oil-gas-mining) for upcoming hearings
2. **Submit written comments:** Even if you cannot attend, written comments become part of the public record
3. **Focus on specific issues:** Water quality impacts, bond adequacy, reclamation timelines, and community health concerns carry the most weight
4. **Bring data:** Reference DEQ monitoring reports, EPA TRI data, and published studies

### Check Water Quality Near Mining Areas (15 minutes)

If you live near mining operations in Utah:

1. **Utah DEQ Water Quality** ([deq.utah.gov](https://deq.utah.gov)) -- Check monitoring data for your area
2. **EPA ECHO** ([echo.epa.gov](https://echo.epa.gov)) -- Look up compliance and enforcement for specific facilities
3. **Test your water:** If on a private well near mining, get annual testing through the Utah Division of Drinking Water or a certified lab
4. **Report contamination:** Contact Utah DEQ's Environmental Response hotline at (801) 536-4123

### File a FOIA / GRAMA Request (30 minutes)

Utah's Government Records Access and Management Act (GRAMA) gives you the right to request state records:

1. **For state records:** File a GRAMA request with Utah DEQ for Kennecott monitoring data, permit modifications, or enforcement records at [deq.utah.gov/general/records-requests](https://deq.utah.gov/general/records-requests)
2. **For federal records:** File a FOIA request with EPA Region 8 for Superfund documents at [epa.gov/foia](https://www.epa.gov/foia)
3. **What to request:** Groundwater monitoring reports, permit amendments, compliance inspections, enforcement actions, and bond adequacy reviews
4. **Tip:** Reference the specific facility name and EPA ID number for faster processing

### Visit Kennecott's Visitors Center

Understanding the scale of operations helps you advocate effectively:

- **Kennecott Visitors Center** -- Open seasonally, provides views of the mine and information about operations
- Seeing the 2.5-mile-wide pit in person makes the scope of environmental impact tangible
- Use the visit to educate family and friends about mining's environmental costs

## Sustained Actions

### Support Organizations

- **Utah Physicians for Healthy Environment (UPHE)** ([uphe.org](https://www.uphe.org)) -- Utah-focused environmental health advocacy, active on Kennecott/Great Salt Lake issues
- **Southern Utah Wilderness Alliance (SUWA)** ([suwa.org](https://suwa.org)) -- Protecting Utah wildlands from mining and extraction
- **Earthjustice** ([earthjustice.org](https://earthjustice.org)) -- Legal challenges to mining permits and enforcement failures
- **Grand Canyon Trust** ([grandcanyontrust.org](https://www.grandcanyontrust.org)) -- Colorado Plateau conservation including mining issues
- **Heal Utah** ([healutah.org](https://healutah.org)) -- Nuclear waste, uranium, and environmental health in Utah

### Push for Mine Bond Reform

- **Contact your state legislators:** Utah's mine bond amounts should reflect actual reclamation costs, not outdated estimates
- **Advocate for third-party reclamation cost estimates** instead of company self-assessments
- **Support "polluter pays" bonding:** Full-cost bonds ensure companies -- not taxpayers -- pay for cleanup
- **Track bond adequacy reviews** at DOGM and submit public comments when bonds are set too low

### Monitor and Document

- Join local watershed monitoring programs near mining areas
- Document visible pollution (tailings dust, water discoloration, unusual odors) with photos, GPS, and timestamps
- Report environmental violations to both Utah DEQ and EPA
- Attend DOGM board meetings to stay informed about permitting decisions
