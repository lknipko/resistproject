# Utah Water Rights & Policy

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Utah faces a water reckoning. The prior appropriation doctrine ("first in time, first in right") allocates roughly 80% of the state's water to agriculture, while population growth strains municipal supplies and Great Salt Lake shrinks to crisis levels. The state has responded with historic investments -- over $300 million in appropriations, a Great Salt Lake Water Trust (HB 410), and a mandatory secondary water metering program by 2030. But fundamental tensions remain: the Bear River development debate, agricultural vs. municipal allocation, and whether Utah can conserve enough water to sustain both growth and ecosystems.

Utah's water choices in the next decade will determine the state's livability for generations.

## Facts

### Prior Appropriation: "First in Time, First in Right"

Utah follows the prior appropriation doctrine -- the legal framework governing water rights across the arid West. Key principles:

- **First in time, first in right:** The earliest water right holder has priority over later users. In a shortage, junior rights are cut before senior rights.
- **Beneficial use:** Water rights must be put to "beneficial use" or they can be forfeited ("use it or lose it")
- **No ownership of water:** Under Utah law, all water belongs to the public. Water rights are rights to *use* water, not own it.
- **Agricultural priority:** Most senior water rights in Utah were established by agricultural users in the 19th and early 20th centuries

In practice, this means approximately **80% of Utah's developed water** goes to agriculture, even as the state's population has grown to over 3.4 million and is projected to reach 5.4 million by 2060.

[source: Utah Division of Water Rights](https://waterrights.utah.gov/) | [source: National Agricultural Law Center - Utah Prior Appropriation](https://nationalaglawcenter.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/NALC-Presentation-2.12.25.pdf)

### Great Salt Lake Crisis and State Response

Great Salt Lake has lost roughly two-thirds of its water since the 1980s, primarily due to upstream diversions for agriculture and municipal use. At its lowest point in 2022, the lake dropped below 4,188 feet elevation -- a historic low that threatened:

- **Toxic dust:** The exposed lakebed contains arsenic, mercury, and other heavy metals that become airborne dust affecting millions along the Wasatch Front
- **Brine shrimp industry:** A ~$57 million annual harvest that supplies most of the world's brine shrimp eggs
- **Migratory birds:** 10 million birds from 338 species depend on the lake -- the most important migratory bird habitat in the Western Hemisphere
- **Snowpack effect:** The lake contributes to lake-effect snowfall that supports Utah's $1.6 billion ski industry

### HB 33 and Water Law Reform

HB 33 (Instream Water Flow Amendments, passed 2022) was a landmark change to Utah water law:

- **New beneficial use:** Recognized instream flow on sovereign lands (including Great Salt Lake) as a "beneficial use" of water for the first time
- **Third option for water owners:** Previously, unused water rights faced "use it or lose it" forfeiture. HB 33 allows owners to **lease unused water** to benefit instream flows instead of wasting it or losing it
- **Water banking:** Enabled a mechanism for water rights holders to temporarily transfer water to environmental flows

This law was essential for Great Salt Lake because it allowed agricultural water rights to be redirected to the lake without permanent forfeiture.

[source: Utah Legislature - HB 33](https://le.utah.gov/~2023/bills/static/HB0033.html) | [source: Great Salt Lake Collaborative](https://greatsaltlakenews.org/latest-news/salt-lake-tribune/very-positive-change-new-utah-law-will-be-an-important-step-toward-saving-the-great-salt-lake)

### Secondary Water Metering Mandate

Utah requires all secondary pressurized water connections (outdoor irrigation water delivered through a separate, unmetered system) to be metered by **January 1, 2030**. This is critical because:

- **60% of Utah's residential water use** goes to outdoor irrigation
- Most secondary water connections are **unmetered** -- users have no idea how much they consume
- Metering alone reduces water use by an estimated **20-30%** without mandatory restrictions
- The state has allocated **$250 million in ARPA grants** for meter purchase and installation, with funds expiring at the end of 2026
- The 2026 budget includes an additional **$15 million** for the metering program
- Only water suppliers are required to install meters, not individual homeowners

[source: Utah Division of Water Resources - Secondary Metering](https://water.utah.gov/secondary-metering/) | [source: KSL - $190M Meter Grants](https://www.ksl.com/article/50452579/utah-board-approves-historic-190m-in-secondary-water-meter-grants-heres-why-that-matters)

### [+] Bear River Development Debate

The Bear River is Utah's last major undeveloped water source. Proposals to build dams and pipelines to divert Bear River water to the Wasatch Front have been debated for decades:

**Arguments for development:**
- Growing population needs additional water supply
- Bear River water is already legally allocated to Utah under interstate compact
- Infrastructure investment supports long-term water security

**Arguments against:**
- Bear River is the single largest source of freshwater inflow to Great Salt Lake
- Diverting Bear River water would accelerate the lake's decline
- Conservation, efficiency, and water recycling can meet future demand at lower cost
- Dam construction would cost billions of dollars
- Water conservation advocates argue Utah should reduce per-capita consumption (currently among the highest in the nation) before developing new supply

The Bear River debate encapsulates Utah's fundamental water question: should the state develop more supply or reduce demand?

[source: Utah Water Strategy Advisory Team](https://water.utah.gov/utah-water-strategy/) | [source: Great Salt Lake Collaborative](https://greatsaltlakenews.org/)

### [+] Conservation Incentives and Challenges

Utah is one of the highest per-capita water consumers in the nation, using roughly **170-220 gallons per person per day** (including outdoor use) compared to a national average of about 80-100 gallons. Contributing factors:

- **Cheap water:** Utah water rates are among the lowest in the West, providing little economic incentive to conserve
- **Large lawns:** Utah's cultural preference for green lawns in a desert climate drives massive outdoor consumption
- **Flat-rate pricing:** Many secondary water systems charge a flat fee regardless of usage
- **Limited recycling:** Utah recycles less treated wastewater than most Western states

Programs addressing conservation:
- **Flip Your Strip** programs pay homeowners to replace park strips with water-wise landscaping (~$1.25/sq ft rebate in some areas)
- **Localscapes** ([localscapes.com](https://localscapes.com)) -- Utah-specific water-wise landscaping guide
- **WaterSMART grants** from the Bureau of Reclamation fund efficiency projects
- Tiered water pricing (paying more for higher use) is gradually being adopted

[source: Utah Division of Water Resources](https://water.utah.gov/) | [source: Utah Water Metering](https://utahwatermetering.com/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Utah's water law was designed for 19th-century agricultural needs in a state with fewer than 300,000 people -- it must continue adapting for a state of 3.4 million and growing
- The Great Salt Lake investments are historic and promising, but whether they are sufficient depends on long-term agricultural water transfers and conservation
- Secondary water metering will be transformative -- people who see how much they use typically reduce consumption significantly
- The Bear River development decision will be the most consequential water choice Utah makes in the coming decades
- Climate change is reducing snowpack and water supply even as demand grows

### Who's Affected

- **All Utahns:** Water availability affects every resident, business, and community
- **Wasatch Front residents:** 80% of Utah's population depends on watersheds that are over-allocated
- **Farmers and ranchers:** Agricultural water transfers could reduce irrigated acreage, but market-based leasing provides compensation
- **Great Salt Lake communities:** Toxic dust from the exposed lakebed affects respiratory health across the Wasatch Front
- **Recreation industry:** Skiing, boating, fishing, and outdoor recreation all depend on water
- **Wildlife:** Great Salt Lake, Bear River Migratory Bird Refuge, and wetland habitats are all water-dependent

### Meter Your Secondary Water (check your status)

If you receive secondary (outdoor irrigation) water:

1. **Check if your system has installed meters** -- contact your water supplier or visit [utahwatermetering.com](https://utahwatermetering.com)
2. **Support your water district's metering plan** -- attend board meetings if you have concerns
3. **Voluntarily track your outdoor water use** even before meters are installed
4. **Once metered:** Monitor your usage and set conservation goals

### Reduce Your Lawn Watering (ongoing)

Outdoor irrigation is 60% of Utah's residential water use. Immediate actions:

1. **Flip Your Strip:** Replace park strips with water-wise landscaping. Many districts offer rebates (~$1.25/sq ft). Check [conservewater.utah.gov](https://conservewater.utah.gov) for programs in your area
2. **Water at the right time:** Before 10 AM or after 6 PM only -- midday watering loses up to 50% to evaporation
3. **Follow the Weekly Lawn Watering Guide:** [conservewater.utah.gov/weekly-lawn-watering-guide](https://conservewater.utah.gov/weekly-lawn-watering-guide/) -- science-based schedules by location
4. **Adjust sprinklers:** Fix overspray onto sidewalks and streets. A single misaligned sprinkler head wastes thousands of gallons per season
5. **Consider Localscapes:** [localscapes.com](https://localscapes.com) -- Utah-designed approach to water-wise yards that still look beautiful

### Attend Utah Water Policy Meetings (varies)

Your voice in state water policy matters:

1. **Utah Water Strategy Advisory Team:** Meets regularly to develop long-term water strategy. Agendas and meeting info at [water.utah.gov/utah-water-strategy](https://water.utah.gov/utah-water-strategy/)
2. **Legislative Interim Committee on Water:** Meets between legislative sessions to develop water bills. Check [le.utah.gov](https://le.utah.gov) for schedules
3. **Your local water conservancy district:** Board meetings are open to the public and decisions directly affect your water rates and conservation programs
4. **Great Salt Lake Advisory Council:** Advises the legislature on lake policy

### Conserve at Home

- **Fix leaks:** A single dripping faucet wastes 3,000+ gallons per year
- **Install low-flow fixtures:** Showerheads, faucet aerators, and dual-flush toilets
- **Upgrade irrigation:** Drip irrigation uses 30-50% less water than sprinklers for gardens and flower beds
- **Collect rainwater:** Legal in Utah (up to 2,500 gallons with registration)
- **Xeriscaping:** Replace thirsty lawns with native and adapted plants -- [waterwise plants for Utah](https://extension.usu.edu/cwel/)
