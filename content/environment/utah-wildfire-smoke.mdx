# Utah Wildfire Smoke & Health

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Utah's valleys are uniquely vulnerable to wildfire smoke. The same mountain topography that traps winter inversion pollution also traps wildfire smoke transported from fires across the West. In August 2021, PM2.5 in Salt Lake County reached 75.8 micrograms per cubic meter -- more than double the federal 24-hour standard of 35. The American Lung Association ranks Salt Lake City-Provo-Orem as the **9th worst metro in the U.S. for ozone** and **19th worst for short-term particle pollution**. An MIT study estimates over **450 premature deaths annually** in Utah from poor air quality.

Nationally, wildfire smoke PM2.5 contributes to an estimated **11,400-24,100 deaths per year** across the contiguous United States. Wildfire smoke PM2.5 is approximately **5 times more toxic** than general PM2.5 due to higher concentrations of carbonaceous and polar organic compounds. As fire seasons grow longer and more severe, Utah residents need to understand the risks and take protective action.

## Facts

### Utah's Double Air Quality Problem

Utah faces two distinct but compounding air quality crises:

**Winter inversions (November-February):**
- Cold, dense air gets trapped in valleys by surrounding mountains, creating a "bowl" effect
- Vehicle emissions, industrial sources, and wood burning accumulate under the inversion layer
- PM2.5 concentrations build the longer the inversion lasts, sometimes persisting for weeks
- The Wasatch Front's geography makes it one of the worst inversion-prone areas in the nation

**Summer/fall wildfire smoke (June-October):**
- Smoke from fires in California, Oregon, Idaho, Nevada, and within Utah drifts into the valleys
- The same mountain topography that traps inversion smog also traps wildfire smoke
- An atmospheric feedback loop -- "fire inversions" -- can lock smoke in valleys for days, mimicking winter inversion conditions
- Utah's valleys are "popular resting spots for smoke from distant fires"

[source: Utah DEQ - Inversions](https://deq.utah.gov/air-quality/inversions) | [source: Utah DEQ - Wildfire Impact](https://airquality.utah.gov/communication/news/wildfires-impact-on-our-environment) | [source: ScienceDaily - Fire Inversions](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2019/09/190912134354.htm)

### [+] Health Impacts of Wildfire Smoke

Wildfire smoke PM2.5 is not just regular particulate matter. Research shows it is approximately **5 times more toxic** than general ambient PM2.5 due to higher concentrations of carbonaceous and polar organic compounds.

**Acute effects (hours to days of exposure):**
- Respiratory irritation: coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath
- Eye irritation, runny nose, sore throat
- Worsening of asthma, COPD, and other respiratory conditions
- Increased emergency room visits for heart attacks and strokes
- Reduced lung function even in healthy adults

**Chronic effects (repeated seasonal exposure):**
- **72.5%** of deaths associated with long-term PM2.5 exposure are from cardiovascular diseases, mental disorders, endocrine diseases, and digestive diseases
- People highly exposed to wildfire smoke have a **7% higher mortality rate** over three years compared to minimally exposed populations
- Children, elderly, pregnant women, and those with pre-existing conditions face the greatest risk
- Long-term cognitive effects from repeated PM2.5 exposure are an active area of research

[source: PNAS - Wildfire Smoke Mortality](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2403960121) | [source: Science Advances - Wildfire Smoke Toxicity](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adw5890) | [source: UC Berkeley - Repeated Exposure](https://publichealth.berkeley.edu/articles/spotlight/research/wildfire-smoke-raises-risk-of-death-after-the-air-clears)

### [+] National Wildfire Smoke Mortality Data

  Major studies quantify the scale of the wildfire smoke health crisis:

  - Wildfire smoke PM2.5 causes an estimated **11,400-24,100 deaths per year** in the contiguous United States
  - An MIT study estimates **over 450 deaths annually in Utah** are attributable to poor air quality (all sources, not just wildfire)
  - Wildfire smoke PM2.5 was approximately **5 times more toxic** than general PM2.5 in causing mortality
  - The western United States bears the heaviest burden, with annual mean smoke PM2.5 levels increasing significantly since 2006
  - Wildfire season length has increased by **84 days** since the 1970s in the western U.S.

  [source: Science Advances - Wildfire Smoke Mortality](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adw5890) | [source: PNAS - Long-Term Smoke Exposure](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2403960121)

### [+] Utah's Wildfire Prevention Efforts

In the 2025 legislative session, Utah consolidated wildfire funding through **H.B. 307 "Wildfire Funding Amendments"** -- combining three separate funds into the new **Utah Wildfire Fund** focused on pre-fire, active, and post-fire activities.

**Prescribed burns:**
- The BLM treats roughly **70,000 acres annually** in Utah through prescribed fire
- Purpose: reduce fuel loads, restore natural fire regimes, improve wildlife habitat, and reduce risk of catastrophic wildfire
- For every acre treated with prescribed fire, there is less fuel available for uncontrollable wildfire
- Utah DEQ's Smoke Management Program regulates prescribed burns to minimize air quality impacts

[source: Pew Charitable Trusts - Utah Wildfire Action](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2026/03/25/utah-takes-action-on-wildfire-risk-funding-in-2025) | [source: USU Extension - Prescribed Fire](https://extension.usu.edu/forestry/publications/utah-forest-facts/025-prescribed-fire-on-public-lands)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Utah residents face a growing "pollution sandwich" -- bad air in winter from inversions, bad air in summer/fall from wildfire smoke, with only spring offering reliably clean air
- Climate change is making both problems worse: warmer winters shorten inversions slightly, but hotter, drier summers produce more and larger wildfires
- The health costs are real and measurable -- 450+ premature deaths per year in Utah, thousands of ER visits, and long-term chronic disease
- Indoor air protection is no longer optional during smoke events -- it is a health necessity, especially for vulnerable populations
- Federal forest management funding directly affects Utah's air quality, because fires in California, Oregon, and Idaho send smoke into Utah valleys

### Who's Affected

- **Everyone on the Wasatch Front:** Geography makes the entire valley population vulnerable to smoke trapping
- **Children:** Developing lungs are more susceptible to PM2.5 damage. Schools may restrict outdoor activities during smoke events
- **Elderly and chronically ill:** Heart disease, COPD, asthma, and diabetes patients face acute risks during smoke events
- **Pregnant women:** PM2.5 exposure is linked to preterm birth and low birth weight
- **Outdoor workers:** Construction, landscaping, agriculture, and recreation workers have prolonged exposure
- **Low-income households:** Less likely to have HEPA filters, air conditioning (to keep windows closed), or the ability to relocate during events

## Quick Actions

### Monitor Air Quality Daily (2 minutes)

During fire season (June-October), check air quality every morning before going outside:

1. **AirNow** ([airnow.gov](https://www.airnow.gov)) -- Official EPA real-time AQI data. Set up email or app alerts for your zip code
2. **PurpleAir** ([map.purpleair.com](https://map.purpleair.com/)) -- Community sensor network with hyperlocal readings (often more responsive than official monitors)
3. **Utah DEQ** ([air.utah.gov](https://air.utah.gov/)) -- Utah-specific air quality conditions by county
4. **AQI Action Levels:**
   - 0-50 (Green): Good -- no restrictions
   - 51-100 (Yellow): Moderate -- unusually sensitive individuals should reduce prolonged outdoor exertion
   - 101-150 (Orange): Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups -- children, elderly, and those with lung/heart disease should limit outdoor time
   - 151-200 (Red): Unhealthy -- everyone should reduce prolonged outdoor exertion
   - 201+ (Purple/Maroon): Very Unhealthy to Hazardous -- stay indoors

### Protect Your Indoor Air (30 minutes setup, then ongoing)

**HEPA air purifier (best option, ~$100-300):**
- Run continuously in your bedroom and main living area during smoke events
- Look for purifiers rated for your room size (check CADR -- Clean Air Delivery Rate)
- Replace filters according to manufacturer schedule (more frequently during smoke season)

**DIY Corsi-Rosenthal Box (~$65, 30 minutes to build):**
- Attach four MERV-13 furnace filters to a box fan with duct tape, sealed with a cardboard base
- EPA testing confirms no fire hazard (use fans manufactured after 2012)
- Studies show these can be **more effective than commercial HEPA purifiers** at a fraction of the cost
- Reduces PM2.5 by approximately **75%** in a room
- Build guide: [UC Davis Corsi-Rosenthal Box](https://engineering.ucdavis.edu/news/science-action-how-build-corsi-rosenthal-box)

**Additional indoor air steps:**
- Keep windows and doors closed during smoke events
- Set HVAC to recirculate (not fresh air intake) and upgrade to MERV-13 filters
- Avoid activities that add indoor particles: cooking on gas stoves, burning candles, vacuuming without HEPA filter
- Create a "clean air room" -- one room with a purifier running, door closed, towel under the door gap

### Protect Yourself Outdoors

- Wear an **N95 or KN95 respirator** on days with AQI above 150 -- cloth and surgical masks do not filter PM2.5
- Reduce outdoor exertion -- exercise increases the volume of air (and particles) you inhale
- If you must work outdoors, take frequent breaks in filtered indoor spaces
- Limit children's outdoor play time when AQI exceeds 100

### Check School and Activity Restrictions

- **Utah school districts** set their own outdoor activity restrictions based on AQI -- check with your school
- Most restrict outdoor recess at AQI 101+ and cancel outdoor sports at 151+
- Youth sports leagues should have air quality policies -- ask your league if they don't

### Support Prescribed Burns

When you see or hear about prescribed burns planned near your community:
- **Do not call 911 to report them** -- prescribed burns are announced in advance and closely monitored
- **Support the practice publicly** -- prescribed burns cause short-term smoke but prevent catastrophic wildfires that produce far worse and longer-lasting smoke
- **Comment in favor** when agencies propose prescribed burn plans -- they need public support to continue the practice
- **Follow Utah DEQ's Smoke Management Program** updates at [deq.utah.gov](https://deq.utah.gov/air-quality/smoke-management-program)

## Sustained Actions

### Build a Community Air Monitoring Network

- **Purchase a PurpleAir sensor** (~$250 outdoor) and add it to the global network -- the more sensors, the better the data for your neighborhood
- **Join or start a community monitoring group** -- coordinate with neighbors to cover gaps in official monitoring
- **Document smoke events** with photos, timestamps, and sensor readings -- this data supports advocacy for stronger protections
- **Share data with researchers** -- PurpleAir data feeds into studies at Stanford, UC Berkeley, and the University of Utah

### Support Organizations

- **Utah Physicians for a Healthy Environment (UPHE)** ([uphe.org](https://www.uphe.org)) -- Science-based health advocacy for clean air, led by physicians. UPHE has led campaigns for school air purifiers, pollution source reduction, and enforcement actions against violators for over 16 years
- **American Lung Association - Utah** ([lung.org](https://www.lung.org)) -- Annual "State of the Air" report cards, advocacy for stronger PM2.5 standards, and resources for people with respiratory conditions
- **Clean Air Task Force** ([catf.us](https://www.catf.us)) -- National organization working on air pollution policy and technology solutions
- **Moms Clean Air Force** ([momscleanairforce.org](https://www.momscleanairforce.org)) -- Parent-led advocacy focused on protecting children from air pollution

### Advocate for Local Preparedness

- **Push your school district** to invest in classroom air purifiers (UPHE has been leading this effort statewide)
- **Ask your employer** about indoor air quality policies during smoke events -- many workplaces lack plans
- **Advocate for clean air shelters** -- libraries, community centers, and senior centers should have HEPA filtration and serve as public refuges during severe smoke events
- **Support your local fire district** in wildfire preparedness and fuel reduction programs
- **Attend city council meetings** to advocate for air quality emergency response plans

### Reduce Your Contribution to Local Air Pollution

Utah's air quality problem is the sum of wildfire smoke (can't control) plus local emissions (can control):

- **Drive less** -- vehicle emissions are the largest local source of PM2.5 and ozone precursors. Use UTA transit, carpool, bike, or work from home when possible
- **Don't burn wood** on no-burn days -- Utah DEQ issues mandatory no-burn orders during inversions
- **Maintain your vehicle** -- properly tuned engines and inflated tires reduce emissions
- **Choose electric** when replacing vehicles, lawn mowers, snow blowers -- small gas engines are disproportionately polluting
- **Support clean energy** -- reducing fossil fuel combustion improves both local and regional air quality
