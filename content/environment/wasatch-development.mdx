# Wasatch Front Development & Habitat Loss

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Utah's Wasatch Front -- the narrow corridor between the Wasatch Mountains and the Great Salt Lake -- added nearly 37,000 new residents in a single year (2023-2024), accounting for 73% of the state's growth. The four Wasatch Front counties (Salt Lake, Utah, Davis, Weber) are projected to add hundreds of thousands more residents through 2050. This growth is consuming foothills, fragmenting wildlife migration corridors, threatening canyon watersheds, and converting agricultural land to subdivisions at an accelerating pace.

The $1.4 billion Little Cottonwood Canyon gondola, the 600-acre Point of the Mountain redevelopment, and rapid expansion in Utah County are reshaping the landscape. Local planning and zoning decisions made in the next few years will determine whether the Wasatch Front retains the open space, clean water, and wildlife that define it -- or becomes another sprawling megalopolis.

## Facts

### Population Growth and Development Pressure

The Wasatch Front is one of the fastest-growing metropolitan corridors in the United States:

- **37,000 new residents** added along the Wasatch Front between 2023 and 2024 alone
- **73% of Utah's total growth** concentrated in four Wasatch Front counties
- **18,000 new housing units** permitted in 2023-2024 (down from a peak of 28,000 in 2022)
- **83% of Utah's projected growth** through 2060 will fall in five counties -- Salt Lake, Utah, Davis, Weber, and Washington
- Population growth projected at **18% in the 2020s**, 14.5% in the 2030s, continuing through mid-century
- Eight of the ten fastest-growing Wasatch Front cities are in Utah County

[source: Kem C. Gardner Policy Institute](https://gardner.utah.edu/news/wasatch-front-communities-added-nearly-37000-new-residents-and-nearly-18000-new-housing-units-between-2023-and-2024/) | [source: Utah Long-Term Demographic Projections](https://d36oiwf74r1rap.cloudfront.net/wp-content/uploads/Projections-Brief-Final-Updated-Feb2019.pdf)

### Wildlife Corridor Fragmentation

The Wasatch Mountains support critical wildlife populations that depend on connected habitat:

- **20,000 mule deer and 3,000 elk** rely on Wasatch migration corridors to reach winter range in the Morgan Valley foothills
- Mountain lions require **20+ square mile ranges** -- habitat loss from development and road construction is leading to population declines
- The Wasatch Front provides habitat for **millions of migratory birds** annually, with agricultural lands, open spaces, and wetlands covering 31-76% of critical shorebird, waterbird, and waterfowl habitat
- Development fragments corridors through **roads, fencing, lighting, and noise** -- animals avoid areas within 500-1,000 meters of human activity
- Utah DWR manages 194 Wildlife Management Areas protecting over 500,000 acres statewide, but development pressure is encroaching on unprotected corridors

[source: Utah Wildlife Migration Initiative](https://wildlifemigration.utah.gov/land-animals/corridors/) | [source: Conservation Biology Study - Wasatch Mammals](https://conbio.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/csp2.12708)

### [+] Little Cottonwood Canyon Gondola

  UDOT selected Gondola B with Phased Implementation as its preferred alternative for Little Cottonwood Canyon in July 2023, proposing the **world's longest gondola** connecting the valley floor to Snowbird and Alta ski resorts.

  **Key facts:**
  - Estimated cost: **$1.4 billion** of taxpayer money
  - Phase 1 (enhanced bus service, tolling, parking restrictions): Funded, began fall 2025
  - Actual gondola construction: **Not planned until 2042-2050**
  - Supporters: UDOT leadership, Governor Cox, Snowbird Resort ownership
  - Opposition: Save Our Canyons, Friends of Little Cottonwood Canyon, Town of Alta, homeowners near the canyon mouth

  **Why opponents object:**
  - The gondola serves only two private ski resorts, not the broader canyon
  - It threatens the canyon's watershed, which supplies drinking water to 350,000+ residents
  - Enhanced bus service (Phase 1) may solve congestion without a gondola
  - Homeowners near the canyon mouth have filed lawsuits to block UDOT from using their neighborhood for gondola infrastructure
  - The Mayor of Alta has spoken out against the project

  [source: Save Our Canyons - No Gondola](https://saveourcanyons.org/issues/no-gondola-in-little-cottonwood-canyon) | [source: UDOT Gondola Timeline (Salt Lake Tribune)](https://local.sltrib.com/gondola/)

### [+] Point of the Mountain Redevelopment

  The 600-acre former Utah State Prison site in Draper -- known as "The Point" -- is the largest urban redevelopment project in Utah history.

  - **$2.3 billion** in private-sector investment committed through Innovation Point Partners
  - First 100-acre phase: Shopping, entertainment, main street, high-density housing -- construction began 2025
  - Vision: 15-minute walkable community with river-to-mountain trail connectivity
  - Infrastructure installation (roads, water, gas, parks) underway

  **Conservation concerns:**
  - The site sits at a critical wildlife movement pinch point between the Wasatch and Oquirrh Mountains
  - Development intensity will increase traffic, light pollution, and noise in surrounding foothills
  - Opportunity to incorporate wildlife-friendly design is now, during planning -- not after construction

  [source: KSL - The Point Redevelopment](https://www.ksl.com/article/50801055/redevelopment-plans-announced-for-first-phase-of-the-point-at-former-state-prison-site) | [source: HOK Master Plan](https://www.hok.com/projects/view/point-of-the-mountain-master-plan-utah/)

### [+] Jordan River Corridor

  The Jordan River, flowing 51 miles from Utah Lake to the Great Salt Lake, is undergoing **Utah's largest riparian restoration effort in state history:**

  - **$5 million** America the Beautiful Challenge grant, matched by local funds
  - **70-acre** Big Bend Nature Park restoration (8400-8800 South)
  - **20,000 native trees and shrubs** being planted
  - Invasive species removal (phragmites, tamarisk) at 12+ locations
  - Trail reopening and safety improvements along the Jordan River Parkway

  The Jordan River corridor is the Wasatch Front's most important urban wildlife habitat, connecting the Great Salt Lake wetlands to Utah Lake. It supports beaver, mink, great blue heron, bald eagles, and dozens of migratory species.

  [source: Jordan River Commission - Restoration Projects](https://jordanrivercommission.gov/restoration-projects/) | [source: Utah DNR Grant Initiatives](https://ffsl.utah.gov/state-lands/grant-funded-initiatives-help-reshape-the-jordan-river-landscape/)

### [+] Snyderville Basin and Parleys Canyon

  Summit County, at the top of Parleys Canyon, is experiencing rapid growth driven by proximity to Park City and the tech corridor:

  - **Senate Bill 26** (passed March 2025) promotes Kimball Junction development -- 165 affordable housing units, transit center, mixed-use plaza
  - Dakota Pacific project: **885 residential units** approved at Park City Tech Center
  - Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) planned from Kimball Junction to Old Town Park City by 2028
  - Snyderville Basin General Plan under update to manage growth

  Development at the top of Parleys Canyon directly affects the watershed that feeds into the Salt Lake Valley.

  [source: KPCW - Kimball Junction Development Bill](https://www.kpcw.org/summit-county/2025-03-10/utah-lawmakers-pass-bill-promoting-kimball-junction-development)

### Foothills and Open Space

Salt Lake City's Foothills Estates Residential District (FR-1) requires lots of 43,000+ square feet for eco-friendly development. Salt Lake County maintains a Foothills and Canyons Overlay Zone regulating construction, grading, and vegetation removal.

However, development pressure continues to push into higher-elevation foothills across the Wasatch Front, including areas without overlay protections. Utah County's explosive growth has been particularly aggressive in converting agricultural land and foothill habitat to residential subdivisions.

## Analysis

### What This Means

- Growth is inevitable -- but sprawl is a choice. How communities zone and plan in the next decade will determine the Wasatch Front's character for generations
- Wildlife corridors, once severed, cannot be reassembled -- mule deer and elk require continuous connected habitat to survive
- Canyon watersheds supply drinking water to 1+ million residents -- development above water sources affects everyone downstream
- The gondola debate is a proxy for a larger question: who do the canyons belong to -- private resorts or the public?
- Open space bonds and conservation easements are the most effective tools for permanent protection, but they require public support and funding

### Who's Affected

- **All Wasatch Front residents:** Water quality, air quality, recreation access, and quality of life depend on open space and watershed protection
- **Wildlife:** Mule deer, elk, mountain lions, migratory birds, and native fish all depend on connected, undeveloped corridors
- **Outdoor recreation economy:** Utah's outdoor recreation industry generates **$12.3 billion annually** -- it depends on public access to undeveloped landscapes
- **Agricultural communities:** Prime farmland in Utah County is being converted to housing at an accelerating rate
- **Future generations:** Land paved today is lost permanently -- conservation decisions are irreversible

## Quick Actions

### Attend Your City or County Planning Commission Meeting (1-2 hours)

This is the single most impactful thing you can do. Local planning commissions make the zoning and development decisions that determine what gets built and where. Most meetings are poorly attended -- your voice carries enormous weight.

1. **Find your planning commission schedule:**
   - Salt Lake City: [slc.gov/planning](https://www.slc.gov/planning/)
   - Salt Lake County: [slco.org/planning-transportation](https://slco.org/planning-transportation/)
   - Utah County: [utahcounty.gov](https://www.utahcounty.gov/Dept/ComDev/)
   - Summit County: [summitcountyutah.gov](https://www.summitcountyutah.gov/591/Snyderville-Basin-Planning-District)
2. **Review the agenda** before the meeting -- look for rezoning requests, subdivision approvals, and general plan amendments
3. **Speak during public comment** -- you typically get 3 minutes. State your name, address, and specific concerns about wildlife, water, or open space
4. **Bring neighbors** -- numbers matter in local government

### Comment on Development Proposals

When you see a new subdivision, rezoning, or development proposal in your area:

1. **Check your city/county website** for public comment periods
2. **Submit written comments** citing specific impacts: water use, traffic, wildlife corridors, viewshed, stormwater runoff
3. **Request environmental review** for projects near foothills, canyons, or waterways
4. **Ask about open space requirements** -- many jurisdictions require developers to set aside a percentage as open space

### Support Open Space Bonds

When your city or county puts open space bonds on the ballot, **vote yes and encourage your neighbors to do the same**. Open space bonds are the most effective tool for permanent land protection at the local level. Salt Lake County, Summit County, and Wasatch County have all passed successful open space bonds.

### Engage in the Gondola Debate

- **Submit comments** to UDOT on Little Cottonwood Canyon transportation plans
- **Attend public meetings** about canyon transportation -- [UDOT Wasatch Back project](https://wasatchback.udot.utah.gov/)
- **Support enhanced bus service** (Phase 1) as an alternative to the gondola
- **Contact your state legislators** about the $1.4 billion price tag and who benefits

## Sustained Actions

### Support Conservation Organizations

- **Save Our Canyons** ([saveourcanyons.org](https://saveourcanyons.org)) -- Protecting the Wasatch canyons, mountains, and foothills since 1972. Leading the opposition to the LCC gondola and advocating for 80,000 acres of Central Wasatch protection
- **Utah Open Lands** ([utahopenlands.org](https://www.utahopenlands.org)) -- Utah's largest local land trust, with 67,000+ acres protected through conservation easements since 1990
- **Jordan River Commission** ([jordanrivercommission.gov](https://jordanrivercommission.gov)) -- Coordinating the largest riparian restoration effort in Utah history along the Jordan River corridor
- **Conserve Utah Valley** ([conserveutahvalley.org](https://conserveutahvalley.org)) -- Protecting canyons, foothills, open spaces, and waters in Utah's fastest-growing county
- **Friends of Little Cottonwood Canyon** ([lccfriends.org](https://lccfriends.org)) -- Advocating for responsible canyon transportation solutions
- **Wasatch Front Regional Council** ([wfrc.org](https://wfrc.org)) -- Regional planning body working on open space preservation and transportation alternatives

### Volunteer for Restoration Projects

- **Jordan River restoration** -- Tree planting, invasive species removal, trail maintenance through the Jordan River Commission
- **Trail maintenance** -- Volunteer with local trail groups to maintain access to foothills and canyons
- **Habitat monitoring** -- Document wildlife sightings with [iNaturalist](https://www.inaturalist.org) and [eBird](https://ebird.org) to build baseline data for conservation arguments
- **BioBlitz events** -- Participate in community species inventories organized by local nature centers and conservation groups

### Engage in Long-Term Planning

- **Wasatch Choice 2050** -- The Wasatch Front Regional Council's long-range plan includes open space preservation. Attend public input sessions at [wfrc.org](https://wfrc.org)
- **General plan updates** -- When your city updates its general plan, show up and advocate for wildlife-friendly development standards, open space requirements, and foothill protections
- **Run for office** -- Planning commissions, city councils, and county councils are often uncontested or decided by small margins. If you care about land use, consider serving

### Make Consumer Choices That Reduce Sprawl

- **Choose density over sprawl** when selecting housing -- townhomes and condos consume less land per unit than single-family homes on large lots
- **Support local agriculture** -- Buy from Utah farms and ranches to keep agricultural land economically viable
- **Use public transit** -- UTA ridership supports the case for transit-oriented development instead of highway expansion
