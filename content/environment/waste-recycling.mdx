# Waste and Recycling: America's Broken System

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Americans generate **4.9 pounds of trash per person per day** — among the highest rates on Earth. The US recycling rate sits at **32%**, but that figure flatters the reality: after China's 2018 National Sword policy banned most recyclables, plastic landfilling increased **23%** and dozens of cities simply stopped recycling programs. Only **9% of all plastic ever produced** has actually been recycled. Landfills are the **third-largest source of US methane emissions**, and the petrochemical industry plans to double plastic production by 2030. Seven states have now passed Extended Producer Responsibility laws to make manufacturers pay for the waste they create.

The system is broken — but it's not inevitable.

## Facts

### How Much Waste the US Generates

The United States generated **292 million tons** of municipal solid waste (MSW) in 2018 — the most recent year for which EPA has comprehensive data. That works out to approximately **4.9 pounds per person per day**. Of that total:

- **94 million tons** (32.1%) were recycled or composted
- **146 million tons** (50%) were landfilled
- **34 million tons** were combusted for energy

Food was the **single largest component of landfills**, making up about 24% of landfilled material. [source: EPA Facts and Figures on Materials, Waste and Recycling](https://www.epa.gov/facts-and-figures-about-materials-waste-and-recycling/national-overview-facts-and-figures-materials)

### [+] The Plastic Recycling Myth

The recycling number printed on plastic containers is a **resin identification code**, not a recycling guarantee. A 2017 landmark study by UC Santa Barbara industrial ecologist Roland Geyer, published in *Science Advances*, found that of the **6.3 billion tons** of plastic waste generated by 2015:

- Only **9%** had been recycled
- **12%** had been incinerated
- **79%** accumulated in landfills or the natural environment

If current trends continue, roughly **12 billion metric tons** of plastic waste will be in landfills or the natural environment by 2050. [source: Geyer et al., Science Advances 2017](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.1700782)

### [+] China's National Sword Shattered US Recycling

For decades, the US exported the majority of its recyclables — especially low-grade plastics and mixed paper — to China for processing. In January 2018, China's **National Sword policy** banned imports of most recyclable materials with contamination rates above 0.5%.

The impact was immediate:
- **Plastic landfilling in the US increased 23.2%** following implementation
- Cities including Deltona, Florida, suspended curbside recycling pickup
- Phenix City, Alabama, stopped accepting all plastics
- Rural and small-town programs were hit hardest
- The cost of recycling programs surged, with municipalities charged more and profits collapsing

[source: University at Buffalo Study on National Sword Impact](https://www.buffalo.edu/news/releases/2022/03/029.html) | [source: Yale E360 - Piling Up](https://e360.yale.edu/features/piling-up-how-chinas-ban-on-importing-waste-has-stalled-global-recycling)

### [+] Landfills and Methane

Municipal solid waste landfills are the **third-largest source of human-related methane emissions** in the United States, accounting for approximately **14–17%** of total US methane emissions in 2022. Methane is a greenhouse gas roughly 80 times more potent than CO2 over a 20-year period.

Food waste is the primary driver: decomposing organic matter in oxygen-deprived landfills produces methane rather than CO2. This is why reducing food waste and composting are among the highest-impact waste interventions available.

Harvard researchers have found that EPA's standard accounting methods may **underestimate landfill methane emissions by 51%**, meaning the actual climate contribution of landfills could be nearly double the official figure. [source: EPA Landfill Gas Basics](https://www.epa.gov/lmop/basic-information-about-landfill-gas) | [source: Harvard Study on Landfill Methane Underestimation](https://seas.harvard.edu/news/epa-underestimates-methane-emissions-landfills-urban-areas)

### [+] Food Waste: 30–40% of the Food Supply

USDA estimates that **30–40% of the US food supply** is wasted — approximately **133 billion pounds** worth **$161 billion** annually at the retail and consumer levels. The average American family of four loses about **$1,500 per year** to uneaten food.

Food is the **single largest category of material placed in municipal landfills**, where it generates methane. The US and EPA set a national goal in 2015 to reduce food waste 50% by 2030. Progress has been limited. [source: USDA Food Loss and Waste](https://www.usda.gov/about-food/food-safety/food-loss-and-waste) | [source: EPA 2030 Food Waste Goal](https://www.epa.gov/sustainable-management-food/united-states-2030-food-loss-and-waste-reduction-goal)

### [+] E-Waste: The Global Crisis

A record **62 million metric tonnes** of electronic waste (e-waste) were produced globally in 2022 — up **82% from 2010** — and annual generation is increasing by 2.6 million tonnes per year. The world is on track for **82 million tonnes** of e-waste annually by 2030.

Only **22.3%** of e-waste is properly collected and recycled. The rest is often exported to developing nations lacking proper waste management systems. The EU alone illegally ships an estimated **352,474 metric tonnes** of e-waste to developing countries annually, primarily to Africa.

The unrecycled e-waste contains an estimated **$62 billion worth** of recoverable natural resources — gold, silver, copper, rare earth elements — that are instead burned or buried. [source: Global E-waste Monitor 2024, UNITAR](https://unitar.org/about/news-stories/press/global-e-waste-monitor-2024-electronic-waste-rising-five-times-faster-documented-e-waste-recycling)

### [+] PFAS Contamination in Recycled Paper

One underreported problem with paper recycling: **PFAS "forever chemicals"** from grease-resistant food packaging contaminate the entire recycled paper stream. A 2024 study in *Environmental Science and Pollution Research* found PFAS in paper recycling grades across the supply chain:

- Post-consumer samples: **213 ng/g** average PFAS concentration
- Retail samples: **159 ng/g**
- Pre-consumer samples: **121 ng/g**

Of 12 non-grease-resistant recycled paper packaging samples tested, **four showed unintentional PFAS contamination** — meaning PFAS from other packaging migrated into supposedly clean materials. This contaminates food packaging made from recycled paper and undermines the circular economy. [source: Springer Environmental Science and Pollution Research, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11356-024-33250-9)

### [+] Extended Producer Responsibility: The Policy Solution

**Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)** laws require the companies that produce packaging to pay for its end-of-life collection and recycling — rather than shifting those costs to taxpayers and municipalities.

As of early 2026, **seven states** have enacted packaging EPR laws:

| State | Status |
|-------|--------|
| Maine | Passed 2021, implementation beginning 2026–2027 |
| Oregon | Passed 2021, enforcement began July 2025 |
| California | Passed, implementation ongoing |
| Colorado | Passed, implementation ongoing |
| Maryland | Passed |
| Minnesota | Passed |
| Washington | Passed |

More than 30 additional states are considering EPR legislation. No federal EPR law for packaging currently exists. [source: Tonkon Torp EPR Law Update, 2025](https://tonkon.com/alerts/extended-producer-responsibility-laws-fall-2025-update/) | [source: Proskauer EPR Guide 2025](https://www.proskauer.com/alert/the-2025-guide-to-epr-packaging-compliance)

### [+] Plastic Production Is Accelerating

Even as recycling fails to keep pace, plastic production continues to grow:

- Current global plastic production: **~380 million tons/year**
- Projected production by 2050: **up to 1,800 million tons/year** — nearly a **5x increase**
- The IEA projects petrochemicals will account for **nearly half of global oil demand growth** through 2050
- Plastics are increasingly the growth market for fossil fuel companies as transportation electrifies

This means no amount of recycling improvement can solve the plastic waste crisis without addressing production. [source: IEA Future of Petrochemicals](https://www.iea.org/reports/the-future-of-petrochemicals)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The recycling system the US built assumed materials would be processed cheaply overseas. That model collapsed in 2018 and has not been rebuilt.
- Most plastic labeled as recyclable is not actually recycled — the recycling symbol is not a guarantee, it is a resin code.
- Landfills are a significant and growing contributor to climate change through methane emissions.
- The industry plans to increase plastic production dramatically as fossil fuel use in transportation declines — making the packaging waste crisis worse.
- EPR laws represent the only structural solution: shifting waste costs from taxpayers back to producers creates financial incentives to design less wasteful packaging.

### Who's Affected

- **Low-income communities and communities of color:** Landfills and incinerators are disproportionately sited near low-income and minority communities, exposing residents to air and water pollution.
- **Municipalities and taxpayers:** When recycling markets collapse, cities pay more to landfill what used to be sold as a commodity.
- **Developing nations:** US and European e-waste is exported to countries with weaker environmental regulations, where workers — often children — sort and burn electronics in open air, inhaling toxic fumes.
- **Future generations:** Plastic waste accumulates in landfills and oceans for centuries. The decisions made now about plastic production lock in consequences that will last generations.

### Find Out What Actually Gets Recycled in Your City

Recycling rules vary widely by municipality. Before putting something in the bin:
1. Search "[your city] what can I recycle" or call your waste hauler directly
2. Avoid "wish-cycling" — putting non-recyclables in the bin contaminates the entire load
3. Look up your city's actual recycling contractor and whether they currently have markets for what they collect

### Contact Your City Council About Composting

Composting food waste diverts the largest landfill category and eliminates the methane it would generate. If your city doesn't offer curbside composting:
1. Find your city council representative at [usa.gov/local-governments](https://www.usa.gov/local-governments)
2. Email or call asking them to add or expand composting pickup
3. Reference cities that have done this successfully (San Francisco composting rate: 80%)

### Start Composting at Home

Home composting keeps food and yard waste out of landfills:
- **Backyard composting:** Bin or pile, free after initial setup
- **Worm bins (vermicomposting):** Works in apartments, produces rich fertilizer
- **Bokashi:** Fermentation system that handles meat and dairy, works indoors
- [EPA Composting Guide](https://www.epa.gov/recycle/composting-home)

### Reduce Plastic Purchasing

The most effective recycling is not buying the unrecyclable material in the first place:
- Bring reusable bags, bottles, and containers
- Choose products with cardboard, glass, or metal packaging over plastic
- Buy concentrates and refillables where available
- Avoid single-use plastics: straws, cutlery, coffee pods

### Support Take-Back Programs

Many manufacturers and retailers offer take-back programs for hard-to-recycle items:
- **Electronics:** Best Buy, Staples, and many manufacturers take back e-waste
- **Batteries:** Call2Recycle drop-offs at major retailers ([call2recycle.org](https://www.call2recycle.org))
- **Medications:** DEA Drug Take-Back Program ([dea.gov/takebackday](https://www.dea.gov/takebackday))
- **Paint:** PaintCare drop-off locations ([paintcare.org](https://www.paintcare.org))

### Write to Grocery Chains on Packaging

Major grocery chains can directly pressure suppliers to reduce packaging. Write to the CEO or sustainability team of your primary grocery store asking them to:
- Expand packaging-free bulk sections
- Eliminate non-recyclable packaging on store brands
- Partner with EPR programs voluntarily

- **Zero Waste USA** ([zerowaste.org](https://zerowaste.org)) — National network advocating for zero-waste policies and EPR legislation
- **Plastic Pollution Coalition** ([plasticpollutioncoalition.org](https://www.plasticpollutioncoalition.org)) — Advocacy for reducing plastic production, not just improving recycling
- **National Recycling Coalition** ([nrcma.org](https://nrcma.org)) — Industry and advocacy group working to improve US recycling infrastructure

### Push for State EPR Laws

If your state has not yet passed EPR legislation, contact your state legislators and ask them to introduce or support packaging EPR bills. Use the [NRCM EPR Resource Page](https://www.nrcm.org/programs/sustainability/epr-for-packaging/) to find model legislation and talking points.

### Support Food Waste Reduction

- Advocate for your school district or workplace to implement food waste composting
- Push local restaurants to partner with food rescue organizations like [Too Good To Go](https://www.toogoodtogo.com) or [Feeding America](https://www.feedingamerica.org)
- Support the US 2030 Food Waste Reduction Goal at the local level
