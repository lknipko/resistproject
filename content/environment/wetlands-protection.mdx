# Wetlands Protection

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** In *Sackett v. EPA* (2023), the Supreme Court eliminated federal Clean Water Act protections for any wetland not directly and continuously connected to navigable waters. An NRDC analysis found that at least 60% of individual wetlands -- tens of millions of acres -- lost federal protection overnight. The United States has already lost over half its original wetlands since the 1780s, with loss rates accelerating 50% since 2009. Wetlands prevent $8,000 per acre per year in flood damage, filter drinking water, store up to 216 metric tons of carbon per acre, and support 40% of all plant and animal species. Fewer than half of states have their own wetlands protection programs. The current administration proposed a new WOTUS rule in November 2025 that would further narrow the definition of protected waters.

Wetlands are not wastelands. They are critical infrastructure -- and they are disappearing.

## Facts

### Sackett v. EPA: The Decision That Changed Everything

On May 25, 2023, the Supreme Court ruled 9-0 in *Sackett v. EPA* that the EPA had overstepped its authority in regulating the Sacketts' property in Idaho. But the 5-4 majority opinion, written by Justice Alito, went much further than the specific case. It established a new legal test: the Clean Water Act only protects wetlands with a "continuous surface connection" to navigable waters, making them "indistinguishable" from those waters.

This eliminated federal protection for:
- **Isolated wetlands** not physically touching a navigable river, lake, or stream
- **Wetlands connected only by groundwater** -- even if they feed into protected waters underground
- **Prairie potholes** -- millions of small seasonal wetlands across the Great Plains critical for waterfowl
- **Vernal pools** -- seasonal pools that support endangered species in California and elsewhere
- **Pocosins and Carolina Bays** -- unique wetland types in the Southeast

Justice Kavanaugh's concurrence warned that the majority's test "will have significant repercussions for water quality and flood control throughout the United States."

[source: Supreme Court - Sackett v. EPA](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/22pdf/21-454_4g15.pdf) | [source: Harvard Law Review - Sackett v. EPA](https://harvardlawreview.org/print/vol-137/sackett-v-epa/)

### The Scale of Loss

Modeling by the Natural Resources Defense Council found that **at least 60% of individual wetlands** in the lower 48 states are no longer protected by the Clean Water Act after Sackett. The most conservative estimate still showed 60% of wetlands losing protection.

An Earthjustice analysis mapped the wetlands most at risk by state. States in the arid West, Great Plains, and Southeast face the greatest losses because their wetlands are more likely to be geographically isolated.

[source: NRDC - Study on Sackett Impacts](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/25032025/supreme-court-sackett-decision-threatens-wetlands-nrdc-study-finds/) | [source: Earthjustice - Wetlands at Risk Map](https://earthjustice.org/feature/sackett-epa-wetlands-supreme-court-map)

### How Much Has Already Been Lost

| Period | Wetlands Lost | Rate |
|--------|--------------|------|
| 1780s to 1980s | ~110 million acres (50% of original 220 million) | ~550,000 acres/year |
| 2009 to 2019 | 670,000 acres (area of Rhode Island) | ~67,000 acres/year |
| Post-Sackett (projected) | Accelerating -- no federal brake on destruction | Unknown |

As of 2019, approximately 116.4 million acres of wetlands remained in the lower 48 states, covering less than 6% of the land area. Loss rates increased 50% between the 2009-2019 study period compared to earlier decades, driven by development, agriculture, and planted forestry.

[source: U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service - Wetlands Status and Trends 2024 Report](https://www.fws.gov/press-release/2024-03/continued-decline-wetlands-documented-new-us-fish-and-wildlife-service-report)

### Why Wetlands Matter: The Numbers

**Flood Control:**
- Maintaining just 15% of a watershed in wetlands can reduce flood peaks by up to 60%
- Wetlands along the Charles River in Boston save an estimated $17 million per year in flood damage
- Coastal wetlands prevented $625 million in property damage during Hurricane Sandy
- One acre of wetland can store 1-1.5 million gallons of floodwater

**Water Filtration:**
- Wetlands trap sediment, absorb nutrients, and break down pollutants before water reaches rivers and aquifers
- A single South Carolina bottomland hardwood swamp was found to remove pollutants equivalent to a water treatment plant -- saving an estimated $5 million
- The Houston-Galveston region's wetlands provide an estimated $15 billion per year in water quality, flood protection, and other ecological services

**Carbon Storage:**
- Wetlands store between 81 and 216 metric tons of carbon per acre
- Peatlands (a type of wetland) cover just 3% of Earth's land but store twice as much carbon as all the world's forests combined
- Destroying wetlands releases stored carbon as CO2, turning a carbon sink into a carbon source

**Biodiversity:**
- 40% of all plant and animal species live or breed in wetlands
- Half of all federally listed endangered species depend on wetlands
- 150+ bird species and 200+ fish species depend on U.S. wetlands
- Freshwater species populations have declined 83% since 1970

[source: EPA - Why Are Wetlands Important](https://www.epa.gov/wetlands/why-are-wetlands-important) | [source: EPA - Functions and Values of Wetlands](https://www.epa.gov/sites/default/files/2016-02/documents/functionsvaluesofwetlands.pdf) | [source: U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service - Wetlands and Endangered Species](https://www.fws.gov/story/2023-04/why-healthy-wetlands-are-vital-protecting-endangered-species)

### The Clean Water Act Section 404 Permit Program

Before Sackett, Section 404 of the Clean Water Act required anyone wanting to discharge dredged or fill material into "waters of the United States" -- including wetlands -- to obtain a permit from the Army Corps of Engineers. The basic rule: no discharge is permitted if a less damaging alternative exists or if the nation's waters would be significantly degraded.

After Sackett, the Corps can only require permits for wetlands meeting the "continuous surface connection" test. The 2021 Nationwide Permits expired in March 2026 and the Corps proposed new permits in June 2025, further narrowing what requires review.

In November 2025, EPA and the Army Corps published a proposed new WOTUS rule that would further narrow the definition of protected waters beyond even the Sackett decision.

**Mitigation banking** -- where developers offset wetland destruction by restoring or creating wetlands elsewhere -- only applies to permitted fills. If no permit is required, there is no mitigation requirement. Wetlands can be destroyed with no offset.

[source: EPA - Section 404 Permit Program](https://www.epa.gov/cwa-404/permit-program-under-cwa-section-404) | [source: Morgan Lewis - Proposed WOTUS Rule 2025](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2025/12/proposed-waters-of-the-united-states-rule-seeks-to-clarify-and-limit-clean-water-act-jurisdiction)

### States Stepping Up (and Falling Behind)

Fewer than half of states had their own wetlands protection programs before Sackett. Since the decision, states have split:

**States that strengthened protections:**
- **Colorado** -- First state to pass post-Sackett legislation (HB 24-1379), creating a state dredge and fill permitting program
- **California** -- Existing state wetlands policy already covered isolated wetlands
- **Washington** -- Boosted funding for state regulators anticipating increased permit workload
- **New Mexico** -- $7.6 million appropriation for state surface water quality protections
- **Maryland** -- Added citizen suit provisions through the Clean Water Justice Act
- **North Carolina** -- Executive action to increase wetlands protection

**States that rolled back protections:**
- **Indiana, Tennessee, Missouri** -- Joined efforts to weaken state-level wetlands rules
- **Illinois, Iowa** -- Agricultural industry successfully blocked wetlands protection legislation

[source: Clean Water Action - Colorado First State to Restore Protections](https://cleanwater.org/2024/05/23/year-sackett-v-epa-colorado-becomes-first-state-restore-protections-wetlands-and-streams) | [source: Earthjustice - Local Champions Stepped Up](https://earthjustice.org/article/when-the-supreme-court-gutted-clean-water-protections-local-champions-stepped-up)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The primary federal tool for protecting wetlands (Section 404 permitting) has been dramatically weakened
- Developers can now fill isolated wetlands without any federal permit or mitigation requirement
- States without their own programs have no regulatory backstop -- wetlands can be destroyed freely
- The cumulative loss of "isolated" wetlands degrades entire watersheds, even if each individual loss seems small
- Flood damage, water treatment costs, and species extinction will accelerate as wetlands disappear
- The proposed 2025 WOTUS rule signals the administration intends to narrow protections further, not restore them

### Who's Affected

- **Homeowners in floodplains:** Every acre of wetland destroyed increases flooding downstream -- FEMA estimates that wetland loss accounts for increasing flood insurance costs
- **Drinking water users:** Wetlands filter water naturally; losing them increases treatment costs for municipal water systems
- **Hunters, anglers, and birders:** Wetlands support the waterfowl, fish, and migratory birds these communities depend on -- the outdoor recreation economy generates $887 billion/year
- **Farmers:** Wetlands recharge groundwater and reduce erosion; their loss can harm the very agriculture that sometimes replaces them
- **Coastal communities:** Coastal wetlands buffer storm surge -- their loss increases hurricane and storm damage
- **Environmental justice communities:** Low-income communities are least able to absorb increased flooding and water quality degradation
- **Taxpayers:** Replacing natural wetland services with engineered solutions costs billions -- Boston's $17 million in annual flood protection from wetlands would cost far more to replicate with infrastructure

### Check Your Local Wetlands (5 minutes)

Find out what wetlands are near you and whether they are protected:

1. **National Wetlands Inventory Mapper:** [fws.gov/program/national-wetlands-inventory/wetlands-mapper](https://www.fws.gov/program/national-wetlands-inventory/wetlands-mapper) -- Zoom to your area to see mapped wetlands
2. **Click any wetland polygon** for classification details and metadata
3. **Note:** NWI maps are not comprehensive -- unmapped wetlands exist, especially smaller ones
4. **Check your state:** Does your state have its own wetlands protection program? Search "[your state] wetlands protection program" or check with your state environmental agency

### Oppose Local Wetland Development

When development is proposed on or near wetlands in your community:

1. **Attend planning and zoning meetings** -- public comment periods are your opportunity to object
2. **Request environmental review** -- even without federal requirements, many local governments require environmental impact assessment
3. **Document the wetland** -- photos, species observations (via iNaturalist), and water quality data create a public record
4. **Contact your local Waterkeeper** ([waterkeeper.org](https://www.waterkeeper.org)) or Audubon chapter for support
5. **File a complaint** if you witness unauthorized wetland fill -- report to your state environmental agency and EPA's [tips and complaints portal](https://echo.epa.gov/report-environmental-violations)

### Attend Army Corps Permit Hearings

When the Army Corps of Engineers issues public notices for Section 404 permits (dredge and fill permits), you can comment:

- Search for public notices at your [local Corps district](https://www.usace.army.mil/Locations/) website
- Comments are most effective when they cite specific environmental impacts: species habitat, flood risk, water quality, cultural resources
- Request a public hearing if one has not been scheduled -- the Corps must consider this request

### Comment on the Proposed WOTUS Rule

The November 2025 proposed WOTUS rule would further narrow federal water protections. Submit comments at:

- [Regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov) -- search for the EPA-HQ-OW WOTUS docket
- Cite specific wetlands in your community that would lose protection
- Reference the economic value of wetland services (flood prevention, water filtration)
- Note any personal experience with flooding or water quality issues

### Volunteer for Wetland Restoration

Hands-on restoration is one of the most direct ways to protect and rebuild wetlands:

- **Ducks Unlimited** ([ducks.org/volunteer](https://www.ducks.org/volunteer)) -- 4,000+ events per year, including habitat restoration workdays
- **Local Audubon chapters** -- many organize wetland planting days and invasive species removal
- **Your state wildlife agency** -- search "[your state] wetland restoration volunteer"
- **National Wildlife Refuges** -- many accept volunteers for habitat work; find yours at [fws.gov/refuges](https://www.fws.gov/refuges)

### Push for State-Level Protection

If your state does not have its own wetlands protection program, it is the single most important gap to fill:

- Contact your **state legislators** and ask them to introduce state dredge and fill permitting legislation (modeled on Colorado's HB 24-1379)
- Support **state environmental agency funding** -- agencies need staff and resources to process permits and enforce protections
- Advocate for **no-net-loss policies** at the state level -- requiring mitigation for any wetland destruction
- Push for **state adoption of the "significant nexus" test** -- the broader standard for wetlands protection that was overturned by Sackett

### Citizen Wetland Monitoring

Community monitoring creates data that can be used in legal and regulatory proceedings:

- **Document wetlands** in your area with photos, GPS coordinates, and species observations
- **Test water quality** upstream and downstream of wetlands to demonstrate filtration value
- **Report changes** -- new construction, filling, draining, or degradation of wetlands you monitor
- **Use iNaturalist** ([inaturalist.org](https://www.inaturalist.org)) to document species in local wetlands -- this data is used by researchers and regulators
- **Join SciStarter** ([scistarter.org](https://scistarter.org)) to find wetland-focused citizen science projects near you

### Reduce Your Flood and Water Footprint

- **Support green infrastructure** in your community -- rain gardens, bioswales, and constructed wetlands that mimic natural wetland functions
- **Reduce impervious surfaces** on your property -- permeable pavers, rain barrels, and native plantings reduce runoff
- **Oppose floodplain development** in your community -- building in floodplains increases flood risk for everyone and often destroys wetlands
- **Know your flood risk** -- check FEMA flood maps at [msc.fema.gov](https://msc.fema.gov/)
