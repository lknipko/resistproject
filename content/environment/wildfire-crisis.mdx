# Wildfire Crisis

## Quick Summary

**Key Point:** Wildfire seasons in the western U.S. have lengthened from 5 months to over 7 months since the 1970s. The January 2025 Los Angeles fires killed 30 people and destroyed over 16,000 structures in the most destructive 48-hour fire disaster in California history. In 2026, wildfires have already burned 127% more acreage than the 10-year average. Yet the proposed FY2026 budget cuts the U.S. Forest Service by 65%, slashes FEMA's disaster response workforce by 41%, and has already halved the rate of prescribed burns and fuel reduction work.

Wildfire smoke now kills an estimated 40,000 Americans per year -- a number projected to reach 70,000 by 2050. Smoke from western wildfires travels thousands of miles, degrading air quality for hundreds of millions of people across the continent.

## Facts

### Fire Seasons Are Getting Longer

Since the 1970s, western U.S. wildfire seasons have extended from approximately 5 months to over 7 months. Parts of Southern California, New Mexico, Texas, and Arizona now experience roughly two additional months of fire weather compared to 50 years ago.

The cause is climate-driven: warmer temperatures lead to earlier snowmelt, drier vegetation, and longer periods of low humidity and high winds. Spring fire weather has expanded by about a month in New Mexico, Arizona, and Colorado. Summer fire weather has grown by three weeks in Southern California, Utah, and Arizona.

[source: NOAA - Wildfire Climate Connection](https://www.noaa.gov/noaa-wildfire/wildfire-climate-connection) | [source: Climate Central - Longer Fire Weather Seasons](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-matters/longer-more-intense-fire-weather-seasons)

### [+] Recent Wildfire Devastation

**January 2025 -- Los Angeles:**
The Palisades and Eaton fires in January 2025 became the most destructive 48-hour fire disaster in recorded California history:
- 30 people killed
- Over 16,000 structures destroyed
- 37,000+ acres burned
- 180,000+ people evacuated
- Estimated $76-131 billion in economic damage

**2025 National Season:**
- 77,850 wildfires burned 5 million acres (below the 10-year average of 7.6 million)
- Despite lower total acreage, the LA fires demonstrated that catastrophic loss concentrates where fires hit populated areas

**2026 Season (as of March):**
- 15,000+ fire starts, 1.5 million+ acres burned -- 127% above the 10-year average
- Ranked first for fire ignitions by late March in any year of the past decade
- Extremely dry winter across the western U.S. has elevated risk for a severe season

[source: NIFC - Wildfire Statistics](https://www.nifc.gov/fire-information/statistics/wildfires) | [source: Inside Climate News - 2026 Records](https://insideclimatenews.org/news/27032026/us-wildfires-already-setting-records-this-year/)

### [+] 45 Million Homes in the Fire Zone

The wildland-urban interface (WUI) -- where development meets wildfire-prone vegetation -- is the fastest-growing land use type in the contiguous United States:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Homes in WUI areas | ~45 million (39% of all U.S. homes) |
| WUI land area growth (1990-2020) | 33% increase |
| New homes built in WUI (2010-2020) | 2.6 million |
| Share of building losses from wildfire occurring in WUI | 86-97% |
| Share of wildfires occurring in WUI | 33% |

Although the WUI covers less than 10% of U.S. land, it accounts for roughly one-third of all wildfires and the vast majority of structure losses. Population growth and suburban expansion continue to push more homes into fire-prone areas.

[source: PNAS - Rapid Growth of the US WUI](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1718850115) | [source: USFA/FEMA - Wildland Urban Interface](https://www.usfa.fema.gov/wui/)

### [+] Wildfire Smoke: A National Health Crisis

Wildfire smoke is not a local problem. Smoke from the 2023 Canadian wildfires blanketed the eastern United States for weeks, reaching as far as Europe. Fine particulate matter (PM2.5) from wildfire smoke is especially dangerous because it penetrates deep into the lungs and enters the bloodstream.

- **Current impact:** An estimated 40,000 Americans die annually from wildfire smoke exposure (2011-2020 average)
- **Projected impact:** Under current emissions trends, smoke-related deaths could reach 70,000 per year by 2050
- **Even with aggressive climate action** (below 2 degrees C warming), deaths would still exceed 60,000/year by 2050
- **Delayed mortality:** Smoke exposure increases death risk for up to three years after the initial event
- **Respiratory effects:** Each 1 ug/m3 increase in 3-day average fire smoke PM2.5 increases asthma risk by 8%

Children, elderly, pregnant women, and people with heart or lung conditions are most vulnerable.

[source: Nature - Long-Range PM2.5 from Canadian Wildfires](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09482-1) | [source: ScienceDaily - Wildfire Smoke Deaths Could Reach 70,000/Year](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2025/09/250918225016.htm) | [source: PNAS - Wildfire Smoke PM2.5 and Mortality](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2403960121)

### [+] Budget Cuts to Fire Management

The FY2026 budget proposes devastating cuts to wildfire preparedness and response:

**U.S. Forest Service:**

| Category | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| Overall USFS budget cut | 65% |
| Proposed staffing reduction | 26% (~10,000 employees) |
| Employees who already left via buyouts/departures (2025) | ~5,000 |
| Prescribed burn and fuel treatment rate (2025) | Less than half of prior 4-year average |
| State Fire Capacity grants | Eliminated ($76 million) |
| Volunteer Fire Capacity grants | Eliminated ($21 million) |
| State, Private and Tribal Forestry account | Eliminated ($283 million) |
| Forest and Rangeland Research account | Eliminated ($300 million) |

The Forest Service treated less than 1.7 million acres in the first nine months of 2025, compared to an average of 3.6 million acres per year over the prior four years. Fewer staff means fewer prescribed burns, less thinning, and more fuel accumulation -- setting up larger, more destructive fires.

**FEMA Disaster Response:**

| Category | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| FEMA permanent staff reduction | 15% |
| Disaster response staff reduction | 41% |
| Surge workforce reduction (teams deployed after major disasters) | 85% |
| Overall staff reduction | More than 50% |
| Employees who departed in 2025 | 2,000+ |
| Pre-existing staffing shortage (GAO 2023) | 6,000+ positions unfilled |

### [+] The Prescribed Fire Gap

Forest scientists overwhelmingly agree that prescribed burns -- intentionally set, carefully controlled fires -- are the most effective tool for reducing catastrophic wildfire risk. Fire has been a natural part of western forest ecosystems for millennia, and decades of fire suppression have allowed dangerous fuel loads to accumulate.

Yet prescribed burning has slowed dramatically:
- USFS prescribed burn rate dropped by more than 50% in 2025 due to staffing losses
- The 10-for-1 deregulation mandate (EO 14192) creates additional barriers to new burn plans
- Liability concerns discourage private landowners and state agencies from conducting burns
- Air quality regulations sometimes prevent burns on the best weather days

The result: forests grow denser, fuel loads increase, and when fire inevitably arrives, it burns hotter and more destructively.

[source: Stocktonia News - USFS Falling Behind on Wildfire Prevention](https://stocktonia.org/news/environment/2025/12/10/u-s-forest-service-wildfire-prevention-trump-cuts/)

## Analysis

### What This Means

- The combination of longer fire seasons, more homes in the WUI, reduced fuel treatment, and slashed response capacity is a formula for increasingly catastrophic fire disasters
- FEMA's proposed 85% surge workforce cut means fewer federal responders when the next major fire hits -- and most states lack the capacity to fill that gap
- Eliminating Forest Service research funding undermines the science needed to adapt to changing fire conditions
- Wildfire smoke is becoming a national public health emergency affecting hundreds of millions, not just those near the flames
- The climate-fire feedback loop: fires release stored carbon, which warms the climate, which creates conditions for more fire

### Who's Affected

- **45 million households in the WUI:** Directly at risk from wildfire and increasingly uninsurable
- **Firefighters:** Facing longer seasons, larger fires, and workforce cuts. Federal wildland firefighter pay remains below livable wages in many areas.
- **Rural communities:** Dependent on federal fire response and Forest Service grants that are being eliminated
- **Everyone who breathes:** Wildfire smoke travels thousands of miles. The 2023 Canadian fire smoke affected air quality from New York to Miami to London.
- **Homeowners and renters:** Insurance companies are pulling out of fire-prone areas, leaving millions without coverage
- **Agricultural communities:** Smoke damage reduces crop yields and livestock health

### Create Defensible Space Around Your Home (This Month)

If you live in or near a fire-prone area, defensible space dramatically improves your home's chances of surviving a wildfire -- even without firefighter intervention.

**Zone 1 (0-5 feet from home):**
- Use noncombustible materials (gravel, stone, concrete) directly against the house
- Remove dead leaves from gutters, roof, and deck
- Screen vents with 1/8-inch metal mesh to block embers
- Move firewood, propane tanks, and flammable materials away from the house

**Zone 2 (5-30 feet):**
- Space trees at least 10 feet apart (crown to crown)
- Remove dead branches within 6 feet of the ground
- Keep lawn mowed and irrigated
- Choose fire-resistant plants (low-growing, moist, deciduous)

**Zone 3 (30-100 feet):**
- Thin trees to reduce density
- Remove dead wood and brush
- Create fuel breaks using driveways, gravel paths, or stone walls
- Clear vegetation along property access roads for emergency vehicles

**Full guide:** [Firewise USA Defensible Space](https://www.nfpa.org/education-and-research/wildfire/firewise-usa) | [Ready for Wildfire](https://www.readyforwildfire.org/prepare-for-wildfire/get-ready/defensible-space/)

### Sign Up for Air Quality Alerts (5 minutes)

- **AirNow** ([airnow.gov](https://www.airnow.gov)) -- Set up free email/text alerts for unhealthy air in your zip code
- **Fire and Smoke Map** ([fire.airnow.gov](https://fire.airnow.gov)) -- Real-time wildfire smoke tracking
- **PurpleAir** ([map.purpleair.com](https://map.purpleair.com)) -- Community air sensors with real-time PM2.5 readings
- When AQI exceeds 100, limit outdoor activity. Above 150, stay indoors with windows closed.

### Get an Air Purifier with HEPA Filter

During smoke events, a HEPA air purifier in your sleeping area can significantly reduce indoor PM2.5 exposure:
- Look for purifiers rated for your room size with **true HEPA** filters (not "HEPA-like")
- Run on high during smoke events, especially overnight
- **DIY option:** Tape a MERV-13 furnace filter to a box fan (~$30 total) -- research shows this reduces indoor PM2.5 by 40-60%
- Replace HVAC filters with MERV-13 or higher during fire season
- Keep windows and doors closed; set HVAC to recirculate (not fresh air intake)

### Support Your Local Community Wildfire Protection Plan

Community Wildfire Protection Plans (CWPPs) are locally developed plans that identify fire risks and prioritize mitigation projects. Having a CWPP qualifies your community for federal grant programs including Community Wildfire Defense Grants and FEMA BRIC funding.

- **Check if your community has a CWPP:** [Fire Adapted Communities CWPP Database](https://fireadapted.org/cwpp-database/)
- **If no plan exists:** Contact your local fire department about starting one
- **If a plan exists:** Attend meetings, volunteer for fuel reduction projects, participate in updates
- **CWPP development guide:** [USFA - Creating a CWPP](https://www.usfa.fema.gov/blog/how-to-create-a-community-wildfire-protection-plan/)

### Volunteer for Local Fire Departments

Volunteer firefighters make up approximately 65% of the U.S. fire service, and many departments are critically short-staffed:
- Contact your local volunteer fire department to ask about joining
- Many departments train volunteers at no cost -- typical programs run 60-100 hours
- Even if you cannot fight fires, departments need volunteers for logistics, administration, public education, and support roles
- Find your local department through the [USFA Fire Department Registry](https://apps.usfa.fema.gov/registry/)

## Sustained Actions

### Join the Firewise USA Program

[Firewise USA](https://portal.firewise.org) is a national program that helps communities organize to reduce wildfire risk:
1. Form a committee of residents and local fire stakeholders
2. Conduct a community risk assessment
3. Develop a multi-year action plan for fuel reduction
4. Organize annual community work days (chipping, clearing, defensible space)
5. Some insurance companies offer discounts to residents in Firewise-recognized communities

Over 2,000 communities are already Firewise-recognized. If yours is not, start the process at [portal.firewise.org](https://portal.firewise.org).

### Support Prescribed Fire Organizations

- **Prescribed Fire Training Exchanges (TREX)** -- The Nature Conservancy runs training programs where volunteers learn prescribed fire skills alongside professionals. Find events at [nature.org](https://www.nature.org/en-us/what-we-do/our-priorities/tackle-climate-change/climate-change-stories/prescribed-fire/)
- **Coalition of Prescribed Fire Councils** ([prescribedfire.net](https://www.prescribedfire.net)) -- Network of state-level prescribed fire councils advocating for good fire policy
- **Your state prescribed fire council** -- Most western and southern states have active councils accepting volunteers
- Advocate for **prescribed fire liability reform** at the state level -- liability concerns are a major barrier to conducting burns

### Engage Local Government

- Push your city or county to adopt **WUI building codes** requiring fire-resistant roofing, vent screening, and defensible space for new construction
- Advocate for **land-use planning** that limits new development in high-risk fire zones
- Support local **fuel reduction programs** and community chipping services
- Attend county planning meetings when new WUI developments are proposed
- Push for local **smoke readiness plans** -- clean air shelters, public HEPA filtration, and emergency notification systems

### Emergency Preparedness

**Build a wildfire go-bag:**
- Important documents (copies or digital)
- Medications (1-week supply)
- N95 masks (for smoke)
- Phone chargers and battery packs
- Water and non-perishable food (3 days)
- Cash
- Change of clothes
- Pet supplies

**Create an evacuation plan:**
- Know at least two exit routes from your neighborhood
- Sign up for local emergency alerts (county sheriff or emergency management)
- Establish a meeting point for family members
- Practice the plan annually
- Do not wait for an official evacuation order if conditions feel dangerous -- leave early
