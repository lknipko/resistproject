# The ACA Marketplace Crisis

## Quick Summary

The enhanced premium tax credits that made Affordable Care Act marketplace plans affordable for millions **expired at the end of 2025** and were not renewed. As a result, **2026 premiums roughly doubled** for subsidized enrollees, and marketplace enrollment fell from **22.1 million to 19.2 million** — a drop of nearly 3 million people. A bipartisan Senate compromise to restore some subsidies has stalled. The lapse compounds other 2026 health-coverage shocks — Medicaid work requirements, rural hospital closures, and changes to preventive-care coverage — squeezing households from multiple directions at once.

## Facts

### Key Points

- Enhanced premium tax credits (first enacted in 2021 and extended in 2022) **lapsed December 31, 2025**
- Subsidized enrollees saw out-of-pocket premiums **roughly double** for 2026 coverage
- Marketplace enrollment fell from **22.1M to 19.2M** as higher costs pushed people off coverage
- A Collins–Moreno compromise to partially restore subsidies has not advanced in the Senate

## Analysis

### What This Means

The ACA marketplace is the main coverage option for people who don't get insurance through work or public programs — including gig workers, early retirees, and small-business owners. Doubling net premiums prices out the healthiest enrollees first, which can push remaining premiums higher still.

### Who's Affected

- **Self-employed and gig workers** without employer coverage
- **Older adults not yet Medicare-eligible**, who face the steepest premium increases
- **Rural communities**, where marketplace instability compounds hospital closures
