# AI Regulation: The Fight for Accountability

## Quick Summary

Artificial intelligence is being deployed at scale in hiring, policing, healthcare, and elections — with minimal federal oversight. The Trump administration signed **Executive Order 14365** (December 2025) creating a DOJ task force to **sue states** that regulate AI, and released a **National AI Policy Framework** (March 2026) pushing Congress to preempt state laws entirely. Sen. Blackburn's 291-page **TRUMP AMERICA AI Act** is the most comprehensive federal AI bill ever proposed. Meanwhile, states are leading: **Colorado's AI Act** takes effect June 30, 2026; **Illinois HB 3773** is already in effect; **NYC Local Law 144** has required AI hiring audits since 2023. In *Mobley v. Workday*, a federal court authorized a **class action** against AI hiring discrimination (February 2026). **31 states** now have deepfake election laws — the 2026 midterms are the first cycle with AI deepfakes at "industrial scale." Seven federal agencies use **facial recognition** with multiple documented wrongful arrests. The core battle: the administration wants to strip states of the power to protect citizens from AI harms.

### Key Points

- **Executive Order 14365 (December 11, 2025):** Trump signed an order creating a **DOJ AI Litigation Task Force** to challenge state AI laws in federal court. Directs the FTC to classify state-mandated AI bias mitigation as a "per se deceptive trade practice." Evaluates conditioning federal funding on state compliance with federal AI policy. [source: EO 14365 — Full Text](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/) | [source: EO 14365 — Fact Sheet](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/12/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-ensures-a-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence/)

- **National AI Policy Framework (March 20, 2026):** White House released a four-page blueprint calling for a unified federal AI standard and **preemption of state laws**. Addresses child protection, intellectual property, free speech, innovation, and workforce. Would preclude states from regulating AI model development or imposing liability on developers for third-party misuse. Non-binding but signals legislative direction. [source: White House — National AI Framework](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/)

- **TRUMP AMERICA AI Act (March 2026):** Sen. Blackburn released a 291-page discussion draft — the most comprehensive federal AI bill ever proposed. Key provisions: federal preemption of state AI laws, duty of care on developers, ban on AI companion chatbots for minors, declares unauthorized AI training on copyrighted works is NOT fair use, Section 230 sunset, anti-censorship audits, quarterly workforce reporting. [source: Sen. Blackburn — TRUMP AMERICA AI Act](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/3/technology/blackburn-releases-discussion-draft-of-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence/3b3b6458-b6c7-478b-9859-374949586765)

- **AI Civil Rights Act (S.3308 / H.R.6356):** Introduced by Sen. Markey and Rep. Clarke. Requires pre-deployment assessments, independent audits, and community consultation. Mandates "reasonable measures" to prevent algorithmic harm. Does NOT preempt stronger state laws. [source: S.3308 — Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/3308)

- **GUARDRAILS Act (March 20, 2026):** Introduced by Rep. Beyer with ~30 House cosponsors. Would **repeal** EO 14365 to preserve state authority over AI regulation. Senate companion by Sen. Schatz. [source: Rep. Beyer — GUARDRAILS Act](https://beyer.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=9009)

The Senate voted **99-1** to strip it — a rare bipartisan rejection. [source: NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/big-beautiful-bill-ai-moratorium-ted-cruz-pass-vote-rcna215111)

- **Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205) takes effect June 30, 2026.** Requires developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems to use "reasonable care" to prevent algorithmic discrimination. Annual impact assessments, risk management policies, and consumer disclosure of AI-driven adverse decisions. Attorney general has exclusive enforcement authority. [source: Colorado SB 24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205)

Prohibits using zip codes as a proxy for race. Requires notice to employees and applicants. [source: Seyfarth Shaw — Illinois AI Law](https://www.seyfarth.com/news-insights/legal-update-new-illinois-ai-law-requires-employee-notice-affirms-existing-employer-nondiscrimination-duties.html)

Penalties: $500-$1,500 per violation. NYC Comptroller found enforcement currently ineffective — DCWP committed to reforms. [source: NYC Rules — Local Law 144](https://rules.cityofnewyork.us/rule/automated-employment-decision-tools-updated/)

California's deepfake law was struck down as too broad. [source: Public Citizen — Deepfake Legislation Tracker](https://www.citizen.org/article/tracker-legislation-on-deepfakes-in-elections/)

### [+] Federal Legislation

**Executive Order 14365 — "Eliminating State Law Obstruction of National AI Policy" (December 11, 2025)**

The EO establishes federal policy to "sustain and enhance the United States' global AI dominance through a minimally burdensome national policy framework." Key directives:

- **DOJ AI Litigation Task Force** (effective January 10, 2026): Authorized to challenge state AI laws in federal court
- **FTC directive**: Issue a policy statement by March 11, 2026 classifying state-mandated AI bias mitigation as a "per se deceptive trade practice"
- **FCC directive**: Consider a federal reporting and disclosure standard for AI models
- **Funding leverage**: Evaluate conditioning federal funding to states on AI policy compliance

[source: EO 14365](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/)

**National AI Policy Framework (March 20, 2026)**

A four-page White House blueprint with legislative recommendations to Congress:
- Unified federal AI standard with preemption of state laws
- States retain power over generally applicable laws, zoning, and government's own use of AI
- Carve-out for state regulation of AI in law enforcement and public services
- Non-binding — does not impose legal obligations on its own

[source: White House — AI Framework](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/)

**TRUMP AMERICA AI Act (Senator Blackburn, March 2026)**

291-page discussion draft — the most comprehensive federal AI bill proposed:
- Federal preemption of state AI laws ("one rulebook")
- Duty of care on AI developers to prevent foreseeable harm
- Bans AI companion chatbots for minors; safeguards for users under 17
- Declares unauthorized AI training on copyrighted works is NOT fair use
- Section 230 sunset (Communications Decency Act liability shield)
- Anti-censorship audits (the "4 Cs" framework: children, creators, conservatives, communities)
- Quarterly workforce reporting on AI-related job effects

[source: Sen. Blackburn](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/3/technology/blackburn-releases-discussion-draft-of-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence/3b3b6458-b6c7-478b-9859-374949586765)

**AI Civil Rights Act (S.3308 / H.R.6356)**

Introduced December 2, 2025 by Sen. Markey (D-MA) and Rep. Clarke (D-NY):
- Pre-deployment impact assessments for high-risk AI systems
- Independent audits and community consultation
- "Reasonable measures" to prevent algorithmic harm
- Does NOT preempt stronger state laws
- Approximately 30 cosponsors in the House

[source: S.3308](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/3308) | [source: CRS AI Report](https://www.congress.gov/crs_external_products/R/PDF/R48555/R48555.2.pdf)

**GUARDRAILS Act (March 20, 2026)**

Introduced by Rep. Beyer (D-VA) with Reps. Matsui, Lieu, Jacobs, and McClain Delaney. Senate companion by Sen. Schatz (D-HI). Would repeal EO 14365 to preserve state authority over AI regulation. ~30 House cosponsors.

[source: Rep. Beyer](https://beyer.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=9009)

### [+] State AI Laws

**Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205) — Effective June 30, 2026**
- Applies to developers and deployers of "high-risk" AI systems
- Requires "reasonable care" to protect consumers from algorithmic discrimination
- Deployers must: implement risk management policy, complete annual impact assessment, disclose AI-driven adverse decisions
- Attorney general has exclusive enforcement authority; violations are unfair trade practices
- Affirmative defense available if violations discovered and cured internally

[source: Colorado SB 24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205)

**Illinois HB 3773 — Effective January 1, 2026**
- Amends the Illinois Human Rights Act
- Prohibits employer use of AI that discriminates against protected classes
- Prohibits use of zip codes as proxy for protected characteristics
- Requires notice to employees and applicants when AI is used in employment decisions
- Builds on Illinois's existing Artificial Intelligence Video Interview Act (AIVIA, 2020)

**NYC Local Law 144 — In Effect Since July 2023**
- Annual independent race/gender bias audits of AI hiring tools
- Public disclosure of audit results
- Advance notice to job candidates
- Penalties: $500-$1,500 per violation
- NYC Comptroller found enforcement currently ineffective (misrouted complaints, superficial reviews)

[source: NYC Rules](https://rules.cityofnewyork.us/rule/automated-employment-decision-tools-updated/)

**California (Multiple Laws)**
- **AB 2013** (GAI Training Data Transparency Act) — effective January 1, 2026
- **SB 942** (California AI Transparency Act) — requires detection tools and watermarks; effective August 2, 2026
- **SB 53** (Transparency in Frontier AI Act) — safety protocols and incident reporting for large models
- **AB 325** (Preventing Algorithmic Price Fixing Act) — bars common pricing algorithms; effective January 1, 2026

### [+] AI and Hiring Discrimination

**Mobley v. Workday (N.D. Cal., February 2026)**

A federal court authorized class notice for claims that Workday's AI screening software unlawfully filtered out applicants based on age, race, and disability. This is the most-watched AI hiring case of 2026, testing whether AI vendors can be held liable for discriminatory outcomes in hiring algorithms used by their clients.

**HireVue Complaint (March 2025)**

Civil rights groups filed against Intuit and HireVue over an AI assessment system that penalized an Indigenous and Deaf woman for her speech patterns, alleging violations of the ADA, Title VII, and Colorado anti-discrimination law.

**EEOC Enforcement (2026)**

The EEOC's 2026 enforcement focus is on **disparate impact theory**: employers are liable if AI hiring tools produce substantially lower selection rates for protected groups, even without discriminatory intent. The agency has signaled it will pursue cases where AI screening has a measurable adverse effect on protected classes.

### [+] AI Deepfakes and Elections

**Federal Level:**
- No comprehensive federal statute specifically regulates AI-generated political deepfakes
- The **Take It Down Act** (2025) requires platforms to remove AI-generated non-consensual sexual content
- Proposed Federal Rule of Evidence 707 would regulate admissibility of AI-generated evidence in courts

**State Level:**
- **31 states** now have laws regulating deepfakes in elections (up from 28 in 2025)
- Most require disclosure/labeling of AI-generated political content
- Montana, South Dakota, Maine, Tennessee, and Vermont passed new laws in 2026
- California's deepfake law was struck down by a federal judge as too broad and content-discriminatory

**2026 Midterms:**
- First election cycle where political deepfakes are deployed "at industrial scale"
- AI-generated robocalls, synthetic candidate images, and manipulated video already documented in primary campaigns
- Election officials lack standardized detection or response tools

[source: Public Citizen — Deepfake Tracker](https://www.citizen.org/article/tracker-legislation-on-deepfakes-in-elections/)

### [+] AI Surveillance and Policing

**Facial Recognition:**
- Seven federal law enforcement agencies (within DOJ and DHS) use facial recognition
- As of 2023, only 3 of 7 had specific policies governing its use (GAO report)

**Documented Wrongful Arrests:**
- Texas man wrongly arrested via Macy's facial recognition
- Three wrongful arrests by Detroit Police — all victims were Black
- Black man in Georgia jailed nearly a week based on facial recognition error
- Colorado woman held at gunpoint with her children due to an automated license plate reader error

**Circumvention Tools:**
- Veritone's "Track" tool uses body attributes (not faces) to track people, effectively circumventing facial recognition bans
- Used by 400+ customers including police departments

**City-Level Bans:**
- **San Francisco:** Ban on facial recognition by city agencies
- **Boston:** Prohibition on facial recognition by city departments
- **Portland, OR:** Ban on facial recognition by city AND private entities
- **Somerville, MA:** Complete ban on facial recognition
- **Santa Cruz, CA:** Ban on predictive policing tools (since 2020)

[source: GAO — Facial Recognition](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-24-107372) | [source: NAACP — Predictive Policing Brief](https://naacp.org/resources/artificial-intelligence-predictive-policing-issue-brief)

### [+] AI in Healthcare

**FDA 2026 Guidance Updates:**
- FDA loosened oversight for certain AI-enabled digital health products (January 2026)
- Clinical Decision Support (CDS): enforcement discretion for tools providing a single clinically appropriate recommendation, if clinician can independently review logic
- Expanded "general wellness" policy for non-invasive consumer wearables
- Greater emphasis on transparency: labeling must include performance measures and known bias sources
- Quality Management System updates aligning with ISO 13485:2016

**Key Concern:** AI diagnostic tools are being deployed in hospitals and clinics with varying levels of bias testing. Studies have documented AI systems performing worse for patients of color, women, and older adults. Without robust federal standards, patients may not know when AI is involved in their care decisions.

## Analysis

### What This Means

The fight over AI regulation is fundamentally a fight over who protects citizens from algorithmic harm. States like Colorado, Illinois, and California have passed laws requiring transparency, audits, and accountability when AI systems make consequential decisions about people's lives — hiring, healthcare, housing, policing. The Trump administration's EO 14365 and National AI Framework would **strip those protections** by preempting state laws in favor of a weaker federal standard designed to minimize burden on AI companies.

If federal preemption succeeds, the Colorado AI Act's anti-discrimination requirements would be nullified before they take effect. Illinois's hiring protections would be overridden. NYC's audit requirements could be challenged. The practical effect: AI companies would face one national standard — written with industry input and designed for "minimal burden" — instead of the stronger accountability requirements states have enacted.

The 99-1 Senate vote to strip the 10-year AI moratorium from the reconciliation bill shows bipartisan resistance to the most extreme preemption proposals. But the administration is pursuing preemption through executive action (EO 14365) and legislative proposals (TRUMP AMERICA AI Act) simultaneously.

### Who's Affected

- **Job seekers:** AI screens out millions of applicants annually based on opaque criteria. The *Workday* class action shows these systems can discriminate by age, race, and disability at scale. Without state laws like Illinois HB 3773, applicants may never know AI was involved.
- **Communities of color:** Facial recognition has a documented higher error rate for Black faces. Predictive policing tools disproportionately target Black and Latino neighborhoods. AI hiring tools have been shown to penalize speech patterns associated with non-white applicants.
- **Patients:** AI diagnostic tools are being deployed in hospitals with varying bias testing. Without transparency requirements, patients may not know when AI is involved in their care decisions — or that the tool performs worse for their demographic.
- **Voters:** With no federal deepfake law and the 2026 midterms approaching, AI-generated disinformation can flood the information space. 31 states have some protections; 19 have none.
- **Workers:** AI is increasingly used in performance monitoring, scheduling, and termination decisions. The TRUMP AMERICA AI Act's quarterly reporting requirement would surface AI-driven job displacement — but federal preemption would eliminate stronger state protections for workers.
- **Everyone:** The precedent being set — that the federal government will actively sue states that try to protect their citizens from AI harm — has implications far beyond AI regulation.
