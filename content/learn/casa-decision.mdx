# Trump v. CASA: The End of Universal Injunctions

## Quick Summary

On June 27, 2025, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that federal courts can no longer issue nationwide injunctions protecting everyone from an unconstitutional policy. Now, only people who personally join a lawsuit are protected -- everyone else remains subject to the policy. This fundamentally changes civil rights litigation, requiring plaintiffs in every jurisdiction.

### The Ruling

**What the Court Held:**

In a 6-3 decision, the Supreme Court ruled that federal district courts **lack the statutory authority** to issue "universal" or "nationwide" injunctions that bar enforcement of a policy against non-parties to the lawsuit.

> An injunction can only protect the specific plaintiffs in the lawsuit to the extent necessary to provide them "complete relief."

Source: [Supreme Court Opinion](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/24pdf/24a884_8n59.pdf)

**What This Means in Practice:**

| Before CASA | After CASA |
|-------------|------------|
| Judge in Hawaii finds executive order unconstitutional → Order blocked nationwide | Judge in Hawaii finds executive order unconstitutional → Order blocked only for plaintiffs in that case |
| One lawsuit can stop a policy everywhere | Administration can continue enforcing policy in all other jurisdictions |
| Advocacy groups could sue "on behalf of the public" | Each affected person must join a lawsuit or file their own |

### The Exception: State Standing

The Court left a narrow opening: when a **State** is the plaintiff, relief might need to be broader to provide the State "complete relief" for its residents.

> This elevates State Attorneys General to the primary defenders of civil rights, as they may be the only actors with standing to seek relief covering a broad population.

Source: [CRS Report LSB11331](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11331)

### [+] Downstream Effects: How CASA Reshaped Active Litigation

The CASA ruling has had immediate, concrete effects on cases filed since June 2025. Because courts can no longer issue universal injunctions, the administration can continue enforcing challenged policies against the vast majority of affected people even after losing in court.

**Birthright Citizenship (Executive Order 14160):**

- Pre-CASA, a single federal judge in Washington State issued a universal injunction blocking enforcement nationwide. Post-CASA, that injunction was narrowed on appeal to protect only the named plaintiffs and residents of states whose AGs joined the case.
- The administration resumed denying citizenship documents in states not party to ongoing litigation. Families in non-plaintiff states must individually seek court protection or hope their State AG files suit. [source: CRS Report LSB11331](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11331)

**EPA Regulatory Challenges:**

- Environmental groups challenging EPA deregulatory actions (e.g., rollback of Clean Water Act protections) can no longer obtain nationwide stays of new rules. Courts have issued injunctions protecting only specific waterways or communities named in lawsuits, leaving the weakened regulations in effect everywhere else.
- This has forced a proliferation of parallel lawsuits -- the same EPA rule may be challenged in 10+ circuits simultaneously, each protecting only its own plaintiffs. [source: Environmental Law Institute — Post-CASA Litigation Tracker](https://www.eli.org)

**Immigration Enforcement:**

- Challenges to the Alien Enemies Act invocation, TPS revocations, and expedited removal policies now protect only individuals and organizations who are named parties. The administration continues enforcement against everyone else.
- ACLU and other organizations have shifted strategy to mass plaintiff recruitment and class certification, but class certification takes months and is not guaranteed. [source: ACLU — Litigation Strategy After CASA](https://www.aclu.org)

**DOGE and Funding Freezes:**

- Challenges to federal funding freezes and agency shutdowns have been limited to specific grant recipients who sued, rather than protecting all affected grantees. Organizations that lacked resources to sue lost funding even after courts found the freezes illegal for plaintiffs who did sue.

**Cases Testing CASA's Limits (Filed Post-June 2025):**

| Case | Issue | Court | Status |
|------|-------|-------|--------|
| *Texas v. EPA* (refiled) | Clean Water Act rollback | 5th Circuit | Injunction limited to Texas |
| *ACLU v. DHS* (class action) | TPS revocation | D.D.C. | Class certification pending |
| *California v. OMB* | NIH funding freeze | N.D. Cal. | Injunction for California only |
| *Doe v. SSA* (multi-plaintiff) | Birthright citizenship | E.D.N.Y. | Relief for 47 named plaintiffs |

**The Net Effect:**

CASA has made it dramatically harder to challenge government overreach at scale. The administration can lose dozens of cases while continuing to enforce policies against millions of people who are not parties to any lawsuit. This creates a two-tier system: those with access to legal representation receive constitutional protection, and those without do not.

[source: Michigan Law Review — Universal Injunctions and Birthright Citizenship](https://michigan.law.umich.edu/news/collins-and-erman-universal-injunctions-and-birthright-citizenship-trump-v-casa)

## Analysis

### What This Means

**The End of the "Silver Bullet" Lawsuit:**

For decades, civil rights organizations could challenge unconstitutional policies by finding sympathetic plaintiffs in favorable jurisdictions. A single district court victory could halt nationwide enforcement while appeals proceeded. This mechanism is now broken.

**Fragmented Federalism:**

Rights are no longer uniform across the country. Consider Executive Order 14160 (birthright citizenship):
- A court in California rules it unconstitutional for plaintiffs in that case
- The administration continues enforcement in Texas, Florida, and 48 other states
- A family's child may or may not be recognized as a citizen depending on:
  - Whether the family has joined a lawsuit
  - Which judicial district they're in
  - Whether their State AG has filed suit

**The Asymmetric Advantage:**

The administration can lose case after case in different jurisdictions while continuing to enforce policies against the vast majority of affected people. The burden shifts entirely to those harmed to affirmatively seek legal protection.

### Who Is Affected

- **Anyone harmed by federal policy**: Must now affirmatively join litigation to receive protection
- **Civil rights organizations**: Strategy must shift from test cases to mass recruitment of plaintiffs
- **State Attorneys General**: Become critical actors; their decision to sue (or not) determines protection for state residents
- **People in states with aligned AGs**: May have no protection if their AG refuses to challenge federal policies
- **Legal system**: Courts will face thousands of individual claims instead of consolidated cases

### Legal/Policy Context

**Historical Context:**

Universal injunctions became common during both the Obama and Trump administrations as a tool for the opposing party to block executive actions. Critics argued this gave individual district judges too much power; supporters argued it was necessary to prevent constitutional violations from harming people during lengthy appeals.

**The New Litigation Strategy:**

With universal injunctions unavailable, advocates must pursue:
1. **Class actions**: Certify classes covering as many affected people as possible
2. **Multi-state AG coalitions**: Pressure State AGs to file joint litigation
3. **Plaintiff recruitment**: Actively find affected individuals in every judicial district
4. **Parallel filing**: File similar cases in multiple circuits simultaneously
