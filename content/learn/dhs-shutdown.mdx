# DHS Shutdown: 76 Days Without Funding

## Quick Summary

The longest DHS shutdown in American history ended on **April 30, 2026** after 76 days — and the funding fight is now resolved. On **June 10, 2026, Trump signed the $70 billion immigration-enforcement reconciliation bill** into law (House 214-212 on June 9, Senate 52-47 on June 5), funding ICE and CBP through the end of his term with **few accountability provisions**. Only Sen. Lisa Murkowski (R-AK) broke from Republicans in the Senate. The bill includes **$38.2B for ICE**, **$9.5B for CBP recruitment**, and **$7.5B for ICE recruitment**. The controversial **$1.776 billion "anti-weaponization fund"** — created from a DOJ settlement with Trump over leaked IRS tax returns — was formally abandoned, and later **voided by a federal judge on July 13**. Trump had appointed **Bill Pulte** (no intelligence experience) as acting Director of National Intelligence on June 2. With the money now secured, ICE has tripled its arrest quotas (see [ICE Detention & Deportation](/learn/ice-detention-deportation)).

### [+] What Happened

**The Trigger:**

On January 24, 2026, U.S. citizen Alex Pretti was killed by CBP agents during an immigration enforcement operation in Minneapolis. Pretti was not an enforcement target — he was a 37-year-old registered nurse. This was the second U.S. citizen killed by federal agents in Minneapolis that month (Renee Nicole Good was killed January 7).

The killings intensified an existing dispute over DHS funding. H.R. 7147 — the DHS Appropriations Act for FY2026 — had passed the House on January 22 with $64.4 billion in funding but no accountability amendments. When it reached the Senate, Democrats demanded:

- Mandatory body cameras for ICE and CBP agents
- Use-of-force reporting requirements
- Independent investigations of deaths in custody
- Restoration of sensitive location protections

Republicans refused to add these provisions. Democrats blocked the bill from advancing. When the existing continuing resolution expired on February 14, DHS funding lapsed.

**The Standoff:**

Neither party has been willing to compromise:
- Republicans argue Democrats are playing politics with national security
- Democrats argue that funding enforcement without oversight enables more killings
- The White House has blamed Democrats for "reckless" shutdown behavior
- DHS itself has framed the shutdown as Democrats' fault in official communications

### [+] Deal Negotiations

**Final Resolution (April 30, 2026):**

After 76 days and multiple failed attempts, Congress resolved the shutdown by splitting the difference: fund most of DHS through normal appropriations, but exclude ICE and CBP — the agencies at the center of the accountability dispute. The path to resolution:

**Previous impasse (March 27-28):**

- **Senate (voice vote, 2:20 a.m.):** Passed a bipartisan bill funding all of DHS **except ICE and CBP**, which Democrats have refused to fund without accountability reforms. This would have ended the shutdown for TSA, Coast Guard, FEMA, CISA, and Secret Service [source: NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/27/g-s1-115366/senate-dhs-tsa-deal)
- **House (213-203):** Speaker Johnson rejected the Senate bill and instead passed an 8-week stopgap funding all of DHS (including ICE and CBP) through May 22. Senate Democrats called this "dead on arrival" [source: Roll Call](https://rollcall.com/2026/03/27/house-opts-for-stopgap-funding-as-dhs-standoff-deepens/)
- **Trump TSA directive:** Trump signed a memo directing DHS to issue TSA paychecks "as soon as Monday," though the legal basis for paying employees during a funding lapse is unclear [source: CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/27/politics/live-news/trump-administration-news)

**Previous failed attempts:**
- Multiple short-term continuing resolutions were proposed and rejected
- A bipartisan "skinny" funding bill was floated but collapsed over enforcement provisions
- March 26: Senate vote fails 54-46 (7th failed attempt) — only Fetterman (D-PA) crosses party lines
- Democrats' counteroffer with ICE reform provisions dismissed by Republicans

**Final deal (April 30, 2026):**

The Senate's earlier approach — funding all of DHS except ICE and CBP — ultimately prevailed. The House passed it by voice vote on April 30, and Trump signed it the same day. TSA officers received back pay. All DHS components except ICE and CBP are funded through September 30, 2026.

To fund ICE and CBP, Republicans turned to **budget reconciliation** — a process that requires only a simple majority (no filibuster). The Senate passed the budget resolution 50-48 on April 23, and the House adopted it 215-211 on April 29. Senate committees released a $72 billion reconciliation bill on May 4. This approach means Democrats have no ability to attach accountability provisions to ICE/CBP funding.

## Analysis

### What This Means

The 76-day DHS shutdown illustrated how government funding has become a hostage in political disputes. Essential services — aviation security, disaster response, cybersecurity — were degraded not because of policy disagreement about whether they should exist, but because of a standoff over enforcement accountability after federal agents killed two U.S. citizens.

The shutdown ended, but the underlying dispute did not. By funding ICE and CBP through budget reconciliation — a process requiring only 51 Senate votes — Republicans bypassed the filibuster and eliminated any Democratic leverage over enforcement accountability. This is unprecedented: immigration enforcement has never been funded through reconciliation. The $72 billion reconciliation bill contains no body camera mandates, no use-of-force reporting requirements, and no independent oversight provisions — the exact reforms Democrats demanded as a condition of funding.

The broader precedent is also concerning. If enforcement agencies can be funded through reconciliation whenever normal appropriations stall, the minority party's ability to demand accountability provisions is permanently weakened — regardless of which party holds the majority.

### Who's Affected

- **TSA officers and DHS employees:** Received back pay after the April 30 funding bill; however, the 76-day ordeal caused hundreds of resignations and lasting institutional damage
- **Air travelers:** Spring break 2026 saw the worst TSA delays in history — 4.5+ hour waits — before funding was restored
- **ICE/CBP workforce:** Still operating without normal appropriations; funded through emergency measures while reconciliation moves forward
- **Immigrants and communities:** The reconciliation bill's $38.2B for ICE and $22-26B for CBP — with no accountability provisions — funds mass enforcement operations without the oversight Democrats demanded after the Minneapolis killings
- **Election security:** CISA operations were degraded for months ahead of 2026 midterms; full funding restored but damage to partnerships with state/local officials may persist
- **Communities in disaster zones:** FEMA assistance was delayed during the shutdown; now fully funded

### Historical Context

This was the longest DHS shutdown in American history, more than doubling the 35-day government shutdown of 2018-2019 (which affected all of government, not just DHS). The 2018-2019 shutdown was triggered by a dispute over border wall funding. The 2026 shutdown was triggered by accountability concerns after U.S. citizens were killed by federal agents — a fundamentally different dynamic.

The resolution — funding most of DHS through normal appropriations but carving out ICE/CBP for reconciliation — sets a new precedent for how enforcement agencies can be funded without bipartisan agreement on oversight.
