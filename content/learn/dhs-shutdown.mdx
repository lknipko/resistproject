# DHS Partial Government Shutdown

## Quick Summary

The Department of Homeland Security has been partially shut down since **February 14, 2026** — now **42 days** — due to a congressional standoff over immigration enforcement. TSA officers are working without pay, hundreds have quit, and airport security wait times have hit **4.5+ hours** during spring break travel. Essential DHS staff — including TSA, CBP, Coast Guard, FEMA, and CISA — are working without full compensation. On March 27, the Senate passed a partial funding bill by voice vote that would fund all of DHS **except ICE and CBP** — but the House rejected it hours later (213-203), passing its own rival 8-week stopgap that Senate Democrats called "dead on arrival." Trump signed a TSA directive that could allow paychecks as soon as Monday. For background on the Minneapolis killings that triggered this shutdown, see [Federal Law Enforcement Escalation](/learn/federal-law-enforcement).

## Facts

### Key Points

- **DHS has been unfunded since February 14, 2026** — the shutdown was triggered by a congressional standoff over immigration enforcement funding and accountability in the wake of two U.S. citizens killed by federal agents in Minneapolis: Renee Nicole Good (January 7) and Alex Pretti (January 24). For details on the killings, see [Federal Law Enforcement Escalation: The Minneapolis Killings](/learn/federal-law-enforcement). - **TSA officers are working without pay.** Hundreds have quit, creating staffing shortages at airports nationwide. Wait times at security checkpoints have ballooned, particularly during spring break travel season [source: DHS.gov — Spring Break Under Siege](https://www.dhs.gov/news/2026/03/17/spring-break-under-siege-democrats-reckless-dhs-shutdown-forcing-tsa-officers-work)

- **Essential DHS staff continue working without full pay**, including TSA, Customs and Border Protection (CBP), Coast Guard, FEMA, and the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) [source: SSA.gov — Shutdown Guidance](https://www.ssa.gov/news/en/advocates/2026-02-02.html)

- **No deal despite multiple attempts.** On March 26, the Senate's 7th attempt to advance full DHS funding failed 54-46, short of the 60-vote threshold — only Democrat John Fetterman (PA) voted with Republicans. On March 27, the Senate passed a partial funding bill by voice vote that would fund all of DHS **except ICE and CBP**, but the House rejected it hours later in a 213-203 vote, passing its own 8-week stopgap instead. Senate Democrats called the House bill "dead on arrival." - **This is one of the longest partial government shutdowns** in recent history, exceeding the 2018-2019 DHS funding standoff in severity of impact on airport operations

### [+] What Happened

**The Trigger:**

On January 24, 2026, U.S. citizen Alex Pretti was killed by CBP agents during an immigration enforcement operation in Minneapolis. Pretti was not an enforcement target — he was a 37-year-old registered nurse. This was the second U.S. citizen killed by federal agents in Minneapolis that month (Renee Nicole Good was killed January 7).

The killings intensified an existing dispute over DHS funding. H.R. 7147 — the DHS Appropriations Act for FY2026 — had passed the House on January 22 with $64.4 billion in funding but no accountability amendments. When it reached the Senate, Democrats demanded:

- Mandatory body cameras for ICE and CBP agents
- Use-of-force reporting requirements
- Independent investigations of deaths in custody
- Restoration of sensitive location protections

Republicans refused to add these provisions. Democrats blocked the bill from advancing. When the existing continuing resolution expired on February 14, DHS funding lapsed.

**The Standoff:**

Neither party has been willing to compromise:
- Republicans argue Democrats are playing politics with national security
- Democrats argue that funding enforcement without oversight enables more killings
- The White House has blamed Democrats for "reckless" shutdown behavior
- DHS itself has framed the shutdown as Democrats' fault in official communications

### [+] Impact on TSA & Air Travel

- **TSA officers working without pay** — paychecks have been missed since the shutdown began
- **Hundreds of TSA officers have resigned** rather than continue working unpaid
- **Airport security wait times hit 4.5+ hours** at some airports — the worst delays in TSA history
- **Spring break travel** (March 2026) has been severely disrupted, affecting millions of travelers
- **Some smaller airports** have reduced hours or TSA staffing to skeleton crews
- TSA leadership has warned that continued staffing losses could compromise aviation security

[source: DHS.gov — Spring Break Under Siege](https://www.dhs.gov/news/2026/03/17/spring-break-under-siege-democrats-reckless-dhs-shutdown-forcing-tsa-officers-work)

### [+] Impact on Other DHS Components

**Customs and Border Protection (CBP):**
- Border agents continue working without pay
- Processing capacity at ports of entry reduced
- Irony: the shutdown over immigration enforcement is itself degrading border operations

**Coast Guard:**
- Active-duty members working without pay
- Search and rescue operations continuing but with reduced capacity
- Maritime safety inspections delayed

**FEMA:**
- Disaster response capability degraded
- New disaster declarations cannot be fully processed
- Communities recovering from recent disasters face delays in assistance

**CISA (Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency):**
- Election security support disrupted ahead of 2026 midterms (see [Election Security: Dismantling the Defenses](/learn/election-security) for full analysis)
- Critical infrastructure protection reduced
- Cybersecurity threat monitoring capacity diminished

### [+] Deal Negotiations

**Current Status (as of March 28, 2026):**

Congress remains at an impasse after dueling votes on March 27:

- **Senate (voice vote, 2:20 a.m.):** Passed a bipartisan bill funding all of DHS **except ICE and CBP**, which Democrats have refused to fund without accountability reforms. This would have ended the shutdown for TSA, Coast Guard, FEMA, CISA, and Secret Service [source: NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/27/g-s1-115366/senate-dhs-tsa-deal)
- **House (213-203):** Speaker Johnson rejected the Senate bill and instead passed an 8-week stopgap funding all of DHS (including ICE and CBP) through May 22. Senate Democrats called this "dead on arrival" [source: Roll Call](https://rollcall.com/2026/03/27/house-opts-for-stopgap-funding-as-dhs-standoff-deepens/)
- **Trump TSA directive:** Trump signed a memo directing DHS to issue TSA paychecks "as soon as Monday," though the legal basis for paying employees during a funding lapse is unclear [source: CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/27/politics/live-news/trump-administration-news)

**Previous failed attempts:**
- Multiple short-term continuing resolutions were proposed and rejected
- A bipartisan "skinny" funding bill was floated but collapsed over enforcement provisions
- March 26: Senate vote fails 54-46 (7th failed attempt) — only Fetterman (D-PA) crosses party lines
- Democrats' counteroffer with ICE reform provisions dismissed by Republicans

## Analysis

### What This Means

The DHS shutdown illustrates how government funding has become a hostage in political disputes. Essential services — aviation security, border protection, disaster response, cybersecurity — are being degraded not because of policy disagreement about whether they should exist, but because of a standoff over enforcement accountability.

The human cost falls on federal employees working without pay and on the traveling public facing degraded security and massive delays. The longer the shutdown continues, the more permanent the damage: experienced TSA officers who quit will not return, institutional knowledge leaves with them, and recruitment becomes harder.

### Who's Affected

- **TSA officers and DHS employees:** Working without pay for 6+ weeks; many have families and cannot absorb missed paychecks indefinitely
- **Air travelers:** Severe delays, particularly during spring break — millions affected
- **Communities in disaster zones:** FEMA assistance delayed
- **Election security:** CISA operations degraded ahead of 2026 midterms
- **Border communities:** CBP capacity reduced at ports of entry
- **Maritime safety:** Coast Guard operations at reduced capacity

### Historical Context

This is not the first time DHS has been at the center of a shutdown fight. In 2018-2019, a 35-day government shutdown (the longest in US history) was triggered by a dispute over border wall funding. The pattern of using government shutdowns as leverage over immigration policy has intensified.

Key difference: the 2026 shutdown was triggered specifically by accountability concerns after U.S. citizens were killed — not by a dispute over wall funding or border policy. This represents a new dimension in the shutdown dynamic.
