# Federal Law Enforcement Escalation: The Minneapolis Killings

## Quick Summary

Renee Nicole Good (January 7) and Alex Pretti (January 24) were both killed by federal agents during immigration enforcement operations in Minneapolis. Neither was an enforcement target. Both were U.S. citizens.

    Two days before Pretti's death, the House passed H.R. 7147 providing $64.4 billion in DHS funding while rejecting all oversight amendments.

### [+] The Killings

### Renee Nicole Good (January 7, 2026)

**The Victim:**
- 37-year-old U.S. citizen
- Minneapolis resident

**What Happened:**

According to witnesses and video footage, Renee Nicole Good was shot and killed by an ICE officer while attempting to drive away from a confrontation involving ICE agents.

**Official Account (ICE):**
- Claimed Good "weaponized her vehicle"
- Labeled the incident an act of "domestic terrorism"
- Asserted the officer fired in self-defense

**Disputed Account (Mayor Frey, witnesses):**
- Described agents' use of force as "reckless"
- Disputed the characterization of events
- Called for independent investigation

### Alex Pretti (January 24, 2026)

**The Victim:**
- 37-year-old U.S. citizen
- Registered nurse at the VA Health Care System
- Minneapolis resident

**What Happened:**

Less than three weeks after Good's killing, federal agents shot and killed Alex Pretti during immigration enforcement operations in Minneapolis.

**Circumstances (per video evidence and witness reports):**
- Pretti was attempting to defend a legal observer who had been shoved and sprayed with chemical agents by federal officers
- Pretti was pepper-sprayed and tackled before being shot by an agent
- Pretti was not the target of the enforcement operation

### [+] The Pattern: Suppression of Observers

### Tactics Against Non-Targets

The Minneapolis operations have been characterized by aggressive tactics against people who are not enforcement targets:

**Documented tactics:**
- Use of chemical irritants against observers and protesters
- Pepper spray projectiles
- Physical force against individuals recording operations
- Agents operating unmarked or wearing masks

### Fourth Amendment Concerns

The right of citizens to observe and document police activity in public spaces is protected by the First and Fourth Amendments. These tactics raise questions about:
- The legality of dispersing observers
- Use of force against non-targets
- Destruction of evidence by preventing recording

### [+] The Funding: H.R. 7147

### DHS Appropriations Act, 2026

Two days before Alex Pretti's killing, the House passed H.R. 7147:

| Provision | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Total DHS Funding** | $64.4 billion |
| **ICE Funding** | ~$10 billion |
| **Detention Beds** | Increased funding |
| **Enforcement Operations** | Increased funding |
| **Oversight Amendments** | Rejected |

**Vote:**
- Passed with support from seven House Democrats
- Democratic amendments to add oversight or limit funding were rejected

### Operation Metro Surge: End of Operations

Tom Homan announced the formal end of Operation Metro Surge on February 12, 2026, following the two citizen deaths, sustained protests, and growing congressional pressure. Trump concurred with the announcement. The end of Metro Surge does not affect ongoing enforcement operations nationwide.

### [+] ACLU Class-Action: Racial Profiling (Tincher v. Noem)

On December 17, 2025, the ACLU of Minnesota filed a class-action lawsuit (Tincher et al. v. Noem et al.) alleging constitutional violations by federal agents participating in Operation Metro Surge. A second class-action was filed on January 15, 2026, alleging widespread **racial profiling** -- arrests based solely on ethnic appearance or accent in violation of the Fifth Amendment's Due Process and Equal Protection clauses [source: ACLU of Minnesota](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/aclu-of-minnesota-and-aclu-comment-on-the-trump-administration-announcing-an-end-to-operation-metro-surge)

**Court Findings:**

A federal judge found that ICE and CBP agents likely engaged in **"unconstitutional policies"**, with as many as **23 immigrants** appearing to have been stopped or questioned about their immigration status based solely on their race or ethnicity. However, the judge declined to issue an injunction blocking the practices. [source: CBS Minnesota](https://www.cbsnews.com/minnesota/news/judge-rules-immigration-agents-minnesota-ice-surge-illegally-detained-people-racial-profiling/)

### [+] ACLU First Amendment Lawsuit

The ACLU filed a class action in February 2026 alleging DHS violated the First Amendment rights of protesters, journalists, and legal observers during Minneapolis enforcement operations. The suit accuses agents of arresting non-participants without probable cause and using force against people exercising free speech and press rights.

- **Claims:** Unlawful seizure of legal observers; arrest and detention of journalists without probable cause; use of force against non-participants
- **Plaintiffs:** 14 individuals including witnesses, a church pastor, and a freelance journalist
- **Relief sought:** Injunction against targeting non-participants; individual damages

[source: ACLU — First Amendment Enforcement Lawsuit](https://www.aclu.org)

### Don Lemon Arrest

Journalist and former CNN anchor **Don Lemon** was arrested in Minneapolis and charged alongside eight co-defendants with unlawful assembly and obstruction of a federal enforcement operation. The charges stem from Lemon following a group of protesters into a church and livestreaming the encounter. Lemon has pleaded not guilty. The case is being watched nationally as a test of press freedom limits during immigration operations (see also [Press Freedom](/learn/press-freedom)).

### Federal Pressure on Universities

President Trump threatened to withdraw federal funding from universities that permit what he characterized as "illegal protests" against immigration enforcement. The threat has prompted First Amendment concerns from ACLU, FIRE, and the American Association of University Professors.

### [+] Accountability Barriers

#### State Investigation Blocked by Federal Government

The Minnesota Bureau of Criminal Apprehension (BCA) initially began investigating both killings. However, the FBI informed the BCA that the **U.S. Attorney's Office had reversed course**: the investigation would now be led solely by the FBI, and the **BCA would no longer have access to case materials, scene evidence, or investigative interviews** necessary to complete an independent investigation [source: Minnesota DPS — BCA Statement](https://dps.mn.gov/news/bca/bca-statement-regarding-investigation-ice-fatal-shooting-minneapolis)

This means:
- **No independent state investigation** is possible without federal cooperation
- Federal agents are effectively being investigated only by federal authorities
- State and local officials who witnessed events cannot conduct parallel inquiries
- The decision to block state investigators was made **after** the administration faced political pressure over the killings

#### Structural Accountability Gaps

The Trump administration restructured key oversight agencies in ways that **eliminated avenues for investigation** [source: NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/national-security/demands-grow-ice-accountability-minneapolis-trump-administration-cutba-rcna252875):

| Oversight Body | Status | Impact |
|----------------|--------|--------|
| DHS Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties | Targeted for closure | Investigates excessive force by DHS agents |
| Office of the Immigration Detention Ombudsman | Targeted for closure | Monitors detention conditions and complaints |
| CISA Ombudsman | Targeted for closure | Handled citizen complaints about DHS operations |
| DHS Inspector General | Staff cuts | Reduced capacity for independent audits |

#### *Bivens* Limitations

A critical barrier to justice is the Supreme Court's limitation on *Bivens* actions (lawsuits against federal officials for constitutional violations). Unlike state officers who can be sued under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983, federal agents enjoy **near-total immunity** from civil damages -- and there is no equivalent federal statute for ICE or CBP agents.

Can state officials prosecute federal agents? Legal scholars are divided. The Supremacy Clause creates significant barriers, though some argue state murder charges could potentially apply when federal agents exceed their lawful authority [source: WBEZ](https://www.wbez.org/immigration/2026/01/09/can-state-officials-prosecute-the-feds-calls-grow-after-minneapolis-shooting)

### [+] The DHS Shutdown Connection

The Minneapolis killings directly triggered the DHS partial government shutdown that began February 14, 2026 and continues as of March 28. The sequence:

1. **January 7**: Renee Nicole Good killed by ICE officer
2. **January 22**: House passes H.R. 7147 with no oversight amendments
3. **January 24**: Alex Pretti killed by CBP agents
4. **February 12**: Operation Metro Surge formally ended
5. **February 14**: **DHS shutdown begins** when Democrats refuse to fund DHS without accountability measures including mandatory body cameras, use-of-force reporting, and independent investigations

The shutdown has now lasted **42 days**, leaving TSA officers working without pay, degrading FEMA disaster response, and compromising election security through CISA. For full coverage see [DHS Partial Government Shutdown](/learn/dhs-shutdown).

### [+] Federal Enforcement Trends

The Minneapolis killings occurred in the context of a broader escalation of federal immigration enforcement:

**Shooting Deaths by Federal Immigration Officers:**

Federal immigration officers have been involved in an increasing number of fatal shootings since the expansion of enforcement operations in January 2025 [source: PBS — Shooting Deaths in Mass Deportation Effort](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/a-look-at-shootings-by-federal-immigration-officers):

- Enforcement operations expanded dramatically under executive orders signed January 20, 2025
- Operations shifted from targeting individuals with criminal records to broader community sweeps
- "Sensitive location" policies (protecting churches, schools, hospitals) were rescinded
- Body camera requirements remain inconsistent across ICE and CBP

**ICE Accountability Infrastructure:**

The Brookings Institution documented how ICE's expansion has **outpaced its accountability mechanisms** [source: Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/ice-expansion-has-outpaced-accountability-what-are-the-remedies/):

- ICE's enforcement budget has grown significantly while oversight staffing has been cut
- Use-of-force incident reporting is not standardized across field offices
- Independent review of shootings is not required by policy
- The Minneapolis killings highlighted the absence of mandatory body cameras for ICE officers

## Analysis

### What This Means

**The Accountability Vacuum:**

A critical barrier to justice in these cases is the Supreme Court's limitation on *Bivens* actions (lawsuits against federal officials for constitutional violations).

| State Police | Federal Agents |
|--------------|----------------|
| Can be sued under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 | Near-total immunity from civil damages |
| State/local oversight mechanisms | No equivalent federal statute for ICE |
| Body camera requirements in many jurisdictions | Inconsistent documentation requirements |

Without a specific statute authorizing lawsuits against ICE agents, families of victims have limited legal recourse.

**The Escalation Pattern:**

The two killings within three weeks suggest a shift in rules of engagement:
- Aggressive neutralization of anyone perceived as interfering
- Treating bystanders and observers as threats
- Use of lethal force in situations involving vehicles

**The Political Context:**

The passage of H.R. 7147 two days before Pretti's death—with increased enforcement funding and rejected oversight amendments—signals legislative endorsement of current tactics. The Senate vote on this bill is the next pressure point.

### Who Is Affected

- **U.S. citizens in enforcement zones**: Both victims were American citizens, not enforcement targets
- **Legal observers**: Targeted with chemical agents and force
- **Immigrant communities**: Living under heightened enforcement with militarized tactics
- **Healthcare workers**: Pretti's profession highlights the arbitrary nature of who gets caught in operations
- **Families of victims**: Face near-impossible path to accountability

### The Broader Pattern

These killings occur in the context of multiple reinforcing policy changes:

- **Schedule F** enabling removal of DHS personnel who might resist aggressive tactics (see [Schedule F](/learn/schedule-f))
- ***Trump v. CASA*** limiting judicial ability to issue broad injunctions against enforcement policies (see [CASA Decision](/learn/casa-decision))
- **Increased funding** for enforcement without corresponding oversight -- H.R. 7147 allocated $64.4 billion with no accountability amendments
- **Dismantled oversight**: DHS's Office for Civil Rights and Civil Liberties, the Immigration Detention Ombudsman, and other watchdog bodies have been targeted for closure
- **State investigations blocked**: The FBI took over both Minneapolis investigations and cut off the state BCA from evidence access
- **DHS shutdown**: The resulting standoff has degraded all DHS operations for 42+ days (see [DHS Shutdown](/learn/dhs-shutdown))
- **Federal courts found "unconstitutional policies"** in Operation Metro Surge but declined to issue injunctions, leaving no effective check on enforcement tactics
