# US-Iran Military Conflict

## Quick Summary

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a massive coordinated military campaign against Iran — nearly 900 strikes in the first 12 hours — without a congressional declaration of war or authorization to use military force. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed on the first day. After months of fighting, a **14-point ceasefire was signed at Versailles on June 17** — Trump and Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian, hosted by French President Macron after the G7 summit — but it **collapsed by July 8**, when Trump declared it "over" at the NATO summit in Ankara and the U.S. resumed strikes. Since then the U.S. has hit **Bandar Abbas for six straight nights**, Iran has attacked **five Gulf states** — including its own mediator, Oman — and on **July 12 declared the Strait of Hormuz closed**. Oil jumped **~9% to about $78/barrel** (July 13) as Hormuz tanker traffic fell roughly 60%. Iran's Health Ministry reports **at least 38 killed and 400+ injured** since fighting resumed in July. On **June 24, the Senate passed a War Powers resolution 50-48** directing withdrawal (Cassidy, Collins, Paul, and Murkowski joining Democrats) — the first time both chambers have done so — though it lacks the force of law.

## Facts

### Key Points

- **No congressional authorization:** The strikes were launched without a declaration of war, without an Authorization to Use Military Force (AUMF), and without advance approval from Congress. Top lawmakers were notified only shortly before the first strikes [source: NPR — Iran strikes launched without congressional approval](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/28/nx-s1-5730203/iran-israel-trump-congress-strikes-reaction)
- **Administration cited Article II:** The White House relied on the president's commander-in-chief powers under Article II of the Constitution, the same authority cited for the June 2025 strikes on Iranian nuclear sites [source: CNN — Are Trump's strikes against Iran legal?](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/28/politics/legal-experts-iran-strikes-congress-war-powers)
- **Supreme Leader killed on Day One:** Ali Khamenei was killed in the initial strikes on February 28. His son Mojtaba Khamenei was subsequently named as Iran's new supreme leader [source: CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/live-updates/us-iran-war-israel-strikes-regime-targets/)
- **Congress voted against restraining the war:** The House rejected a War Powers resolution 212–219 on March 4–5. The Senate blocked a similar measure. Both votes fell largely along party lines [source: NPR — House rejects war powers measure](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/04/nx-s1-5735867/war-powers-congress-iran)
- **Trump demands unconditional surrender:** As of March 6, Trump stated "There will be no deal with Iran except UNCONDITIONAL SURRENDER" and said the goal includes destroying Iran's navy, eliminating its ballistic missile program, and ensuring it cannot obtain a nuclear weapon [source: Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/06/trump-iran-war-unconditional-surrender)
- **Regional escalation:** Strikes have drawn in Lebanon (Hezbollah), disrupted shipping in the Strait of Hormuz and Red Sea, and prompted the U.S. to order non-emergency staff to leave Saudi Arabia [source: CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/09/iran-war-updates-oil-brent-wti-crude-110-gulf-gcc-uae-saudi-arabia-kurds.html)
- **Over 1,750 people killed in Iran** as of March 26, 2026 (week 4); the UN estimates 330,000+ displaced. **47% of Americans oppose** the military action while 40% support it (Emerson poll, March 2026) [source: Al Jazeera death toll tracker](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/1/us-israel-attacks-on-iran-death-toll-and-injuries-live-tracker) | [source: CNN — Day 26](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/26/world/live-news/iran-war-us-israel-trump)
- **Strait of Hormuz near-total shutdown:** 3,000+ vessels stranded as shipping through the world's most critical oil chokepoint has ground to a halt. The US temporarily lifted sanctions on some Iranian oil exports to manage global price spikes [source: CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/09/iran-war-updates-oil-brent-wti-crude-110-gulf-gcc-uae-saudi-arabia-kurds.html)

### [+] Legal Authority

**The administration did not seek or receive congressional authorization.**

The White House relied on Article II of the Constitution, which grants the president authority as commander-in-chief. This is the same justification used for the June 2025 "Operation Midnight Hammer" strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities.

**No AUMF was invoked.** Unlike the 2001 AUMF (targeting al-Qaeda) or the 2002 AUMF (authorizing force against Iraq), there is no congressional authorization specific to Iran. The administration has not publicly argued that any existing AUMF covers the current operations.

**Legal experts are skeptical of the administration's authority.** From a legal analysis at JURIST:

> "There is no authorization, no demonstrated imminence, and no exit plan. The strikes on Iran represent one of the most consequential unilateral presidential military actions in American history."

[source: JURIST — No Authorization, No Imminence, No Plan](https://www.jurist.org/commentary/2026/03/no-authorization-no-imminence-no-plan-the-iran-strikes-and-the-rule-of-law/)

The ACLU stated:

> "Our founding fathers and the Constitution give war authority power to Congress, and Congress alone."

[source: ACLU — Condemns unconstitutional military strikes on Iran](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/aclu-condemns-president-trumps-unconstitutional-military-strikes-on-iran)

**International law experts also question legality.** The strikes lack UN Security Council authorization. The US has stated it is acting under the right of self-defense under the UN Charter, but legal scholars note the strikes appear to be offensive rather than defensive in nature, and Iran had not attacked the US or Israel when the February 28 campaign began.

## Analysis

### What This Means

The United States is engaged in what legal scholars and the ACLU characterize as an **unauthorized war** — the largest unilateral military action by the executive branch since the 2003 invasion of Iraq, itself authorized by Congress under a specific AUMF. The 2026 conflict has no equivalent congressional authorization.

This matters beyond the immediate military situation. When a president can launch a full-scale war — killing a foreign head of state, striking a dozen cities, deploying hundreds of aircraft — without congressional approval, the constitutional requirement that Congress alone declares war becomes effectively meaningless. The War Powers Act of 1973 was specifically written to prevent this after the undeclared wars in Korea and Vietnam. Congress has now failed to pass War Powers resolutions in **four separate votes** across both chambers, each time by narrow margins. On May 1, the 60-day War Powers deadline arrived — and the president simply declared hostilities "terminated" while maintaining a naval blockade and active military operations. The constitutional constraint Congress relied on has been effectively bypassed.

The administration's strategy has shifted from "unconditional surrender" to an indefinite ceasefire backed by a naval blockade — but the underlying question remains: can the president wage an open-ended undeclared war by calling it something else?

### Who's Affected

- **US military personnel:** 13 service members killed, 381+ wounded; 2,200–2,500 additional Marines deployed; Hormuz operations ongoing
- **Iranian civilians:** 3,636+ killed (including 1,701 civilians) as of early May; 330,000+ displaced; civilian infrastructure including schools and hospitals damaged
- **American consumers:** Oil prices above $118/barrel with gas topping $4/gallon; Strait of Hormuz blockade disrupts ~20% of global seaborne oil trade
- **Allies in the region:** Gulf state casualties at 28 killed; US ordered staff to leave Saudi Arabia
- **Israeli civilians:** 1,900+ wounded in Iranian retaliatory missile attacks; Lebanon casualties at 2,618+ killed
- **Americans broadly:** A war without congressional authorization weakens the constitutional checks on executive war-making that protect future administrations from the same precedent — regardless of which party holds power

### [+] Constitutional Questions

**The core constitutional issue:**

Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution gives Congress the power "To declare War." The Founders deliberately placed this power in the legislature rather than the executive to prevent a single person from committing the nation to war.

The War Powers Act of 1973 (50 U.S.C. §§ 1541–1548) was Congress's attempt to codify this principle after Korea and Vietnam:
- The president must notify Congress within 48 hours of deploying forces into hostilities
- Forces must be withdrawn within 60 days unless Congress declares war or authorizes the conflict
- Congress can direct withdrawal at any time by concurrent resolution

**Why this is different from past conflicts:**

Prior administrations have exercised military force under existing AUMFs (the 2001 and 2002 authorizations are still technically in effect and have been stretched to cover many operations). The Iran strikes are not plausibly covered by either:

- The 2001 AUMF targeted al-Qaeda and "associated forces" — not the Iranian government
- The 2002 AUMF authorized force against Iraq, not Iran
- No new AUMF for Iran has been proposed, debated, or passed

The administration's reliance on Article II alone — with no AUMF, no declaration of war, and no imminent threat to US territory — represents an exceptionally broad assertion of unilateral executive war-making power.

**The 60-day clock:** Under the War Powers Act, the 60-day withdrawal deadline begins when hostilities commence. **The deadline arrived on May 1, 2026.** Rather than seek authorization or begin withdrawing forces, Trump wrote to Congress claiming hostilities had "terminated" — while simultaneously maintaining a naval blockade of Iran and conducting military operations in the Strait of Hormuz. Defense Secretary Hegseth argued the ceasefire "pauses" the clock. Legal scholars across multiple institutions rejected both arguments, noting that a blockade is itself an act of war under international law, and that the War Powers Act does not contain a "pause" provision. Congress has not taken legal action to enforce the deadline, and no federal court injunction has been sought. The constitutional constraint that was designed to prevent exactly this scenario has been effectively bypassed.
