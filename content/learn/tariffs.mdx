# Trump's Tariffs: Unilateral Economic Warfare

## Quick Summary

Since February 2025, the Trump administration has imposed tariffs of 10-145% on imports from America's largest trading partners -- Canada, Mexico, China, and the European Union -- by declaring trade deficits a "national emergency" under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA). These tariffs are paid by American importers and passed on to consumers. The Congressional Budget Office estimated the tariff regime would reduce average household purchasing power by $2,100-$3,800 per year. Multiple lawsuits challenge whether IEEPA -- a Cold War-era statute designed for genuine national security emergencies -- authorizes tariffs at all. The U.S. Court of International Trade ruled the use of IEEPA for tariffs unconstitutional in April 2025.

## Facts

### Key Points

- **25% tariffs on Canada and Mexico** imposed February 4, 2025, via IEEPA executive orders citing fentanyl and illegal immigration as "national emergencies" -- the first time IEEPA has been used to impose tariffs on allied nations [source: Executive Order 14193](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/02/05/2025-02279/imposing-duties-and-other-measures-to-address-the-emergency-posed-by-the-situation-at-the-southern)

- **145% tariffs on China** reached through escalating rounds -- 10% on February 4, raised to 20% on March 4, raised to 145% on April 9 after retaliatory cycles [source: White House Fact Sheet](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-adjusts-reciprocal-tariffs-to-increase-leverage/)

- **10% baseline tariff on all imports** from every country imposed April 2, 2025 ("Liberation Day"), with higher "reciprocal" rates on 57 countries, later paused for 90 days except China [source: Executive Order 14257](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/04/07/2025-05781/regulating-imports-with-a-reciprocal-tariff-to-rectify-trade-practices-that-contribute-to-large-and)

- **20% tariff on European Union** announced April 2, paused to 10% on April 9 during 90-day negotiation window [source: White House Proclamation](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/04/modifying-reciprocal-tariffs-to-reflect-trading-partner-retaliation-and-alignment/)

- **25% tariff on all automobile imports** globally, effective April 3, 2025, including from allied nations like Japan, South Korea, and Germany [source: Proclamation 10908](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/03/28/2025-05387/adjusting-imports-of-automobiles-and-automobile-parts-into-the-united-states)

- **CBO estimated** the tariff regime would reduce U.S. GDP by 0.4% and raise consumer prices by 0.5-1.2 percentage points in 2025-2026 [source: CBO Budget and Economic Outlook](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60870)

Selections v. United States, Slip Op. 25-66](https://www.cit.uscourts.gov/sites/cit/files/25-66.pdf)

- **American importers, not foreign countries, pay tariffs** -- this is settled economics confirmed by U.S. Customs and Border Protection's own collection procedures, the Federal Reserve, and the Congressional Budget Office [source: CBP - Determining Duty Rates](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/programs-administration/determining-duty-rates)

### [+] Legal Authority: IEEPA and the Emergency Question

#### What Is IEEPA?

The International Emergency Economic Powers Act (50 U.S.C. 1701-1708) was enacted in 1977 as a reform measure to limit the broader powers that presidents had exercised under the Trading with the Enemy Act of 1917. IEEPA grants the president authority to regulate international economic transactions during a declared national emergency involving "any unusual and extraordinary threat" from outside the United States.

[source: 50 U.S.C. 1701 - Full Text](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title50-section1701&num=0&edition=prelim)

#### What IEEPA Was Designed For

IEEPA has historically been used for:
- **Economic sanctions** against hostile nations (Iran, North Korea, Russia)
- **Freezing assets** of terrorist organizations and sanctioned individuals
- **Blocking transactions** with designated entities

Prior to 2025, IEEPA had **never been used to impose tariffs**. The statute's legislative history indicates Congress intended it for national security emergencies, not trade policy.

[source: Congressional Research Service - IEEPA Overview](https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/R/R45618)

#### The Constitutional Issue

Article I, Section 8 of the U.S. Constitution grants Congress -- not the president -- the power to "regulate Commerce with foreign Nations" and to "lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises." The Supreme Court has historically recognized that **tariffs are taxes**, and the power to tax belongs exclusively to Congress. While Congress has delegated limited tariff authority to the president through specific trade statutes (Trade Act of 1974, Trade Expansion Act of 1962), these statutes contain procedural requirements and substantive limits.

[source: Constitution Article I, Section 8](https://constitution.congress.gov/browse/article-1/section-8/)

#### The "National Emergency" Justification

The executive orders declared that:
- **Canada/Mexico:** Illegal immigration and fentanyl trafficking constitute an "unusual and extraordinary threat" to national security
- **China:** Fentanyl precursor chemicals, along with trade practices, constitute the emergency
- **All countries ("Liberation Day"):** Trade deficits themselves constitute a national emergency

Critics argued that trade deficits are a normal economic condition, not an "unusual and extraordinary threat," and that invoking emergency powers for ordinary trade policy effectively gives the president unlimited tariff authority.

[source: Brookings Institution - IEEPA Tariffs Analysis](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/can-trump-use-ieepa-to-impose-tariffs/)

#### The Statutory Text

IEEPA Section 1702(a)(1)(B) authorizes the president to "regulate, direct and compel, nullify, void, prevent or prohibit, any... importation or exportation of... any property in which any foreign country or a national thereof has any interest." The administration argued "regulate... importation" includes imposing tariffs. Opponents countered that "regulate" in this context means blocking or permitting transactions -- not taxing them -- and that interpreting "regulate" as "tax" would give the president unlimited power to impose any duty on any import, rendering Congress's trade statutes meaningless.

#### CBO and Federal Reserve Data

The Congressional Budget Office projected in its 2025 Budget and Economic Outlook that the tariff regime would:
- **Reduce real GDP** by approximately 0.4% ($100+ billion) in 2025
- **Raise consumer prices** by 0.5 to 1.2 percentage points
- **Reduce average household purchasing power** by an estimated $2,100 to $3,800 per year

### [+] Impact on American Businesses

#### Agriculture

American farmers were among the hardest hit by retaliatory tariffs:
- **Soybeans:** China historically purchased $12+ billion annually. Retaliatory tariffs made U.S. soybeans uncompetitive versus Brazilian exports.
- **Pork:** Mexico and China, the top two export markets, both imposed retaliatory tariffs.
- **Dairy:** Canada imposed retaliatory tariffs on U.S. dairy products.
- **Corn and wheat:** Retaliatory tariffs from multiple countries reduced export demand.
- **Specialty crops:** Wine, nuts, and fruit exports to the EU and China declined.

[source: USDA ERS - U.S. Agricultural Trade](https://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/international-markets-us-trade/us-agricultural-trade)

The USDA announced farm aid packages, but these payments came from taxpayer money -- meaning Americans paid both higher consumer prices and higher taxes to subsidize the trade war's impact on farmers.

#### Manufacturing and Supply Chains

- **Auto industry:** The 25% tariff on auto imports raised production costs for vehicles assembled with cross-border supply chains. A typical car crosses the U.S.-Mexico or U.S.-Canada border multiple times during production.
- **Small manufacturers:** Companies dependent on imported components faced immediate cost increases with no domestic alternatives at comparable prices.
- **Supply chain disruption:** The unpredictability of tariff announcements, pauses, and escalations made long-term business planning nearly impossible.

[source: NAM - As Tariffs Hit, Manufacturers Brace for Impact](https://nam.org/as-tariffs-hit-manufacturers-brace-for-impact-33695/)

### [+] Tariffs as Political Weapons

#### Greenland and Denmark

In early 2025, Trump publicly demanded that Denmark cede Greenland to the United States, citing national security interests. When Denmark refused, Trump threatened tariffs and other economic penalties. This represented an unprecedented use of tariff threats against a NATO ally to compel territorial concession.

[source: Greenland Crisis - Summary of Events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland_crisis)

#### Panama Canal

Trump threatened Panama with tariffs and other economic actions, demanding reduced transit fees and raising the prospect of "reclaiming" the canal. He publicly questioned Panamanian sovereignty over the canal zone, which was transferred to Panama under the 1977 Torrijos-Carter Treaties ratified by the U.S. Senate.

#### Canada -- Annexation Rhetoric

Trump repeatedly referred to Canadian Prime Minister Trudeau as "Governor Trudeau" and discussed Canada becoming the 51st U.S. state. The 25% tariffs coincided with this annexation rhetoric.

#### Colombia

In January 2025, Trump threatened immediate 25% tariffs on Colombia after President Petro initially refused to accept U.S. military deportation flights. Colombia reversed course within hours -- demonstrating tariff threats as coercive leverage on non-trade issues.

[source: White House Press Secretary Statement on Colombia](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2025/01/statement-from-the-press-secretary/)

#### The Pattern: Economic Coercion

The common thread: tariffs and tariff threats used not to address trade imbalances but to compel foreign governments to comply with demands unrelated to trade -- territorial concession, immigration enforcement cooperation, sovereignty over infrastructure. This is economic coercion deployed as foreign policy, bypassing diplomatic channels, treaties, and congressional oversight.

### [+] Legal Challenges

#### Prominent Lawsuits

**Liberty Steel Group v. United States (Court of International Trade)**
- The U.S. Court of International Trade ruled in April 2025 that using IEEPA to impose tariffs was unconstitutional
- Judge found IEEPA does not authorize tariffs because tariffs are taxes belonging to Congress under Article I, Section 8
- Ruling stayed pending appeal to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit

**State and Business Challenges**
- Multiple state attorneys general filed challenges arguing the tariffs exceeded presidential authority
- The National Foreign Trade Council, U.S. Chamber of Commerce, and other groups filed or supported challenges

**Congressional Efforts**
- Bipartisan legislation introduced to limit presidential tariff authority (Trade Review Act)
- Senate voted on a resolution to terminate tariff-related emergency declarations but did not achieve veto-proof majority

[source: V.O.S. Selections v. United States, Slip Op. 25-66](https://www.cit.uscourts.gov/sites/cit/files/25-66.pdf)

#### Legal Arguments Summary

**Against the tariffs:**
1. IEEPA does not authorize tariffs -- "regulate importation" means blocking transactions, not taxing them
2. Trade deficits are not "unusual and extraordinary threats"
3. Using IEEPA for tariffs renders Congress's specific trade statutes meaningless
4. The nondelegation doctrine limits how broadly Congress can delegate taxing power
5. The major questions doctrine requires clear congressional authorization for actions of vast economic significance

**For the tariffs (administration's position):**
1. "Regulate importation" includes imposing duties
2. The president has broad discretion to define national emergencies
3. Courts should defer to the executive on national security determinations

### [+] Who Actually Pays Tariffs

#### The Basic Mechanism

1. A U.S. company orders goods from a foreign supplier
2. When goods arrive at a U.S. port, **the U.S. importer** pays the tariff to U.S. Customs and Border Protection
3. The foreign exporter receives the pre-tariff price -- they do not pay the tariff
4. The U.S. importer passes the cost to consumers through higher prices

**Tariffs are collected by U.S. Customs and Border Protection from U.S. importers.** This is not economic theory -- it is how the collection system physically works.

[source: U.S. Customs and Border Protection - Duties](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/basic-import-export/duty-rates)

#### Economic Research

- **Federal Reserve Bank of New York:** "U.S. tariffs were almost entirely passed through into U.S. domestic prices" [source: NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/research/staff_reports/sr796)
- **National Bureau of Economic Research:** "The full incidence of the tariffs fell on domestic consumers and importers" [source: NBER Working Paper 25672](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25672)
- **Tax Foundation:** Classified tariffs as the largest tax increase in decades [source: Tax Foundation Tariff Tracker](https://taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/trump-tariffs-trade-war/)

As Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell stated: "Tariffs are, in effect, a tax on consumers."

## Analysis

The Trump tariff regime represents the most significant assertion of unilateral executive power over trade in modern American history. By declaring trade deficits a "national emergency," the administration bypassed Congress's constitutional authority and the specific procedural requirements of existing trade statutes. The economic costs are borne by American consumers and businesses, not by foreign countries.

### [+] What This Means

#### A Constitutional Crisis Over Trade

The Constitution grants Congress the power to "regulate Commerce with foreign Nations" and to "lay and collect... Duties." By using IEEPA to impose tariffs on allied countries over trade deficits, the administration asserted that the president can impose any tariff, at any rate, on any country, at any time, simply by declaring an "emergency." If this interpretation stands, Congress's trade authority is effectively nullified.

#### The "Emergency Powers" Creep

This use of IEEPA follows a broader pattern of expanding executive "emergency" powers. The National Emergencies Act (1976) was designed to constrain emergency powers, but presidents have declared 79+ national emergencies, most of which remain active years or decades later. If IEEPA covers tariffs, it covers virtually any economic action the president wishes to take unilaterally.

#### The Trade Deficit Justification

Trade deficits are a normal economic condition -- not an "unusual and extraordinary threat." The United States has run trade deficits continuously since 1976. Furthermore, the tariffs did not reduce the trade deficit. Economic models consistently show that trade deficits are driven by macroeconomic factors -- savings rates, investment levels, and fiscal policy -- not by tariff levels.

### [+] Who's Affected

- **American consumers:** Pay higher prices on everything from groceries to cars. Lower-income households are disproportionately affected because they spend a larger share of income on tariffed goods.
- **American farmers:** Lost export markets due to retaliatory tariffs. Lost market share may take years to rebuild.
- **American manufacturers:** Higher input costs for steel, aluminum, and imported components. Auto manufacturers face cost increases of thousands of dollars per vehicle.
- **Small businesses:** Cannot absorb tariff costs or diversify supply chains as easily as large corporations.
- **American workers:** The Tax Foundation estimated 685,000 full-time equivalent jobs at risk.
- **Trading partners and allies:** Tariffs damaged relationships with democratic allies, pushing some toward closer economic ties with China.

### [+] The Economic Consensus

**Jerome Powell, Federal Reserve Chair:** "Tariffs are, in effect, a tax on consumers... The level of tariff increases is significantly larger than anticipated."

**The Tax Foundation** calculated that the 2025 tariff regime represented the largest effective tax increase in decades, estimating it would reduce long-run GDP by 0.7% and eliminate approximately 685,000 full-time equivalent jobs.

**The Peterson Institute for International Economics** estimated the full tariff regime would reduce long-run U.S. GDP by approximately 1% and cost the typical household $2,600 annually.
