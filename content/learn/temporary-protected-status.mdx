# Temporary Protected Status Under Threat

## Quick Summary

Temporary Protected Status (TPS) shields people from deportation when their home countries are unsafe due to war or disaster, and lets them work legally. On **June 25, 2026**, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in *Mullin v. Doe* that **courts cannot review** the Department of Homeland Security's decision to end TPS — foreclosing challenges to how those terminations are carried out. The immediate effect: protections end for **roughly 350,000 Haitians and about 4,000 Syrians**. Advocates warn the ruling paves the way to strip status from up to **1.3 million people from 17 countries**, including Venezuela, Somalia, and Ethiopia. Justice Kagan, joined by Sotomayor and Jackson, dissented.

### Key Points

- The Court held that a provision of the TPS statute bars judicial review of DHS's procedural compliance when ending status [source: CRS](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11446)
- The majority also rejected an equal-protection claim, finding insufficient evidence that race motivated the decision
- Roughly **1.3 million** people from 17 countries currently hold TPS and could be affected by further terminations [source: RAICES](https://raicestexas.org/live-analysis/scotus-allows-tps-termination)

## Analysis

### What This Means

TPS holders have often lived and worked in the U.S. legally for years — some for over a decade. By removing judicial review, the decision makes it far harder to challenge terminations even when the process is flawed, shifting the entire question to the political branches.

### Who's Affected

- **Current TPS holders** — hundreds of thousands lose work authorization and face deportation to countries the U.S. itself has deemed unsafe
- **Employers and communities** that depend on long-settled TPS workers
- **Future designations** — the ruling weakens a core humanitarian protection for people from any country in crisis
